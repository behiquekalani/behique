## Script 1

**Hook:** How to turn your job skills into your first digital product. (5 sec)

**Main Point:** (45 sec)
Your job gives you three things that are hard to fake: process knowledge, mistake knowledge, and insider language.

Process knowledge is how things actually work in your field, not how textbooks say they work. That gap is your product.

Mistake knowledge is what not to do. Most beginners make the same mistakes. You've already made them. A guide that helps someone avoid what took you a year to learn is genuinely valuable.

Insider language is how professionals in your field actually talk. Using the right terminology builds instant credibility with your target audience.

Combine those three elements into a guide or template, price it at $47 or higher, and you have a legitimate digital product based on experience that no AI can replicate.

## Script 2

**Hook:** Why a small email list beats a large social following every single time. (5 sec)

**Main Point:** (45 sec)
Three reasons your email list is worth more than your follower count.

Reason one: you own it. A social platform can change its algorithm, reduce your reach, or suspend your account. Your email list cannot be taken from you. It's an asset, not a rented audience.

Reason two: attention is higher. The average email open rate is 20 to 40 percent. The average organic social reach is under two percent. When someone opens your email, they've committed a real moment of attention. That attention converts better.

Reason three: personalization scales. You can segment your list, send different messages to different groups, and talk directly to someone's specific situation. Social media is broadcast. Email is conversation.

Start building your list from the very first day.

**CTA:** Save this and add an email sign-up to your content stack. (5 sec)

## Script 3

**Hook:** The mindset shift that changes how you think about pricing forever. (5 sec)

**Main Point:** (45 sec)
Most people think about price from the seller's perspective. "How much did I put into this?" or "What's the minimum people will pay?"

The right frame is the buyer's perspective. "What is this outcome worth to this specific person?"

If your product helps someone get their first client, and that client pays them $3,000, your product is worth hundreds of dollars to that person. Not $27.

If your template saves a business owner four hours per week, and their time is worth $100 per hour, your template is worth $400 per month in recovered time. Price accordingly.

Stop pricing based on what's in the file. Price based on what changes in the buyer's life. That's the frame that changes everything.
