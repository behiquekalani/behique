## Script 1

**Hook (5s):** Three questions that help you write a better hook every single time.

**Main Point (45s):**
Question one: would I stop scrolling for this? If your honest answer is no, rewrite it. The standard is not "is this informative." The standard is "does this stop a moving thumb." Those are different bars. Question two: does this promise a specific outcome or provoke a specific emotion? Vague hooks fail. Specific hooks and emotional hooks succeed. "Three ways to grow your business" is vague. "Why your sales page is losing you money every day" is specific and emotional. Know the difference. Question three: is there a reason to keep reading beyond the first sentence? A great hook creates a gap between what the reader knows and what they want to know. That gap pulls them forward. If the hook answers its own question, there is no reason to continue. Leave the gap open and close it in the body. Practice these three questions on every piece of content you write this week.

## Script 2

**Hook (5s):** Why time is your most underpriced asset when you work for yourself.

**Main Point (45s):**
When you work for someone else, your time has a fixed price. An hourly rate or an annual salary. When you work for yourself, the value of your time can fluctuate dramatically based on what you spend it on. An hour writing a sales page might generate ten thousand dollars in sales over the next six months. An hour answering emails might generate zero. Most solo business owners do not think about this clearly enough. They fill their day with the low-value tasks that feel productive without asking whether this hour is creating a future return. The practice of ruthlessly identifying your highest-value hour and protecting it is what separates the solo business that grows from the solo business that grinds without progress. Time audit your last week. Identify the one hour that generated the most downstream value. Then protect more of your days for that type of hour.

## Script 3

**Hook (5s):** Three trust signals every digital product needs to earn the first sale.

**Main Point (45s):**
Trust signal one: social proof. A testimonial, a review, a case study. Real words from a real person who got a real result. If you are pre-launch and have none, offer the product free to two or three people in your target audience and collect their honest feedback. That feedback becomes your first social proof. Trust signal two: visible expertise. Something on your sales page or about page that demonstrates you have earned the right to teach this topic. A track record, a result, a credential, or a compelling personal story. Buyers need to believe you know what you are talking about before they hand over money. Trust signal three: low-friction guarantee. A clear and generous refund policy removes the financial risk from the buyer's decision. When someone knows they can get their money back if the product does not deliver, the barrier to purchase drops significantly. All three of these signals can be added to any sales page in an afternoon.
