## Short 1 — Comfort as Ceiling
**Title:** Comfort Is Not The Goal — It's The Ceiling (Here's Why)
**Hook (0-3s):** Comfort is not the goal. Comfort is the ceiling.
**Body (3-55s):** This reframe changes everything about how you make daily decisions. Your brain is built to conserve energy. It will always vote for the path of least resistance. Left unchecked, this drive shrinks your world. Every time you choose the easier option, you're also drawing the boundary of who you can become. The person you're capable of being exists on the other side of the friction you keep avoiding. This is not about dramatic suffering. It's about the small daily choice to do the hard thing when the easy thing is right there. Make that choice often enough and your threshold for difficulty expands permanently. That expansion is the only growth that lasts.

## Short 2 — The Attention Tax
**Title:** Your Phone Is Costing You More Than You Think (The Real Math)
**Hook (0-3s):** Your phone is not a tool. It's a leash.
**Body (3-55s):** Here's the math most people haven't run. The average person checks their phone 96 times per day. Research shows each interruption costs 23 minutes of cognitive recovery time. That's 2,208 minutes per day in lost recovery time. Which is 36 hours. Per week. A full work week, every single week, lost entirely to notification-induced friction. And that's before counting the quality degradation of the work you do in a fractured state. Deep work requires uninterrupted blocks. You can't reach flow in 10-minute intervals. The builders who protect their attention produce months of work in weeks. This is not metaphor. It's literally a time multiplication.
**End screen (55-60s):** Subscribe. Screenshot your weekly screen time and post it below. That number is telling you something.

## Short 3 — Building in Public
**Title:** Why Hiding Your Work Is Keeping You Stuck (Build in Public)
**Hook (0-3s):** The biggest mistake builders make is hiding their work.
**Body (3-55s):** Perfectionism is fear in a productive costume. It feels like high standards. It operates like avoidance. The people who build the biggest audiences and the most successful products are not the people who waited until it was perfect. They're the people who started messy and iterated in public. Building in public does three things simultaneously. It forces accountability. It generates early feedback that saves you from building the wrong thing. And it builds an audience before you ever have a product to sell. The messy middle of a build is actually more valuable than the polished launch. People connect to process. They buy from builders they've watched grow.
**End screen (55-60s):** Subscribe. What are you building right now? Start building in public in the comments today.
