## Script 1

**Hook (5s):** Three content types that build audiences and generate sales at the same time.

**Main Point (45s):**
Type one: problem-naming content. You describe a frustration your audience has in more detail than they can articulate themselves. When someone reads it and thinks "that is exactly it," they follow you because you understand them. That understanding is trust. Type two: process content. You show exactly how you do something. Step by step, no fluff. This positions you as someone who has done the thing, not just someone who talks about it. Proof of competence drives sales. Type three: perspective content. You share an opinion or a belief about your field that is slightly different from the conventional wisdom. Perspective content attracts the people who already think like you. Those people are your best customers because alignment was there from the start.

## Script 2

**Hook (5s):** Why most solo business owners stall at the same point, and how to push through it.

**Main Point (45s):**
The stall almost always happens between the first win and the first system. You get one sale, maybe a few. You feel validated. Then the next question hits: how do I do this reliably? And without an answer, momentum dies. Here is what pushes through it. First, document what worked. Write down exactly what you did to get your first customer. Every message, every post, every platform. That documentation becomes your first system. Second, repeat the exact same process ten more times before you change anything. You cannot optimize what you have not tested more than once. Third, identify the one bottleneck. Is it traffic? Is it the offer? Is it the sales page? Fix one bottleneck at a time. Most stalls are caused by trying to fix everything at once.

## Script 3

**Hook (5s):** Three pricing mistakes that are costing your solo business real money.

**Main Point (45s):**
Mistake one: pricing based on your time. Buyers do not care how long something took you to make. They care what it does for them. Price on outcome, not hours. If your product saves someone twenty hours a month, it is worth hundreds of dollars even if you built it in a weekend. Mistake two: anchoring your price to your own income level. If a thousand dollars sounds like a lot to you, you will struggle to charge it. Your buyer's financial reality is different from yours. Research the market and price accordingly. Mistake three: never raising prices. Your first price should be your lowest price. As your reputation grows, your prices should grow with it. Creators who never raise prices leave years of income on the table. Review your pricing every quarter and adjust.
