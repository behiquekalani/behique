## Script 1

**Hook (5s):** The framework for writing hooks that stop people from scrolling.

**Main Point (45s):**
A great hook does one of three things. It creates curiosity by starting an incomplete idea. It triggers recognition by naming a specific pain the reader is experiencing. Or it makes a bold, counterintuitive claim that challenges what they already believe.

The structure of most high-performing hooks follows a simple pattern. State an unexpected truth or an uncomfortable question. Make it specific, not vague. And keep it to one sentence.

The thing most people get wrong is that they write hooks about themselves. "I learned something amazing." Nobody cares yet. Write hooks about the reader's situation and what they stand to gain or avoid. Make the reader the center of the hook, not you.

## Script 2

**Hook (5s):** How to build a solo business around your actual lifestyle, not someone else's model.

**Main Point (45s):**
The problem with most business advice is that it describes someone else's ideal day. Wake up at 5am, cold plunge, two hours of deep work before the family wakes. That works for some people. For others it is a recipe for resentment and eventual abandonment.

Start with your real constraints. What hours do you actually have? What work do you genuinely enjoy doing versus tolerate? What would make you proud to explain to someone you respect?

Design the business around honest answers to those questions, not around the aspirational identity you think you should have. A business built around your real life will outlast one built around someone else's ideal. Sustainability is a strategy.

## Script 3

**Hook (5s):** Three truths about building an audience that nobody wants to say out loud.

**Main Point (45s):**
Truth one: Most of your early followers will never buy from you. They followed for a piece of content, not because they are your ideal customer. This is fine. Build the audience anyway. The right buyers emerge over time.

Truth two: The people who comment the most are rarely your best customers. Engaged commenters are often other creators in your space, not buyers. Your best customers often lurk, say very little publicly, and then buy when the offer is right.

Truth three: You will outgrow early followers as your content gets sharper. As you get clearer about who you serve, some early followers will leave. That is not failure. It is refinement. A smaller, more aligned audience outperforms a larger, scattered one every time.
