## Script 1

**Hook (5s):** Three ways to find your niche if you have no idea where to start.

**Main Point (45s):**
Method one: look at your search history. The things you have spent the most time learning about are your natural areas of depth. When you have consumed dozens of articles, videos, and books on a topic, you have built expertise whether you realize it or not. Method two: look at the problems people bring to you. If friends, colleagues, or strangers online keep asking you for the same type of advice, that is the market telling you something. The questions other people ask you reveal the gap between your knowledge and theirs. Method three: look at your own painful past. Every problem you struggled with and eventually solved is a potential niche. Your personal transformation is the proof of concept. You are not just selling information. You are selling the road out of a place your future customer is currently stuck.

## Script 2

**Hook (5s):** Why building an audience before you have a product is a smart strategy.

**Main Point (45s):**
Most people build the product first and then try to find buyers. The smarter sequence is the reverse. When you build an audience first, three things happen. First, you get market research for free. The comments, questions, and engagement on your content tell you exactly what problems your audience wants solved. You are not guessing what to build. You are responding to what they are already asking for. Second, you build trust before you ask for money. An audience that has been following you for months already believes in your expertise. The conversion rate on a product launch to a warm audience is dramatically higher than to cold traffic. Third, you validate demand before you invest in production. A simple poll, a question post, or a waitlist tells you if people will actually pay before you spend weeks building.

## Script 3

**Hook (5s):** Three habits that separate consistent creators from inconsistent ones.

**Main Point (45s):**
Habit one: they write before they edit. Consistent creators produce a first draft without judging it. The internal critic is silenced during creation and invited in only during revision. Mixing creation and editing in the same moment kills output. Habit two: they set a publishing schedule and treat it like an appointment with a client. Not a suggestion, not a goal for the week. A commitment. Missing a self-imposed deadline once makes it easier to miss the next one. The schedule is the accountability structure when there is no external boss. Habit three: they batch related work. Research sessions, writing sessions, and editing sessions are kept separate. This reduces the cost of context switching and allows you to reach deeper focus in each mode. These are not glamorous habits. But they are the ones that actually produce a body of work over time.
