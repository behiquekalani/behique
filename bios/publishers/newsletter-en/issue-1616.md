# Email Sequences That Actually Sell

Most email sequences fail because they were written to impress rather than to serve. They show off the product instead of solving the reader's problem first.

## The Structure That Works

The best sequences follow a simple arc. Email one earns attention by delivering immediate value. Email two deepens the problem the reader already knows they have. Email three introduces the solution, not your product, but the conceptual solution. Email four shows proof. Email five makes the offer.

By the time you make the offer, the reader has already been helped three times. They are not being sold. They are being invited.

## Why Most Sequences Feel Gross

Bad sequences start selling in email one. The reader signed up for something useful and immediately got a pitch. Trust is broken before it was built. This is the fundamental mistake.

The goal is to be so useful that buying feels like a logical continuation. Not a pivot.

## Writing the Individual Emails

Each email should do one thing. Not three things. One. If you cannot describe the purpose of an email in a single sentence, rewrite it.

Keep them short. Most business emails are too long because the writer did not take time to cut. A three-paragraph email that lands one idea performs better than a ten-paragraph essay that makes five points.

## This Week's Action

Map your existing welcome sequence or write one from scratch. For each email, write one sentence describing the one thing it does. If any email does not have a clear purpose, cut it or rewrite it.
