# The Mental Model That Changes How You Work

Most people optimize for effort. Winners optimize for output.

## The Idea

There is a mental model called "return on time invested." It is simple. Before starting any task, ask: what is the actual return I get if this works? Not just money. Impact, learning, audience growth, business foundation. Anything that compounds. Then ask: how much time will this take?

Divide the output by the input. That ratio tells you whether the task deserves your attention or not. Most to-do lists are full of low-ratio work. Admin. Minor edits. Emails. Meetings. These feel productive because they generate activity. They rarely generate momentum.

High-ratio work looks different. Writing a cornerstone piece of content that drives traffic for two years. Building a product once that sells indefinitely. Creating a system that removes a recurring task from your schedule. These take more upfront investment but the return per hour is orders of magnitude higher.

The reason most people avoid high-ratio work is that it is harder. It requires clarity, deep focus, and often a tolerance for uncertainty. Low-ratio work is easier because it has clear completion criteria. You know when the email is sent. You never know exactly when the long-form essay will pay off.

Train yourself to choose the harder, higher-return work first. Every day.

## Why It Matters

Optimizing for effort means you get tired. Optimizing for output means you get results. The shift changes which tasks you start with, how you evaluate your week, and whether you feel like you are building something or just maintaining something. One direction compounds. The other does not.

## One Action

At the start of your next work session, write down the three most important tasks you could do. Rank them by expected return, not urgency. Work on the highest-return task first, even if it is uncomfortable. Do this for one week and notice the difference.
