# Mental Models That Change How You See Problems

The quality of your thinking determines the quality of your decisions. Thinking tools matter.

## First Principles vs. Analogy
Most people reason by analogy. "This worked for someone else, so I'll try it." First principles thinking breaks the problem to its foundation. "What is actually true here? What are the constraints? What would the solution look like if I ignored how it's been done before?"

Analogy is fast and often wrong. First principles is slow and often right. The skill is knowing which one the situation calls for. Most business problems benefit from first principles at the strategy level and analogy at the execution level.

## Inversion
Instead of asking "how do I succeed?" ask "how would I fail?" List every way this could go wrong. Then eliminate those risks. Inversion surfaces the non-obvious. The obstacles you don't see are the ones that stop you.

This is not pessimism. It's preparation. The person who has thought through failure is the person who knows how to avoid it.

## Second-Order Thinking
Every decision has first-order effects and second-order effects. The first-order effect is obvious. The second-order effect is where things get interesting. If I lower my price, sales might go up. That's first order. But my profit per unit drops, my customer quality declines, and I need more volume to sustain the business. That's second order.

Most decisions look different at second order. Think two steps ahead before committing.

## This Week's Action
Take one current business decision. Apply inversion. Write down five ways it could fail. For each failure mode, write one thing you can do now to reduce that risk.
