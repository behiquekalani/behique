# Issue 2548: Price Is a Positioning Signal

**The Idea:** Most people underprice because they are afraid of rejection. They set low prices to minimize the risk of someone saying no. But low prices send a signal that has nothing to do with affordability. Low prices signal low value.

When someone sees a $9 product, they do not think "great deal." They think "this must not be that valuable." When they see a $97 product on the same topic, they think "this person has done the work." The price creates an expectation before they ever open the product.

Pricing is positioning. It communicates your confidence in your own work. It signals who your ideal customer is. Premium prices attract buyers who take implementation seriously. Low prices attract browsers who complain about the smallest issues.

This is not about charging more for the same thing. It is about recognizing that the price is part of the product experience. A $497 course gets treated differently than a $27 course by the same buyer. They do the work when they paid more for it.

The practical implication: price based on the value the outcome delivers, not the time it took you to create the product. A ten-page PDF that saves someone $10,000 is worth far more than $20.

**Why It Matters:** Underpricing does not just hurt revenue. It attracts the wrong customers, devalues your work in the market, and trains buyers to expect less from you.

**One Action:** Look at your lowest-priced product or offer. Ask whether the price reflects the value of the outcome. If not, raise it today and update the positioning to match.
