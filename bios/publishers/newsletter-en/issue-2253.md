# How to Price What You Know

Low prices do not attract more buyers. They attract worse ones.

## The Idea

Most first-time creators underprice everything. They set $7 price tags on products that solve $500 problems because they are afraid of rejection. The irony is that low prices often produce fewer sales, not more, because they signal low value.

Pricing is communication. A $7 guide says "this might help a little." A $97 guide says "this solves a real problem." Both might be the same document. The price shapes how the buyer perceives the outcome before they even open it.

Price based on the value of the outcome, not on the hours you spent building it. If your product helps someone land a client worth $5,000, charging $50 is giving away value. Charging $300 is still a ten-to-one return for the buyer. That is an easy yes.

The practical formula: find out what the problem costs your buyer in money, time, or stress. Price your solution at ten to twenty percent of that number. A product that helps someone save fifteen hours a week is worth a hundred dollars even if it took you one day to build.

High prices also attract better buyers. People who pay more take the product more seriously. They implement it. They get results. They leave better testimonials. They are less likely to refund. Better buyers create better social proof, which makes the next sale easier.

The fear of high prices is really a fear of being told no. Being told no by a stranger on the internet is not a problem. It is information.

## Why It Matters

Pricing correctly is the difference between a side project and a real business. Underpricing keeps you working more for less. The right price rewards both you and the buyer.

## One Action

Pick your lowest-priced product. Research what the outcome is worth to the buyer. Calculate ten to twenty percent of that number. If your current price is lower, raise it this week and see what happens to conversion.
