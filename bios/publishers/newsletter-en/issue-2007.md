# Issue 2007: The Problem With Having Options

**One insight. No fluff.**

Options feel like freedom. They feel like power. In small doses they are both of those things. Past a certain threshold, having too many options is a trap that looks exactly like abundance.

When you have ten viable paths forward, you do not commit fully to any of them. You keep one foot in each direction, which means you move in none of them with real force. The opportunity cost is invisible because each option you are not pursuing looks like it might be the better one. You stay in a permanent state of potential, which is comfortable and produces nothing.

Constraints force commitment, and commitment is where the interesting work happens. The person who says yes to one thing and no to everything else builds depth. The person who keeps all options open builds breadth but stays shallow. Both can feel like progress from the inside. The difference shows up in what you actually produce over a five-year window. Depth compounds. Breadth stays flat. The counterintuitive move is to close doors. Not because the other options are bad, but because open doors bleed focus slowly enough that you never notice the bleeding.
