# Issue 1866: What Nobody Tells You About Shipping Fast

**One insight. No fluff.**

Speed is not just an efficiency gain. It is a feedback mechanism. Every time you ship something quickly, you get information that no amount of internal deliberation could produce. You learn what people actually want versus what you assumed they wanted. The faster you ship, the faster that gap closes. Slow builders are not more careful. They are just more afraid.

The perfectionist instinct is real and it sounds reasonable. "I want to get it right." What that usually means is "I want to avoid the discomfort of being wrong in public." Getting it right in private is not getting it right. It is rehearsing in a vacuum. Real quality emerges from shipping, getting feedback, and iterating. The first version is not supposed to be the best version. It is supposed to exist.

The people building things that matter have learned to tolerate imperfection as part of the process. They ship the rough version, own the gaps, and improve in public. This is not recklessness. It is the correct understanding of how quality actually develops. Done and out in the world beats perfect and in your head every time.
