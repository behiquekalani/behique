# Issue 2016: The Hidden Cost of Context Switching

**One insight. No fluff.**

Every time you switch tasks, there is a loading cost. Your brain does not snap cleanly from one context to the next. It takes ten to twenty minutes to fully engage with something new, which means a day full of small task switches is a day where you never go deep on anything. You are paying the loading cost over and over without ever reaching the depth where the interesting work happens.

This is not a hardware limitation you can train around. It is how cognition works. The implication is that if you are doing work that requires real thinking, the structure of your time matters as much as the quantity. Four hours of uninterrupted focus produces more useful output than eight hours of fragmented attention. Most knowledge workers have this inverted because fragmented attention is easier to schedule and easier to justify.

The practical move is to protect blocks of time that are long enough to matter. Not 25 minutes. Ninety minutes minimum, with no interruption by design. Put your hardest problem in that block. Schedule everything else around it, not the other way. This feels selfish when you first do it because other people's urgency seems to claim priority. It is not selfish. It is the condition under which good work is actually possible.
