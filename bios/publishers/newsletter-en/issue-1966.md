# Issue 1966: Specificity Is the Skill Nobody Teaches

**One insight. No fluff.**

Vague goals produce vague results. Not because of some motivational law, but because your brain cannot act on something it cannot picture. "Get better at writing" is not a goal. "Write one 300-word essay every morning for 30 days" is. The first one stays a feeling. The second one becomes a practice.

Specificity forces you to confront the actual obstacle. When you name the exact thing you want to do, the exact obstacle usually becomes visible immediately. And that is uncomfortable. Vague goals let you want things without feeling the friction of pursuing them. Specificity removes that escape hatch.

This applies to projects, relationships, and skills equally. The builder who ships asks: what is the smallest version of this that proves the idea? The person who never ships asks: how do I build the big vision? Both questions feel like progress. One produces a product. The more precisely you can define what done looks like, the less room there is for your brain to stall you with open-ended dread.
