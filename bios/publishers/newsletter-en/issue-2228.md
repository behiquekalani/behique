# How to Price Your First Digital Product

Pricing too low doesn't make you more competitive. It makes you less credible.

## The Idea

The default instinct for first-time digital product creators is to price low. Under $10. Maybe free, to "build an audience." The logic sounds reasonable: low barrier, more buyers, more feedback, more trust.

The problem is that low prices attract low-commitment buyers. They download, they don't implement, they don't get results. You end up with 200 customers who never opened the product and no testimonials worth using.

Pricing is positioning. A $97 product is perceived differently than a $9 one, even if the content is identical. Higher prices attract people who are serious about the outcome. Serious buyers take action. Buyers who take action get results. Results become testimonials. Testimonials sell the next product.

That said, pricing isn't arbitrary. It should reflect the value of the outcome your product enables. A template that saves 10 hours per week is worth more than $15. A guide that helps someone land a $2,000 client is worth more than $29. Price based on value delivered, not hours worked or pages written.

For most first digital products, $27 to $97 is a solid range. It's accessible enough to not require a sales call but meaningful enough to attract someone who intends to use it. Test a price. Adjust after your first 20 sales.

## Why It Matters

You're not just selling a file. You're selling an outcome. Price the outcome, not the object. This mental shift changes how you write your sales copy, too.

## One Action

Take one product you're considering building or have already built. Calculate the real value of the outcome it delivers. Set your price at 5 to 10 percent of that outcome value. Adjust only if the market pushes back.
