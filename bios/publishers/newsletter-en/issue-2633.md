# Issue 2633: The Hook Is a Contract

**The Idea:** The first line of anything you publish is a contract with the reader. It tells them what they are about to get and whether it is worth their time. If the contract is vague or dishonest, they leave.

Most creators treat the hook as a trick. Something to manufacture curiosity or create false urgency. That approach works once and destroys trust on every repeat visit. The reader clicks, finds that the content did not deliver what the hook promised, and learns not to trust you.

A strong hook makes a specific, honest claim. It identifies a problem the reader has or a belief they hold, and it signals that what follows will do something useful with it. No clickbait required.

The other failure mode is the hook that is technically accurate but boring. It states what the content is about without creating any reason to care. The reader is not looking for information. They are looking for something that makes their current situation make more sense or feel more navigable.

Write the hook last. After you know exactly what you delivered in the piece, write the one line that would make the right reader stop scrolling to find out. Match the energy and specificity of what you actually wrote.

**Why It Matters:** High open rates and low retention mean your hook is working but your content is not. Low open rates mean your hook is failing. Both are fixable. Neither is the same problem.

**One Action:** Rewrite the last three hooks you published. Keep the core claim. Make them more specific about who the piece is for and what they will get.
