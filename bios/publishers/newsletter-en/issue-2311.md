# Price Is a Statement About Value

Underpricing is not humility. It is a failure of positioning.

## The Idea

There is a belief among new solopreneurs that low prices make selling easier. Sometimes they do, in the very short term. But low prices communicate something to the buyer that the seller doesn't intend. They communicate uncertainty. They suggest the seller isn't sure the product is worth more.

Buyers do not have access to your cost structure or your creative process. They use price as a signal of quality. A $10 guide and a $100 guide on the same topic trigger completely different assumptions before anyone reads a word. The $100 guide is taken more seriously. It gets more implementation. It gets better reviews. Not because it is always better, but because the buyer invested more and therefore commits more.

Pricing is not arbitrary. It should reflect the real value of the outcome your product delivers. If your template saves someone five hours of work and they bill at $100 per hour, the template is worth $500 in saved labor alone. Charging $27 is not humble. It is inaccurate.

The practical discipline is to price based on the value of the outcome, then build the marketing that justifies that price through specificity, proof, and clarity. Price first, then justify it. Do not price from fear and justify your way down.

## Why It Matters

Low prices attract price-sensitive buyers who are harder to serve, less committed to implementation, and more likely to ask for refunds. Higher prices attract buyers who are serious about the problem and trust that the solution is real. The buyers at higher price points are usually better customers.

Raise your price. Then work to deserve it. That order is correct.

## One Action

Take your lowest-priced offer. Calculate the value of the outcome it delivers in time saved, money made, or problem avoided. Set a new price at 30% to 50% of that value. Update the listing. Run it for 30 days and see what changes.
