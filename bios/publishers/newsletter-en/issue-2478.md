# Issue 2478: The Mental Model That Changes How You Price

**The Idea:** Most people price based on the cost of their time or the cost of production. This is backwards. It's the seller's perspective applied to a buyer's problem. The buyer doesn't care what it cost you to make. They care what it's worth to them.

Value-based pricing starts with one question: what is the outcome worth to the person buying it? If your product saves someone ten hours per week for a year, or helps them close a client worth $10,000, or gets them a result they've been stuck on for months, the price should reflect a fraction of that value.

The creator who prices based on time spent will always undercharge. A guide that took you five hours to write might deliver fifty hours of value to the buyer. The math is not "five hours times my hourly rate." The math is "fifty hours saved times a fraction of their hourly rate."

Higher prices also filter your audience toward people who take the work seriously. Someone who pays $200 for a guide is more likely to implement it than someone who paid $10. The transformation happens more reliably at higher price points. That's good for your reputation, not just your revenue.

Price is a statement about what you believe your work is worth. Underpricing is not humility. It's a mismatch between the value you create and what you're willing to claim.

**Why It Matters:** Getting pricing right unlocks the math of a sustainable creator business. You can reach a meaningful income with a smaller audience when the price reflects actual value.

**One Action:** Take one product or service you offer. Calculate the concrete outcome it produces for the buyer. Then check if your current price reflects that outcome or just your production cost.
