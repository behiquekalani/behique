# Issue 1996: Mastery Is Not the Goal

**One insight. No fluff.**

Most people treat mastery as the destination. Become excellent enough, and then you can build, create, or ship the thing. But the sequence is backwards. Mastery does not precede production. It emerges from it. The sculptor who has made ten thousand cuts is not good because they studied cutting. They are good because they cut. The doing is the training.

Waiting for mastery before starting is a form of fear with a respectable name. It feels responsible. It feels like preparation. What it actually is, in most cases, is an extended delay that never ends because mastery is a moving target. The more you know, the more you understand what you do not know. Mastery cannot be achieved in preparation. It can only be approached through production.

The right frame is not "am I good enough to do this?" It is "will doing this make me better?" The answer to the second question is almost always yes. Ship the imperfect thing. Build the rough version. Publish the essay that is not as tight as you want it to be. The feedback loop of production is the only thing that actually develops skill at the rate worth developing it. Start before you are ready. Mastery follows.
