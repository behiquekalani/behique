# Issue 2533: The Mental Model That Changes How You Price

**The Idea:** Most people price based on what they think the market will accept. They look at what competitors charge, cut slightly below it, and hope that feels reasonable to buyers.

This is the wrong starting point.

Price based on outcomes, not inputs. The question is not how long you spent making the thing or what others charge for something similar. The question is what it is worth to the person who buys it. What does solving this problem mean for their business, their income, their time, their stress level?

A template that saves someone 10 hours of work is not worth the cost of 10 hours at minimum wage. It is worth what those 10 hours would have been used for if they were not spent building the template. If those hours go toward revenue-generating work, the template is worth a multiple of what it freed up.

This reframe changes the ceiling. When you price based on what you put in, the ceiling is your time. When you price based on what the buyer gets out, the ceiling is their outcome. Those are very different numbers.

The practical application is to build your pricing argument around the outcome first. State what changes for the buyer. State what that change is worth in concrete terms. Then name a price that represents a fraction of that value. The math becomes obvious to both parties.

**Why It Matters:** Underpricing kills business models that could otherwise work. When you shift from input pricing to outcome pricing, you can charge more, serve fewer people, and create better products because you have more margin to work with.

**One Action:** For your current offer, write down the concrete outcome a buyer experiences. Estimate what that outcome is worth in dollars or hours. Price your product at 10 to 20 percent of that value.
