# Issue 2012: How to Think About Reputation

**One insight. No fluff.**

Reputation is not what you think it is. Most people think of it as something you build strategically, through visible actions and public positioning. That is part of it, but the bigger part is simpler: reputation is the residue of your actual behavior over time, especially when no one important is watching.

The colleague who cuts corners on small tasks and delivers brilliantly on high-stakes ones has a reputation. The person who does the same quality of work regardless of who will see it has a different one. The second kind compounds faster because it is trustworthy in situations you cannot predict in advance. Most of the valuable opportunities in life come through people who trust you, and trust is built in exactly those invisible moments.

This has a practical implication for how you spend your time. The work you do when you are tired and behind and nobody will notice is exactly the work that builds the reputation that opens the next door. Not because someone is watching, but because you are. The habits you build in private shape your baseline output in public. The gap between what you do when it counts and what you do when it does not is smaller than most people want to admit.
