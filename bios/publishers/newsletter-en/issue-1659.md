# The Builder's Identity

Most people define themselves by their job title. Builders define themselves by what they're currently making. That's a different orientation, and it changes everything.

## Identity as Compass
When your identity is tied to building, the question isn't "what should my job be?" It's "what should I build next?" That shift removes a lot of the confusion that comes with career planning.

You don't need to know exactly where you're going. You need to know that you build things. Then you find the next thing worth building and start.

## The Consistency of the Builder
Builders build across contexts. The skills transfer. The mindset applies. Whether you're building software, content, a business, or a community, the core activity is the same: take raw material and make something useful from it.

This is a more durable identity than any specific role because it doesn't depend on the stability of any particular market or employer.

## When the Identity Gets Tested
The identity gets tested when a project fails, when the market doesn't respond, when you're between things and don't know what to build next. These moments feel like identity crises only if you defined yourself by the project rather than by the act of building.

The builder who fails at one thing is still a builder. They start the next thing with everything the last one taught them.

## This Week's Action
Write a one-sentence statement of your builder identity that doesn't include your current job, platform, or niche. What you build, and why. Keep it short. Read it every morning for a week.
