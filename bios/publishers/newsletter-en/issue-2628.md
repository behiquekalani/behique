# Issue 2628: The Mental Model Behind Pricing

**The Idea:** Most people price their products based on what feels safe. They look at what others are charging, pick a number slightly lower to seem competitive, and call it a price.

That approach conflates price with value and costs you in two directions. Low prices attract buyers who are primarily motivated by avoiding payment, not by getting results. Those buyers produce the most complaints, the lowest completion rates, and the worst reviews. You also leave money on the table from the buyers who would have paid more and are actually trying to solve something real.

Pricing is communication. A $7 guide says: this is an impulse purchase. A $97 product says: this is a real tool. A $497 course says: I am seriously investing in this outcome. The number signals what kind of buyer should pay attention.

The correct question is not what can I charge but what is this worth to the right person. A guide that helps someone get their first client is worth more than the price of the guide. A course that helps someone systematize their business so they can work fewer hours is worth considerably more than its sticker price.

When you price for the outcome rather than the information, everything changes. You attract buyers who are motivated. You can charge more. You can invest more in the product. The product gets better. Better products produce better outcomes. The cycle reinforces itself.

**Why It Matters:** Low pricing is often a confidence problem disguised as a marketing strategy. Charging what you're worth is the first act of belief in your own product.

**One Action:** Pick one product you've been underpricing. Double the price and update the sales page to explain the outcome, not the contents.
