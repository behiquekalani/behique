# Issue 1907: The Hidden Cost of Keeping Options Open

**One insight. No fluff.**

Optionality feels like intelligence. The person who commits to nothing can pivot to anything. They're flexible. Adaptable. Open. But there's a cost no one talks about: options require maintenance. Every open path you're "keeping alive" takes a slice of your attention, even when you're not actively working on it. The multi-option person is not free. They're just distributed.

Commitment accelerates everything. When you close a door, your brain stops spending energy on the room behind it. That energy goes somewhere useful. You develop deeper knowledge, stronger relationships with the right people, and a reputation that actually means something. The generalist who tries everything is invisible. The specialist who bets on one thing is findable, referrable, compounding.

This doesn't mean you can never change direction. It means that changing direction should cost something and mean something, not be a reflex every time difficulty arrives. Most people abandon things right before the breakthrough because the difficult phase feels like a signal to quit. It's not. It's the filter that removes everyone who wasn't serious. Staying past the difficulty is not stubbornness. It's how depth is built.
