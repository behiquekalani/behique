# Issue 2013: On Asymmetric Bets

**One insight. No fluff.**

Most people evaluate decisions by their probability of success. That is a useful input but it is not the most important one. The most important factor is the asymmetry of the outcome: what do you gain if it works versus what do you lose if it does not.

A 20% chance of a life-changing outcome with a low, recoverable downside is a better bet than a 90% chance of a modest, forgettable outcome. But because people are trained to seek high-probability options, they consistently avoid the asymmetric ones. The vocabulary that describes this sounds reckless, so people talk themselves out of it with language about being responsible.

This thinking applies to small decisions too. Writing online has asymmetric upside: it can reach thousands and cost you a few hours. Staying silent has no downside but also no upside. Building a small product and charging for it has more upside than spending the same hours improving a skill abstractly. The asymmetric bet is not always about money. It is about maximizing what is possible relative to what you are risking. Training yourself to think in these terms changes what you say yes and no to, usually in productive directions.
