# The Hidden Cost of Keeping Options Open

Optionality feels like freedom. Often it is just expensive indecision.

## The Idea
There is a certain type of person who collects unfinished projects, half-started ideas, and "someday" plans. They feel busy. They feel like they are making progress. But the open loops are costing them more than they realize.

Every unresolved decision occupies cognitive space. Every project you start but do not finish stays active in your mental background. Every option you keep open requires maintenance, even if you never act on it. Psychologists call this the Zeigarnik effect: the brain continues to process incomplete tasks until they are resolved.

The cost compounds across a portfolio of open loops. Your attention is divided. Your focus is fragmented. You show up to your most important work already carrying the weight of everything unresolved.

The solution is not to eliminate ambition or stop generating ideas. The solution is to make decisions faster and close loops deliberately. Either commit and execute or decide to not pursue it and release it completely. A clean "no" is worth more than an indefinite "maybe."

Focused builders are not the ones with the most ideas. They are the ones who ruthlessly triage their ideas so that only the best ones get their full attention.

## Why It Matters
You cannot build exceptional things while maintaining a sprawling portfolio of average things. The decision to not do something is as important as the decision to start.

Fewer things, better executed, more done.

## One Action
Make a list of every open project or unresolved decision in your life right now. For each one, make a binary choice: commit with a deadline, or close it deliberately. No more maybes.
