# Niche Down Until It Feels Too Narrow. Then Go Further.

The fear of being too specific costs more revenue than any other mistake in the creator economy.

## The Idea

Every creator goes through the same reluctance. You start to narrow your focus and it feels like you're cutting off potential buyers. "What if I miss people?" What you're actually doing is becoming findable.

A generic creator in a crowded space competes with everyone. A specific creator in a defined niche competes with almost no one. The math is not even close.

The mechanism is trust. When someone lands on your content and it feels like you're speaking directly to their situation, not a broader demographic, not a general problem, their specific version of the problem, you've made a connection that generic content never achieves. That connection is what converts.

"Content strategy for small businesses" is a category. "Content strategy for solo consultants who hate social media but know they need it" is a positioning. The second one finds its audience. The audience feels found. Transactions follow.

The counterintuitive truth is that going narrow expands your reach. Specific content gets shared because specific content produces recognition. "This is exactly me" generates more shares than "this is pretty good."

Most creators undershoot the depth of their niche because they're afraid. The ones who go all the way in are usually shocked at how many people were waiting for exactly that.

## Why It Matters

Your niche is not a ceiling. It's a foundation. Once you're known as the person for a specific problem, you can expand from authority. Expanding from obscurity is much harder.

## One Action

Write out your current positioning in one sentence. Then add three more constraints: industry, situation, and the exact problem. That fourth sentence is your actual niche. Test it in your next three pieces of content.
