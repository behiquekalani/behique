# No Is a Strategy

Every yes you give is a no to something else. Most people track their yeses. Almost nobody tracks the cost of them.

## The Hidden Price of Availability
Being easy to reach feels like a virtue. It's often the opposite. When you say yes to every call, every collab, every side project that sounds interesting, you're spending the one resource you can't replace. Attention is the real currency of creative work.

The person who does fewer things at higher intensity almost always outperforms the person who spreads themselves across ten commitments. Depth beats breadth when the goal is quality output.

## What Saying No Actually Signals
When you say no to something, you're not being difficult. You're communicating your priorities. People with strong boundaries tend to command more respect, not less. Their yes means something because it's rare.

A no given clearly and without apology is more honest than a yes that drains you. The people worth working with understand this. The ones who don't tell you something useful too.

## Building Your No Criteria
The question isn't whether an opportunity sounds good. Most opportunities sound good. The question is whether it aligns with what you're actually building. If it doesn't pull you toward your core goal, it pulls you away from it. That's the only filter you need.

Write down the three things you're building right now. Use that list to evaluate every new request.

## This Week's Action
Identify one commitment you currently hold that isn't serving your actual goals. This week, either end it cleanly or reduce it significantly. Notice what opens up.
