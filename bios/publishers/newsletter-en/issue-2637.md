# Issue 2637: The Case for Fewer Products

**The Idea:** The instinct when revenue is low is to build more products. More SKUs, more offers, more paths to purchase. The logic seems sound. More ways to buy means more buying.

In practice, it usually means less buying.

Attention is finite. Yours and your audience's. Every product you add to your catalog is a product that competes with the others for your marketing attention. You can only genuinely promote one or two things at a time. The rest sit there accumulating no sales while you try to serve all of them equally.

There is also a trust dynamic. A creator with six products looks uncertain. Which one is the real one? Which one do they actually stand behind? The creator with one or two flagship products looks confident. This is the thing. If you need this outcome, here it is.

The business case for fewer products is also simpler: it is easier to get one product to one thousand sales than to get six products to a hundred and sixty-six sales each. The unit economics of focus compound.

Build the second product after the first one is genuinely selling on its own. Not before.

**Why It Matters:** Catalog sprawl is often a way of avoiding the harder work of making one thing great. Depth in one offer beats breadth across many.

**One Action:** Count how many products you currently have. If you have more than three, pick the one with the most potential and focus all promotion on it for the next thirty days.
