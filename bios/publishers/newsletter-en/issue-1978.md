# Issue 1978: Why Credentials Are Losing Their Hold

**One insight. No fluff.**

For most of the 20th century, credentials were a reliable signal. They meant something verifiable: this person spent time, passed tests, and was certified by an institution with a reputation to protect. The signal was imperfect but it worked. There was no better alternative for most employers.

The internet broke this. Not by making credentials worthless, but by creating better signals. A portfolio of real work, a public track record, a body of writing, a product people use. These are harder to fake than a degree and more directly relevant to the actual job. Employers who understand this have shifted. Those who do not are increasingly losing talented people to those who do.

The opportunity for anyone building skills now is to treat credentials as optional rather than mandatory. Not because institutions are bad, but because the alternative signal is available and often stronger. Document what you build. Write about what you learn. Ship things publicly. In five years, that record will do more work than most certificates. The question is whether you are willing to build in the open before you feel ready. That is the only prerequisite.
