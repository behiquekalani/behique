# Pricing Psychology: Why Your Price Is a Story

The number on the price tag is not just a number. It's a message about value, identity, and who this is for.

## Anchoring Changes Everything
The first price a buyer sees becomes the anchor. Everything after is judged relative to it. If you show a $497 option first, your $197 option looks like a bargain. If you show $197 first, your $497 looks expensive. Same products. Different story.

Use anchoring intentionally. Always present your highest-tier option first. Let the comparison do the work.

## The $97 vs. $100 Effect
Price endings matter. $97 signals "this was priced carefully, with the buyer in mind." $100 signals "I rounded up." For most digital products, odd pricing like $37, $67, $97, or $147 outperforms round numbers. It's not a rule. But the pattern is consistent enough to default to.

The exception is luxury positioning. Premium products benefit from round numbers. $300 reads more premium than $297. Know which category you're in.

## What Price Communicates About You
If you raise your price and your sales stay the same, you just gave yourself a raise. If you raise your price and sales increase, you were underpriced and the signal you were sending was too low. Most creators underestimate how much their price signals credibility.

Test your price before you lock it in. Run an offer at two different price points. The market will tell you what you need to know.

## This Week's Action
Look at your current or planned product price. Benchmark three competitors. If you're the cheapest, write a positioning paragraph that justifies a 40% increase. Test it on your next launch.
