# Mining Your Best Content: A System That Actually Works

Your best content is already in your archive. Most creators walk past it every day looking for new ideas when the highest-performing material is sitting unused.

## Read Your Own Analytics Like a Map

Most creators look at analytics for vanity metrics. Total views. Follower count. Impressions. That data is almost useless for improving your content. The useful data is comparative.

Which posts got saved? Which ones got shared without a comment? Which ones generated replies that started conversations? These behaviors tell you what actually connected. Saves mean the person found it useful enough to return to. Shares mean they wanted someone else to see it. Replies mean the idea provoked thinking.

Build a list of your ten highest-performing posts by these metrics. That list is your content strategy.

## Every Hit Contains Multiple Pieces

A single high-performing post is usually a compressed version of a much bigger idea. The tweet that got 500 saves has three more tweets inside it. The newsletter issue that generated the most replies has a course module somewhere in it.

Your job is to mine the original piece. Pull out each sub-idea and expand it. Take the concept that people responded to most strongly and go deeper. Your audience just told you exactly what they want more of.

This approach produces better content than brainstorming from scratch because it's grounded in real signal. You are not guessing what your audience wants. You are responding to what they already demonstrated.

## Repurpose Across Formats, Not Just Channels

Format changes content in meaningful ways. The same idea as a tweet, a short essay, a video script, and an email functions differently. Each format surfaces different aspects of the idea.

Repurposing is not copying. It is translation. The constraint of each format forces you to find the version of the idea that works best in that medium.

## This Week's Action

Pull your top five performing pieces from the last six months. For each one, write down two sub-ideas contained within it that you have not published separately. You now have ten new pieces of content. Build your next week's schedule from that list.
