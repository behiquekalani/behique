# Issue 1875: What Pricing Your Work Too Low Actually Costs

**One insight. No fluff.**

Low prices do not attract better customers. They attract more customers, and more of the wrong ones. The person who buys a $10 product expects $10 of friction. They want instant results, they leave bad reviews when the work requires effort, and they churn fast. The person who pays $500 has already committed to using what they bought. Price filters for seriousness, and seriousness is what you actually want in a customer relationship.

There is also what low pricing costs you. Every dollar below your real value is a negotiation you made against yourself before the customer said a word. It signals uncertainty. It trains your market to see you as a commodity. And it sets a floor that is very hard to raise later without losing the audience you spent years building around that low number.

Price from conviction, not from what you think people will pay. What do you wish someone had handed you three years ago? What would that have been worth in time saved, mistakes avoided, clarity gained? That is closer to the real number. People pay for transformation, not for content. Charge for the outcome. Then deliver it.
