# Build the Course Before You Think You're Ready

The number one reason people never launch a course is that they're waiting to know enough. That moment never arrives. The bar moves every time you approach it.

## You Already Know More Than Your Student

Your ideal student is not where you are now. They're where you were two or three years ago. That gap is your curriculum. Everything you learned between then and now is what they need. You do not need to be the top expert in the world. You need to be reliably ahead of the person you are teaching.

The expertise myth keeps capable people silent. They look at the most prominent voices in their space and compare themselves unfavorably. That comparison is irrelevant. Your student is not choosing between you and the top expert. They're choosing between you and no guidance at all.

## Structure Is the Product

Most people think the valuable part of a course is the information. It is not. The information is freely available online. What you're selling is structure, sequence, and accountability.

A good course takes something overwhelming and breaks it into a path. It tells the student what to do first, what to do next, and how to know when each step is done. That structure has enormous value. The person who could figure it out alone would take three years. You're compressing that into three months.

Build the outline first. That is your actual product. The video recordings are delivery.

## Sell It Before You Record It

Validation should come before production. Write a sales page, run it in front of your audience, and collect pre-orders or waitlist signups. If people want it before it exists, build it. If they don't respond, you've learned something valuable without spending weeks recording.

This approach also improves the course. Pre-sale questions tell you exactly what your students are afraid of and what they actually want. You build the answer to their real problem, not the one you assumed they had.

## This Week's Action

Write a one-paragraph description of a course you could build based on something you've already figured out. Post it as a question to your audience: "Would you find a course on this useful?" Watch the responses. That's your market research.
