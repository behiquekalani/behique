# Stop Optimizing. Start Shipping.

Perfect is the most expensive version of done.

## The Idea
There is a type of procrastination that does not look like procrastination. It looks like preparation. Refining the product. Tweaking the design. Doing more research. Waiting until conditions are better. This is optimization as avoidance.

Real feedback only comes from shipping. You can plan and refine indefinitely and never encounter the information that matters most: whether people actually want what you are building, and whether it works for them in real conditions.

The first version of anything is wrong. Not slightly off, fundamentally wrong. You will discover things about your audience, your product, and your own assumptions that no amount of planning could have revealed. The only way to access that information is to put something real in front of real people.

Shipping fast does not mean shipping garbage. It means shipping something functional and honest about its current state. A simple product that solves one problem cleanly is worth more than a complex product that has never reached a customer.

The creator who ships twelve times per year learns faster than the one who launches once, no matter how polished that one launch is. Iteration compounds. Perfectionism delays.

## Why It Matters
The cost of shipping something imperfect is small. You might get negative feedback. The product improves. The cost of never shipping is large. You learn nothing, earn nothing, and lose months.

Default to shipping.

## One Action
Name one thing you have been "almost ready" to launch for more than two weeks. Set a ship date. Not a "someday" date. A specific date within the next seven days.
