# Issue 1934: Teach What You Are Learning

**One insight. No fluff.**

The standard advice is to only teach what you have mastered. Wait until you are an expert. Wait until you have ten years of experience. Wait until you cannot possibly be wrong. This advice sounds responsible. It is mostly just a way to never start.

Teaching what you are currently learning is one of the most effective ways to accelerate your own understanding. When you have to explain something clearly enough for another person to grasp it, you discover every gap in your own knowledge. The fuzzy parts you were glossing over in your own head become undeniable. You are forced to sharpen them or admit you do not actually understand.

The audience for "I learned this six months ago" is enormous. Most people are not looking for the world's leading expert. They are looking for someone who just solved the problem they are currently facing, in plain language, from recent memory. The two-year-ahead version of yourself explaining things to the two-year-behind version of others is a legitimate and valuable service. You do not need credentials. You need honesty about where you are and what you have figured out so far.
