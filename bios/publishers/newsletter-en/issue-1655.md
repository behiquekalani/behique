# The Self-Education Loop

The traditional model of education is linear. You learn, then you apply. Self-education for builders is a loop. You apply, then you learn what you actually needed to know, then you apply again.

## Why Linear Learning Fails Builders
A course tells you what the instructor thinks is important. The market tells you what actually matters. These are often different lists. When you start from a curriculum rather than a problem, you spend a lot of time learning things that have no immediate application.

That's not useless, but it's slow. The builder who learns in response to specific problems builds a more practical, more durable skillset faster.

## The Loop in Practice
The loop looks like this: build something, hit a wall, learn what you need to get past the wall, build more. Each cycle of the loop produces both output and knowledge. The knowledge is anchored to real experience, which makes it stick differently than abstract learning.

This is why the best practitioners are often self-taught. Not because formal education is bad but because experiential learning is more efficient for applied problems.

## Designing Your Learning
The self-education loop works best when you're intentional about what you're building toward. If you have a clear goal, the walls you hit are predictable. You can read ahead of the walls before you reach them.

Identify the next three problems you'll face in your current project. Learn about those problems now, before you hit them.

## This Week's Action
Identify one skill gap that has slowed your current project in the last two weeks. Spend three focused hours this week closing that gap. Nothing else.
