# Know Your Customer Like You Know a Friend

Most founders write customer personas with demographics. Age, income, location. These are useless. The customer you need to understand is a specific person with specific fears and specific language.

## The Research Most People Skip

Your best source of customer insight is not a survey. It is the words your customer uses in reviews, forum posts, and comments when they do not know anyone is watching. That language is honest. It names the pain directly.

A customer who says "I feel like I am spinning my wheels and getting nowhere" is not the same as "low productivity." The first is a feeling. The second is a category. Write for the feeling.

## What Deep Customer Knowledge Unlocks

When you understand your customer deeply, your headlines write themselves. You are not inventing hooks. You are quoting the thoughts they already have. That is why some copy feels like it is reading your mind. Someone read your mind before writing it.

Product ideas come from the same place. When you know what keeps someone up at night, you know what they will pay to fix. You are not guessing. You are fulfilling a known need.

## Maintaining That Knowledge Over Time

Markets change. What your customer worried about two years ago may not be what they worry about now. The builders who stay relevant do ongoing research. They read forums. They answer comments. They stay close to the people they serve.

Distance from your customer is the first sign of a business about to get disrupted.

## This Week's Action

Spend twenty minutes reading comments, reviews, or forum posts from people in your target market. Write down five exact phrases they use to describe their problems. Use one of them in your next piece of content, verbatim.
