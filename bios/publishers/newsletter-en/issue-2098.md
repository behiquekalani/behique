# Issue 2098: Saying Less

**One insight. No fluff.**

There is a version of expertise that sounds like a lot of words. Qualifications, caveats, context, disclaimers. The person who talks the most in the room is demonstrating something, but it is usually not confidence. Confidence often sounds like brevity. The clear, short answer is harder to give than the long one because it requires you to actually know what you think.

When you cannot state something simply, it usually means you do not understand it well enough yet. Complexity is not depth. It is often the opposite. The deeper your understanding of something, the more clearly you can explain it to someone who has no background in it. Einstein supposedly said something along these lines. The principle holds regardless of source.

In practice, this means writing shorter emails and seeing if they are still complete. Giving recommendations instead of options when you have an opinion. Saying what you actually think before explaining why. Not every idea needs a paragraph. Some need a sentence. Developing the discipline to stop there, to resist the urge to add more words as a signal of effort, produces communication that lands harder and takes less from the person receiving it. Saying less, said well, is almost always the stronger move.
