# Issue 1994: Price Is Information

**One insight. No fluff.**

When you underprice your work, you are not being humble. You are sending a signal. Price is information. It tells the buyer what to expect before they experience anything. A low price says: this is for people who cannot afford better, or this is not that good, or I am not confident in what I made. None of those are the signals you want to send when you have built something genuinely useful.

The counterintuitive truth about pricing is that raising your price often improves how buyers engage with your work. When something costs more, people take it more seriously. They implement it. They show up differently. They get better results, not because the product changed, but because their investment created commitment. Price changes behavior before the product even gets used.

This does not mean charge the maximum the market will tolerate. It means price at the level that reflects the actual value you deliver and that attracts people who are serious about using what you made. The cheapest customers are often the most demanding and the least likely to succeed with your product. Raise your price. Attract people who are invested. The work will serve them better and cost you less.
