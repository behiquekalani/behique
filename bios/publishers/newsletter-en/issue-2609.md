# Issue 2609: The One-Person Business Case

**The Idea:** The most underrated business model of the next decade is one person, one computer, and a small highly engaged audience that trusts them.

This is not a compromise. It's an advantage. A one-person business has zero payroll overhead, zero equity dilution, zero meetings to justify decisions, and zero misaligned incentives. Every dollar of profit goes directly to the person who earned it. Every decision gets made in minutes instead of quarters.

The counterargument is scale. You can't build a billion-dollar company alone. True. But you can build a $500K/year business that gives you more freedom than most people with $2M in venture capital. The comparison is wrong. The goal is not to maximize revenue at all costs. The goal is to maximize the ratio of income to freedom.

The one-person business works best when you treat your mind as the primary asset. When your revenue comes from your thinking, your ideas, your frameworks, and your ability to solve problems in a specific domain, you are not replaceable. You are not a commodity. You are a personal monopoly.

Scale later, if you want to. But first, prove that one person with the right system can live well.

**Why It Matters:** Most people build companies when they should build practices. The one-person model is not a stepping stone to something bigger. For many people, it is the destination.

**One Action:** Calculate what your "enough number" looks like per month. The income that covers your life fully plus a buffer. Now design backwards from that number. How many sales at what price point gets you there?
