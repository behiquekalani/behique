# Issue 2455: Price Is a Signal

**The Idea:** The price you set communicates who your product is for, what category it belongs to, and how seriously you take your own work. Pricing low does not attract more buyers. It often attracts the wrong ones.

Low-ticket buyers are not the same as premium buyers. They have different expectations, different levels of commitment, and different follow-through rates. A person who pays fifty dollars for a course treats it differently than someone who pays five hundred. The investment changes the relationship.

Underpricing also positions you as a commodity. When you compete on price, you attract price-sensitive customers who will leave the moment a cheaper option appears. You can't build a loyal audience by being the cheapest option.

The psychology runs deeper. When people see a price that feels too low for the value being offered, they question whether the value is real. Price anchors perception. A well-priced product signals confidence in what you have created.

This does not mean overpricing for its own sake. It means pricing to reflect the genuine value of the transformation your product delivers. What is the outcome worth to the person who achieves it? Start from that number.

**Why It Matters:** Pricing is one of the highest-leverage variables in any business. Raising prices on the right product with the right positioning often increases both revenue and customer quality simultaneously.

**One Action:** Look at your lowest-priced offer. Calculate what the outcome it delivers is worth to your ideal buyer. If your price is less than ten percent of that number, it is probably underpriced.
