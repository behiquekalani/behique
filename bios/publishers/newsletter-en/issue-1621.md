# The Only Writing Advice That Actually Works

Most people treat short-form writing like a compression task. Take something long, cut it down. That's not writing. That's editing someone else's thinking.

## Write Like You Think

Short-form writing is not about brevity. It's about precision. The goal is not fewer words. The goal is zero wasted words. Every sentence should either move the idea forward or get cut.

The reason most short-form content fails is not the length. It's the thinking behind it. Shallow thinking produces shallow sentences. You cannot polish vague ideas into sharp ones. The clarity has to come first.

Write a rough version with no limit. Then ask: what is the one thing I'm actually saying here? Cut everything that doesn't answer that.

## The Hook Is a Promise

Your first sentence is a contract with the reader. It tells them what they're getting and whether it's worth their time. Most hooks fail because they describe instead of commit.

"I want to talk about productivity" is a description. "You're not lazy. You're bored." is a commitment. The second one pulls because it makes a claim. Claims invite agreement or disagreement. Either way, the reader is now involved.

Practice writing ten opening lines before choosing one. Most writers treat the hook as an afterthought. The best treat it as the whole game.

## Edit Like You Have No Attachment

The hardest part of short-form writing is cutting the thing you're most proud of. That clever sentence you spent ten minutes on. That paragraph that sounds brilliant but doesn't fit.

Cut it anyway. Save it somewhere. But cut it. The reader doesn't know it existed. They only feel whether the piece moves. Attachment to your own words is the enemy of good writing.

## This Week's Action

Pick one piece of content you wrote recently. Find the sentence you are most attached to. Ask honestly: does it move the piece forward, or does it just sound good? Cut it if it's the latter. Notice how the piece improves.
