# Energy Management Over Time Management

You have the same 24 hours as everyone else. What differs is how much of it you can use at full capacity.

## The Idea

The standard productivity advice treats time like the scarce resource. Block your calendar. Batch your tasks. Use time tracking apps. The implied assumption is that more hours equals more output.

But you've had days where you had eight hours and produced almost nothing. You've also had days where you had three hours and shipped something significant. The variable wasn't time. It was energy.

Energy comes in four forms: physical, emotional, mental, and intentional. Physical is your baseline. Sleep, movement, food. When this is depleted, everything else collapses. Emotional is your state. Anxiety, distraction, unresolved conflict all drain it. Mental is your cognitive bandwidth. It's a finite resource that depletes through decisions, switching costs, and complexity. Intentional is your clarity of purpose. Why you're doing what you're doing.

Managing time without managing energy is like scheduling eight hours of driving on a car with no gas.

The high-leverage moves are unglamorous. Consistent sleep. Exercise that actually happens. Protecting your morning before inputs arrive. Designing your environment to reduce small decisions. These aren't hacks. They're the infrastructure that makes every other system work.

When your energy is high, work expands to fill the time productively. When energy is low, even simple tasks feel like resistance.

## Why It Matters

A creator who manages energy produces at higher quality for longer than one who just manages time. The output isn't just more. It's better. And better work compounds in ways that more work rarely does.

## One Action

Track your energy levels at three points tomorrow: morning, midday, and afternoon. Note what you were doing when energy was highest. Protect that window for your most important creative work starting this week.
