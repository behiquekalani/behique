# The One-Page Business Plan You'll Actually Use

Most business plans are written to impress investors and then forgotten. You do not need a fifty-page document. You need one page that tells you what you are doing, who it is for, and how money flows.

## Why Complexity Kills Execution

A business plan you cannot recite from memory is not guiding your decisions. It is sitting in a folder somewhere while you make choices based on habit and impulse. The purpose of a plan is to make your decisions faster and more consistent. If it is too long to remember, it does not serve that purpose.

One page forces you to be clear. Clarity forces you to prioritize. Prioritization is where most solopreneurs fail.

## The Five Questions That Fit on One Page

Who do you help? What problem do you solve? What do you sell? How do people find you? How does money move from them to you? That is the whole plan. If you cannot answer all five clearly, you do not have a business yet. You have a collection of ideas looking for a structure.

These questions are not easy to answer well. They are simple to state and hard to get right. That is the work.

## Revisiting It Without Guilt

Your one-page plan is not a contract. It is a working document. Revisit it every quarter. Cross things out. Rewrite what changed. A plan that evolves is a sign of learning. A plan that never changes is a sign of avoidance.

The goal is not to have the perfect plan. The goal is to have a clear enough picture of the present that your daily decisions point in the same direction.

## This Week's Action

Write your one-page business plan today. Five questions, honest answers, no more than one page. If you already have one, pull it out and check whether it still reflects what you are actually doing.
