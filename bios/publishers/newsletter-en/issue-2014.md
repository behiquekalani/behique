# Issue 2014: The Identity Trap

**One insight. No fluff.**

Identity is useful for motivation. When you think of yourself as the kind of person who does a certain thing, you do that thing more consistently than if you are relying on willpower alone. That part is true. The trap comes when identity becomes a reason to stop questioning your own approach.

Once you are deeply invested in being a certain kind of builder, writer, or thinker, new information that contradicts your method feels like a personal attack. You reject it not because it is wrong but because accepting it would require updating who you are. That is expensive, so you rationalize instead. The identity that once helped you build momentum now helps you stay wrong.

The fix is to hold your identities slightly loosely. Think of yourself as someone who is trying to build well, rather than someone who is a particular kind of builder. The goal is the outcome, not the self-concept. This does not mean you have no principles or no style. It means you are willing to let your methods change as you learn more, without that change feeling like a threat to who you are. The people who improve the longest are the ones who can stay curious about their own assumptions.
