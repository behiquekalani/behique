# Price Is a Signal, Not Just a Number

Low prices don't attract serious buyers. They attract people who aren't committed to the result. Pricing is positioning, and it shapes who you work with.

## The Idea

Most first-time digital product creators underprice. They do it because they lack confidence, because they compare themselves to free content, because they don't want to scare people away. The result is a product that doesn't get taken seriously, buyers who don't implement it, and a creator who feels like the work wasn't valued.

Price communicates something before the product is even seen. A $7 product says "quick win." A $97 product says "serious tool." A $500 product says "this changes something." A $2,000 product says "transformation guaranteed or you're not the right buyer." None of these are inherently better. They attract different people with different commitments.

The question to ask when pricing: who do I want buying this? Because the price filters for that. If you want buyers who will actually use the product and get results, price at the level where they have to think about the purchase. Skin in the game changes behavior. A free or very cheap product gets downloaded and ignored. A $200 product gets opened, worked through, and referenced.

The other truth: your price affects your own perception of the work. When you price something at $20, you treat it like a $20 thing. When you price it at $200, you make sure it deserves that price. The commitment goes up on both sides.

## Why It Matters

Pricing strategically aligns your offer with the audience that will benefit most and invest fully. It also determines your income ceiling. If you need 1,000 buyers at $20 or 100 buyers at $200, the second path is usually more sustainable for a solo operator.

## One Action

Take your current or planned product price. Double it. Write down three things you could add or improve that would make the doubled price clearly worth it. Start with those things.
