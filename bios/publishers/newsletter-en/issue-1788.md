# Issue 1788: The Product That Solves One Problem Sells Better Than the Product That Solves Ten

**One insight. No fluff.**

When you try to build something for everyone, you end up building something that resonates with no one. The promise becomes vague because you are trying to include too many use cases. The customer cannot see themselves clearly in what you are offering. They read your product description and think "that sounds useful" but do not feel the pull to buy it today for a specific reason.

The best products are almost offensively specific. They are for one type of person, at one stage of a problem, trying to get one result. That specificity is not limiting. It is clarifying. The right customer reads the description and thinks "this is exactly what I need." That feeling of recognition is what converts.

When you are building your next product, test the specificity of your promise. Can you name one type of person? Can you name the specific moment they are in when they need this? Can you describe the single result they will have after using it? If any of those answers are vague, the product is not ready to sell. Sharpen the promise before you worry about the traffic.
