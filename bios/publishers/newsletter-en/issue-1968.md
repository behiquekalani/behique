# Issue 1968: Why Most Advice Is Useless

**One insight. No fluff.**

Advice is context-stripped. Someone took a decision they made under specific circumstances, with specific information, specific risk tolerance, and a specific version of themselves, and packaged it into a principle that travels without any of that context. The principle sounds clean. Applied to your situation, it can be completely wrong.

"Follow your passion" is advice that worked for someone whose passion happened to be commercially viable at a moment when the market needed it. For someone else, it produces years of broke idealism. "Take the safe path" is advice from someone whose definition of safety matched their risk tolerance and family situation. Neither is true. Both are true. For someone, at some time.

The skill is not finding better advice. It is developing the judgment to know when advice applies and when it does not. That requires knowing your own situation in detail, understanding the conditions under which the advice was generated, and being willing to reject counsel from people you respect when the context does not transfer. Most people do not do this. They are looking for a shortcut. The shortcut is actually the long way around.
