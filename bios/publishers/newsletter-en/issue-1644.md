# Write for One Person

The biggest mistake in content creation isn't poor quality. It's writing for a crowd. The crowd is abstract. Abstract writing gets ignored.

## The Crowd Problem
When you picture a thousand readers, you write to none of them. You hedge. You qualify. You smooth out the edges that might offend some hypothetical person on the periphery. The result is content that feels safe, generic, and easy to skip.

Writing for one specific person forces you to be concrete. You know their situation. You know what they've tried. You know what's frustrating them right now. That specificity is what makes someone feel seen.

## How to Find Your One Person
Your best reader is usually a past version of yourself. The version that was stuck on the exact problem you've since solved. Write to that person. Don't explain what they should do in the abstract. Explain what you actually did, what worked, and what you got wrong.

That voice, honest and experiential, is what cuts through. It's the difference between content that gets bookmarked and content that gets acted on.

## What Resonance Actually Requires
Resonance happens when a reader thinks "this was written for me." That feeling doesn't come from broad appeal. It comes from precision. The more specific your language, your examples, and your framing, the more people will recognize themselves in it.

Paradox: narrow your audience to deepen your reach.

## This Week's Action
Before your next piece, write one sentence describing the exact person you're writing for. Keep it visible while you write. Reread the draft and cut anything that person wouldn't care about.
