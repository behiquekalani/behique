# Niche Down Until It Hurts

The smaller the audience you speak to, the more each member of it feels like you are speaking directly to them.

## The Idea

Broad targeting is a trap that feels like safety. If you speak to everyone, nobody feels spoken to. The creator who posts "productivity tips for professionals" reaches a vague mass. The creator who posts "time systems for freelance designers with ADHD" speaks directly to a specific person with a specific problem.

Specificity creates recognition. The reader thinks "this is for me." That feeling is what drives follows, shares, and purchases. Broad content produces polite engagement. Specific content produces loyal fans.

The fear of niching is losing potential buyers. In reality, you gain a higher percentage of a smaller pool, and that pool is easier to reach, more likely to trust you, and more willing to pay. A 200-person email list of freelance designers who relate to everything you say outperforms a 2,000-person list of vague professionals who sometimes agree.

The niche does not have to be forever. It is a starting point. You can expand after you have built a reputation in one specific area. Most creators who successfully broadened their audience did it from a narrow foundation. Broad first works for celebrities. Narrow first works for everyone else.

The practical rule: niche down one more level past where you think is too specific. That is usually where the real audience lives.

## Why It Matters

Specificity is the most underused advantage in the creator economy. Most people are too afraid to narrow their focus. The ones who do it find that the work gets easier and the results get better at the same time.

## One Action

Look at your last ten posts. Write down the most specific type of person who would find each one useful. Then write one post specifically for that person, using language they would actually use about a problem they actually have.
