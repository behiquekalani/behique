# Issue 2124: Work That Teaches You While You Do It

**One insight. No fluff.**

Most people separate learning from producing. They study, then they work. This creates a ceiling. The studying phase ends and the person ships what they knew before they started. Nothing they learned in practice makes it back into the process because the two phases don't overlap.

The better model is work that teaches you mid-flight. You build something, encounter a real problem, solve it with new information, and that solution becomes part of the thing you're building. The output contains the learning. The process is recursive. You do not finish and then grow. You grow inside the doing.

This is why shipping matters more than preparing to ship. The classroom version of a problem is always cleaner than the real version. Real problems teach in ways no course can manufacture. The person who ships imperfect work repeatedly will always outlearn the person who waits until they feel ready. Ready is the destination, not the requirement.
