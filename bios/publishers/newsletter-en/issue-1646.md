# Build for the Future, Not the Feed

The algorithm rewards recency. Your audience rewards relevance. Those are not the same thing, and optimizing for one often destroys the other.

## The Algorithm's Short Memory
Chasing trends produces a particular kind of exhaustion. You're always running to catch something that's already moving. By the time you publish your take on the trending topic, the feed has moved on. You worked hard to say something timely and already forgettable.

Content built around trends has a shelf life measured in days. Content built around durable ideas has a shelf life measured in years. The math favors depth.

## What Evergreen Actually Means
Evergreen content isn't boring content. It's content that answers questions people will still be asking in three years. What should I focus on? How do I build something that lasts? How do I get better at thinking?

Those questions don't expire. The person asking them in 2029 has the same need as the person asking them today. Write for that person.

## The Compounding Argument
A post you wrote two years ago that still drives traffic is doing work while you sleep. A post you wrote yesterday about a trending topic is already dead. The compounding case for evergreen content is overwhelming, but most creators don't feel it because it takes patience.

Do the patient thing. Build a catalog of work that ages well. Let the library accumulate.

## This Week's Action
Look at your last ten pieces of content. Identify which ones will still be relevant in two years. Commit to making at least 70% of your output from now on fall into that category.
