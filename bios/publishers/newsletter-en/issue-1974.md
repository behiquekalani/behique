# Issue 1974: The Real Cost of Multitasking

**One insight. No fluff.**

Multitasking does not make you more productive. It makes you feel productive. That distinction matters. The brain cannot execute two cognitively demanding tasks simultaneously. What it does is switch. Fast switching. Each switch carries a cost: attention residue, the lag before you are fully present in the new task. In practice, you get 60-70% of yourself on each task, neither one finished properly.

The economic metaphor is useful here. Every context switch is a transaction fee. Low-stakes switching, like listening to music while doing data entry, is cheap. High-stakes switching, like writing a proposal while checking messages, is expensive. You pay the fee every time, regardless of how brief the interruption. Thirty seconds costs you five minutes of real cognitive depth.

The solution is not working longer. It is protecting longer uninterrupted blocks for the work that requires real thinking. Two hours without interruption on a hard problem is worth six fragmented hours of the same task. Most people have never experienced what genuine single-tasking feels like because the interruptions are constant and self-imposed. Block the tools. Close the tabs. Find out what you are actually capable of.
