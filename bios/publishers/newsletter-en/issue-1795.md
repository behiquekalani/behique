# Issue 1795: Clarity About What You Want Is the Rarest Competitive Advantage

**One insight. No fluff.**

Most people are vague about what they want. Not because they are shallow, but because clarity requires commitment, and commitment means accepting trade-offs. If you say precisely what you want, you also have to acknowledge what you are giving up to get it. That is uncomfortable. Vagueness lets you feel like all options are still open.

The cost of that vagueness is enormous. You cannot make good decisions without a clear target. Every choice, from how you spend your morning to who you collaborate with, is filtered by some idea of what you are building toward. If that idea is fuzzy, the decisions will be scattered. You will optimize for things that do not compound toward anything.

Write down what you actually want. Not what sounds good in conversation. Not what your parents would approve of. Not what the people you follow on social media are building. What you want, specifically, in three to five years. The specificity will feel uncomfortable because it makes the want real. That discomfort is the point. A real want generates real decisions.
