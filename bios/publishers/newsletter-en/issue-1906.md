# Issue 1906: You Don't Have a Motivation Problem

**One insight. No fluff.**

Motivation is not a fuel you run out of. It's a signal you ignore. When you're not doing the work, something specific is in the way. Fear of judgment. Unclear next step. Too large a first action. Boredom with a task that stopped teaching you anything. These are diagnosable problems. "Lack of motivation" is not. It's a category error that prevents you from identifying the actual block.

The fix is to get specific. Ask: what exactly am I avoiding? Not "working on the project," but the precise micro-action that feels impossible right now. Most of the time the honest answer is embarrassingly small. You're avoiding opening the file. You're avoiding sending the email. You're avoiding the blank page because you don't know what sentence comes first. These aren't motivation failures. They're friction points, and friction can be reduced.

Start so small it seems ridiculous. Open the document and write one sentence. Make the call and say one thing. The inertia breaks immediately, and the motivation people claim they're waiting for arrives on its own, about thirty seconds after you start. Motivation follows action. It does not precede it. That sequence alone is worth more than anything else you'll read about productivity.
