# Issue 1918: Your First Version Is Research

**One insight. No fluff.**

The first version of anything is not a product. It's a hypothesis. You're testing whether the thing you think people want is the thing they actually want, whether the mechanism you designed works in practice, and whether you understand the problem well enough to solve it. Most first versions fail these tests in ways you couldn't have predicted from the inside. That's not failure. That's data collection at the most useful stage.

The mistake is treating the first version as the final statement. Holding it too tightly. Over-investing in polish before validation. Building the full feature set before confirming that the core idea resonates. Every hour of polish on an unvalidated product is an hour that might be completely wasted. Not because polish doesn't matter, but because you don't know yet what to polish, because you haven't let real users tell you.

Release the first version to the smallest viable audience. Not a public launch. A private test with ten people who fit your target. Watch how they use it. Listen to where they get confused. Note what they ignore completely. That information reshapes your version two in ways that make it orders of magnitude more useful than if you'd spent the same time building in isolation. Version one's only job is to produce the information that makes version two worth building.
