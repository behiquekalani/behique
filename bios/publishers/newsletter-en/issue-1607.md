# Why Going Deep Beats Going Wide Every Time

Everyone wants to be a generalist because it sounds versatile. But the market does not pay for versatile. It pays for the person who solves a specific problem better than anyone else.

## The Breadth Trap

Breadth feels safe. If you know a little about everything, you cannot fail at any one thing. This is exactly backwards. Shallow knowledge in ten areas means you are not the obvious choice for anything. You are an option of last resort.

The specialist is the obvious choice. The generalist is the backup plan.

## What Deep Expertise Actually Builds

Deep expertise is not just knowledge. It is pattern recognition. After years in one domain you see things quickly that others miss entirely. You diagnose faster, solve better, and charge more for it.

That speed looks like talent from the outside. It is mostly mileage. You build it by staying in the problem longer than is comfortable, not by expanding into adjacent fields before you have mastered the current one.

## When Breadth Has Value

Breadth matters when you are combining deep skills. A copywriter who deeply understands psychology charges more than one who just writes well. A developer who deeply understands product thinking builds better things than one who only codes.

The formula is: one deep skill plus one adjacent deep skill creates a rare combination. That is very different from collecting shallow interests.

## This Week's Action

Name the one skill you would be in the top ten percent of professionals at if you spent focused hours on it for twelve months. Then ask whether your recent weekly schedule reflects that priority.
