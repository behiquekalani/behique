# Issue 2092: The Cost of Keeping Options Open

**One insight. No fluff.**

Optionality feels like safety. If you keep your options open, you can always pivot, always change course, always avoid committing to something that might not work out. This is rational at a surface level. But at a certain point, keeping options open becomes the strategy itself, and it is a bad one.

Optionality has a cost that is easy to miss because it is not paid upfront. The cost is that you never go deep enough on anything to get the compounding returns that come from real commitment. Every time you stay flexible by not fully committing to a direction, you avoid the downside of being wrong. But you also forfeit the upside of being right for long enough to build something real. The math rarely favors the person who hedges everything.

The most interesting work, the most durable businesses, the most developed skills all come from people who chose and stayed chosen long enough to figure something out. Not blind stubbornness. Intentional commitment with regular reassessment. There is a difference between updating your direction based on evidence and updating it based on discomfort. One is good decision-making. The other is avoiding the depth that makes any of this worth doing.
