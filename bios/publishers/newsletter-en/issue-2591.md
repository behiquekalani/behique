# Issue 2591: How to Price When You Do Not Know What to Charge

**The Idea:** Most solopreneurs undercharge. Not because their work is not valuable, but because they price from cost instead of from outcome.

Cost-based pricing sounds like: "This took me 10 hours, I want to make $50/hour, so I'll charge $500." Outcome-based pricing sounds like: "This saves my client $10,000 in wasted time, so $1,500 is a reasonable fraction of that."

The second frame is harder to inhabit when you are starting out, because you are not sure the outcome will happen. So you default to what feels safe. The underpricing feels safe until you realize you need 20 clients to reach the income you want, and 20 clients is unsustainable for one person.

The test for whether a price is right is simple: what would you need to charge to work with 5 clients instead of 20 and still hit your income goal? That number is probably closer to your correct price than what you are charging now.

For digital products, price based on the value of the problem you solve. A guide that helps someone get their first consulting client is worth more than a guide that explains how Instagram works. Problem intensity sets the price ceiling.

**Why It Matters:** Correct pricing is not just about income. It filters for serious buyers, reduces support load, and signals the quality of what you offer before anyone reads a word.

**One Action:** Take your current primary offer and calculate what you would need to charge to reach your monthly income goal with five buyers instead of your current expected volume.
