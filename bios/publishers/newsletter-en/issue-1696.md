# Test the Idea Before You Build the Thing

The most expensive lesson in business is spending three months building something nobody wants. Test first. Build second.

## Why Builders Skip Validation
Building feels like progress. Researching, talking to people, waiting for feedback feels like delay. This is a bias that costs real time and money.

The instinct to build immediately is often just avoidance in disguise. If you build and fail, you can blame the execution. If you test and nobody responds, you have to face that the idea itself might not work. Testing requires accepting that discomfort.

## What a Real Test Looks Like
A real test is not asking friends if they like your idea. Friends are too polite to tell you the truth. A real test is asking someone to pay, preorder, or commit before the product exists.

Post the offer. Describe the outcome. Give a way to buy. See what happens. If people pay, build it. If they don't, adjust the positioning or the idea before spending a hundred hours developing it.

## The Minimum Viable Version
You don't need a complete product to test. You need enough to deliver the promised outcome for one person. A live workshop before a recorded course. A done-for-you service before a DIY product. A Google Doc guide before a designed PDF.

The real test is whether someone values the outcome enough to exchange money for it.

## This Week's Action
Take your next product idea and write a one-paragraph description of what it does and what problem it solves. Share it with five potential customers and ask if they'd pay for it. If three say yes, ask them to put a deposit down. That's your validation.
