# Issue 1985: Boredom Is a Signal

**One insight. No fluff.**

Most people treat boredom as a problem to be solved with the nearest distraction. Phone, video, music, anything that fills the gap. The tolerance for empty mental space has dropped so low that two minutes in a waiting room now requires entertainment. But the discomfort of boredom is not a glitch. It is data.

Boredom points at one of two things. Either you are doing work that does not engage you deeply enough, or you are avoiding something that would. In the first case, the signal is that the work needs to change. In the second, the signal is that the discomfort you keep escaping is exactly where the real work is. Both cases require the same response. Sit with the feeling long enough to hear what it is saying.

The builders and thinkers who produce unusually original work share one habit. They protect their boredom. They do not fill every gap. They let their minds wander without a destination, and they pay attention to where the wandering goes. The best ideas rarely come from focused effort. They come from the mind at rest, making connections that effort would never find. Let yourself be bored. See what surfaces.
