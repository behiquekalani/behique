# Issue 2117: The Real Cost of Distraction

**One insight. No fluff.**

Distraction is not just lost time. It is lost depth. Every interruption resets your cognitive state to shallow. Getting back to where you were before the interruption takes anywhere from 15 to 23 minutes, according to research on attention. So a two-minute distraction costs 25 minutes of depth. Three interruptions in a morning and you have done no real thinking at all.

The math compounds over a career. Thirty years of shallow work versus thirty years of deep work produce outputs so different they are almost incomparable. The person who protected four focused hours a day built something. The person who stayed connected and available built a reputation for responsiveness. One of these compounds. The other is forgotten when the inbox gets archived.

Deep work is the skill that most directly produces outlier results. It is also the skill that modern life systematically destroys. This is not an accident. Platforms are designed to interrupt. Open offices are designed to interrupt. Notifications, by definition, interrupt. The person who protects their focus is not antisocial. They are playing a different game. One where depth is the asset, not availability.
