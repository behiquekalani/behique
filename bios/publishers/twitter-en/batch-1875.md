1. Low prices do not attract better customers. They attract more of the wrong ones. Price filters for seriousness and seriousness is what you actually want.

2. Every dollar you charge below your real value is a negotiation you made against yourself before the customer said anything.

3. The person who pays $500 has already committed to using what they bought. The person who paid $10 wants instant results and will blame the product when the work requires effort.

4. Raise your prices before you feel ready to. The discomfort is the point. If you are not nervous about the number, it is probably not high enough.

5. Charge for the outcome, not the hours. Hours are a cost. Outcomes are a value. Price from what it is worth, not from what it cost you to produce.
