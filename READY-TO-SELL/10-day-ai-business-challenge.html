The 10-Day AI Business Challenge

Build your first AI-powered digital product business in 10 days. One task per day. One real deliverable per day.

By Kalani, Founder of Behike

Before Day 1: How This Works

This is not a course. It is not a workshop. It is not a motivational newsletter that tells you to "believe in yourself" for ten days.

Every day you get one task. The task has a specific output. By the end of the day, you will have a real file, a real piece of copy, or a real account set up. Not notes about doing the thing. The actual thing.

I built my first digital product in a week while studying computer engineering full-time in Puerto Rico. No money to start. No audience. No team. Just a system and a deadline I set for myself.

This challenge is that system, packaged for you.

Set aside 60 to 90 minutes per day. That is all it takes. Some days are faster. None require more than two hours.

By Day 10, you will have a product, a listing, a landing page, a lead magnet, five Instagram posts, and a launch email. Everything you need to make your first sale.

Start Day 1 today. Right now.

Day 1: Pick Your Niche

Today's deliverable: A niche decision doc. One page. Saved to your desktop.

Most people spend weeks on this. They research endlessly, read Reddit threads, watch YouTube videos about "the most profitable niches of 2026," and end up exactly where they started. Stuck.

Here is the framework I use. It has three filters.

Filter 1: What do you know that took you time to learn?

Not what you are passionate about. Not what is trending. What have you already figured out that other people are still struggling with? This could be anything. How to study for a specific exam. How to set up a home server. How to negotiate a lease. How to eat well on $30 a week. How to build automations without knowing how to code.

Write down five things in this category. Do not overthink it. Set a timer for five minutes.

Filter 2: Who is already paying for this information?

Go to Gumroad.com and search each of your five topics. Look for products that have reviews and sales badges. If someone is selling a PDF about your topic for $9 and it has 200 sales, that is $1,800 proof that people pay for this. You are not looking for a gap in the market. You are looking for a market that already exists.

Circle the topics where you see paid products selling.

Filter 3: Can you deliver this in a short, structured format?

Your first product is going to be under 20 pages. It is a guide, a checklist, a template, or a short course. The topic needs to be specific enough that you can cover it completely in that format. "Productivity" is too broad. "How I structure my morning routine as an ADHD college student" is a product.

Your deliverable today:

Open a new document. Write three things.

  1. My chosen niche: one sentence.
  2. Evidence this sells: paste a link to a Gumroad product in this niche that has sales.
  3. My angle: what specific slice of this topic can I cover in under 20 pages?

Save it. That document is your north star for the next nine days.

The rule: Once you choose, you do not change it. Changing niches is how people spend six months building nothing.

Day 2: Validate the Idea

Today's deliverable: A validation report. Five Reddit threads saved. One insight highlighted in each.

You picked your niche yesterday. Today you confirm that people are actively struggling with the specific problem you are going to solve.

The goal is not to find out if a market exists. You already did that on Gumroad. The goal is to find the exact language people use when they talk about this problem. That language is going to write your marketing for you.

The Reddit Research Method:

Go to Reddit. Search your topic with these four phrases, one at a time.

  • "[your topic] help"
  • "[your topic] struggling"
  • "how do I [your topic]"
  • "[your topic] advice"

Read the top ten results for each search. You are looking for three things.

1. The specific moment the problem hits. "I've been trying to do this for three months and I still can't figure out..." That moment, that timeline, that frustration is your hook. It is the opening line of your product description.

2. The solutions people have already tried that did not work. This tells you what not to call your product, and what you need to differentiate yourself from. If everyone on Reddit says YouTube tutorials are too slow and scattered, your product needs to be fast and structured.

3. The exact words people use. Not "productivity challenges." "I keep starting things and never finishing them." Not "information overload." "There is too much conflicting advice and I do not know who to trust." Copy the exact phrases. These are your headlines.

Your deliverable today:

Five threads saved (bookmark or paste the URLs). One insight per thread. A single paragraph at the bottom that answers this question: "What is the one specific thing my audience is most frustrated about?"

That paragraph is the first line of your Gumroad listing copy. You will need it tomorrow.

Day 3: Define Your First Product

Today's deliverable: A product spec document. Title, format, table of contents, price.

This is the day most people make the mistake that kills their launch before it starts. They try to build the most complete, most thorough, most value-packed product they can imagine. They outline a 200-page guide with 12 modules and a bonus course and a private community.

Six months later they have 40 pages written and no product.

Your first product is a $9.99 entry offer. It has one job: to solve one specific problem well enough that someone feels it was worth more than they paid. That is it.

The format options:

  • A PDF guide (10 to 20 pages): Best for step-by-step processes, checklists, and frameworks.
  • A template pack (5 to 10 files): Best for swipe-copy, spreadsheets, Notion databases, or anything someone can use directly.
  • A short ebook (15 to 25 pages): Best for concepts, strategies, and mindset shifts that need some explanation.

Pick the one that fits your topic. If your topic is a process, use a PDF guide. If it is something people fill in or use directly, use templates.

Name your product:

Your title needs to answer one question: what will I be able to do after using this?

Bad title: "The Complete Guide to Productivity"
Good title: "The ADHD Study System: How I Went From Failing to 3.8 GPA in One Semester"

Bad title: "AI for Small Business"
Good title: "The 5 AI Automations I Set Up in a Weekend That Saved Me 10 Hours a Week"

Write three title options. Pick the most specific one.

Table of contents:

List five to eight sections. Each section should be a concrete step or a specific piece of information. Not "Introduction to the Concept." "Step 1: Set up your environment in 15 minutes."

Your deliverable today:

Product title. Format. Price ($9.99 for entry offer). Table of contents with five to eight sections, each named as a concrete deliverable or action.

This document is your build brief. When you write the product, you execute against this spec exactly.

Day 3.5: Write the Product (Do This on Days 3 Through 6)

This is not a separate day. It is background work that runs in parallel.

While you are completing the daily deliverables, you are also writing the actual product. 30 minutes per day on top of the main task. The product is due to be finished by the end of Day 8 so you can export it and upload it on Day 9.

Here is the rule: you write to the spec you created on Day 3. You do not revise the spec. You do not expand the scope. You execute the table of contents exactly.

How to use Claude to write faster:

Claude.ai free tier is your writing assistant here. The way I use it is not to generate the content wholesale. I write the core idea myself, then use Claude to fill gaps, tighten structure, and cut words.

My actual workflow: I write a rough first draft of each section, sometimes just bullet points, sometimes a messy paragraph. I paste it into Claude with this instruction: "Clean this up. Keep my voice. Cut anything that does not directly serve the reader. Do not add anything I did not already say." Then I edit the output.

This takes 20 to 30 minutes per section instead of 60 to 90. You are still doing the thinking. You are just offloading the editing friction.

The file format:

Write in Markdown (.md) or in Google Docs. Canva will be your design tool, not your writing tool. You will paste the finished text into Canva on Day 8 to create the PDF layout.

Keep this rule: finish one section per day. If your product has six sections, it is done in six days. No multitasking between sections.

Day 4: Write the Gumroad Listing Copy

Today's deliverable: Complete Gumroad product description. Ready to paste.

You do not write the product before you write the sales copy. You write the sales copy first.

This sounds backwards. It is actually the most rational thing you can do. Writing the description forces you to articulate the promise you are making. If you cannot describe what the product does in a few clear paragraphs, you do not understand it well enough to build it yet.

Pull out the paragraph you wrote at the end of Day 2. That is your opening.

The structure:

Paragraph 1: The problem.
Describe the frustration your reader is feeling right now. Use the exact language you found on Reddit. Do not editorialize. Do not explain. Just name the reality. "You have been trying to figure out X for three months. Every tutorial gives you different advice. You have bookmarked 40 articles and watched hours of videos and you are still exactly where you started."

Paragraph 2: Why existing solutions do not work.
One to two sentences. Not aggressive, just honest. "Most guides on this are written for people who already understand the basics. This one is not."

Paragraph 3: What this product is.
Specific. Concrete. "A 15-page PDF guide that walks you through X, Y, and Z. Step by step. With a template for each step."

Paragraph 4: The table of contents.
Paste your table of contents from Day 3 as a bullet list. People need to see exactly what they are getting.

Paragraph 5: Who this is for.
Three bullet points. Each one is a specific person in a specific situation. "You are trying to do X but keep running into Y."

Paragraph 6: The close.
Short. Direct. "If you buy this and do not find it useful, email me. I will refund you. No questions." That is it.

A note on price:

$9.99 feels low. It is supposed to feel low. You are not optimizing for revenue on the first product. You are optimizing for proof. Proof that you can build something, list it, and get a stranger to hand over money for it. Once you have that proof, you raise the price on the next product or add an upsell to this one.

The lowest friction path to a first sale is a $9.99 product with a clear promise. Do not argue yourself into a higher price before you have any sales data.

Your deliverable today:

The complete product description, formatted for Gumroad. Under 500 words. Save it as a text file. You will paste it directly into Gumroad on Day 9.

Day 5: Build the Landing Page

Today's deliverable: A working landing page. Hosted on GitHub Pages or Carrd.co. Link saved.

Your Gumroad page is the checkout. Your landing page is the sales pitch. You need both.

The landing page has one job. Get the visitor to click through to Gumroad. That is the only conversion that matters on this page.

Option A: GitHub Pages (free, requires basic HTML)

If you know even a little HTML, this is the move. It costs nothing and you have full control.

Use an existing HTML landing page template as your base. The structure is already done. You are filling in text and colors.

You need five sections:

  1. Headline and subheadline. The headline is a version of your product title. The subheadline is one sentence that clarifies what the reader will be able to do.
  2. The problem block. Three to four sentences from your Gumroad copy.
  3. What is inside. Your table of contents, styled as a list.
  4. Who it is for. Three bullet points.
  5. The CTA button. "Get it on Gumroad" linking to your product URL. You do not have the URL yet, but you can add it on Day 9.

Option B: Carrd.co (free tier, zero code)

If HTML is a blocker, go to carrd.co. Their free tier supports single-page sites with custom content. Use the landing page template. Add your text. Publish. Done.

Design rules:

One font. Two colors maximum. White background. No stock photos. Your product mock-up if you have one (you can create a flat cover in Canva in ten minutes). The page should load in under two seconds and communicate the offer in under five seconds of reading.

The mistake to avoid:

Do not spend Day 5 trying to make the page beautiful. The design does not close the sale. The copy does. You wrote the copy on Day 4. Put it on the page, put a button on it, and move on. You can redesign after you have sales.

Most people spend three days on a landing page and still do not publish it because it "is not ready." It will never feel ready. You publish it anyway.

Your deliverable today:

A live URL. The page does not need to be perfect. It needs to exist and it needs to work.

Day 6: Create 5 Instagram Posts to Launch

Today's deliverable: Five posts written and saved. Images created in Canva.

You are going to launch on Day 10. Before you launch, you need content queued that introduces the product to anyone who finds your profile between now and then. And you need your launch post ready to go.

Here are the five posts and what each one does.

Post 1: The origin story.
One short paragraph about the problem you personally had that led you to create this product. Not a life story. Just the moment. "I spent three weeks trying to figure out X. Every resource I found assumed I already knew Y. So I built my own system. Then I packaged it."

Post 2: The process reveal.
Show the table of contents. Screenshot it, or type it out as a carousel slide. Caption: "Here is exactly what is inside [product name]. Going live in [X] days."

Post 3: The proof point.
One specific result or outcome that the information in your product produces. If you have personal results, use them. If you have data (from your Reddit research), use that. "People who use this framework report X within Y weeks."

Post 4: The objection crusher.
What is the most common reason someone in your audience would not buy this? Price? Time? Skepticism about whether it works for their specific situation? Address that directly. "This is not for people who want X. It is specifically for people who Y."

Post 5: The launch post.
"It is live. [Product name]. [One sentence description]. Link in bio." That is it. Clean. Direct.

What to use for images:

Canva free tier has Instagram post templates. Search "Instagram post minimalist" and you will find dozens that work. Pick one. Use it for all five posts so they look like a coherent brand, not five different experiments.

Stick to two or three colors. Use your product title or a phrase from your niche as a repeating visual element. You are building brand recognition even before launch, so consistency matters more than creativity right now.

If you want to generate concept images, Bing Image Creator is free and produces usable output in under a minute. Generate five options, pick the two or three that fit, bring them into Canva as backgrounds, add your text overlay.

Your deliverable today:

Five captions written and saved in a text file. Five images created in Canva (one per post). Save as PNGs. Folder named "launch-posts" on your desktop.

Do not post yet. Day 10 is launch day.

Day 7: Write the Launch Email

Today's deliverable: One complete launch email. Subject line, body, CTA.

If you already have an email list, even a small one, this email goes out on Day 10. If you do not have a list yet, write it anyway. You will have one. This is practice and infrastructure.

The subject line:

Do not try to be clever. Be clear.

  • "It is live: [product name]"
  • "I built this for you"
  • "[Product name] — what is inside and how to get it"

Test three options. Pick the one that sounds most like something you would say to someone you know.

The email:

Keep it under 300 words.

Open with the problem. One paragraph. Same language from your Gumroad description. You have already written this. Repurpose it.

Tell them what you built. Two to three sentences. Specific. What is it, how long is it, what will they be able to do after reading it.

Tell them where to get it. One sentence. Include the price. "It is $9.99 at [link]."

Tell them why you made it. One sentence. First person. "I made this because I spent three months looking for something like it and it did not exist."

Close. "If you have questions, reply to this email."

One thing people get wrong about email:

They write it like a formal announcement. Like a press release. "We are excited to share that our new product is now available."

That is not how people talk. Write the email like you are sending it to someone you know. Read it out loud before you send it. If it sounds like a corporate newsletter, rewrite it until it sounds like you.

The best launch emails I have seen are under 200 words and feel like a text message from a friend who happened to build something relevant. That informality is not laziness. It is trust.

Your deliverable today:

One complete email saved as a text file. Subject line at the top. Ready to paste into ConvertKit, Mailchimp, or whatever platform you use.

If you do not have an email platform yet, go to ConvertKit.com and set up a free account. It takes twenty minutes. You need this before Day 10.

Day 8: Build Your First Lead Magnet

Today's deliverable: A lead magnet document. Under 5 pages. Exported as PDF.

Your lead magnet is the free thing people get when they subscribe to your email list. It is how you build the list that the Day 7 email will eventually go to.

The lead magnet needs to be related to your paid product but not replace it. It gives people enough to trust you and enough to want more.

The formula:

Take one section of your product. The most useful, most self-contained section. Turn that into a standalone document.

If your product is "The ADHD Study System," your lead magnet could be "The 20-Minute Study Setup: How to Prepare Your Environment So Your Brain Actually Focuses." That is useful on its own. It is also a clear preview of the kind of content in the paid product.

The structure:

  • One page cover with a title, your name, and brand.
  • One paragraph explaining what this is and who it is for.
  • The content. Step by step. Specific. Under four pages.
  • One page at the end with a note about the paid product and a link.

Design:

Open Canva. Search "ebook template." Pick the cleanest one. White background, simple layout. Replace all the text. Export as PDF.

This does not need to be long. A 3-page PDF that is dense with useful information is better than a 10-page PDF padded with filler.

The CTA at the end:

The last page of your lead magnet should mention your paid product. One short paragraph. No pressure. Just: "If you found this useful, I built a more complete version of this system in [paid product name]. It covers X, Y, and Z. You can get it at [Gumroad link]."

That paragraph converts. Not because it is persuasive. Because someone who found the free content useful is already looking for the next step. You are just pointing them toward it.

Your deliverable today:

One PDF, saved to your desktop. Five pages maximum. Ready to upload to your email platform as the lead magnet reward.

Day 9: Set Up Your Gumroad Account

Today's deliverable: A published Gumroad product. Shareable link saved.

If you already have a Gumroad account, you know what to do. If not, this walkthrough gets you live in under an hour.

Step 1: Create your account.

Go to Gumroad.com. Sign up. Use the same email as your business accounts. Username should match your brand name exactly.

Step 2: Add your product.

Click "New Product." Choose "Digital Product." Upload the PDF of your product. If you have not written the product yet, use a placeholder file. You can update it before launch. Right now you need the listing infrastructure to exist.

Step 3: Fill in the listing.

Product name: use the title you decided on Day 3.

Description: paste the copy you wrote on Day 4. Exactly as written.

Price: $9.99.

Cover image: go to Canva, search "Gumroad cover," pick a clean template, add your product title, export as JPG. Upload it.

Step 4: Set up your profile.

Profile photo (your face or brand logo). Bio under 100 words. Link to your landing page. That is all you need.

Step 5: Publish.

Do not set it to private. Publish it. Copy the link. Save it somewhere you will find it.

Step 6: Update your landing page.

Go back to the landing page you built on Day 5. Update the CTA button URL to your Gumroad link. Republish the page.

Your deliverable today:

Your Gumroad product is live. The landing page links to it. You have a shareable product URL.

Day 10: Publish and Share

Today's deliverable: Posted to Instagram. Email sent. Landing page live. Your first real launch.

Everything has been building to today. You have ten real deliverables sitting in folders on your desktop. Today you deploy them.

The sequence:

8:00 AM. Post your launch Instagram post (Post 5 from Day 6). Caption, link in bio updated to your Gumroad or landing page URL.

9:00 AM. Send your launch email (from Day 7) to your list. If your list has zero people, send it to five people you know who fit your target audience. That counts. It is a real send.

10:00 AM. Post Posts 1 through 4 from Day 6 as individual posts or a carousel. Space them out if you want, but get at least one more up today.

Noon. Share the landing page link in any other relevant communities. A Reddit thread if it is appropriate for the subreddit. A Telegram group if you are in one. A Discord server. Wherever your target audience spends time.

End of day. Check your Gumroad dashboard. See how many views you got. If you got a sale, screenshot it. That screenshot is the first proof point for your next product.

If you did not get a sale, that is expected and fine. The first launch of a zero-audience product is about learning the process, not generating revenue. You now have infrastructure, a product, and a list that will compound over time.

The day-after mindset:

Most people shut down after a launch with no sales. They decide the product is bad, or the niche is wrong, or the whole idea was stupid. None of those conclusions are supported by the data from a day-one launch to a zero-person audience.

The correct response to a no-sale launch is: the product exists, the infrastructure works, now I build the audience that will buy it. That is it.

Post once per day in your niche. Give real information. Not teasers. Not "tips you need to know." Actual content that is useful on its own. People who find that content will find the product. Some of them will buy it.

What comes next:

Post consistently for 30 days using the content pillars from your niche. Every three posts, link back to your product. Build the email list with the lead magnet you built on Day 8. Write the next product while the first one sells.

The first $9.99 sale is not about $9.99. It is about knowing the system works.
