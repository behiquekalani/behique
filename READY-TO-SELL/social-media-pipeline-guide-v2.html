The Social Media Pipeline

How to Post on 5 Platforms Without Spending 5 Hours

By Behike | @behikeai

A $19.99 guide for solopreneurs, creators, and one-person businesses who want consistent social media presence without burning out on content production.

Introduction: The Platform Problem

Every creator eventually runs into the same wall.

You know you need to be on Instagram. You know Twitter builds authority. You know TikTok has the reach. You know LinkedIn is where the high-ticket buyers are. And you know Pinterest sends passive traffic for years.

Five platforms. Five formats. Five posting schedules. Five types of content.

Most creators respond to this by either spreading themselves so thin that every platform gets low-quality, inconsistent content, or retreating to one platform and hoping the algorithm is generous. Neither strategy works long-term.

The Social Media Pipeline is a third option. Instead of creating five separate pieces of content for five platforms, you create one piece of core content and let it flow into five platform-native formats. Like water moving through a system of pipes, the same source material feeds every channel without losing pressure along the way.

This guide gives you the complete architecture, the production workflow, the platform-specific formulas, and the weekly system to make it repeatable. By the time you finish this guide, you will have a content operation that produces consistent output across five platforms in one focused day per week.

No agency. No full-time team. One person, one system.

Chapter 1: The Pipeline Architecture

One Hub. Five Channels.

The pipeline begins with a single principle: long-form content is the source of all distribution.

Most social media strategies work in reverse. Creators produce short-form content for each platform independently and occasionally write something long when they feel inspired. The pipeline flips this. You produce one long-form piece per week and let it cascade into short-form formats for every channel.

This is sometimes called the content waterfall, or hub-and-spoke content strategy. The logic is simple. A 1,500-word newsletter contains enough distinct ideas to produce:

  • 1 carousel post (Instagram)
  • 1 Twitter thread (5-8 tweets)
  • 2-3 short clips or scripts (TikTok/Reels)
  • 1 LinkedIn article or post
  • 3-5 evergreen pin descriptions (Pinterest)

That is ten to twelve pieces of content from one source document. Created once. Adapted for each channel's native format.

The Three Phases

The pipeline operates in three phases every week.

Phase 1: Production (2-3 hours). You create the hub content. The newsletter, YouTube script, or long blog post that contains your core idea fully developed.

Phase 2: Extraction (1-2 hours). You pull the individual ideas, insights, quotes, and frameworks from the hub piece and format them for each platform.

Phase 3: Scheduling (30-60 minutes). You load everything into a scheduler and set it to publish throughout the week.

Total time investment: 4-6 hours per week. Total output: a full week of content across five platforms.

Why This Works

The content pipeline solves three problems simultaneously. It eliminates the daily creation grind by concentrating production into one session. It maintains brand consistency because every piece of content this week traces back to one central idea. And it removes the blank page problem because you never start short-form content from nothing. You always start from the long-form piece you already wrote.

Chapter 2: Your Content Hub

The Source That Feeds Everything

Your content hub is the long-form piece you produce once per week. It is the deepest, most developed expression of the idea you want to explore. Everything else is a derivative.

You have three viable hub formats.

Option 1: The Newsletter. A newsletter sent to an owned email list is the strongest hub because it is platform-independent. You own the relationship. When algorithms change, your newsletter still reaches subscribers. A newsletter of 600-1,000 words contains enough substance to feed the full week of content.

Option 2: The YouTube Video Script. If you produce videos, the script is your hub. A 7-10 minute video script is typically 900-1,400 words. Every section of the script becomes short-form content. The introduction becomes a hook. The main points become carousel slides. The conclusion becomes a LinkedIn post.

Option 3: The Long Blog Post or Essay. A 1,200-1,800 word post published on a website or Substack works as a hub. The advantage is SEO. Every piece of short-form content you produce that week also drives search traffic to the long-form source.

How to Choose Your Hub

Pick the format you will actually produce consistently. The most strategically correct choice that you skip most weeks is less valuable than the format you find natural and enjoyable. If you love writing, the newsletter or essay. If you think out loud, the YouTube script. Start with one and build the habit before adding the others.

What Makes a Strong Hub Piece

A strong hub piece makes one clear argument about one specific topic. Not five things about productivity. One counterintuitive claim about why your morning routine is hurting your output. The specificity of the central argument determines how much extractable material it contains.

Weak hub: "Five tools for solopreneurs."
Strong hub: "Why most productivity tools make you less productive and what to use instead."

The second has a point of view. That point of view generates carousel slides, Twitter arguments, TikTok takes, and LinkedIn perspectives almost automatically.

Chapter 3: The Waterfall

Breaking Long-Form Into Platform-Native Pieces

The waterfall is the extraction process. After writing your hub piece, you read it with a different goal: not to edit, but to mine.

You are looking for four types of content:

Standalone insights. Any sentence or paragraph that works as a complete thought on its own. These become Twitter posts, Instagram captions, or Pinterest pin copy.

Numbered frameworks. Any section of your hub piece that lists steps, reasons, types, or stages. These become carousels and Twitter threads.

Quotable moments. The best single sentence in the piece. This becomes a quote graphic, a thread opener, or a TikTok hook.

Specific details. Data points, named tools, specific actions. These become LinkedIn posts that demonstrate expertise.

The Extraction Pass

After writing your hub piece, do one read-through specifically to tag extractable content. Use a simple system: highlight standalone insights in one color, frameworks in another, and quotable moments in a third. You can do this in Notion, Google Docs, or with physical highlights if you print.

A 1,000-word hub piece typically yields 6-10 extractable sections. From those, you produce the full week of platform content.

The Adaptation Rule

Extraction is not copy-paste. Every extracted piece requires adaptation to the platform it is going to. A sentence from your newsletter needs to become a slide in a carousel format. A numbered list needs to become a thread format with hooks between each point. A quote needs a visual container for Pinterest.

The core idea stays identical. The format changes to match what works on each platform.

Chapter 4: Instagram in the Pipeline

Carousel Extraction and Caption Formula

Instagram rewards carousels over single images because carousels create time-on-post. Slides require swiping. Swiping increases engagement metrics. The algorithm rewards what keeps people on the platform.

Every carousel from your pipeline follows the same structure.

Slide 1: The hook. A bold declarative statement that creates curiosity or names a specific problem. "Most solopreneurs price their first product wrong. Here is the framework to fix it."

Slides 2-5: The content. One distinct idea per slide. Four slides, four ideas. Each slide works as a standalone point. Do not split one idea across two slides.

Slide 6: The CTA. Direct the viewer to the next action. Follow the account, save the post, comment with a specific word, or click the link in bio.

The Caption Formula

The caption for a carousel post follows this structure.

Open with your thesis stated as a single plain sentence. No question, no hook, just the most direct version of your main point.

Expand on the thesis in 100-150 words. Add the context that did not fit in the slides. Give the reader something from the caption that was not in the carousel.

Close with a community question or a redirect to another piece of content.

Add 15-20 hashtags below the caption text. Mix audience-size: three to five large hashtags (over 1M posts), seven to ten medium hashtags (100K-1M posts), three to five small hashtags (under 100K posts). The smaller hashtags give you the best chance of appearing in the top posts section.

Carousel Output Target

From one hub piece per week: two carousels. One built from the main framework in the hub piece, one built from a supporting insight or secondary point.

Chapter 5: Twitter/X in the Pipeline

Thread Formula from Long-Form

Twitter threads from hub pieces are the most efficient form of repurposing because the structure already exists. If your hub piece has five main points, you have a five-tweet thread. Add an opener and a closer and you have a seven-tweet thread.

The thread structure:

Tweet 1 (hook): A single sentence that contains the most counterintuitive or specific claim from your hub piece. No question. Declarative and specific.

Tweets 2-6 (content): One expanded point per tweet. Each tweet works as a standalone post. Each is 2-4 sentences maximum. The best threads read like a list of standalone tweets that also form a coherent argument when read in sequence.

Tweet 7 (close): A summary of the main insight plus one direct action the reader can take. Optionally include a link to the full hub piece.

Single Posts From Hub Content

Beyond the thread, extract three to five standalone tweets from the hub piece. These are the most quotable sentences, the most surprising statistics, or the most specific recommendations. Post one per day throughout the week. This keeps your feed active between threads and trains the algorithm that you are a consistent poster.

What Works on Twitter

Twitter rewards specificity, contradiction, and brevity. The more specific the claim, the more the post generates responses. The more it contradicts a common belief, the more it gets shared. Keep tweets under 240 characters unless you are threading. Edit ruthlessly. Every word that does not add meaning removes it.

Chapter 6: TikTok in the Pipeline

B-Roll and Insight Clips

TikTok content from your pipeline takes one of two forms: talking head videos where you explain an insight directly to camera, or B-roll videos where footage plays while your voiceover delivers the insight.

For most solopreneurs starting out, the talking head format is faster and requires no editing skills. Stand in front of a plain wall with good natural light. Speak directly to camera. Keep it under 60 seconds.

The TikTok Script Formula

Every TikTok from your pipeline follows this structure.

Hook (0-3 seconds): The most provocative sentence from your hub piece, delivered directly to camera. No introduction, no "hey guys," no setup. Start with the claim.

Content (4-45 seconds): Three to five sentences that expand on the hook. One idea per sentence. Speak at a slightly faster than comfortable pace. TikTok viewers have been trained for high information density.

Close (45-60 seconds): Restate the core insight as a takeaway. Add one specific action the viewer can take today. End with "follow for more on X topic."

Volume Over Production Quality

TikTok's algorithm rewards consistency and native feel over high production value. A genuine talking-head clip with direct useful information outperforms a heavily produced video with stock footage on most accounts. Post at least three times per week. Volume generates data about what your specific audience responds to.

From Hub to TikTok

Extract two to three specific insights from your hub piece that translate well to spoken delivery. These should be insights that benefit from vocal emphasis and that can be stated clearly in under 60 seconds. The detailed explanation from your newsletter becomes a tight 45-second claim on TikTok.

Chapter 7: LinkedIn in the Pipeline

Authority Repurposing

LinkedIn is the platform where professional depth is rewarded most. The same insight that lives as a tweet becomes a 300-word LinkedIn post with full context, examples, and professional framing.

LinkedIn rewards posts that demonstrate expertise, share process, and invite professional conversation. Personal stories with professional lessons perform extremely well. Data and frameworks drive saves and shares.

The LinkedIn Post Formula

Opening line: The most specific, useful claim from your hub piece. LinkedIn suppresses "click more" behavior when the opening is weak. Make the first line worth reading.

Body (150-250 words): Three to four paragraphs. Each paragraph is two to four sentences. Include at least one specific example, case, or data point. Abstract advice without specificity underperforms on LinkedIn.

Closing question: End with a direct question to the professional audience. "What has worked in your experience?" or "What would you add to this framework?" Comments increase distribution.

Formatting: Use line breaks liberally. Single-sentence paragraphs are normal on LinkedIn. White space makes posts readable on mobile. Avoid bullet points unless the content genuinely requires listing. Walls of text get scrolled past.

Cadence

Post on LinkedIn three to four times per week. Monday and Wednesday morning posts in the 8-10am slot in your target time zone typically outperform other windows. One long post per week, two to three shorter professional takes.

Chapter 8: Pinterest in the Pipeline

Evergreen Visual Pins

Pinterest operates differently from every other platform on this list. It is a search engine with a social layer, not a social network with a search feature. That distinction changes how you approach content.

On Pinterest, content has a long shelf life. A pin posted today can drive traffic in eight months. The platform rewards relevance to search terms, not recency. This makes it ideal for evergreen content from your hub pieces.

Pin Creation from Hub Content

For each hub piece, create three to five pins. Each pin is:

A title that contains the exact search term your target reader would use. Not "Why Your Content Fails" but "Social Media Content Strategy for Solopreneurs."

A visual that communicates the main idea without needing to be read in detail. Text overlays on clean backgrounds outperform photography for informational content.

A description of 100-200 words that uses natural language and includes the main keyword three to four times without stuffing. The description should read like a useful paragraph, not a keyword list.

A link to your hub content, your product page, or your newsletter signup. Pinterest drives traffic. Give it somewhere useful to drive traffic to.

Platform Tools

Canva is the fastest tool for Pinterest graphics. Use a 1000x1500 pixel canvas (2:3 ratio) for standard pins. Keep backgrounds clean. Use one font for the title, one for the body. Pin consistently: three to five pins per day is the standard recommendation for accounts trying to grow. Batch-schedule pins using Tailwind or Pinterest's built-in scheduler.

Chapter 9: The Batch Production System

One Day Per Week Creates Everything

The batch production system concentrates all content creation into a single weekly session. No daily writing. No scrambling for ideas the morning a post needs to go out. One dedicated block, everything produced, everything scheduled.

The weekly pipeline session runs like this:

Monday (or your chosen content day):

90 minutes: Write the hub piece. Newsletter, script, or blog post. Follow your outline. Finish the draft. Do a single editing pass.

45 minutes: Extraction pass. Read the hub piece and tag all extractable content. Identify carousel frameworks, thread hooks, TikTok scripts, LinkedIn posts, and Pinterest pin titles.

60 minutes: Format for each platform. Write two carousel outlines. Write one thread. Write two TikTok scripts. Write two LinkedIn posts. Write three Pinterest pin descriptions.

30 minutes: Create visuals. Build carousel slides in Canva using your brand template. Create Pinterest graphics. Export.

30 minutes: Schedule everything. Load into Buffer, Later, or your preferred scheduler. Set publish times for the full week.

Total: approximately 4 hours.

What Changes When You Batch

The quality of your content improves when you create in batches because you are not making decisions about content while also executing the creation. The decisions happen during the planning phase. The execution happens in the batch session. Separated decisions and execution are faster and produce better output than making every decision in real time.

The consistency of your posting also improves because publication is not dependent on motivation on the day the post goes out. It was scheduled in advance.

The Buffer Rule

Always maintain a buffer of at least seven days of scheduled content. Life will interrupt your production schedule. A client emergency, an illness, travel. When that happens, the buffer absorbs the disruption without any gap in your public output. The buffer is not extra work. It is insurance that your consistency record survives the inevitable interruptions.

Chapter 10: Tools and Automation

Buffer

Buffer is the scheduling and analytics hub for the pipeline. It supports Instagram, Twitter, LinkedIn, Pinterest, and TikTok from one dashboard. The basic plan at $6 per month covers one user and three channels. The Essentials plan at $12 per month covers all five. Schedule posts in advance, view them in a calendar format, and track basic performance metrics.

The most useful Buffer feature for the pipeline is the queue. Set specific posting times for each platform. When you add content to the queue, it automatically fills the next available slot. You do not schedule individual posts manually. You set the schedule once and add to the queue.

n8n for Advanced Automation

n8n is an open-source automation tool that connects apps and automates workflows. For the social media pipeline, n8n is most useful for:

  • Automatically sending your published newsletter to a Notion database as a content source
  • Triggering a reminder workflow when you have fewer than 7 days of scheduled content in Buffer
  • Archiving published posts with their performance data to a tracking spreadsheet

n8n runs on a free self-hosted plan or a cloud plan starting at $20/month. For most solopreneurs, the cloud plan is easiest to start.

CapCut for TikTok and Reels

CapCut is the fastest editing tool for TikTok videos created from pipeline content. The auto-caption feature generates subtitles automatically from the audio. Captions are the single most important element of short-form video because most people watch without sound.

For talking-head TikTok content from the pipeline, the workflow is: record, import to CapCut, auto-caption, add background music at 10-15% volume, export.

Notion for the Pipeline Hub

Keep your pipeline system in Notion. One database for hub pieces (status: writing, draft, final, published). One linked database for platform pieces (linked to the hub piece they came from, status: draft, scheduled, published). One calendar view showing the full week of scheduled content across all platforms.

The Notion setup makes the pipeline visible and prevents ideas from falling through the cracks.

Appendix: Platform-by-Platform Specs

Instagram

  • Feed post image: 1080x1080 (square), 1080x1350 (portrait), 1080x566 (landscape)
  • Carousel: Up to 10 slides, recommended 6-8 for optimal completion rate
  • Caption limit: 2,200 characters
  • Hashtag limit: 30 (recommend 15-20)
  • Reels: 9:16 aspect ratio, 1080x1920, up to 90 seconds for feed distribution
  • Best post times: Tuesday and Wednesday, 9am-11am local time

Twitter/X

  • Character limit: 280 characters per tweet
  • Thread: Up to 25 tweets in a native thread
  • Image: 1200x675 (landscape), 1080x1080 (square)
  • Best post times: Tuesday through Thursday, 9am-3pm local time
  • Link posts reduce reach. Place links in the first comment when reach matters.

TikTok

  • Video ratio: 9:16, 1080x1920
  • Optimal length: 15-60 seconds for broad reach, up to 3 minutes for tutorial formats
  • Caption limit: 2,200 characters
  • Hashtag recommendation: 3-5 specific hashtags, avoid generic trending hashtags
  • Best post times: Tuesday and Friday, 7am-9am and 7pm-9pm

LinkedIn

  • Post character limit: 3,000 characters
  • Article: No limit
  • Image: 1200x627 (landscape) for single images
  • Best post times: Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, 8am-10am local time
  • Links in posts reduce reach. Add links in the first comment.

Pinterest

  • Standard pin: 1000x1500 (2:3 ratio)
  • Wide pin: 1000x1000 (square)
  • Title limit: 100 characters
  • Description limit: 500 characters
  • Best posting time: Saturday 8am-11pm, Sunday 3pm-11pm, Friday evening
  • Posting frequency: 3-5 pins per day for growth
