What is Claude Cowork?

Claude Cowork (also called "computer use") is a feature where Claude can see your screen, move your mouse, click buttons, and type for you. Think of it as having someone sit next to you and operate your computer while you tell them what to do.

You talk. Claude acts. No code. No terminal. No technical knowledge required.

You stay in control the entire time. Claude shows you what it's about to do, and you approve or redirect. It's a collaboration, not autopilot.

How to Access Cowork

  1. Download the Claude Desktop app from claude.ai/download
  2. Open the app and sign in to your Claude account
  3. Look for the computer use toggle or start a conversation and ask Claude to interact with your screen
  4. Grant screen and accessibility permissions when prompted (Mac: System Settings > Privacy & Security)
  5. Start telling Claude what to do in plain English

USE CASE 01

Clean Up My Inbox

Email management: sort, reply, archive — hands free.

The Prompt

Open my Gmail in the browser. Sort my unread emails by importance — anything from a real person goes to the top, newsletters and promotions go to the bottom. Draft replies to the top 3 most important emails. Then archive everything from newsletters I haven't opened in the last 30 days. Don't send anything — just draft and let me review.

What Happens on Screen

1

Claude opens your browser and navigates to Gmail

ON YOUR SCREEN

Your default browser opens. Claude types gmail.com in the address bar. If you're already logged in, your inbox loads. If not, Claude will pause and ask you to log in (it won't enter your password).

2

Claude scans your unread emails

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude scrolls through your inbox, reading sender names, subject lines, and preview text. It mentally categorizes each email: personal, work, newsletter, promotion, notification. You'll see the cursor moving as it reads.

3

Claude identifies your top 3 priority emails

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude tells you in the chat which 3 emails it considers most important and why. Example: "Your top 3 are: (1) Email from your manager about the project deadline, (2) Client asking about invoice, (3) Friend confirming weekend plans."

4

Claude drafts replies

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude clicks on the first email, hits Reply, and types a draft response in the compose window. It reads the email content to write a relevant reply. Then it moves to email #2 and #3, repeating the process. All drafts stay unsent — saved in your Drafts folder.

5

Claude archives old newsletters

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude searches for newsletter senders, checks the dates, selects emails older than 30 days that were never opened, and clicks Archive. You'll see checkboxes being ticked and the archive button being clicked in batches.

How to Review Before Confirming

  • Claude will pause after each major action and ask if you want to continue
  • Check your Drafts folder to review reply drafts before sending
  • If a draft doesn't sound right, tell Claude: "Rewrite the reply to [name] — make it more casual"
  • If it archived something you wanted to keep, tell Claude: "Undo the last archive" or find it in All Mail

Troubleshooting

If This Happens, Do This
  • 2FA popup appears: Claude will pause and ask you to complete the verification. Enter your code, then tell Claude to continue.
  • CAPTCHA shows up: Claude cannot solve CAPTCHAs. Complete it yourself, then say "Done, continue."
  • Page loads slowly: Say "Wait for the page to fully load before clicking anything."
  • Claude clicks the wrong email: Say "Stop. Go back. I meant the email from [specific sender]."
  • Gmail layout looks different: Say "I'm using the new/classic Gmail layout" so Claude can adjust.
  • Claude tries to send instead of draft: Always include "Don't send anything" in your prompt. If it reaches for Send, say "Stop — save as draft only."

USE CASE 02

Research This and Make a Document

From blank page to finished comparison doc — with sources.

The Prompt

Research the top 5 project management tools for freelancers. Open each tool's website and check their pricing page and feature list. Then open Google Docs, create a new document called "PM Tool Comparison," and make a comparison table with columns for: Tool Name, Price (free tier + paid), Key Features, Best For, and My Rating. Add a short recommendation paragraph at the bottom. Don't close any tabs — I want to review the sources.

What Happens on Screen

1

Claude opens your browser and starts researching

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude opens a new browser tab and searches for "best project management tools for freelancers." It reads through the search results, then starts opening individual tool websites — Notion, Trello, Asana, ClickUp, Monday.com (or whichever it determines are the top 5).

2

Claude visits each tool's pricing and features page

ON YOUR SCREEN

For each tool, Claude navigates to the pricing page, reads the tiers and costs, then visits the features page. You'll see it scrolling through each site, spending 20-40 seconds per tool. Each site stays open in its own tab so you can verify later.

3

Claude opens Google Docs and creates your document

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude opens a new tab, goes to docs.google.com, clicks "Blank document," and renames it "PM Tool Comparison." If you're not logged into Google, Claude will pause and ask you to sign in.

4

Claude builds the comparison table

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude inserts a table using Google Docs' table tool (Insert > Table), creates the header row with your specified columns, then fills in each row with the data it gathered. You'll see it typing in each cell, switching between the research tabs and the doc to verify details.

5

Claude writes the recommendation

ON YOUR SCREEN

Below the table, Claude types a 2-3 paragraph recommendation summarizing which tool is best for different freelancer types — solo creatives, client-heavy freelancers, agency-style operations. The document auto-saves in your Google Drive.

How to Guide It When It Gets Stuck

  • "Skip that tool" — If Claude is spending too long on one site or the pricing page is behind a paywall
  • "Add [tool name] instead" — If you want a specific tool included that Claude didn't choose
  • "Use bullet points instead of a table" — If you prefer a different format midway through
  • "Check the pricing page again, I think it changed" — If data looks outdated
  • "Make the recommendation more opinionated" — If the summary is too neutral
  • "Add a column for [X]" — If you want additional comparison criteria

Tips for Better Research Prompts

  • Be specific about how many items to research (top 5, top 3, all options under $20/month)
  • Tell Claude what data to collect — price, features, reviews, screenshots, etc.
  • Specify the output format — table, list, doc, slides, spreadsheet
  • Ask it to keep tabs open so you can verify sources yourself
  • Include your use case so recommendations are relevant: "I'm a freelance designer managing 3-5 client projects"

USE CASE 03

Fill Out This Application / Form

Data entry, applications, registrations — automated and reviewed before submit.

The Prompt

Open this job application form: [paste URL]. Use the personal info I'm about to give you to fill in every field. For work experience, use my resume that's open in the other tab. For any field you're not sure about, leave it blank and highlight it for me. Do NOT submit the form — let me review everything first.

What Happens on Screen

1

Claude opens the form URL

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude clicks into the browser address bar, types or pastes the URL you provided, and the application form loads. Claude scans the entire form to understand what fields exist — text inputs, dropdowns, file uploads, date pickers, etc.

2

Claude switches to your resume tab to gather info

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude clicks on the browser tab where your resume is open (PDF viewer, Google Doc, or Word Online). It reads through your work history, education, skills, and contact info. Then it switches back to the form tab.

3

Claude fills in each field

ON YOUR SCREEN

Starting from the top of the form, Claude clicks into each field and types the appropriate info. For dropdowns, it clicks the menu and selects the right option. For date fields, it uses the date picker. You'll see fields being filled one by one, like someone is typing with purpose.

4

Claude handles tricky fields

ON YOUR SCREEN

For multi-page forms, Claude clicks "Next" or "Continue" to move through pages. For conditional fields (like "If yes, explain"), Claude reads the context and fills accordingly. For fields it can't confidently fill, it leaves them blank and tells you in the chat: "I left the 'Salary Expectation' field blank — want me to fill that in?"

5

Claude stops before submit

ON YOUR SCREEN

Claude finishes filling the form and tells you: "All fields are filled. I left [X] fields blank for your review. The Submit button is at the bottom — I haven't clicked it. Please scroll through and check everything before submitting."

Handling Special Form Elements

Safety Tips

  • ALWAYS include "Do NOT submit" in your prompt. Review everything yourself first.
  • Never ask Claude to enter passwords. Log into sites yourself before starting.
  • Don't paste sensitive data (SSN, bank info) in the chat. Fill those fields manually.
  • Review every field before clicking Submit — especially dollar amounts, dates, and legal agreements.
  • For payment forms: Fill in everything except payment details. Enter card info yourself.
  • Screenshot the completed form before submitting, in case you need a record.
