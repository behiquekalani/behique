Build Log

Notes from the trenches. What I'm building, what broke, and what I learned.

2026-03-21

Day 1: I built 20 products in one night

Started at midnight. By 6am I had 20 digital products listed on Gumroad with landing pages, descriptions, and pricing. No templates. No course telling me what to sell. Just execution.

The idea was simple: stop planning, start shipping. I had a list of things I knew how to do. AI automation guides, Shopify themes, prompt libraries, budget templates. Each one could be a product. So I built them.


The stack was lean. HTML landing pages with inline CSS. A settings widget for theme switching. Gumroad for payments. No monthly fees. No Shopify subscription eating into margins I hadn't earned yet.


Most people spend weeks choosing a color palette. I spent that time writing sales copy and building checkout flows. The products aren't perfect. They don't need to be. They need to exist.


Twenty products. One night. Zero excuses left.

2026-03-19

The $0 AI fleet: 3 computers, zero subscriptions

Running an AI employee system across three machines. Ceiba on the M4, Cobo on the GTX 1080 Ti, Hutia as the bridge. No cloud bills. No API costs. Just local inference and shell scripts.

Everyone talks about AI costs. GPT-4 API bills, cloud compute, monthly SaaS stacks that eat $200 before you've made a dollar. I took a different approach.


Ceiba runs on my M4 MacBook. 16GB unified memory, enough for most inference tasks. Cobo sits on an old desktop with a GTX 1080 Ti. 11GB of VRAM that handles Ollama models without breaking a sweat. Hutia is the bridge, a dedicated machine that keeps everything connected.


The result: an AI fleet that runs 24/7 with zero recurring costs. The machines talk to each other through a task bridge. Ceiba delegates heavy compute to Cobo. Hutia manages the queue. It's not elegant, but it works. And it's mine.


When your infrastructure costs $0/month, every dollar of revenue is profit. That changes the math on everything.

2026-03-15

Why I'm building from Puerto Rico

No Silicon Valley. No VC funding. No co-working space with $12 lattes. Just a desk in PR, fast internet, and the pressure of knowing nobody's coming to save you.

People ask why I don't move to the mainland. Better networking, bigger market, more opportunities. All true. All irrelevant.


Building from Puerto Rico means building with constraints. Slower shipping times for physical products. A smaller local market. Infrastructure that doesn't always cooperate. But constraints breed creativity.


The internet doesn't care where you live. My landing pages load the same speed in San Juan as they do in San Francisco. My AI models don't run slower because they're in the Caribbean. And my overhead is a fraction of what it would be in Austin or NYC.


This island made me. The resilience, the resourcefulness, the ability to make something out of nothing. That's not a limitation. That's a competitive advantage.

2026-03-10

ADHD is not a weakness. It's a different OS.

Diagnosed ADHD. Working with a psychologist. Building systems that work WITH my brain instead of against it. Here's what actually helps.

For years I thought something was wrong with me. Couldn't focus on boring tasks. Could hyperfocus on interesting ones for 14 hours straight. Started a hundred projects, finished twelve. The ones I finished were exceptional.


Getting diagnosed changed the framing. ADHD isn't a deficit of attention. It's a different allocation system. My brain doesn't do well with arbitrary priorities. It does extremely well with things that matter.


So I stopped fighting it. Built systems instead. BehiqueBot captures every idea the moment it hits, so I don't lose the spark trying to remember it later. Pomodoro sessions with hard stops. One task at a time, visually tracked. Small wins logged and celebrated.


The psychologist framework I'm using became the backbone of BehiqueBot. Break tasks small. Validate effort over result. Name avoidance without judgment. Reduce scope when stuck. It works because it respects how my brain actually operates instead of pretending it's something it's not.


I'm not building despite ADHD. I'm building because of how it wired me to think.

2026-03-05

Faith, family, and shipping code at 3am

The real motivation isn't money or status. It's making sure my family never has to worry. Everything I build connects back to that.

At 3am the house is quiet. Everyone's asleep. The screen light is the only thing on. This is when the best code gets written, not because of some productivity hack, but because the silence makes the purpose clear.


I build for my family. That's the foundation. Every product, every automation, every late-night debugging session is connected to a simple goal: build something that provides. Not just money. Security. Freedom. The ability to say "we're good" and mean it.


Faith keeps me grounded when the metrics are flat and nobody's buying. It's easy to lose perspective when you're measuring success in page views and conversion rates. But the work has meaning beyond the dashboard. It's an act of stewardship. Using what I've been given to build something real.


The tech world loves to talk about passion and disruption. My motivation is quieter than that. It's a family that deserves stability. It's a faith that says the work matters even when the results don't show yet. It's the belief that consistent effort, compounded over time, changes everything.
