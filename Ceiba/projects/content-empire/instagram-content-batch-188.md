## Post 3 — How I Track All My Projects Without Losing Any

**Hook:** I never lose track of a project. This is why.

**Slide 1:** I never lose track of a project. This is why.

**Slide 2:** Every project in my workspace has the same structure: goal, deadline, status, task list, notes.

**Slide 3:** Status has four options: Planning, Active, Paused, Done. Nothing sits in an undefined state.

**Slide 4:** Once a week I scan the project list. Active projects get updated. Paused projects get a decision: restart or archive. Done projects move to archive.

**Slide 5:** The result: I always know what's in flight, what's waiting, and what's finished. Nothing disappears into the background.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. Post 4 covers the content pipeline.

**Caption:**
Projects die when they lose definition. You start something, it gets complicated, you set it aside for "a few days," and three months later you find it untouched in a folder with no idea where you left off.

The fix is structure, not motivation. Every project needs a goal, a deadline, and a status. Not because you're a project manager. Because those three things are what a project needs to stay real.

When a project has a goal, you know when it's done. When it has a deadline, it has gravity. When it has a status, you know whether it's alive or needs a decision.

The weekly scan keeps everything honest. Five minutes, once a week, to make sure nothing is silently dying. If something is paused for more than two weeks, it gets decided on. Restart or archive. No zombie projects.

That's the system. Simple enough to maintain. Rigorous enough to work.

**Hook:** My content pipeline lives inside my business workspace. Not in a separate app.

**Slide 2:** Most creators separate their content system from their business system. That split costs context.

**Slide 4:** The pipeline has five stages: Idea, Draft, Review, Scheduled, Published.

**Slide 5:** I process ideas from my inbox directly into the content pipeline. I write drafts in the same page. When something is published, it archives automatically.

**Slide 6:** Save this. The pipeline is how content becomes consistent without becoming stressful.

**Caption:**
Content is not a separate job. It's part of the business. When you treat it as separate, it competes for attention instead of supporting the work.

The fix is integration. Your content pipeline should live inside your business workspace, connected to your products, your projects, and your goals. A post about your service links to that service's page. A thread about a product links to that product's launch project.

When content is integrated, it's easier to plan. You look at what's active in the business and you know what to create. You don't stare at a blank calendar wondering what to post.

The pipeline itself is simple. Five stages. Ideas come in from the inbox. Drafts get written. Published content archives and becomes a reference for future work.

This is how content becomes consistent without requiring constant creative effort. The system does the routing. You just write.

## Post 5 — What I Would Build Differently If I Started Over

**Hook:** If I built this workspace today from scratch, I would do three things differently.

**Slide 1:** If I built this workspace today from scratch, I would do three things differently.

**Slide 2:** First: I would build the inbox before anything else. I spent two weeks building complex databases before I had a reliable capture habit. All that structure was wasted.

**Slide 3:** Second: I would keep the dashboard brutally simple. My first version had 12 sections. I used 3 of them. More sections is not more clarity.

**Slide 4:** Third: I would schedule the weekly review from day one. I built a great workspace and then stopped maintaining it. It took a month before I made the review non-negotiable.

**Slide 5:** The lesson: systems are not built once. They're built in layers. Start minimal. Add only what you actually use.

**Slide 6:** The full template is ready. Built with these lessons already applied. Link in bio.

**Caption:**
I've rebuilt this workspace three times. Each version was more useful than the last, not because I added more, but because I removed what I wasn't actually using.

The pattern was always the same. Start ambitious. Build too much. Use a fraction of it. Get frustrated. Rebuild simpler.

Now I know the order. Build the inbox first. Build the dashboard next. Build the weekly review habit before adding any more structure. Only add pages and databases for things you are already doing, not things you intend to do.

The workspace I use today has far fewer pages than the first version. But it's used daily. Every section earns its place by getting touched at least once a week.

That's the principle I baked into the template. Start with what works. Add the rest later.
