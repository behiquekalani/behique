# LinkedIn Post — batch-1301

I used to think the goal was to get better at everything.

Then I watched a friend who was average at ten things lose every client pitch to someone who was exceptional at one.

He spent five years collecting skills. The other person spent five years going deeper on a single problem. One had a resume. The other had a reputation.

The lesson I took: breadth is useful for conversation. Depth is what people actually pay for.

Most creators chase range because range feels like progress. You learn copywriting, then SEO, then email marketing, then video editing. Each skill is real. But none of them compound into authority when they're scattered.

Authority comes from a point of view that's been tested over time on a specific problem. Not from having many options.

If someone can describe what you're known for in one sentence, you have positioning. If they need a paragraph, you have a portfolio, not a brand.

The question isn't "what else should I learn?" It's "what do I want to go deeper on than anyone else is willing to?"

That's the work. And most people skip it because it feels like closing doors.

It's actually how you open the right ones.
