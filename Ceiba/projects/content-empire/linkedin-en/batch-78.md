# LinkedIn EN — Batch 78
## Topic: Digital products as a professional

---

### Post 78-1

Every knowledge worker is sitting on a product they have not built yet.

The advice you give in every onboarding meeting. The framework you use every time you approach a certain type of problem. The checklist you rebuilt three times because the existing ones were not good enough. The process you documented for your team.

All of that is a product. Not theoretically. Literally.

The only difference between that knowledge and a product you can sell is packaging and distribution. The packaging is putting it into a format someone can use without you present. The distribution is making sure the people who need it can find it.

Both of those are learnable. Neither requires technical expertise. A PDF, a template, a recorded walkthrough. These are accessible formats that knowledge workers can produce without a development team or a design background.

The more specific the problem your product solves, the faster it sells. The less it needs you to explain it, the more it scales.

You already have the knowledge. The product is the missing step.

---

### Post 78-2

The professional who builds a digital product before they feel ready will always outpace the one who waits until the timing is right.

Timing never feels right. The product is never perfect at launch. The audience is never large enough. The market research is never complete.

These are real concerns with a false conclusion. The conclusion being that you should wait.

The actual data from people who have launched: the second product is significantly better than the first. The third is better still. The improvement comes from doing, not from planning.

What changes when you launch early is that you get real feedback from real buyers. That feedback cannot be simulated. No amount of research produces the same clarity as 50 people purchasing something and telling you what they wish it had.

The professionals who build the best digital products are not the most credentialed. They are the ones who launched something early enough to iterate.

Start with a minimum viable product. Solve one specific problem. Price it modestly. Ship it. The feedback will teach you more than any preparation.

---

### Post 78-3

There is a type of digital product most professionals overlook because it does not feel like a product.

Templates.

A Notion workspace you built for your own project management. A slide deck structure you use every time you pitch. A spreadsheet that automates a calculation you do every week. A framework document you reach for when approaching a specific type of problem.

These are products. They require no new expertise to create. They are things you already have.

The reason they sell is simple. Templates collapse time. The buyer wants the outcome of building that system without the hours it took you to build it. They are paying you for the scar tissue.

Templates sell faster than courses because the value is immediately visible. There is no learning curve to unlock the value. You download it, you use it, it works.

If you have never sold anything before and you want to understand what product-market fit feels like, build a template. Solve one workflow. Put it on Gumroad for $15. See what happens.

That first sale will change how you think about what your expertise is worth.
