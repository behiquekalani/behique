The best business advice I ever ignored for too long:

Solve one problem for one person better than anyone else.

I wanted to solve many problems. For many people. Because that felt like more opportunity.

What it actually was: more confusion for potential customers, more complexity in my operations, more dilution of my energy, and less clarity in my marketing.

The businesses that scale the fastest usually start with something embarrassingly narrow. One customer profile. One pain point. One solution. The narrowness feels like a limitation until you realize that being the clear choice for a specific person is far more powerful than being a decent option for everyone.

When your ideal customer reads your about page, they should feel like you're describing them. Not a category of people they vaguely fit into. Them. Their specific situation, their specific frustration, their specific goal.

That specificity is what converts. Vague value propositions produce vague interest. Sharp specificity produces purchase decisions.

Narrow down until it feels uncomfortable. That's usually about where the clarity starts to work.

If you had to describe your ideal customer in two sentences, what would you say?
