## Post 186 — Presenting Results to Clients

Delivering great work and communicating great work are two different skills. You need both.

Most professionals underinvest in the communication side because they assume the work speaks for itself. It does not. Data does not translate itself into business impact. You have to do that translation, or the client does it for you, usually incorrectly.

Before your next client presentation, go back to the original brief. What outcome did they say success looked like at the start of the engagement? Your presentation needs to open there, not with your deliverables or your process.

The structure that consistently works is three steps: here is where you were, here is what changed, here is what that means for your business. Every metric you include must connect to that third step. If you cannot answer "so what does this mean for your business" about a number, remove it from the deck. You are not demonstrating your effort. You are communicating their result.

The clients who renew are almost always the ones who understood clearly what they received. Most professionals lose renewals not because the work was bad but because the client never fully grasped the value.

The translation step takes 30 minutes before a presentation. Skipping it costs you the client.

## Post 187 — Expert vs Seeming Expert

You can be the most skilled person in a room and still be the last one to get hired if no one knows how you think.

This is not a cynical observation. It is how trust actually works. Before someone pays you, they need evidence that you understand their problem and that you have solved something like it before. That evidence does not come from your resume alone. It comes from the accumulated record of your public thinking over time.

Being an expert is knowing how to solve the problem. Seeming like an expert is making that knowledge visible before the client is in the room.

The professionals who grow steadily are the ones who write about their decisions, document their frameworks, and share what they learned from the projects that did not go as planned. None of that requires exaggeration. It requires showing up consistently with something real.

One post per week where you share a genuine insight from your actual work compounds into a body of credibility that operates independently of any single engagement or referral.

Your expertise already exists. The gap is between what you know and what is visible to the people who could pay for it.

Start closing that gap this week.

## Post 188 — Priorities in Small Teams

In a small team, one week of misdirected effort is a problem you cannot easily recover from.

When you are a team of three or four, a misaligned week is not a small inefficiency. It is a meaningful fraction of your total capacity lost. There is no buffer of headcount to absorb it.

The mistake most small teams make is treating urgency and importance as the same thing. Urgent tasks feel like they must happen today. Important tasks are the ones that change where the business is in 90 days. Most small teams spend most of their time on urgent and almost no time on important, not because they are lazy but because urgent is louder.

The solution is a single weekly decision made before the work begins: what is the one thing this team must complete by Friday that takes priority over everything else. One thing. Not a ranked list of five. One.

This requires the leader to be willing to say no to other things that also matter. That is the hardest part of the job. But a team aligned on one clear priority consistently outperforms a team navigating five competing ones.

Run a 20-minute meeting at the start of each week. Set one anchor priority. Assign clear ownership. Close with every person able to state exactly what they are responsible for by Friday.

Clarity does not motivate your team. But the absence of it quietly exhausts them.

## Post 189 — The Hidden Cost of Bad Communication

Projects rarely fail in one dramatic moment. They fail across dozens of small miscommunications that nobody addressed.

A brief that was not detailed enough. A meeting that ended without written agreements. A scope change mentioned in passing that nobody recorded. Each of those is a small debt. The problem is that small debts compound.

By the time the project is struggling, the communication failures have stacked into something expensive: rework that should not have been necessary, additional meetings to re-clarify decisions that should have been documented, client trust eroding because deliveries keep needing correction.

The direct cost is time. Rework and corrections are measurable. The indirect cost is harder to see but worse: your team loses confidence in the process, your client loses confidence in you, and the energy that should drive the project goes into managing friction instead.

Three habits prevent most of this. Confirm verbal agreements in writing immediately, not later that day, immediately. Document scope changes the moment they happen, not during the retrospective. Ask clarifying questions before you execute, not after you have already done the work.

Each of those habits takes minutes. Not having them takes days.

## Post 190 — Negotiating Your First Big Client

Large clients do not choose the cheapest option. They choose the one that gives them the clearest picture of what they are buying.

Their primary concern is not the price. It is the risk of spending money and receiving something other than what they needed. Your proposal has one job: eliminate that risk before it becomes an objection.

That means being specific. Specific about the process, the deliverables, the timeline, and what success looks like in terms they can measure. When a client can read your proposal and visualize the result clearly, the negotiation stops being about trust and starts being about details.

The most common mistake in proposals for large clients is writing something generic that could apply to anyone. That signals immediately that you did not take the time to understand their specific situation. And if you do not understand their situation, you cannot solve their problem.

Before writing a word of the proposal, answer three questions. What is the actual problem underneath the one they described to you? What does it cost their business to leave that problem unsolved? Who else inside the organization cares about this decision?

With that foundation, your proposal stops being a quote for services and becomes a solution to a specific, costly problem. That is a different kind of conversation entirely.
