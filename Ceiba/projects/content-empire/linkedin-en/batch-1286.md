I priced my first product too low because I thought price was about what I deserved.

It is not. Price is about what your customer believes they will gain.

When I charged $7 for something that saved someone three hours of work per week, I was not being humble. I was being incoherent. I was pricing based on my own insecurity about the value I was providing, not based on the actual value the product delivered.

The result was predictable. Low-price customers are, on average, more demanding and less satisfied than higher-price customers. This is counterintuitive but consistent. When someone pays $7, they expect $7 worth of care and attention. When someone pays $97, they expect $97 worth, and they usually come prepared to do the work required to get there.

Higher prices filter for the customers who are serious. That is not manipulation. That is matching your offer to the audience most likely to benefit from it.

It took me a while to separate my self-worth from my pricing decisions. But once I did, everything improved. Better customers. Better feedback. Better relationships. More focused work.

If you are underpricing, ask yourself honestly whether the number reflects your product's value or your own comfort level.

Those are two very different things.

What would you charge if you were certain your product delivered exactly what it promises?
