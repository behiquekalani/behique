I tracked my time for one month and did not like what I found.

Not because I was lazy. Because I was busy doing real work that was not the right work.

The analysis revealed three categories. Things only I could do, things someone or something else could handle, and things that should not happen at all. The third category was surprisingly large. Entire workflows I had built to manage processes that existed because of bad initial decisions I had never revisited.

The instinct when you find that kind of waste is embarrassment. You feel like you should have known better. But the honest view is that every system accumulates cruft over time. Priorities shift. Products evolve. What made sense six months ago becomes overhead you have been carrying without noticing.

The audit is not a judgment. It is maintenance.

Since doing that analysis I run a quarterly review of everything I do regularly. Not to optimize each task but to ask whether it should exist. The bar is simple: does this contribute to output, relationships, or revenue? If the answer is no, it gets cut.

This process has freed more useful hours than any productivity technique I have tried. Because the issue was never how I was spending my time. It was what I was choosing to spend it on.

What is one recurring task in your week that has never been questioned?
