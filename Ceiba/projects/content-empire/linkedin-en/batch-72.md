# LinkedIn EN — Batch 72
## Topic: Monetizing Expertise

---

### Post 1

Your expertise has a market value that your salary doesn't reflect.

That's not a criticism of employment. It's an observation about how knowledge is priced differently when it's packaged versus when it's rented by the hour.

When you're employed, your expertise is priced by the labor market. Benchmarks, comp bands, industry averages. The price is set by supply, demand, and negotiation in a constrained context.

When you package and distribute that same expertise directly, the price is set by the value of the outcome you produce for the buyer, and the specificity with which you can address their problem.

A financial analyst who writes a $197 guide on how early-career professionals can structure their first investment portfolio isn't in competition with their employer. They're in a different market entirely.

The person who buys it doesn't care about your title. They care about the result.

The expertise you use in your professional work every day is solving problems for someone, at scale. The people who've figured this out stopped waiting for permission to monetize it outside the walls of their organization.

### Post 2

Three ways to monetize expertise ranked by time-to-first-dollar:

1. Consulting or fractional work. Fastest path. Your employer pays you for this already. Other companies will too. Define the problem you solve, set a rate, and find two to three organizations that need it. No product required.

2. Digital products. Slower to first dollar, but the ratio of effort to revenue is better over time. One well-positioned guide, template, or framework sells repeatedly without adding more of your time.

3. Content and audience. Slowest to direct revenue but highest leverage long-term. An audience that trusts your expertise becomes the distribution channel for everything else.

Most professionals start with consulting because the feedback loop is fast. The mistake is staying there permanently and never building the distribution or products that generate income without direct time investment.

The path is: service validates the knowledge, products scale the knowledge, audience compounds the knowledge.

### Post 3

The bottleneck to monetizing expertise is rarely the expertise itself.

It's the positioning.

Positioning is the answer to three questions:
1. Who specifically is this for?
2. What specific problem does it solve?
3. Why is this person the right guide for that problem?

Most professionals have clear answers to questions one and two from their work experience. The breakdown happens at question three.

The answer to "why you" doesn't require credentials or a degree. It requires a track record, even a small one, and the willingness to make that track record visible.

Content is the mechanism. Writing a LinkedIn post that demonstrates your thinking on a domain problem is the first form of proof. A case study from a project at work is the second. A piece of work that you've done and documented publicly is the third.

Positioning builds before the product exists. The mistake is waiting until the product is ready to start building the case for why someone should buy from you.
