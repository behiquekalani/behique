## Post 536: You Don't Have a Time Problem
You have a priority problem.

Every person I know who says they don't have time to build their project is spending three hours a day consuming content made by people who found the time to build theirs.

I'm not saying this to be harsh. I lived this for years. I told myself I was learning. I was reading about entrepreneurship, watching breakdowns of successful companies, following the people I wanted to become. And at some level, that was useful. But at another level, it was sophisticated procrastination dressed up as self-improvement.

The shift happened when I started treating my own work with the same seriousness I gave to other people's work. When I stopped waiting to feel ready and started treating the hour I had like it actually mattered.

You have more time than you think. But time without intention just becomes more of the same.

Here's what worked for me: pick one thing that, if it existed, would change your situation. Not a dozen things. One. Then find one hour per day that currently belongs to passive consumption and redirect it. Not forever. Just for thirty days.

At the end of thirty days you'll either have something real, or you'll know exactly what's actually blocking you. Both are valuable.

The constraint you think is time is almost always energy or clarity or commitment. Time is just the easiest thing to blame.

What's the one thing you'd build if you actually protected time for it?

## Post 537: What I Learned Building Products Nobody Asked For
Some of my most important products were also my least successful ones.

I built a tool once that I thought was obviously needed. Elegant solution to a real problem I had. Spent six weeks on it. Showed it to ten people who all said "that's cool" and then never used it again.

At the time, that felt like failure. Looking back, it was a master class.

First lesson: "that's cool" means nothing. Real signal is someone pulling out their wallet, asking where they can share it, or reaching back out unprompted a week later. Everything else is polite noise.

Second lesson: I built it because I wanted to build it. I told myself I was solving a user problem but I was really solving a builder itch. Those are different, and confusing them is expensive.

Third lesson: the market doesn't care about your solution, only about its own pain. If you can't clearly articulate the pain before you describe the product, you're building from the wrong direction.

The products that worked came from the opposite process. Start with the complaint. Find the person who's frustrated enough to pay to make it stop. Build the minimum thing that makes it stop. Then listen to what they say next.

I still build things for myself sometimes. But I know the difference now. And I don't call it a business until the pain is verified.

What's a product you built that taught you more from its failure than its success?

## Post 538: The Network Nobody Tells You To Build
Everyone tells you to network. Almost nobody tells you how.

The networking advice you've heard is mostly wrong. Not because connection doesn't matter, but because the version being sold is fundamentally transactional. Go to events. Add value. Build relationships. These things are not wrong, they're just incomplete in a way that produces hollow connections and full LinkedIn inboxes.

The network that actually changes your life is built differently. It's built through the work itself.

When you write clearly about what you're doing and thinking, you attract people who think similarly. When you ship things publicly, you create evidence of who you are that people can respond to. When you go deep on a specific problem, you become findable by everyone else who has that problem.

The relationships that have changed my trajectory the most weren't made at events. They happened because someone read something I wrote and reached out. Because I was working on something specific enough that it resonated with someone who needed exactly that.

Depth attracts depth. Breadth attracts noise.

Build your network by building your body of work. Not by collecting cards and sending follow-up emails into the void.

The right people find you when you're doing the work loudly enough.

What's the most meaningful professional connection you made through the work itself, not through intentional networking?

## Post 539: Why Your First Version Should Embarrass You
If your first version doesn't embarrass you, you shipped too late.

This is advice I resisted for a long time because I have high standards and the idea of releasing something imperfect felt like a personal failure. Then I watched people with lower standards ship faster, learn faster, and build better things than me by the time I was ready to release my first "polished" version.

Here's what shipping early actually does. It creates real feedback loops that don't exist in your head. It forces you to defend your decisions to someone who doesn't share your assumptions. It shows you which parts of the product people actually use versus the parts you thought were clever.

Here's what polishing early actually does. It optimizes against your own imagination, which is the least reliable tester in the world. It delays the moment of contact with reality. It creates emotional investment in the current version that makes it harder to change what needs changing.

The embarrassment of an early version is information. It tells you exactly what matters to people. The features you cut for the MVP that users immediately ask for? Build those next. The features you lovingly crafted that nobody mentions? You now know what to deprioritize.

Ship embarrassed. Iterate in public. The discomfort is the tuition.

What's one thing you've been waiting to polish before shipping?

## Post 540: The Real Cost of Working Alone
Working alone feels efficient until it isn't.

I spent the first years of building mostly solo. Head down, figure it out, don't need anyone else. There's a real kind of freedom in that. You move fast. Nobody slows you down with meetings or consensus. You make decisions and live with them.

But there's a hidden cost that only shows up slowly. When you work alone, your feedback loop is you. And you are not an unbiased evaluator of your own work. You can't see what you're blind to. You optimize for what you value. You miss the category of problems you've never had.

The shift for me was finding one or two people who were building in adjacent spaces, who weren't my direct competition, who were honest enough to tell me when something wasn't working. Not a mastermind. Not a formal accountability group. Just a few people who understood the game and weren't afraid to be direct.

That small shift compounded faster than almost anything I did in terms of tools or systems. Because the bottleneck in early-stage building usually isn't execution. It's perspective.

You don't need a co-founder or a full team. But you do need someone who can see your blind spots. Someone who'll say "that's not the problem you think it is" and you'll actually hear it.

Efficiency isn't just speed. It's the ratio of effort to useful output. Working alone optimizes for speed. Working with the right people optimizes for direction.

Who's your best honest feedback source right now?
