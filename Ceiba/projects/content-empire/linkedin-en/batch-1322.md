Most people treat their first product like a job interview.

They spend months perfecting it. Polishing every detail. Afraid to launch until it's "ready."

Meanwhile, the market has already moved on.

Here's what I've learned from building 100+ digital products: the first version is a hypothesis. That's it. You're not launching a finished product. You're launching a question.

The question is: does this solve a problem people will pay to fix?

The answer comes from real buyers, not from your own head.

The fastest way to get that answer is to ship something that's 80% done. Something real, functional, and honest about what it is. Then let the market tell you what's missing.

Your first sale will teach you more than six months of refinement.

Your first refund request will show you exactly what to fix next.

You don't need permission to ship. You need courage to be wrong in public and iterate anyway.

That's what separates people who build businesses from people who build portfolios no one uses.

What's the one thing stopping you from shipping this week?
