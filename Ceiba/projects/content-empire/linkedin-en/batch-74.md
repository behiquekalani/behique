# LinkedIn EN — Batch 74
## Topic: Building Digital Products as a Professional

---

### Post 1

You already have the raw material for a digital product. You use it at work every day.

The frameworks you apply when solving a recurring problem. The process you've refined over years that makes you faster than your peers. The mental models that let you spot what others miss. These are productizable.

The barrier isn't knowledge. It's packaging.

Packaging means: identifying the specific person who needs this, defining the specific outcome it produces, and presenting it in a format they can consume and apply without your direct involvement.

That last part is the product. It works without you in the room.

Most professionals never build this because they've been trained to associate their expertise with their time. The consulting model, the billable hour, the project fee. All of it ties value delivery to presence.

A digital product decouples the value from your presence. You build it once. It delivers value repeatedly.

The professional who builds this while employed has no financial pressure. That's the structural advantage most founders would pay for.

### Post 2

The minimum viable digital product for a working professional is simpler than you think.

It doesn't require a course platform, a marketing team, or months of production. It requires:

- A specific problem you've solved
- A clear process for solving it
- A buyer who has that problem right now

The format can be a PDF guide, a template, a Notion system, a video walkthrough, or a short structured email course. The format is not the product. The transformation it enables is the product.

Here's the test: can someone go from problem to solution by consuming this without asking you a follow-up question? If yes, you have a product.

Sell it to five people before you optimize anything. The feedback from those five will tell you more than six months of planning.

The professionals who make this work don't wait until it's perfect. They ship something functional, observe how people use it, and improve based on real behavior, not assumptions.

### Post 3

Three reasons professionals build digital products and then stop before they see results:

1. They measure too early. A product that's been live for two weeks with no sales is not a failed product. It's a product with no distribution yet. Distribution takes time to build.

2. They built for the wrong person. The most common version: they built something they wish they had early in their career, but they're marketing it to people at a different stage. Specificity of audience is the first filter.

3. They conflate the product with the business. The product is one component. The business includes content that builds awareness, a platform that builds trust, an offer that converts interest to purchase, and a fulfillment mechanism. Most professionals build the product and ignore everything else.

The solution to all three is the same: stay in motion. Adjust distribution before you abandon the product. Refine the audience targeting before you rebuild the offer. Build the business components sequentially, not simultaneously.

A digital product that fails to sell has a distribution problem, not a product problem. Fix the right variable.
