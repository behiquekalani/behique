# LinkedIn EN — Batch 77
## Topic: Finding your knowledge niche as a professional

---

### Post 77-1

Most professionals undervalue what they know because they are surrounded by people who know the same things.

That is the expertise blindspot. The thing that is obvious to you because you have been doing it for five years is completely opaque to someone who just started. The gap between your current knowledge and someone else's starting point is your niche.

The mistake is looking for a niche in the wrong direction: toward what you want to learn, toward what sounds prestigious, toward what seems like it has a big market.

The more reliable direction is backward. What did you struggle with two or three years ago that you no longer struggle with? That past struggle is your niche. You understand the problem from the inside. You know what the wrong answers look like. You know what actually works.

Lived frustration is more valuable than theoretical expertise. The person who solved the problem is more credible to someone currently in the problem than the person who studied the problem.

Start there. Your niche is probably already sitting in your work history.

---

### Post 77-2

The knowledge niche that works is specific enough to feel personal.

"Marketing" is not a niche. "Email marketing for independent consultants who have never built a list" is a niche. The second one is immediately recognizable to the person it describes. The first one describes half the internet.

Specificity creates recognition. When someone reads your niche description and thinks "that is exactly me," you have their full attention. That attention converts into subscribers, clients, and sales at a completely different rate than broad positioning.

The fear most professionals have about going specific is that the audience will be too small. This is almost always the wrong fear. The more common reality is that the audience is large enough and the competition is thin because most people chose the safe, broad positioning.

You do not need millions of people to build a meaningful professional practice. You need a few hundred people who feel like you were built for them.

Specificity is the shortcut to that feeling.

---

### Post 77-3

Here is the 24-hour exercise for finding your professional niche:

List five problems you have solved in your career that were not in any job description. The informal expertise. The skills you developed because you had to, not because someone trained you.

For each one, write one sentence: "I help [specific person] do [specific outcome] without [specific obstacle they expect]."

The sentence that feels most natural is your niche. Not the one that sounds most impressive. The one that sounds most like a normal conversation you have already had.

Your niche is almost always something you have already been doing informally. You have been giving this advice to colleagues. You have been solving this problem for others who asked you how you did it. You have been thinking about this topic long after the workday ends.

The niche-finding exercise is not creative. It is just surfacing what is already there.
