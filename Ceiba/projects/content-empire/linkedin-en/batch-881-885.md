## Post 881: The Most Valuable Thing You Can Do in Your 20s
Invest in skills that compound.

Not degrees. Not credentials. Not job titles.

Skills.

There's a specific category of skill that produces returns for decades. Writing. Sales. Systems thinking. Building online. Understanding how money moves.

These don't expire. They don't get outsourced. They don't become irrelevant when a company restructures.

Most people in their 20s do the opposite. They invest in credentials that get them a job rather than skills that would make them indispensable anywhere.

The difference shows up a decade later.

The person who spent their 20s developing genuine writing skill has a built-in distribution channel for anything they create. The person who spent them accumulating job titles has a resume.

This is not anti-career. A career built on deep skills is far more stable than one built on titles.

The practical version:
- Spend one hour per day building a skill nobody can take from you
- Build in public so the skill becomes reputation
- Ship regularly so you have proof of competence, not just claims
- Repeat for five years without expecting immediate return

The compounding starts quietly. Then it accelerates fast enough that people who weren't watching assume you got lucky.

You didn't get lucky. You started earlier than they thought.

What skill are you building right now that nobody can take from you?

## Post 882: Your Price Tells People What to Think of Your Work
Pricing is not just a number. It's a signal.

When you price too low, you don't just leave money on the table. You tell buyers that your work isn't worth serious investment. You attract people who need a deal more than they need the result. You create a customer base that's harder to satisfy and less committed to the outcome.

When you price appropriately, you do the opposite. You attract people who take the purchase seriously. They implement. They get results. They refer others. They come back.

The practical implication: stop competing on price in a market where nobody else is either.

The creators who undercharge are not being humble. They're being afraid. They're pricing for the reaction they expect from their least qualified buyer instead of the value they deliver to their best one.

How to approach pricing instead:

Think about the outcome your product produces. What is that outcome worth in time, money, or peace of mind? Price close to that value, not close to what it cost you to create.

Then test. Raise the price until you feel slight discomfort. That's usually the right direction.

The market almost always supports prices higher than creators expect.

And the version of you who charges appropriately gets to serve better clients, deliver better work, and reinvest in building something worth even more.

What would you charge if you believed completely in the value of your work?

## Post 883: The Framework I Use for Every Content Decision
Every piece of content I make passes through a three-question filter.

Question one: does this teach something or shift perspective?

If yes, it earns attention. If it's purely informational without insight, it's replaceable by a Google search.

Question two: does this speak directly to one specific person?

Not a demographic. A person. If I can picture who is reading this and what they're dealing with, the content will land. If it's written for "everyone interested in content creation," it'll resonate with no one.

Question three: does this move someone closer to taking an action?

Not necessarily buying. Could be following, saving, sharing, implementing an idea. Content that doesn't produce some kind of movement is decoration.

This filter sounds simple. But applying it to every post changes your content significantly.

Most content fails question two. It's written about a topic rather than for a person. The fix is to write the first draft however it comes out, then rewrite the opening sentence as if you're speaking directly to the one person you most want to help.

Most content also fails question three. It teaches, but it doesn't close the loop with a direction. Adding a question, a challenge, or a resource at the end converts passive readers into active participants.

Run your last five posts through these three questions. See what happens.

What's the one question that most filters your content?

## Post 884: Nobody Succeeds Alone and Pretending Otherwise Is Ego
The self-made myth is a great story.

One person. Raw talent. No help. Built an empire.

It's almost never true.

Behind every creator success story there's usually: a mentor whose thinking shaped their approach. A community that gave early feedback. A partner who covered logistics while they focused. Platforms built by others that gave them distribution. Courses that compressed years of learning. Tools that automated what would have taken months manually.

This matters because the people who believe the self-made myth tend to avoid asking for help. They treat collaboration like weakness. They spend months figuring out alone what a conversation could have solved in an hour.

The most effective builders I know are also the most willing to learn from others, pay for expertise they don't have, build relationships with people doing adjacent work, and admit what they don't know.

This is not softness. It's efficiency.

You don't have to earn the right to learn from someone who's already done what you're trying to do. You just have to ask well and implement what they share.

Your network is not a vanity metric. It's the actual infrastructure your business runs on. Invest in it deliberately.

Reach out to one person this week who is where you want to be. Ask one specific question. Start there.

Who's one person you've been meaning to reach out to but haven't yet?

## Post 885: The Day I Stopped Trying to Go Viral
Chasing virality is the fastest way to become a creator with no real audience.

Viral posts reach everyone. Which means they reach no one in particular. Which means the people who find you through them often have no connection to your actual work or your actual offer.

I had a post reach 100,000 people. The engagement was exciting for 48 hours. Then I checked: almost none of those people bought anything. Almost none became loyal followers. Almost none matched the profile of who I actually wanted to reach.

That was the lesson.

Viral reach is a terrible measure of content success. The right measure is resonance with the right people.

A post that reaches 500 people and makes 50 of them feel like you're reading their mind will outperform the viral post in every metric that matters: conversions, followers who stay, and word-of-mouth referrals.

Optimize for depth of connection, not breadth of reach.

This means getting more specific, not less. Niching down, not expanding. Writing for the person who needs your exact perspective, not the person who might casually be interested.

It also means being okay with posts that don't perform by vanity metrics but that generate one DM saying "this is exactly what I needed to hear."

That DM is the signal. Follow it.

What's a post you wrote that didn't go viral but clearly hit someone in the right way?
