Everyone talks about morning routines. Nobody talks about what you do in the first five minutes of your workday.

That's where your actual productivity is decided.

Most people open their laptop, check notifications, answer messages, and fall into reactive mode before they've made a single intentional choice. The rest of the day follows that pattern. You respond, you attend, you react. You get to the end of the day with a full calendar and an empty sense of progress.

The alternative is a five-minute ritual: before you open anything, write down the one thing that matters most today. Not the three most important things. One.

Then do that thing first, before your inbox, before Slack, before the news.

This sounds simple. It's not easy because the pull toward reactive behavior is strong. The inbox feels urgent. Notifications feel important. But most of them are other people's priorities packaged as yours.

Your most important work rarely comes with a notification attached to it. It waits quietly for you to choose it.

Five minutes. One thing. First.

What's the one thing on your list tomorrow that would make everything else feel easier if it was done?
