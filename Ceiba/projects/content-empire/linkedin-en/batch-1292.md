Automation changed my business, but not in the way I expected.

I thought the point was to remove work. Do less, earn more. The dream version of what automation promises.

What it actually did was reveal how much of my time I had been spending on things that should never have been done at all.

When you automate a bad process, you just get a faster bad process. The automation does not fix the underlying logic. It accelerates it. So before I could automate intelligently, I had to audit what I was doing and ask honestly whether each piece needed to happen at all, whether it needed to happen the way I was doing it, and only then whether it could be handled by a system instead of my attention.

The result was not a business that ran without me. It was a business where my attention was reserved for the things only I could do: the ideas, the voice, the judgment calls, the relationships.

Everything else either got automated, simplified, or eliminated.

That distinction matters. The goal is not to be busy. The goal is to make progress on the things that create actual value. Automation helps you protect the time for those things, but only if you know what those things are first.

Start with clarity. Build the system second.

What task are you automating right now that maybe just should not be done at all?
