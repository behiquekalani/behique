The most underrated skill in business is knowing when to stop optimizing.

Optimization feels like progress. You tweak the headline, test a new thumbnail, adjust the email subject line. You're always improving. But there's a point where you're not improving the thing. You're avoiding shipping the next thing.

I've seen creators spend three weeks on a landing page that was fine after day two. The extra weeks didn't convert more customers. They postponed getting the next product out.

Perfectionism is a real problem but it usually disguises itself as responsibility. "I just want to get it right." But right enough is a decision, not a discovery.

Set a standard before you start. Know what done looks like. When you hit that standard, ship it and move.

The data you get from a real audience will teach you more in one week than three weeks of internal optimization. Real people tell you things you cannot predict in isolation.

The loop is: build, ship, learn, improve. Not: build, improve, improve, improve, maybe ship.

Iteration only works if you actually release the iteration.

What's something you've been "almost ready to launch" for too long?
