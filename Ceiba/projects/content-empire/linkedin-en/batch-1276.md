Every skill you have is a product waiting to exist.

Most people don't see it that way. They see their skills as things they do for employers or clients. Things that disappear when the job ends.

But any skill that solves a real problem is transferable. And if it's transferable, it's packagable.

I spent years doing things for companies that I eventually realized I could package and sell directly. Systems I had built. Processes I had documented. Frameworks I had developed through trial and error.

None of it was mine on paper. All of it was mine in my head. And my head goes with me everywhere.

The knowledge you've accumulated over a career is not small. It's actually enormous. You just undervalue it because you're too close to it.

What feels obvious to you is a breakthrough to someone two steps behind you. What feels like basic knowledge in your field is advanced knowledge to everyone outside it.

You don't need to be the world's leading expert. You need to be further along than the person you're helping.

Take inventory of what you know. There's a product in there. Probably several.

The question isn't whether you have something worth selling. The question is whether you'll do the work to package it.
