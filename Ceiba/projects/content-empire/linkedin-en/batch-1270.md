There's a moment in every project where you stop believing in it.

It's around 60% complete. The excitement is gone. The end isn't close enough to feel real. You've seen every flaw up close for too long.

Most projects die here.

Not because of execution problems. Not because the idea was bad. Because the creator confused a normal phase of the process with a signal to quit.

I've been at that 60% mark many times. I've quit there and I've pushed through there. The difference in outcomes is not subtle.

The things I quit at 60%: nobody ever saw them. They became folder names I scroll past.

The things I pushed through: some of them made money. Some of them found an audience. All of them taught me something I couldn't have learned without finishing.

Finishing is its own skill. It's separate from creativity and strategy and talent. You can be gifted and still never finish anything. You can be ordinary and build something real just by being someone who finishes.

The best advice I ever got was not about marketing or positioning or pricing.

It was: finish the thing.
