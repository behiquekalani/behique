The student who graduates and immediately starts building something owns more of their future than the one who spends two years waiting for the right job.

I say this as a student.

Not because jobs are bad. Because the window for experimentation is smaller than it looks. Life gets heavier. Obligations stack up. The appetite for risk shrinks.

Right now, the cost of failure is low. A failed project in college costs you time and a bruised ego. A failed project at 35 with a mortgage costs more.

So the real question isn't whether to build something. It's whether you're using the low-stakes window you have.

You don't need a business degree to start a newsletter. You don't need funding to launch a digital product. You don't need a team to build a tool that solves a problem you actually have.

What you need is the willingness to look like a beginner in public while you figure it out.

That willingness is worth more than any credential. Because it compounds into experience, audience, and income that no employer can take from you.

Start something. Now. While the cost is low.
