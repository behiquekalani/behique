## Post 1 — The Framework Most Copywriters Never Teach
**Hook:** Most copy fails because it describes features instead of shifting beliefs.
**Slide 1:** Most copy fails because it describes features instead of shifting beliefs.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Features tell people what your product does. Belief shifts tell people what becomes possible. The gap between those two is where conversions live.
- Slide 3: The framework that works: Problem. Agitation. Solution. But most people rush to the solution before the reader has fully felt the problem.
- Slide 4: Spend 60% of your copy on the problem and agitation. When readers feel deeply understood, they trust the solution.
- Slide 5: Your product is not the hero. The reader's transformation is the hero. Your product is the tool that makes it possible.
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for copy frameworks that actually move people to act.
**Caption:** Most people writing copy are describing their product when they should be describing their reader's life. The difference between copy that converts and copy that gets scrolled past is not cleverness or clever wordplay. It is how accurately you can articulate the problem the reader already has. When someone reads your copy and thinks "this is exactly what I've been dealing with," you have earned their attention. Everything that comes after that moment is just logistics. The frameworks that convert are built on deep problem clarity first, solution credibility second, and a call to action that feels like relief instead of pressure. If your copy is not converting, start by asking whether you have earned the right to present a solution yet.

## Post 2 — AIDA Is Old. Here Is What Replaced It.
**Hook:** AIDA still works. Most people just use it wrong.
**Slide 1:** AIDA still works. Most people just use it wrong.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Attention. Interest. Desire. Action. The classic framework. The problem is that most people frontload action and skip desire entirely.
- Slide 3: Desire is built through specificity. Not "you will make more money" but "you will close your laptop at 3pm on a Tuesday because your work is done."
- Slide 4: The modern version adds a fifth element: Conviction. After desire, the reader needs to believe the outcome is actually possible for them specifically.
- Slide 5: The full sequence: Get attention. Build interest through relevance. Create desire through specificity. Build conviction through proof. Then call to action.
**Slide 6:** Save this post. Use this sequence the next time you write a sales page.
**Caption:** AIDA is 130 years old and it still shows up in every high-converting sales page written today. Not because it is magic, but because it maps to how humans actually make decisions. You need to be noticed first. Then relevant. Then desirable. Then believable. Then easy to act on. Where most copy breaks down is in the belief gap between desire and action. People can want something and still not buy it because they do not believe it will work for them. That is where testimonials, case studies, and specific proof points live. Fill that gap and your conversion rate changes. Ignore it and you will write passionate copy that produces nothing.

## Post 3 — The Before/After/Bridge Framework
**Hook:** Three sentences. That is all you need to write a hook that stops the scroll.
**Slide 1:** Three sentences. That is all you need to write a hook that stops the scroll.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Before: describe the reader's current reality. Be specific enough that they feel seen. "You are posting every day and still getting 12 likes."
- Slide 3: After: describe the world they want to live in. Not vague. Specific and visual. "You post once, it gets shared 400 times, and your DMs fill up."
- Slide 4: Bridge: introduce the mechanism that gets them from before to after. The bridge is what you sell. Make it feel inevitable, not like a gamble.
- Slide 5: This works in captions, email subject lines, ad copy, and landing pages. It is the most portable framework in copywriting.
**Slide 6:** Try writing a before/after/bridge for your offer right now. See what you have.
**Caption:** The Before/After/Bridge framework is the fastest way to write copy that connects because it mirrors exactly how the human brain processes change. We are wired to notice gaps between where we are and where we want to be. When your copy names that gap with precision, the reader stops. When you then present a credible path across it, they lean in. The key word is precision. Vague "before" states feel generic. Vague "after" states feel like hype. The specificity is what makes the reader feel like you wrote this for them personally. Write the before like you have been inside their head. Write the after like you have already been where they are going. Write the bridge like it is the only logical next step.

## Post 4 — The PAS Framework: Why It Outperforms Positive Copy
**Hook:** Positive copy rarely converts as well as copy that leads with pain.
**Slide 1:** Positive copy rarely converts as well as copy that leads with pain.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Problem. Agitation. Solution. PAS is the framework behind most high-converting direct response copy ever written.
- Slide 3: Problem: name the specific, concrete problem your reader has right now. Not a category of problems. The exact thing keeping them up at night.
- Slide 4: Agitation: expand the problem. Show them what happens if it stays unsolved. What it costs them in time, money, relationships, opportunity.
- Slide 5: Solution: present your offer as the clear path out. At this point, the reader is ready. You have earned the right to ask for something.
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. More copy frameworks that translate directly to sales.
**Caption:** People buy to avoid pain more reliably than they buy to gain pleasure. That is not cynicism. That is behavioral economics. The PAS framework works because it respects that reality instead of fighting it. Leading with a problem is not manipulative when the problem is real and the solution actually addresses it. What makes PAS feel authentic versus predatory is the accuracy of the problem you name. If you can articulate the reader's situation better than they can articulate it themselves, you build instant credibility. The agitation phase is where most people get uncomfortable and pull back. Do not pull back. Let the reader sit with the weight of the unsolved problem long enough that the solution feels genuinely valuable.

## Post 5 — The Four U's: The Fastest Headline Audit You Can Run
**Hook:** Your headline is doing one of two things: pulling people in or losing them forever.
**Slide 1:** Your headline is doing one of two things: pulling people in or losing them forever.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: The Four U's framework: Useful. Urgent. Unique. Ultra-specific. Run every headline through this filter before you publish.
- Slide 3: Useful asks: does this promise a clear benefit? Urgent asks: does this give someone a reason to care right now instead of later?
- Slide 4: Unique asks: could this line exist on a competitor's page or is it yours specifically? Ultra-specific asks: is there a number, name, or detail that makes this concrete?
- Slide 5: Most headlines score 2 out of 4 at best. Headlines that score 4 out of 4 reliably outperform everything else in testing.
**Slide 6:** Save this. Audit your next three headlines before you post them.
**Caption:** The headline is the most important piece of copy you will write and the one most people spend the least time on. It does not matter how strong your body copy is if the headline does not earn the read. The Four U's framework is a quick diagnostic, not a formula. Not every headline needs all four. But running your headline through the filter tells you exactly what it is missing. A headline that is useful but not specific is forgettable. One that is urgent but not unique sounds like everyone else. The goal is to push the score as high as you can without making the headline sound like a checklist. When all four elements are present naturally, the headline does not feel optimized. It just feels true.
