**Thread 1**
**Topic:** The formula most people use to price knowledge is broken

Tweet 1:
Most people price their knowledge products by looking at competitors and going slightly lower. That's a strategy for staying poor. Price based on the outcome, not the product.

Tweet 2:
A $27 ebook that saves someone $5,000 in mistakes is not worth $27. A $200 course that generates someone $10,000 in new income is not worth $200. The price is anchored to the wrong number.

Tweet 3:
Value-based pricing works like this: what is the outcome worth to the buyer? A fraction of that number is your price. If your knowledge saves someone twenty hours a week, what are twenty hours worth to them?

Tweet 4:
The objection is "but people won't pay that." Some won't. Those aren't your buyers. The people who actually need the outcome and understand the value will pay a fair price for a clear result.

Tweet 5:
Rewrite your offer around the outcome, not the content. Not "50 pages about productivity" but "a system that saves you ten hours a week." Then price the outcome.

**Thread 2**
**Topic:** Why free content destroys your ability to charge

Tweet 1:
Giving everything away for free trains your audience to expect free. Then when you launch a paid product, they're confused. The problem was not the launch. It was everything before the launch.

Tweet 2:
Free content should demonstrate competence, not replace the product. The free version shows you know what you're talking about. The paid version shows them how to do it themselves.

Tweet 3:
The line between free and paid is clarity. Free content is broad. Paid content is specific. Free is the map. Paid is the turn-by-turn directions for their exact situation.

Tweet 4:
This does not mean withhold value. It means structure what you share. Free posts build trust. Paid products build results. Different job, different price.

Tweet 5:
Audit your free content today. Is it giving away the exact thing you're charging for? Or is it building trust and pointing to a specific outcome they can only get by buying? Adjust accordingly.

**Thread 3**
**Topic:** The three price points every knowledge business needs

Tweet 1:
A knowledge business with one price point is leaving most of its potential revenue untouched. Buyers make decisions at different thresholds. You need at least three.

Tweet 2:
Low ticket ($9-$49): low friction entry. Turns followers into customers. Proves they can trust you. Does not require a sales call. One clear problem, one clear solution.

Tweet 3:
Mid ticket ($97-$497): a complete system. More depth, more implementation. This is usually a course, a toolkit, a template library, or a workshop. Solves a real problem end to end.

Tweet 4:
High ticket ($500+): access and accountability. Coaching, consulting, done-with-you. The outcome is transformation, not just information. This is where most of the profit lives.

Tweet 5:
The buying journey: someone finds you through free content, buys something small to test trust, gets a result, comes back for the mid-ticket, and eventually buys the high-ticket. Design each step deliberately.

**Thread 4**
**Topic:** How to raise your prices without losing buyers

Tweet 1:
Raising prices is simple. Most people make it complicated by announcing it, apologizing for it, or justifying it in a way that signals insecurity. Just raise the price and improve the offer.

Tweet 2:
The cleaner approach: improve the offer first. Add a component that increases the perceived value. Then raise the price. You're not charging more for the same thing. You're offering more for a fair price.

Tweet 3:
Anchor the increase. "Previously $97, now $147 because I added live Q and A sessions." The anchor tells the buyer the old price, which makes the new price feel earned rather than arbitrary.

Tweet 4:
Use deadline pricing occasionally. A price increase announced in advance creates urgency without manipulation if the deadline is real. It also rewards buyers who act early.

Tweet 5:
The deeper truth: buyers who balk at a $50 price increase are not your best buyers. Your best buyers pay for results, not discounts. Raising prices filters for the buyers you actually want.

**Thread 5**
**Topic:** The psychological trap that keeps knowledge creators undercharging

Tweet 1:
The reason most knowledge creators undercharge is not strategy. It's a belief that their knowledge is common. They think: anyone could learn this. What they miss is that most people haven't, and they don't want to.

Tweet 2:
You are not being paid for information. You are being paid for the shortcut. The buyer could read every book you've read, test everything you've tested, make every mistake you've made. Or they could pay you to skip all of that.

Tweet 3:
The shortcut has enormous value. Years of someone's time, compacted into a product that takes hours to consume. That is the trade. The question is whether you're pricing the shortcut or pricing the content.

Tweet 4:
The test: would your buyer pay $500 to skip two years of trial and error? If yes, and you're charging $29, you are underpricing the shortcut by roughly one hundred times.

Tweet 5:
This week: write out exactly what mistakes your buyer will avoid by using your product. What years? What failures? What costly experiments? That list is your real pricing anchor.
