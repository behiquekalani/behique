## Post 1: The Post Type That Outperforms Everything

After three years of posting on LinkedIn and studying what performs, I keep coming back to the same format.

The personal story with a professional lesson.

Not the polished narrative. Not the before-and-after with a clean resolution. The specific, honest account of something that happened, followed by what it actually meant.

The algorithm rewards comments over any other engagement signal. Personal stories generate more comments than frameworks or listicles because they create emotional response. People want to share their version of the same experience.

The format: one specific incident. What you assumed. What actually happened. What you took from it.

Keep it under 300 words. Keep the language direct. Resist the urge to make it perfectly tidy. The rough edges are what make it feel real.

The posts I have written that felt slightly too honest, the ones where I was not sure I should publish them, have consistently outperformed the ones I was confident about.

The confidence means you have optimized for approval. The discomfort means you have said something true.

LinkedIn rewards truth more than polish.

## Post 2: Why Data Beats Opinion on LinkedIn

I ran an experiment for ninety days.

I alternated between two types of posts: opinion-based takes on business and marketing, and data-backed observations with a specific source or personal measurement.

The opinion posts got more immediate engagement. Reactions, quick agreement, surface-level interaction.

The data posts got more comments, more shares, and more profile visits. By the end of ninety days, the data posts had driven three times more inbound connection requests.

The reason is credibility signaling.

On LinkedIn, the audience is professional. They are trained to evaluate claims based on evidence. When you make a claim and back it with a number, a source, or a documented personal result, you are operating in the frame they already trust.

Opinion is easy to produce. Data is work. The audience knows this. They reward the work.

The practical implication: before you post your next take, ask whether there is a number attached to it. Your own analytics. A study. A specific tracked result from something you tested.

Even approximate numbers, "roughly 40% of the emails I sent," perform better than clean opinions without a reference point.

Your credibility on LinkedIn is built post by post. Each data point you include is a deposit in that account.

## Post 3: The First Line Is Everything

I have tested hundreds of LinkedIn post openings.

The single variable that most determines whether someone clicks "see more" is the first line.

Not the topic. Not the length. Not the structure. The first line.

The patterns that work:

A number in the first line. "I lost $2,000 on my first product launch." The number makes it specific and real. It creates a question: what happened and why?

A direct challenge to a common belief. "Consistency is not what grows a LinkedIn following." The person who has heard the opposite wants to know where this is going.

A scene that does not immediately explain itself. "In 2021 I deleted my whole content backlog." The incomplete context creates tension. The reader wants resolution.

What does not work: starting with context. "As someone who has been in marketing for years..." That is a resume, not a hook. The reader is already gone.

You have approximately one second of attention before the scroll resumes. The first line either earns the next line or it does not.

Write ten first lines before you pick one. The tenth is almost always better than the first.

## Post 4: The Format That Gets Saved

The most valuable engagement metric on LinkedIn is not likes or comments.

It is saves.

When someone saves your post, they are signaling intent. They want to come back to it. They found it useful enough to preserve. They may share it with someone else later.

The format that gets saved most consistently: the structured framework.

"Three things I learned about pricing digital products after six product launches."

"The five questions I ask before writing any piece of content."

"Four tools I use every week that almost no one in my network knows about."

The numbered structure tells the reader exactly what they are getting and how long it will take. It reduces friction. The promise of a finite, useful list is a low-risk commitment.

The content inside the framework must be specific. Not platitudes. Not things the reader already knows. The insight that earns a save is the one that makes the reader think: I would not have thought of it that way on my own.

Obvious insights save nobody's time.

The specific, earned, non-obvious insight is the one worth writing. That is the one that gets saved and shared.

## Post 5: What Three Years on LinkedIn Taught Me About What Works

I have posted consistently on LinkedIn for three years.

Here is what I know now that I did not know when I started.

The posts that performed best were never the ones I predicted would. They were the ones that felt slightly too specific, too personal, or too niche to appeal to a broad audience.

The posts that flopped were almost always the ones where I tried to appeal to everyone.

LinkedIn rewards specificity in a way that most platforms do not. The audience is professional. They are evaluating your content through the lens of whether it is relevant to their specific situation. Generic wisdom does not clear that bar.

The other thing I learned: relationships matter more than reach.

The people who hired me, bought my products, or referred me to someone who did, were almost never people who found me through a viral post. They were people who had been reading my content for six to twelve months and built trust over time.

LinkedIn is not a broadcast platform. It is a relationship platform that happens to have a feed.

The creators and professionals who treat it like a broadcast tool get broadcast results: reach, impressions, surface engagement. The ones who treat it like a relationship tool get business results: clients, collaborations, revenue.

I know which one I am optimizing for.
