## TikTok 1: The Economics of Staying Broke as a Creator
**Hook (0-2s):**
99% of creators are running a charity. Not a business.

**Script:**
Here's the math that nobody wants to talk about.

If you spend 20 hours a week creating content, and you make zero dollars, you are not a creator. You are a volunteer.

Volunteering is fine if that's the choice. But most creators are not choosing to volunteer. They're just confused about what business they're in.

The creator economy is not a charity auction where views eventually convert to cash. It's a marketplace. And in a marketplace, you need something to sell.

Every hour you spend creating without a monetization plan is an hour you're subsidizing your audience's entertainment with your own time and energy.

The fix is not to stop creating. The fix is to attach a business to the creation.

Ask yourself: what could someone pay me for today? Not in six months when you have 10k followers. Today. With the audience you already have, even if it's 50 people.

The answer to that question is your business model. Build that first. Then create content to support it.

## TikTok 2: The 2026 Skill Hierarchy
**Hook (0-2s):**
In 2026, this skill hierarchy will determine who wins online.

**Script:**
At the bottom of the hierarchy: tactics. Posting schedules, hashtag strategies, platform hacks. These have a shelf life of months.

One level up: tools. Knowing how to use AI, automation, no-code. These last a few years. They're worth learning but they're not the foundation.

One level up: frameworks. The ability to structure thinking, spot patterns, connect ideas across domains. This lasts a decade.

At the top: writing. Not because writing is the oldest skill. Because writing is how ideas become money.

A written idea can become a post, a video script, a product, a sales page, an email, a book, a course. Every format you see online starts as writing.

If you can write with clarity, you can build across every platform and every format. If you can't, you're dependent on someone who can.

The people winning online right now are not the ones with the best cameras or the best tools. They're the ones with the clearest ideas, most precisely expressed.

Invest there first.

## TikTok 3: From Content Library to Product in 72 Hours
**Hook (0-2s):**
You can turn your content archive into a sellable product this weekend.

**Script:**
Day one. Open your content archive. Pick your most-saved, most-shared, most-commented posts. Identify the theme that connects the top 10.

Write that theme as a problem statement. "Most [audience] struggle with [problem] because [reason]." That's your product premise.

Day two. Go through each post and expand it. Add what you couldn't say in the short format. Add the nuance, the example, the case study you left out. Each piece of content becomes a chapter or a section.

Build a framework that connects them. Give it a name. Three-step frameworks work. Five-step frameworks work. Name each step something memorable.

Day three. Write an intro that explains who this is for, what they'll know by the end, and why you're the person to teach it. Add a conclusion with the next step.

Export as PDF. Set up a Gumroad product page. Price it between $17 and $47 for a first product. Publish.

You haven't created something new. You've organized something that already existed.

That's the fastest path to your first product sale.

## TikTok 4: What Branding Actually Means for One Person
**Hook (0-2s):**
Branding for a solopreneur is not what the marketing textbooks say it is.

**Script:**
Traditional branding is for companies. Logo systems, color palettes, brand voice guidelines, style sheets. All useful for a company with a team to implement them.

For one person building something? Branding is simpler and harder at the same time.

Simpler because you are the brand. Your voice, your opinions, your way of framing problems. You don't need a committee to approve your messaging. You just need to know what you think.

Harder because that requires actual conviction. Most people don't have a point of view strong enough to build a brand on. They have opinions that shift with the room they're in.

To build a brand as a solopreneur, you need one thing: a thesis you're willing to defend publicly, consistently, over time.

The thesis has to be specific enough to repel some people. If everyone agrees with you, your thesis is too soft to build on.

The thesis has to be based on real experience or real research. Borrowed opinions don't hold up under questioning.

Say the same true thing in many different ways. That's your brand.

## TikTok 5: The Anti-Viral Growth Strategy
**Hook (0-2s):**
What if going viral is actually a trap?

**Script:**
Bear with me on this.

Going viral gives you a burst of attention from people who don't know you, most of whom will never buy from you, many of whom will unfollow once they realize your regular content is different from the one thing that went viral.

Viral content is often the content furthest from your core message. It caught a trend, it hit the right emotion, it got shared by the algorithm. That doesn't mean it represents your brand.

The people who chased virality and built businesses on it now have massive audiences they can't monetize. Because the audience came for the wrong thing.

The alternative is what I'd call earned growth. You grow slowly, from the right people. Every person in your audience arrived because your content specifically spoke to their specific situation.

That audience is smaller. It's also more valuable. It buys more. It refers more. It trusts more.

Slow, intentional growth is not the boring option. It's the profitable option.

Chase depth. Virality will happen or it won't. Either way, you'll have a real business.
