## Script 1: What Is an Income Floor and Why You Need One

**Hook (0-3s):** Most people chase income ceilings. Nobody talks about income floors.

**Body:**
An income ceiling is the maximum you can earn. An income floor is the minimum you will earn no matter what.

Building an income ceiling is exciting but unstable. One bad month wipes everything out.

Building an income floor is less exciting but permanent.

The floor strategy: build predictable, recurring, low-maintenance income streams that cover your baseline expenses. Not luxuries. Baseline. Rent, food, bills.

Once your floor covers baseline, every additional dollar is risk capital. You can invest, experiment, and take swings without fear.

The fear of failure that stops most people from building is the absence of a floor.

Build the floor first. Build the ceiling after.

$500 a month in recurring digital product income is a floor. It's not financial freedom. But it's the beginning of a very different relationship with risk.

## Script 2: The 3 Income Streams That Build a Floor

**Hook (0-3s):** You need three specific types of income to build a floor. Here's what they are.

**Body:**
Stream one: a low-ticket digital product. Something priced between fifteen and forty dollars that solves a specific problem. It sells passively, delivers automatically, and requires no ongoing labor per sale.

Stream two: a recurring component. A newsletter, a membership, a small subscription. Even fifty people paying ten dollars a month is five hundred dollars that shows up on the first of every month.

Stream three: a service or consulting offer at a higher price point. Not for scale, but for floor stability. Two clients paying five hundred dollars a month is a thousand dollars that is predictable.

Three streams. None of them is enough on its own. Combined, they build a floor that holds even when one stream has a bad month.

A bad month in digital product sales doesn't break you if your subscription and service income still runs.

Diversification at the floor level, not the ceiling level.

## Script 4: How Long Does It Actually Take to Build a Floor

**Hook (0-3s):** Honest timeline: here's how long a real income floor takes to build.

**Body:**
Month one to three: you are building infrastructure. Content, product, email list. You are making very little money. This is normal.

Month three to six: you have proof. Some sales, some subscribers, some signal. The floor is visible but not yet stable.

Month six to twelve: if you have not quit, you are building real momentum. Revenue is climbing month over month. You can start to see the floor taking shape.

Month twelve to eighteen: a real floor is possible. Not guaranteed, but possible with consistent execution.

This is not a get-rich timeline. This is a build-a-real-business timeline.

The people who quit at month three see none of this. The people who stay see the compounding.

The only way to shortcut the timeline is to get more specific faster, and to distribute your content to audiences that already exist. Guest posts, collaborations, borrowed audiences.

But even with shortcuts, this is a twelve-month project. Plan accordingly.

## Script 5: The Mistake That Keeps the Floor From Forming

**Hook (0-3s):** This one mistake is why most creators never build a stable income floor.

**Body:**
They optimize for income ceiling when they should be optimizing for income floor.

They spend months building a high-ticket offer before they have a trusted audience.

They launch a $500 course before they have a proven $20 product.

They try to close big deals before they have testimonials.

The floor is built from trust, not from premium pricing.

The right sequence: a free, valuable lead magnet that builds your list. A low-ticket product that proves your audience will pay you. A mid-ticket product for people who want more. A recurring subscription for the ones who want to stay.

That sequence is slow. But it builds a floor that holds.

Skipping steps to chase big income fast is why most creator businesses are unstable at month twelve.

Build in sequence. Patience with the process is a competitive advantage.
