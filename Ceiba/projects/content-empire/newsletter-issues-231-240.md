## Issue 231: The Content Format That Converts Best

**Subject line:** The format nobody talks about converts best

**Preview text:** It's not video. It's not short-form.

---

I spent six months testing every content format across every platform I was active on.

Video, threads, carousels, long-form posts, short-form posts, newsletters. I tracked which formats drove clicks, which drove replies, which drove actual sales. The results contradicted almost everything I had assumed going in.

**The content format that converts best is not the most viral one. It is the most specific one.**

Not specific as in niche. Specific as in: it names a person's exact problem with enough precision that they feel seen. The format that does this most reliably, across every platform and audience size I have seen, is the before-and-after narrative.

Here is why it works. The before-and-after structure does three things simultaneously. It names the painful state the reader is currently in, which creates immediate recognition. It shows a believable transformation that happened for someone like them, which creates hope. And it attributes the change to a specific decision or tool, which creates a call to action.

Video can do this. So can text. The format is almost irrelevant. What matters is whether the structure is present.

The mistake most creators make is producing content that informs without transforming. They share facts, frameworks, and tips. The reader learns something but does not move. The before-and-after narrative makes the reader feel the gap between where they are and where they could be, and that gap is what drives action.

When I rewrote my top-performing posts into this structure, conversion to email signups doubled within two weeks. The content was not more valuable. It was more specific in the right way.

The test is simple. Read your last five pieces of content. Does each one name a specific painful state and show a specific path out of it. If the answer is no, you have a structure problem, not a quality problem.

**Tool this week:** Beehiiv. If your content is converting readers but not capturing them, you need a place to send them. Beehiiv is the newsletter platform built for this exact problem.

The format is not the lever. The precision of the pain point is.

## Issue 232: Building a Digital Product Business on 2 Hours a Day

**Subject line:** Two hours a day built a real business

**Preview text:** Not productivity hacks. A different model.

---

Most people hear "two hours a day" and assume it means working fast, staying focused, drinking the right coffee.

That is not the point. The point is that the architecture of a digital product business does not require more than two hours a day once the core pieces are in place. The issue is that most people spend those two hours on tasks that do not compound.

**The distinction that changed everything for me: there is work that builds capacity and work that depletes it.**

Writing a newsletter issue builds capacity. The audience grows, the trust deepens, the archive becomes a sales tool. Answering individual DMs depletes it. You help one person and the hour is gone.

The two-hour model requires ruthless prioritization of the tasks that compound. In the first stage, that means writing and publishing consistently, even when the audience is small. In the second stage, it means building one system at a time, usually starting with the email funnel, until distribution runs without daily management.

What two hours actually looks like in practice: one hour of writing, anchored to a weekly schedule. Thirty minutes on product or distribution. Thirty minutes on anything else, including responding to the audience, reviewing metrics, planning. That hour of writing is non-negotiable. Everything else shifts.

The reason this model works is that digital products have near-zero marginal cost. Once a product exists, selling more copies costs nothing additional. The leverage is in the writing and the funnel, not in the delivery. Two hours building those things compounds in a way that eight hours of unstructured hustle does not.

The constraint also forces good decisions. When you only have two hours, you cannot afford to start four projects. You finish one. Finishing one thing is more valuable than starting ten.

**Tool this week:** Notion. Use it to keep a single running list of your two highest-leverage tasks each week. Not a full task manager. Just two tasks. That constraint alone will change what you prioritize.

Two hours is not a limitation. It is a forcing function that builds a better business than six hours of scattered work ever would.

## Issue 233: The Platform Hierarchy for Solopreneurs in 2026

**Subject line:** Not every platform deserves your time

**Preview text:** Here is where to be and where to stop.

---

I get asked which platforms to be on more than almost any other question.

My answer is always the same: fewer than you think, and with a clearer purpose than you currently have for any of them.

The platform landscape in 2026 has not changed in the way most people fear. The same fundamental dynamics apply. Platforms that own your audience are rented land. Platforms that send audience to something you own are the actual goal.

**The hierarchy I use is this: email first, then everything else.**

Email is the only channel where you own the relationship completely. No algorithm decides who sees your message. No platform change removes your access. An email list of five hundred engaged people is worth more than fifty thousand followers on any platform that can limit your reach tomorrow.

The second tier is the discovery platform where your specific audience actually spends time. For most solopreneurs targeting professionals or digital-native buyers, that is currently LinkedIn, Instagram, or short-form video depending on the format of your content. The purpose of this tier is not to build an audience here. The purpose is to move people from here to your email list.

The third tier is long-form content that creates permanent discovery, either YouTube or a blog. This tier requires more investment but produces content that works for years rather than days. A YouTube video published today will still drive traffic in three years. An Instagram post published today has a shelf life of roughly forty-eight hours.

The mistake is treating all platforms as equal and spreading effort across six of them. That produces six mediocre presences instead of one strong one. Choose one discovery platform, drive everything to email, and add the long-form tier when you have the capacity to do it consistently.

**Tool this week:** Beehiiv. If you are not actively building an email list yet, start today. Your discovery platform can change. Your email list is portable.

The platform you are not on is not the reason for slow growth. The absence of a system that converts attention into owned audience is.

## Issue 234: What Separates $100/Month Sellers From $10K/Month Sellers

**Subject line:** The gap between $100/mo and $10K/mo is not effort

**Preview text:** It is one structural difference.

---

I have talked to a lot of people at different stages of building a digital product business.

The ones generating a hundred dollars a month are not less smart or less hard-working than the ones generating ten thousand. In most cases the quality of their products is comparable. The difference is structural, not motivational.

**The structural difference is this: the $10K seller has a funnel. The $100 seller has a product.**

A product sitting on a page is not a business. It is an inventory item. Someone has to find it, trust the creator enough to buy it, and then buy it again or tell others. Without a system that creates those conditions repeatedly and automatically, revenue stays at the level of whatever traffic happens to stumble onto the page.

The funnel has four components. Discovery, which is consistent content that reaches new people in the relevant problem space. Conversion, which is an email list that captures interested people before they leave. Trust, which is the newsletter or email sequence that deepens the relationship over days and weeks. And sale, which is the moment when someone who already trusts you sees an offer that matches a problem they have.

At the $100/month level, most sellers have discovery but none of the other three. They post content, people occasionally click through, a few buy. No list, no sequence, no deliberate offer. Each sale is isolated.

At the $10K/month level, every sale feeds the next one. Email subscribers receive content that makes them more likely to buy. Buyers receive follow-up that makes them more likely to buy again or refer others. The funnel runs with or without new content being produced that week.

The path from one to the other is not about making a better product. It is about building the system around the product that creates the conditions for trust at scale.

**Tool this week:** ConvertKit. The simplest tool for building the email sequence component of this system. Free tier works fine for the first thousand subscribers.

The product is not the business. The system that sells the product is the business.

## Issue 235: The Complete Guide to Not Burning Out as a Creator

**Subject line:** Burnout is not about working too hard

**Preview text:** It is about building the wrong way.

---

I burned out once, early on.

Not from working too many hours, though there were too many of those. I burned out because I was building in a way that required my full presence every single day with no compounding return. Every week started at zero. Every week I had to produce or the whole thing stalled.

That model is not sustainable at any work volume.

**Burnout for creators is almost always a systems failure, not a willpower failure.**

When your business requires you to be actively present for it to function, you have not built a business. You have given yourself a job that is more demanding than most employment because there is no ceiling on the hours and no separation between work and identity.

The shift that prevented a second burnout was building systems that produced output without my daily involvement. Scheduled content, automated email sequences, digital products that sell without my attention. Once those were in place, the work became additive rather than continuous.

The second factor is identity separation. Creators who burn out tend to have collapsed their sense of self into their metrics. When the numbers are good, they feel good. When engagement drops, they spiral. This is not a mindset problem to be solved with journaling. It is a structural problem to be solved by building multiple sources of feedback that are not algorithmic, including relationships, skills developed for their own sake, and work that has value regardless of whether anyone sees it.

The third factor is scope. Most burnout I have observed happens in people who are running more fronts than they can sustain. Three platforms, two product ideas, a service business, and a newsletter simultaneously. Each one is underbuilt because the attention is fractured. Finishing one thing produces energy. Managing ten incomplete things depletes it.

The answer to burnout is not rest, though rest helps. The answer is redesigning how you build so that the output compounds rather than resets.

**Tool this week:** A simple weekly review template. Every Sunday, write three sentences: what got done, what got skipped, what changes next week. The practice builds self-awareness faster than any productivity system.

Burnout is the tax on building without systems. Pay it once, then build differently.

## Issue 236: How to Turn One Idea Into 30 Pieces of Content

**Subject line:** One idea. Thirty pieces of content. Here is the system.

**Preview text:** Most creators generate too much. Distribute too little.

---

Last week I published thirty pieces of content from a single idea.

Not thirty variations of the same sentence. Thirty genuinely distinct pieces, each one formatted for a different platform and audience context, each one complete on its own terms.

The idea was simple: why digital products fail in the first ninety days. That is one idea. Here is what it became.

**The repurposing system has three stages.**

Stage one is the anchor piece. For me this is usually a newsletter issue or a long-form LinkedIn post. The anchor piece contains the full argument, the nuance, the evidence, and the conclusion. It takes the most effort and produces the most value per piece. Everything else derives from this.

Stage two is extraction. From the anchor piece, I pull the following: five to seven standalone claims that can become short-form posts. Two or three contrarian takes that can become threads. One hook statement that can open a carousel. One question that can become a poll. The data point or specific example that can become a visual.

Stage three is format adaptation. Each extracted element gets reshaped for the specific constraints of the platform it is headed to. The thread version is conversational and has white space. The carousel version is visual and scannable. The short-form video is the hook plus the most counterintuitive claim plus a one-sentence payoff.

The discipline this requires is counterintuitive. Instead of moving immediately to the next idea after publishing the anchor piece, you slow down and extract. This feels inefficient when you are doing it. It feels like multiplication when you look at your publishing calendar a week later.

The time investment for the full extraction from one anchor piece is roughly two hours beyond writing the anchor itself. The output is four to six weeks of content across multiple platforms from a single thinking session.

**Tool this week:** Castmagic or Otter.ai. Record yourself explaining your idea out loud first. The verbal version often contains the most natural extractions because you include the context automatically.

You do not need more ideas. You need a better extraction system for the ones you already have.

## Issue 237: The Email Subject Line Formula That Gets 40%+ Open Rates

**Subject line:** The subject line formula that actually works

**Preview text:** It is not clever. It is specific.

---

I tested more subject lines than I can count before I understood what was actually driving opens.

The conventional wisdom is that you want curiosity, urgency, or personalization. All of those can work, but none of them explain the results consistently. The real driver is something simpler.

**The subject lines with the highest open rates are the ones that make the reader feel like the email is specifically about them.**

Not addressed to them by name. Not personalized with their location or their last purchase. Specifically about the problem they are actively thinking about at this moment in their life as a subscriber of your newsletter.

The formula that produces this consistently is: specific pain point or specific belief, followed by a specific reframe that challenges it. Not a question necessarily. Often just the statement of the reframe is enough.

Examples of what this looks like in practice. Not "How to grow your audience" but "Why growing your audience is the wrong goal." Not "Pricing your product" but "Why your product is priced too low even if it is not selling." Not "Email marketing tips" but "The email sequence most creators skip that drives 40% of revenue."

The pattern is: name the thing the reader cares about, then immediately destabilize their current assumption about it. That pattern creates a tension in the mind that requires resolution. The reader opens the email to resolve the tension.

What does not work is vague intrigue. Subject lines that hint at a secret without naming the topic. Those produce opens from curious people who are not your specific audience. The open rate looks good but the click rate and the conversion suffer because the people who opened were not the people you wrote for.

What also does not work is pure announcement. "Newsletter issue 237" tells the reader nothing about why they should stop scrolling and open this right now.

**Tool this week:** Your own inbox. Look at the emails you actually open and ask what made you open them. Your behavior as a reader is the most accurate research you have access to.

The subject line is not a hook. It is a commitment device. It tells the right reader that this one is for them.

## Issue 238: Building Systems Before You Need Them

**Subject line:** Build the system before the problem arrives

**Preview text:** Reactive infrastructure is the most expensive kind.

---

Six months ago I did not have a system for onboarding new email subscribers.

It did not matter because I had fifty subscribers. When I hit five hundred, I had the same non-system I had at fifty. At a thousand, I was still sending manual welcome emails when I remembered to. By the time I built a proper onboarding sequence, I had already lost the critical first-week relationship with hundreds of people who would never get it back.

**Building systems reactively is the most expensive way to run a digital business because the cost is invisible.**

When you do not have a system, you do not see the damage in real time. You see it later, in churn rates and low engagement and conversion numbers that never explain themselves fully. By then the window for that subscriber or that customer or that opportunity has passed.

The counter-strategy is to build systems one stage ahead of where you are. Not two stages. Not ten stages. One.

If you have zero subscribers, build a welcome sequence for the first one. It takes ninety minutes and it will be ready when the first hundred people arrive. If you have one product, build the upsell sequence before you launch the second product. When the second product exists, the infrastructure that connects them is already there.

The mental shift required is treating infrastructure time as protected time, not as discretionary time. Most creators have no scheduled time for building systems. They build them in crises, when a process breaks or when growth demands it. That is the most stressful and least effective time to build anything.

One hour per week dedicated exclusively to building or improving one system is enough. Not execution, not content, not client work. System building. The compounding effect of that hour over six months produces a business that can scale without breaking.

**Tool this week:** n8n or Make. Pick one and build one automation this week. Not the most important one. Just one. The habit of building systems matters more than the specific system you start with.

The system you did not build last year is costing you something right now. Build the next one before you need it.

## Issue 239: What I'd Do in the First 30 Days Starting From Zero

**Subject line:** First 30 days from zero. Here is the exact plan.

**Preview text:** No audience. No product. No money. Start here.

---

I get some version of this question every week: if you were starting over with nothing, what would you do first.

Not a theoretical question anymore. I have thought through this seriously because the answer reveals the actual order of operations that matters, which is different from the order most advice recommends.

**The first thirty days are not about building. They are about positioning.**

Most people start by creating content or building a product. Both of those are downstream of a decision you have not made yet, which is: what problem do you solve for which specific person.

Days one through ten should produce one output: a clear sentence that names the specific person, the specific problem they have, and the specific transformation you help them make. Not a mission statement. A positioning sentence. "I help [specific person] go from [specific painful state] to [specific desired state]."

This takes longer than expected because most people try to be too broad. The instinct is to avoid narrowing the audience because narrowing feels like reducing opportunity. The opposite is true. A broad positioning statement attracts no one because no one feels like it was written for them.

Days eleven through twenty: publish ten pieces of content based on the positioning sentence. Not polished content. Honest, specific content about the problem you named. The goal is not reach. The goal is to clarify your own thinking and begin building a reference point that shows you understand the problem deeply.

Days twenty-one through thirty: build one thing that captures the audience that finds the content. Either a free resource that earns an email address or a simple low-price product that tests whether people pay for your solution. Not both. One.

Thirty days of that, done consistently, puts you ahead of most people who have been "starting" for a year.

**Tool this week:** Gumroad. Free tier, no monthly fee. Put your first product or lead magnet up in thirty minutes. The simplicity is a feature, not a limitation.

Starting over is not the hardest scenario. Starting over with clarity about the order of operations is actually the advantage.

## Issue 240: What Writing 240 Newsletters Taught Me

**Subject line:** 240 issues. Here is what I actually learned.

**Preview text:** About communication, consistency, and what readers want.

---

Issue 240.

I did not know I would write this many when I started. I am not sure I believed, early on, that I would still be writing when the number got this high.

Here is what I actually learned, and I am trying to make this list honest rather than motivational.

**On communication:**

The clearest thinking you have on a topic is not good enough for a newsletter. The newsletter requires that you take your clearest thinking and then remove half of it. Not because simplicity is a virtue for its own sake, but because the reader is reading in a context of competing demands on their attention. The ideas that land are the ones that fit in a single sitting with a single clear takeaway.

Writing two hundred and forty issues taught me that I was consistently overexplaining. Not because the ideas were too complex, but because I did not yet trust the reader to fill in the gaps. Learning to leave space for the reader is a skill that takes years to develop.

**On consistency:**

Consistency is not the same as frequency. Publishing every week on schedule matters less than having a clear point of view that the reader can predict and trust. Some of my most consistent periods in terms of reader engagement were also weeks where I published less frequently. Consistency is a relationship property, not a calendar property.

**On what readers actually want:**

They do not want information. They have more information than they can use. They want a specific person, with a specific point of view, to make sense of a problem they are sitting with. The newsletter issues that generate the most replies are never the most comprehensive. They are the most specific, the ones where I named something the reader was feeling but had not articulated yet.

The last thing: keep writing anyway, especially when it feels like no one is reading. The issues I wrote when the list was small built the voice that the larger audience found later. You are not writing for the current audience. You are writing to become the version of yourself the right audience will eventually find.

Thank you for being here. On to 241.

**Tool this week:** Go back and read the first newsletter issue you ever sent. The distance between that voice and your current one is the evidence of everything these issues have built.

The work compounds. Keep writing.
