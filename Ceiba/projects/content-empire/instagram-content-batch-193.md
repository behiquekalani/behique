## Post 1 — The Tell That Gives AI Writing Away Every Time

**Hook:** There is one pattern in AI writing that readers recognize before they consciously notice it.

**Slide 1:** There is one pattern in AI writing that readers recognize before they consciously notice it.

**Slide 2:** The pattern is structural perfection. Every paragraph has three points. Every post has an introduction, a body, and a conclusion. Every argument is balanced. Everything is resolved.

**Slide 3:** Human writing has texture. It skips ahead. It sits on one point too long. It starts a sentence and changes its mind. It ends on tension sometimes. It is structurally imperfect in ways that signal a real person was there.

**Slide 4:** The easiest way to break AI structure: cut the introduction entirely. Start mid-thought. Start with the second sentence you would have written. That's where the real thing begins.

**Slide 5:** Also: end without resolving everything. "I don't have a clean answer for this yet. But here's what I know so far." That kind of ending is not weak. It's honest. And honest is the one thing AI cannot fake.

**Slide 6:** Read your AI draft out loud. Anywhere you would stop to breathe or pause in real speech, add a period. Anywhere it sounds like a text, delete the connector. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:**
The best piece of feedback I ever got on my writing was: "This sounds like someone who read about the topic, not someone who lived it."

That's what AI writing sounds like at its default. Comprehensive. Accurate. Assembled. But not lived.

The fix is not to add more emotion or more exclamation marks. The fix is to add specificity that could only come from your actual experience. The name of the tool you actually use. The specific mistake you made last Tuesday. The number from your own analytics dashboard.

AI can describe the general pattern. Only you can describe your particular version of it. The particular version is what makes it human.

When you revise an AI draft, your job is to find every generality and replace it with your specific. "Many creators struggle with consistency" becomes "I posted twice in January and felt like I'd failed the entire year." The second sentence is a real person. The first is a content calendar.

Write from your specific. Edit out the general. That's the whole method.

## Post 2 — 7 Words That Make AI Copy Sound Human

**Hook:** The difference between AI writing and human writing is often seven specific word choices.

**Slide 1:** The difference between AI writing and human writing is often seven specific word choices.

**Slide 2:** Replace "utilize" with "use." Replace "leverage" with "use" again, or just cut it. Replace "facilitate" with "help." Replace "implement" with "do." These words are the fingerprint of committee-speak.

**Slide 3:** Replace "in order to" with "to." Replace "due to the fact that" with "because." Replace "at this point in time" with "now." Every one of these is a filler that AI loves and humans do not actually say.

**Slide 4:** The seventh shift: replace the passive voice with active. "Mistakes were made" is AI. "I got it wrong" is human. The passive hides the actor. The active shows that someone was there.

**Slide 5:** Run this test: read your post and underline every word you would never say out loud to a friend. Every underlined word is a target. Replace it with the spoken version.

**Slide 6:** Good writing sounds like a smart person talking, not like a document being read. Edit toward the spoken version every time. Follow @behikeai for more editing frameworks.

**Caption:**
Language models were trained on formal writing, academic papers, business communication, and the kind of content that gets indexed and cited. That training produces a certain vocabulary. You know it when you see it.

"Utilize" instead of "use." "Leverage" as a verb. "In today's fast-paced world." "It is important to note." "Furthermore." "In conclusion."

These words are not wrong. They are signals. They signal that a system optimized for formal correctness wrote this. And your reader, even without knowing why, picks up on that signal and stops feeling addressed.

The cure is simple but requires consistent attention: after every AI draft, do one pass specifically for the vocabulary of formality. Every time you see a word you would not say in conversation, replace it with what you would say.

This is not dumbing down your writing. It is humanizing it. Intelligence does not live in the vocabulary of academia. It lives in the precision of thought. You can be precise with plain words. In fact, precision with plain words is harder and more impressive than precision with jargon.

Edit for the spoken version. Always.

## Post 3 — The Specificity Rule for Human-Sounding Content

**Hook:** Any sentence that could describe a thousand different people will not connect with any of them.

**Slide 1:** Any sentence that could describe a thousand different people will not connect with any of them.

**Slide 2:** "Many entrepreneurs struggle with time management." That sentence is true and completely useless. It connects with no one because no one is "many entrepreneurs." They are one specific person with one specific situation.

**Slide 3:** "If you're running three projects at once and sleeping six hours, time management advice that assumes a clean schedule isn't for you." That's a specific person. They read that and think: you're talking about me.

**Slide 4:** AI generates the general case by default. Your job as the editor is to take every general claim and ask: what version of this is true for the specific person I'm writing for?

**Slide 5:** The specificity ladder: vague claim, specific claim, specific claim with an example, specific claim with a number, specific claim with a name. Each rung feels more human than the last. Climb as high as you honestly can.

**Slide 6:** Go back to your last AI draft. Find one general sentence. Make it specific. Then do the next one. That edit loop is how AI content becomes real content. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:**
I used to think good writing required good vocabulary. Now I think it requires good specificity.

Vocabulary is cheap. Specificity costs you something. It requires you to know your reader well enough to name their exact situation. It requires you to use real examples instead of hypotheticals. It requires you to put a number on things instead of waving at them vaguely.

AI is very good at the general case. It has read more general cases than any human ever will. But it has not lived your reader's specific situation. You have, or you've talked to people who have.

That lived specificity is the commodity you bring to the collaboration. The AI handles the structure and the filler. You handle the details that make someone stop scrolling.

When I say "a 40% open rate," that is more convincing than "a high open rate." When I say "Tuesday at 11am when I should have been sleeping," that is more vivid than "late at night." The number, the day, the specific time: those details are the fingerprints of reality.

Specificity is how you prove you were there.

## Post 4 — The One Edit That Transforms Any AI Draft

**Hook:** One edit, done consistently, is responsible for most of the difference between forgettable AI content and content that gets shared.

**Slide 1:** One edit, done consistently, is responsible for most of the difference between forgettable AI content and content that gets shared.

**Slide 2:** The edit: after every AI draft, cut the first paragraph completely. The first paragraph is almost always the warmup. The real thing starts in paragraph two.

**Slide 3:** AI is trained to ease readers in. It introduces the topic, provides context, acknowledges multiple perspectives. That's a formality ritual. Your reader doesn't need the warmup. They need the thing.

**Slide 4:** "In today's competitive landscape, many creators find themselves overwhelmed by the demand for consistent content." Cut it. What comes next is the actual post.

**Slide 5:** Test it: take your last AI draft. Delete the first paragraph. Read from the second. Almost always, the second paragraph is the stronger opening. The AI was just being polite before getting to the point.

**Slide 6:** Start in the middle. Delete the introduction. Begin where the tension is. That's the edit. That's the whole thing. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:**
This sounds too simple to be worth saying. It is worth saying because it works every single time.

AI has a formality instinct. It learned to write from content that has introductions. Business articles have introductions. Academic papers have introductions. Reports have introductions. The model learned that writing starts with a warmup and proceeds to substance.

Your Instagram post does not have the luxury of a warmup. You have three seconds. The first sentence is everything.

When you take an AI draft and delete the first paragraph, you are not losing information. You are removing the handshake before the conversation. The conversation was already better.

I've done this with hundreds of AI drafts. The second paragraph is the right opening about 80% of the time. The remaining 20%: the second paragraph needs one word changed to function as the opening, and then it's right.

Train yourself to see the first paragraph as a first draft of your second paragraph. Then cut it. Start where it gets real.

## Post 5 — Why Opinion is the Best Humanizer

**Hook:** The fastest way to make AI content sound human is to add one sentence the AI would never generate on its own.

**Slide 1:** The fastest way to make AI content sound human is to add one sentence the AI would never generate on its own.

**Slide 2:** That sentence is an honest, unpopular opinion. "I think most productivity content is designed to make you feel like you're doing something rather than actually helping you do something." An AI will not volunteer that.

**Slide 3:** AI is trained to be helpful, harmless, and balanced. It avoids strong opinions because strong opinions can be wrong and wrong answers are penalized in training. That caution is a bug for content creators.

**Slide 4:** Opinion is the primary signal of a human voice. Not emotion. Not storytelling. Opinion. When someone reads a take that could not have been generated by a committee, they know a person was there.

**Slide 5:** The formula: write the AI draft, then add one sentence that starts with "I think" or "My honest take:" and finish it with something you actually believe, not something that sounds safe. Then don't delete it when you get nervous.

**Slide 6:** Your opinions are your content differentiation. Every other creator can use the same AI. No other creator can have your specific take. Use it. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:**
Content without opinion is content without a reason to exist.

There are already thousands of posts explaining how to use AI for content creation. There are thousands of posts about productivity, about business, about copywriting. The factual content has been covered. The framework has been shared. The research exists.

What doesn't exist is your specific take on it. The part where you disagree with the consensus. The part where you tried the popular method and found it didn't work. The part where you think everyone else is measuring the wrong thing.

That's the only thing AI cannot generate and your competitor cannot replicate. Your actual opinion, formed by your specific experience, filtered through your specific values.

I add at least one sentence of genuine, slightly uncomfortable opinion to every AI-assisted piece I publish. Not to be provocative. Because that sentence is the only sentence that could only come from me. Everything else is accelerated research.

The opinion is the fingerprint. Leave it in. Do not sand it down. The reader who agrees with it strongly is your person. Find them.
