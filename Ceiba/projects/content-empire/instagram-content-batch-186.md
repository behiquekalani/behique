## Post 2 — The 4 Folders That Hold Everything

**Hook:** Four folders. Every piece of information you will ever capture fits inside one of them.

**Slide 1:** Four folders. Every piece of information you will ever capture fits inside one of them.

**Slide 2:** This is the PARA system. Built by Tiago Forte. It's the backbone of every serious second brain.

**Slide 3:** P — Projects. Things with a deadline and a defined outcome. Active work.

**Slide 4:** A — Areas. Ongoing responsibilities with no end date. Health, finances, content.

**Slide 5:** R — Resources. Topics you're learning or might reference later. Not active, still useful.

**Slide 6:** A — Archive. Everything that's done, dormant, or no longer active. Searchable, out of the way.

**Caption:**
Most people's filing systems collapse because they're trying to organize by topic instead of by use.

PARA changes that. It organizes by actionability. Projects are what you're doing now. Areas are what you're responsible for. Resources are what you might use. Archive is what you're done with.

Four folders. Every note, file, and idea you will ever have fits into one of these.

The reason this works is because it mirrors how you actually think. When you open your workspace, you don't want to browse an encyclopedia. You want to see what's active and get to work.

Build PARA in Notion and your second brain stops being a graveyard of notes and starts being a functioning system.

## Post 4 — The Weekly Review Is What Makes It Stick

**Hook:** Without a weekly review, your second brain becomes a digital junk drawer.

**Slide 1:** Without a weekly review, your second brain becomes a digital junk drawer.

**Slide 2:** You capture. You save. Things pile up. Nothing gets processed. You stop trusting the system.

**Slide 3:** That's the death loop. And the weekly review is what breaks it.

**Slide 4:** Once a week, you do three things: clear your inbox, update your project pages, archive what's done.

**Slide 5:** It takes 20 to 40 minutes. It keeps the system clean, current, and trustworthy. And when you trust your system, you use it.

**Slide 6:** Post 5 covers the exact weekly review template I use. Follow to get it.

**Caption:**
The reason most Notion setups fail is not the structure. It's the maintenance.

Every system needs a reset. Without one, notes pile up in your inbox, projects go stale, and you stop trusting that the system reflects reality. Once you stop trusting it, you stop using it.

The weekly review is how you maintain trust. It's not a long process. You clear the inbox. You update what's active. You archive what's done. You look at the week ahead and adjust.

That's it. But when it becomes a ritual, everything changes. Your workspace stays accurate. Your mind stays clear. You start your week knowing exactly where you stand.

I've been doing this for over a year. The week I skip it is always the week things feel scattered.
