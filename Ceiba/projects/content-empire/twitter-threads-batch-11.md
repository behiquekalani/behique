## THREAD 1: The ADHD productivity stack that actually works (5 tools, all free)

---

**Tweet 1 (hook)**
The ADHD productivity advice that gets shared most is written by people who don't have ADHD.

Here is the stack that actually works. 5 tools. All free. Built around how the brain actually functions, not how productivity books say it should.

---

**Tweet 2**
First: understand the real problem.

ADHD doesn't make you lazy. It makes the gap between "I should do this" and "I'm doing this" enormous.

Every tool on this list closes that gap. None of them require willpower you don't have.

---

**Tweet 3**
Tool 1: Telegram (message yourself)

Your inbox is already on your phone. When an idea hits, send it to yourself instantly. Voice notes work too.

No app to open. No category to choose. No friction between the thought and the capture.

This single habit recovered more lost ideas than any other change I made.

---

**Tweet 4**
Tool 2: Obsidian (local notes, free)

One folder. One note per project. Open it in two keystrokes.

The point is not the features. The point is that it's on your machine, opens instantly, and never asks you to log in.

Speed of access is everything when attention is limited.

---

**Tweet 5**
Tool 3: Forest or any Pomodoro timer

25 minutes of work, 5 minutes off.

The reason this works for ADHD specifically: it removes the question of "how long do I work on this." That question is a trap. The timer answers it before your brain can spiral.

Start the timer before you're ready.

---

**Tweet 6**
Tool 4: A plain text file called today.md

Not a task manager. Not a project tracker. One file.

Write three things in it every morning. The three things that would make today a win.

Close the file. Work. Open it at the end of the day to close the loop.

---

**Tweet 7**
Tool 5: Voice memos (default app on your phone)

ADHD thinking is often faster than typing.

When a problem is too complex to write down, talk through it. Record yourself thinking. Transcribe later if needed.

I've solved 30-minute problems in 4-minute voice memos.

---

**Tweet 8**
The pattern across all five tools:

Zero login friction. Zero categorization overhead. Zero maintenance.

The moment a system requires you to maintain it, you've added a task that competes with the work. That's the loop that kills productivity for ADHD brains.

Simpler is not a tradeoff. It's the feature.

---

**Tweet 9**
The thing nobody tells you: you don't need better discipline.

You need an environment where the default action is the right action.

These tools set that up. The brain does the rest.

---

**Tweet 10 (closer)**
All five are free. None require accounts. All run on your existing devices.

The stack works because it gets out of the way.

## THREAD 2: I built 6 web apps in one week. Here is what I learned.

---

**Tweet 1 (hook)**
Six web apps. One week. No sleep deprivation, no outsourcing.

Here is the honest breakdown of what that required, what broke, and what I'd change.

---

**Tweet 2**
First, what "built" means here.

Not production-ready SaaS. Functional prototypes. Each one does one thing and does it well enough to test the idea with real users.

That distinction matters because the goal was learning, not shipping.

---

**Tweet 3**
Day 1-2: The first app took 14 hours.

Too much time planning the architecture before writing a single line. This is the classic mistake. The plan always changes on first contact with reality.

Hour 14, I deleted half the plan and shipped what was left.

---

**Tweet 4**
Day 3: The second app took 6 hours.

Same complexity as the first. The difference was constraint. I allowed myself two components and one API call. That's it.

Constraint is not a limitation. It's a forcing function for clarity.

---

**Tweet 5**
Day 4: Apps 3 and 4. Both done in one day.

By this point the template was clear. One user problem. One input. One output. A UI that doesn't require explanation.

I stopped building features and started building solutions.

---

**Tweet 6**
Day 5-6: Apps 5 and 6.

These were harder because I got ambitious. Added a database, an auth system, and a third API.

Both apps took twice as long and were half as usable. The complexity cost more than it contributed.

I shipped them anyway, noted what to cut.

---

**Tweet 7**
The actual lesson from the week:

Speed in software is mostly a function of how clearly you can define what the thing is NOT.

Every time I added a feature, I was hedging. Every time I removed one, I was deciding.

Deciding is what builds software. Hedging builds scope creep.

---

**Tweet 8**
The other lesson: AI handles the syntax, not the architecture.

I used Claude for all six builds. It wrote components fast. It also suggested the wrong database schema twice and missed an edge case that broke the auth flow.

The thinking is still yours. The typing is just faster.

---

**Tweet 9**
What I'd do differently:

Write the README before writing any code. Not because anyone will read it, but because it forces you to describe what the thing does in plain language before you touch the keyboard.

If you can't describe it, you can't build it.

---

**Tweet 10 (closer)**
Six apps in a week is not impressive. Anyone can do it.

The useful part is what those six builds taught me about my own decisions under pressure.

Build fast. Break things deliberately. Notice what the failure tells you.

## THREAD 3: The problem with every AI writing tool (and what I built instead)

---

**Tweet 1 (hook)**
Every AI writing tool has the same problem.

It optimizes for output that sounds like writing. Not output that sounds like you.

Here is the difference, and what I built to close the gap.

---

**Tweet 2**
The pattern across every major AI writer: ChatGPT, Jasper, Copy.ai, all of them.

They produce text that is grammatically correct, structurally sound, and completely anonymous.

You could swap the author on any piece and nobody would notice. That's not a small problem.

---

**Tweet 3**
Voice is not style. That's the thing most people miss.

Style is surface: sentence length, word choice, formatting.

Voice is the specific way one person thinks. The observations they make. The things they find worth saying. The connections they draw that nobody else would.

AI can copy style. It can't copy voice.

---

**Tweet 4**
The fix is not a better prompt. A better prompt gets you better imitation.

The fix is a voice document. A detailed record of how you actually write, what you say, what you never say, and examples of your real output.

Then you use that as context, not as a style guide.

---

**Tweet 5**
What's in the voice document I built:

7 adjectives that describe my writing. Specific vocabulary rules, words I use and words I ban. 7 real writing samples across different content types. Platform-specific calibrations. Banned patterns that make content sound generic.

One document. Used in every AI session.

---

**Tweet 6**
The result: content that needs a 5-minute edit, not a 30-minute rewrite.

Not because the AI is better. Because the context is more specific.

Garbage in, garbage out is still the rule. A voice document is just better input.

---

**Tweet 7**
What I built beyond the document:

A personal content pipeline. AI drafts using the voice doc. I do one edit pass. It ships.

The pipeline doesn't generate content for me. It drafts content that sounds close enough to me that the edit is surgical, not structural.

---

**Tweet 8**
The thing AI writing tools will never solve by themselves:

Taste. The editorial judgment to know what's worth saying.

That's not a technical problem. It's a thinking problem. You still have to do the thinking.

The tool just handles the production.

---

**Tweet 9**
If you want to build your own:

Start with the question "what do I never say?" That's the most useful constraint. The bans are easier to articulate than the rules, and they do more work.

---

**Tweet 10 (closer)**
The AI writing tool that works is the one trained on you.

Not your genre. Not your industry. You specifically.

Build that context before you build anything else.

## THREAD 4: How to make $500/month selling digital products with no audience

---

**Tweet 1 (hook)**
$500/month from digital products with zero audience is not a reach.

It's a math problem. Here is the math, and the exact sequence to make it work.

---

**Tweet 2**
The math first.

$500/month at $10/product = 50 sales.
$500/month at $25/product = 20 sales.
$500/month at $50/product = 10 sales.

10 sales a month is not a viral moment. It's a small stream. Small streams are findable.

---

**Tweet 3**
The wrong sequence: build product, build audience, sell.

Most people do this. They make the thing first, then try to find people to buy it.

By the time they have an audience, they've forgotten why they made the product.

---

**Tweet 4**
The right sequence: find the question, answer it, charge for the answer.

Step 1: Find a question people are already asking. Reddit, YouTube comments, Facebook groups, Gumroad reviews. Real questions from real people.

Step 2: Build the specific answer to that question.

Step 3: Put it where the question is being asked.

---

**Tweet 5**
Platform specifics.

Gumroad: easiest to start, takes a percentage, works with zero traffic.
Lemon Squeezy: same model, cleaner checkout.
Payhip: free to start, lower fees.

Pick one. Ship. The platform choice matters less than having something to sell.

---

**Tweet 6**
The no-audience distribution stack:

Pinterest: drives evergreen traffic to product pages. One good pin drives clicks for months.
Reddit: answer questions in relevant subreddits. Put your product in the bio, not the comment.
YouTube: one tutorial video that answers the question your product solves. The video is the funnel.

---

**Tweet 7**
Pricing in the no-audience phase.

Price for conversion, not for margin.

$9-15 for the first product. The goal is not maximum revenue per sale. The goal is getting the first 10 buyers so you have proof this product works.

Raise the price after the proof exists.

---

**Tweet 8**
What the product should be.

Not a course. Not an ebook with fluff padding.

A specific answer to a specific question. A template, a checklist, a guide, a swipe file. Something that takes 30 minutes to use and saves 5 hours.

Specificity sells. Comprehensiveness paralyzes.

---

**Tweet 9**
The 30-day path to $500.

Week 1: Find the question. Research Reddit and YouTube comments. Write down every question people ask that you can already answer.
Week 2: Build the answer. One focused product. 5-15 pages.
Week 3: List it. Write the Gumroad page. Post in two communities.
Week 4: Iterate. What are people asking in the comments? Build the next product.

---

**Tweet 10 (closer)**
No audience needed. No ads needed. No viral moment needed.

Just a specific question and a useful answer.

That's the business.

## THREAD 5: The Telegram trick that saves me 2 hours a day

---

**Tweet 1 (hook)**
Most people use Telegram to receive messages.

I use it as an operating system.

Here is the setup that saves 2 hours a day and runs most of my capture, task, and content pipeline.

---

**Tweet 2**
The core idea: Telegram is the fastest input method on your phone.

Faster than any notes app. Faster than any task manager. It opens instantly, drafts save automatically, and it works offline.

Most people ignore this because they only think of it as a messaging app.

---

**Tweet 3**
Setup 1: Message yourself.

Create a chat with your own account. This is your inbox.

Every idea, task, voice note, link, and screenshot goes here. One tap. No category selection. No friction.

The inbox gets processed once a day. Until then, everything lives here.

---

**Tweet 4**
Setup 2: Bot for transcription.

I have a bot that receives voice notes and returns transcripts.

I record ideas while walking, while cooking, while driving. The transcript arrives in 30 seconds. I copy it to wherever it belongs.

This alone recovers ideas I used to lose.

---

**Tweet 5**
Setup 3: Scheduled messages.

Telegram lets you schedule messages to yourself at any time.

I use this as a reminder system. If I know I need to do something at 3pm Tuesday, I schedule a message to myself for 3pm Tuesday.

It shows up like a notification with context already attached.

---

**Tweet 6**
Setup 4: Pinned messages as a dashboard.

You can pin up to 5 messages in any chat.

I pin: today's three priorities, the current project file link, a link to my Gumroad store, my weekly review checklist, and my top content idea.

Every morning I open the chat and the dashboard is already there.

---

**Tweet 7**
Setup 5: Telegram channels as a personal knowledge base.

Private channels you control function like Substack or a blog, but private.

I have one channel where I post observations, ideas I want to keep, quotes, and notes. It's searchable. It persists. It costs nothing.

---

**Tweet 8**
The time math.

Before this setup: context-switching between notes apps, task managers, and reminders took 15-20 minutes per hour of actual work.

After: capture takes seconds. Retrieval takes one search. The overhead went from significant to near zero.

2 hours a day is conservative.

---

**Tweet 9**
What this is really about.

Every minute spent managing your tools is a minute not spent on the work.

The best productivity system is the one that gets out of the way fastest.

Telegram is already on your phone. You're already looking at it. The cost to add these setups is one afternoon.

---

**Tweet 10 (closer)**
The trick is not Telegram specifically.

The trick is using the tool you already reach for instead of building a parallel system you have to remember to use.

One less app. One less login. One less reason to not capture the thing.
