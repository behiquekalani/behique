## Script 01: "The Task List That Actually Works"

**Hook (0-3s):**
My to-do list has exactly 3 items on it. That's on purpose.

**Body (3-50s):**
I used to keep lists with 20, 30 things on them. Color-coded. Organized by category.

Never finished them. By day three the list had more items than when I started.

The problem: long lists don't tell your brain what to do. They tell your brain there's too much to do. Which triggers the freeze.

Three items. That's it. Pick the three things that, if done today, make the day a success.

Everything else goes to a separate "later" list that I don't look at during the day. Out of sight, not deleted.

The shift: from "here's everything I need to do" to "here's what I'm doing today."

Small list. Full focus. More gets done than it sounds like.

## Script 02: "Why I Can't Work in Silence"

**Hook (0-3s):**
Silence kills my focus. This took years to accept.

**Body (3-50s):**
Everyone talks about a "distraction-free environment." No phone. No noise. No interruptions.

That environment makes my brain go looking for stimulation. Every ambient sound becomes interesting. I notice everything.

What actually works for me: brown noise. Constant, non-rhythmic background sound.

Not music. Music is too structured, I start following it. Not silence. Too much space for thoughts to wander.

Brown noise gives my brain just enough input to stop searching, so I can actually focus on the thing in front of me.

I use a free app. It cost nothing. It works every time.

If you have ADHD and the "silent room" advice isn't working, it's not because you're doing focus wrong. It's because that advice wasn't written for your brain.

## Script 03: "The 25-5 Rule vs My Version"

**Hook (0-3s):**
Pomodoro didn't work for me until I changed one thing.

**Body (3-50s):**
Classic Pomodoro: 25 minutes on, 5 minutes off. Repeat four times, take a longer break.

The problem I ran into: I have ADHD. When I finally get into focus, a timer going off at 25 minutes breaks that state. Getting back in takes 10 to 15 minutes.

So the 5-minute break ends up costing more than it saves.

My version: I start the timer but I don't stop when it goes off. The timer is for starting, not stopping.

When it hits 25 minutes, I note it and keep going if I'm in flow. When I feel myself drifting, I stop. Sometimes that's at 18 minutes. Sometimes it's at 55.

The point of the timer isn't to schedule rest. It's to commit to a start.

That shift made Pomodoro actually useful for me.

## Script 04: "How I Study When I Can't Read"

**Hook (0-3s):**
Some days I literally cannot read. Here's what I do instead.

**Body (3-50s):**
I'm talking about those days when your eyes pass over the same paragraph three times and nothing registers.

For me that happens when I'm overstimulated or under-slept. The text just doesn't land.

What works on those days: audio.

I convert my notes to audio using text-to-speech. I take the textbook content and ask Claude to summarize it in plain language first, then I listen to that.

Walking helps. I go outside and listen to the summary while walking. Movement plus audio inputs information in a way that sitting still and staring at a screen does not.

It's not ideal. I retain less than deep reading.

But it's better than the zero I retain staring at a page for two hours.

Working with what's actually happening in your brain is more useful than forcing a method that isn't working.

## Script 05: "The Capture System That Saved My Grades"

**Hook (0-3s):**
Ideas and tasks were falling through the cracks. I fixed it with one habit.

**Body (3-50s):**
The problem with ADHD and studying: your brain generates a lot of thoughts at the wrong time. You're in the middle of calculus and suddenly you remember you need to send an email. Or you get a good idea for a project.

If you stop to handle it, you lose the calculus thread. If you ignore it, you lose the idea and spend mental energy trying not to forget it.

The fix: a capture inbox. One place, frictionless entry.

For me it's a Telegram bot I built. Send any thought, task, or idea. It gets logged, categorized, and stored. I review it once a day.

The act of capturing removes the thought from working memory. I stop trying to hold it. That frees up brain space for the actual task.

You don't need a custom bot. A Notes app works. A sticky note works. The habit matters more than the tool.

## Script 06: "Why I Stopped Fighting My Sleep Schedule"

**Hook (0-3s):**
I'm not a morning person. Pretending I was made everything worse.

**Body (3-50s):**
Years of trying to wake up at 6 AM because productivity culture said that's when the disciplined people work.

What actually happened: I woke up exhausted, produced nothing useful until noon, and felt like a failure before lunch.

Here's what I noticed instead. My brain is sharp from around 10 PM to 2 AM. Coding, writing, deep work. That's when things connect.

Mornings are fine for admin tasks, emails, routine. Not for hard problems.

So I stopped scheduling hard work in the morning. I do easy tasks early and deep work late.

My grades went up. My output went up. My mood went up.

Sleep science says most ADHD brains run on a delayed circadian rhythm. That's not a character flaw. It's biology.

Work with your actual schedule, not the one you think you should have.

## Script 07: "The One Rule That Keeps Me From Drowning in Projects"

**Hook (0-3s):**
I had 7 projects open at once. Here's the rule that fixed it.

**Body (3-50s):**
Classic ADHD trap: shiny object syndrome. Everything is interesting. Everything feels urgent. You start six things and finish none.

I know because I lived this for a long time.

The rule I use now: one primary project per week. Everything else is in maintenance mode or paused.

Maintenance mode means: I check in on it for 15 minutes per day. I don't advance it. I just make sure nothing is broken.

Paused means: I don't look at it until primary is done.

The hardest part is the moment a new idea comes in and feels more exciting than what I'm currently building. That's the test.

I write the new idea down in the capture system and tell myself: it will be there when this is done.

Most of the time, it still looks good two weeks later. Some of the time it doesn't. Both outcomes are useful.

## Script 08: "Body Doubling Made Me 3x More Productive"

**Hook (0-3s):**
Working alone kills my focus. Working "with someone" fixes it.

**Body (3-50s):**
Body doubling is a real thing. Having another person present, even if they're doing something completely different, helps ADHD brains stay on task.

The theory is that the social context activates something that solo work doesn't.

I don't always have someone to work with in person. So I use virtual body doubling.

There are YouTube streams with people studying in real time. You can watch them work while you work.

There are Discord servers for focus sessions. Cameras on, everyone working silently.

There's even a website called Focusmate that pairs you with a stranger for 50-minute sessions.

I use YouTube study streams most often. Lo-fi background, someone visible on screen, and I stay on task for 2 to 3 hours without forcing it.

## Script 09: "Why I Don't Use a Planner Anymore"

**Hook (0-3s):**
Planners don't work for my brain. Here's what replaced them.

**Body (3-50s):**
Traditional planner: schedule your day in hourly blocks. 9 AM: email. 10 AM: project work. 1 PM: lunch.

The problem: ADHD is not a linear condition. My energy is not predictable. Some days I'm sharp at 9 AM. Some days I'm not useful until 2 PM.

A rigid hourly schedule that I constantly fail to follow doesn't help me. It gives me more things to feel bad about.

What I use instead: a daily intention list. Three things. No times attached.

When I have focus, I do one of the three. When I don't, I do maintenance tasks.

At the end of the day I look at what got done. Not what didn't.

The shift is from scheduling to noticing. I pay attention to when I'm sharp and I stack my hard work there. Over time that pattern becomes predictable enough to use.

## Script 10: "The Real Reason ADHD Is Hard in School"

**Hook (0-3s):**
School is not designed for how ADHD brains learn. That's the actual problem.

**Body (3-50s):**
Here's what I mean.

ADHD brains tend to go deep on things they find interesting and completely disengage from things they don't. This is not laziness. It's how the brain processes novelty and reward.

School asks you to give equal attention to 7 different subjects, most of which don't connect to anything you care about, for 8 hours a day.

That's a hard constraint for any brain. For an ADHD brain it's nearly impossible without a strategy.

The strategy that worked for me: find the angle.

Every subject has an angle that connects to something I actually care about. Calculus connects to simulation and AI. History connects to systems and power dynamics. Chemistry connects to materials and manufacturing.

Once I found the angle, engagement came. Not always. But enough.

You're not broken for struggling with the format. The format was built for a different kind of brain.
