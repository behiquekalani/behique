## ISSUE 11

**Subject: The system I use to stop buying things I don't need**

---

Hey,

About 18 months ago I kept ending up with things I did not need. A mechanical keyboard still in the box. A course I never opened. Three items I returned after the window closed, so I just kept them.

I knew I was doing it. The awareness was not the problem. I needed a delay between the urge and the purchase.

---

### This Week's Build

The impulse cooldown protocol is a simple three-step loop.

Step one: write it down. The moment you want to buy something, you put it on the list. Not in a cart. Not a wishlist. A plain list with the item name and the date you wrote it.

Step two: wait 24 hours. Minimum. You do not act on the impulse the same day. The list is not a wishlist. It is a holding area.

Step three: check the list the next day. If you still want it and you can afford it, consider buying it. If the urge is gone, cross it off.

That is the whole protocol. I ran it for 30 days as an experiment. I wrote down 32 items. I bought two of them. The other 30 lost their urgency within 24 hours.

The math: $1,040 in potential impulse spending. I spent maybe $80. The delay did the work, not discipline.

---

### Tool of the Week

**Apple Notes / Google Keep** - the simplest possible tool for the impulse list. A note on your phone you can open in under five seconds. No friction. No special app needed.

The tool does not matter. The behavior does. But the lower the friction to write it down, the more reliably the protocol works.

Free. iOS and Android.

---

### The Number

**80%** - the percentage of impulse urges that disappeared on their own after a 24-hour wait. The number comes from my own 30-day tracking. The math is not scientific. But the pattern held consistently.

---

### What I'm Thinking About

Most financial advice focuses on earning more or investing more. The leak is quieter than that.

The leak is the gym membership you still have from eight months ago. The subscription you forgot to cancel. The course you bought on a Wednesday night and never opened.

Those are not big decisions. That is why they stay invisible. The impulse cooldown protocol makes them visible before the money leaves your account.

## ISSUE 12

**Subject: I built a wellness environment in a browser tab**

---

Hey,

At some point I had four wellness apps open to do one focused work session.

Spotify for music. A separate Pomodoro timer. A breathing app my therapist recommended. A noise generator for background sound. Four apps. Four places to get distracted switching between them.

That setup lasted about two weeks before I stopped using most of it. Not because the tools were bad. Because the overhead was too high.

---

### This Week's Build

Ceiba Sanctuary is a browser tab that combines the pieces I was juggling into one interface.

Breathing companion. Pomodoro timer. Ambient sound controls. All in one tab. No app to download. No account required. You open it, it runs.

The breathing companion uses a four-four-four pattern. Four seconds inhale, four seconds hold, four seconds exhale. I run two cycles before starting the timer. That transition from scattered to focused takes about 90 seconds.

The Pomodoro is 25 minutes by default. Adjustable. When the session ends, the tab pulses and a five-minute break screen appears. Then the next cycle starts.

I use it during study sessions. Open the tab, do two breath cycles, set the timer, switch to the work file. The Sanctuary runs behind the work tab and pulses when it is time to stop.

The reason this works better than four separate apps: context switches are where focus breaks. Fewer apps means fewer transitions.

---

### Tool of the Week

**One Tab browser extension** - free extension that collapses all your open browser tabs into a single list. I use this before starting a Sanctuary session to reduce visual clutter. Open tabs are a source of distraction even if you are not clicking on them.

Free. Chrome and Firefox.

---

### The Number

**4** - the number of separate apps I was using to run a single work session before building Sanctuary. Reducing that to one tab removed three context switches from the start of every work block.

---

### What I'm Thinking About

Wellness tools have the same problem as budgeting tools. They add friction instead of removing it.

An app that requires a login, a notification permission, a setup screen, and a daily streak to feel useful is competing with every other distraction for your attention. That is a bad design for someone whose brain already struggles with transitions.

The tools that actually get used are the ones you can open in under five seconds and get value from immediately. That is the design constraint Sanctuary was built around.

## ISSUE 13

**Subject: The 5-category budget I use (and why simple beats complete)**

---

Hey,

Every budgeting system I tried before the one I use now had the same problem. Too many categories.

There was a version I built once that had 47 line items. Groceries broken out from dining out. Clothing broken out from accessories. Subscriptions split into entertainment and productivity. I spent 40 minutes setting it up and checked it twice before abandoning it.

The system did not fail because I was lazy. It failed because no system with 47 line items gets followed consistently by a human with a full life.

---

### This Week's Build

The five-category system has one category for every area where money actually moves.

1. Fixed. Rent, insurance, subscriptions that do not change. These are the obligations that happen whether you think about them or not.

2. Food. Groceries and restaurants together. Not separate. The goal is to see the total, not to optimize the sub-split.

3. Transport. Gas, transit, rideshare, parking. One number.

4. Fun. Everything discretionary. Clothes, entertainment, eating out with friends, random purchases. One number. This category does not require justification. It requires a limit.

5. Savings. The target transfer. Not what is left over. A committed number that moves first.

That is the full system. Five numbers. One side of a sticky note.

---

### The Weekly Review

The system only works if you check it. Once a week. Ten minutes. I do it Sunday morning before anything else.

The review is not an audit. You are not looking for violations. You are looking at the actual numbers and comparing them to the planned numbers.

Food went over this week. Fine. I know. I adjust the next week or accept the trade-off.

Fun is almost empty and it is Wednesday. That is useful information. I either spend differently the rest of the week or I note it and plan for next month.

Ten minutes of looking at five numbers is a sustainable habit. An hour of categorizing 47 transactions is not.

---

### Tool of the Week

**Google Sheets** - free. The five categories live in a spreadsheet with five rows. Income at the top, five categories below, one row at the bottom showing what is left. No formulas beyond basic subtraction.

If you want the exact template I use, it is available separately as the Personal Budget Template.

Free. Works on any device.

---

### The Number

**47** - the number of budget categories in the version of my budget that never got followed. The version I actually follow has five.

---

### What I'm Thinking About

Completeness is not the goal of a budget. Consistency is.

A simple system you check every week beats a complete system you check once and abandon. The 47-category version was technically more accurate. The five-category version is the one that actually changed my spending.

Good enough and sustainable beats perfect and abandoned every time.
