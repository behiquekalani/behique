## Thread 1: The case for going deep on one platform before going wide

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
The advice to "be everywhere" is ruining more creator businesses than any algorithm change ever has.

Here's why deep beats wide, especially at the start.

**Tweet 2:**
Being everywhere requires producing different content for different formats on different schedules.

Done well, that's a team job.

Done by one person trying to also build a business, it produces mediocre content across all platforms instead of excellent content on one.

**Tweet 3:**
The compound effect of platform depth:

When you post consistently on one platform for 12 months, the algorithm learns who you are.

Your best old content surfaces to new people. Your recent content gets initial distribution because of your history.

That doesn't happen if you've posted 3 times and moved on.

**Tweet 4:**
The depth advantage: on a single platform, you learn what your specific audience responds to.

You test formats. You notice which posts get saved, which ones get comments, which ones drive newsletter signups.

That feedback is gold. You can only accumulate it with enough volume.

**Tweet 5:**
The right time to expand to a second platform:

When the first platform is running on a system (not on your constant attention).

When you have enough content to repurpose rather than create fresh for everything.

When you have data about what works so you can adapt it, not guess.

**Tweet 6:**
The question to ask: "If I can only be on one platform for the next 6 months, which one gives my specific content the best chance of reaching my specific audience?"

Answer that. Go there. Stay there.

The multi-platform strategy can wait.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
One platform, 6 months, consistent output.

This is the advice that compounds.

"Be everywhere" is the advice that spreads you thin and produces nothing worth following.

## Thread 2: The real cost of the "post everywhere" strategy

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Every platform you add to your content strategy has a hidden cost that nobody puts in the spreadsheet.

Here it is:

**Tweet 2:**
The obvious cost: time.

Creating content for Instagram, LinkedIn, TikTok, Twitter, YouTube, and a newsletter is not possible at quality for one person.

Something gets cut. Usually quality. Then you wonder why nothing's working.

**Tweet 3:**
The less obvious cost: attention.

Every platform you're on demands a different mental model.

What performs on TikTok is not what performs on LinkedIn. The tone, the format, the hook, the CTA, the optimal length. All different.

Juggling multiple mental models is cognitive overhead that has no return if you're not yet winning on any one of them.

**Tweet 4:**
The most hidden cost: diluted signal.

If you're getting 200 views on Instagram, 150 on TikTok, and 80 on LinkedIn, you have no idea which of those is the right platform for your content.

None of them have enough data to tell you.

Concentrate the volume on one platform and the signal becomes clear fast.

**Tweet 5:**
The rationalization: "I'll just repurpose everything."

This works at scale. It doesn't work when you're still figuring out what resonates.

Repurposing good content is efficient. Repurposing untested content is efficient failure.

**Tweet 6:**
The counter-argument: "What if my audience is on multiple platforms?"

Probably yes. But your audience is not equally accessible on all platforms.

One platform will outperform the others for your specific content type and audience. Find that one first.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
The real costs of posting everywhere:
- Time (obvious)
- Cognitive overhead (underrated)
- Diluted signal (the most damaging)

Pick one. Win there. Then expand.

## Thread 3: How to choose the right platform for your business

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
"Which platform should I be on?"

Wrong question.

"Which platform gives this specific content type the best reach to this specific audience?" Right question.

Here's how to answer it:

**Tweet 2:**
Start with content type.

If your content is deeply visual (fashion, food, design, lifestyle): Instagram or TikTok.

If your content is written reasoning or frameworks (business, productivity, finance): Twitter or LinkedIn.

If your content is educational with high production value: YouTube.

If your content is fast-paced entertainment or trends: TikTok.

**Tweet 3:**
Then look at audience density.

Which platform do the people you want to reach actually use?

Not which platform is growing fastest. Not which one you personally enjoy. The one where your target reader already spends time.

**Tweet 4:**
Then look at your own strengths.

If you're a natural writer, the short-form video platforms will feel like a grind.

Choosing the platform that aligns with your content strengths means you'll produce consistently without burning out.

Sustainable output beats optimal format every time.

**Tweet 5:**
The test: spend 30 days posting consistently on the platform you think is right.

Track engagement, saves, follows, and link clicks.

At 30 days, you have enough data to know if this platform is worth doubling down on or abandoning.

30 days of real data beats any amount of advice about which platform is best in the abstract.

**Tweet 6:**
What the data often reveals: the platform you were most skeptical about is the one that performs.

Because it's usually the platform with less competition in your niche.

An obvious insight: go where the density of competitors is lower, not where the total audience is larger.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
The platform selection framework:
1. Content type alignment
2. Audience density
3. Your sustainable output strengths
4. 30-day test with real data

## Thread 4: The minimum viable multi-platform strategy (for when you're ready to expand)

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
At some point, one platform isn't enough.

But "being everywhere" is still the wrong answer.

Here's the minimum viable multi-platform strategy:

**Tweet 2:**
The structure: one primary platform, one secondary platform.

Primary: where you create original content. Where you invest the most time and attention.

Secondary: where you distribute adapted versions of what you already made.

Two platforms. Not three, not five.

**Tweet 3:**
The adaptation principle: don't create fresh content for the secondary platform.

Take what performed well on the primary and adapt the format.

A Twitter thread that performed well becomes a LinkedIn post.

A LinkedIn long-form becomes a Twitter thread.

A newsletter section becomes a Twitter thread.

**Tweet 4:**
The time budget: if primary platform gets 70% of your content time, secondary gets 30%.

Not equal. Not even close to equal.

The primary is where you compound. The secondary is where you capture overflow audience.

**Tweet 5:**
When to add a third platform:

When the secondary is running on a system and not on active attention.

When you have enough content history that the third platform gets launched with a backlog, not from scratch.

Most creators add a third platform too early and dilute the first two.

**Tweet 6:**
The email list remains outside this structure.

It's not a platform. It's an owned channel.

Whatever happens on your social platforms, the list is yours.

Build it in parallel to everything else. It's the one asset none of these platforms can take from you.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
Minimum viable multi-platform:
- One primary (invest 70%)
- One secondary (adapt from primary, 30%)
- Email list always building in parallel

Expand only when systems replace active attention.

## Thread 5: The single-platform creator who out-earns the multi-platform creator

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
I've seen creators with 50,000 followers across 4 platforms earn less than creators with 8,000 followers on one platform and a 2,000-person email list.

Here's why.

**Tweet 2:**
Distributed attention produces shallow relationships.

A follower who sees you once every few days across 4 platforms never feels like they know you.

A follower who reads you every Tuesday in their inbox for 6 months trusts you in a way that scattered social presence cannot produce.

**Tweet 3:**
Revenue follows depth of relationship, not breadth of reach.

The creator with 8,000 deeply engaged followers and a $97 product sells more units than the creator with 50,000 scattered followers and the same product.

Conversion rate tracks with trust. Trust tracks with depth.

**Tweet 4:**
The deep engagement signals: replies, saves, direct messages, purchases, recommendations to friends.

These come from people who feel they know you.

They don't come from people who saw your face on three different platforms this week without context.

**Tweet 5:**
The compounding advantage of platform focus: every post you publish on one platform is training data for the algorithm about you.

Your content history creates a gravity well. New content gets initial distribution based on your track record.

That gravity well doesn't exist if you've published 20 posts in the last year on a platform.

**Tweet 6:**
The counterintuitive outcome: the creator who went narrow and deep has more time.

Fewer platforms. More consistent output per platform. Better results per hour of content production.

The multi-platform creator works more hours for worse results per platform.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
Revenue follows depth of relationship.
Depth comes from consistent presence on fewer surfaces.

Single platform, deep engagement, email list.

That combination outearns scattered presence almost every time.
