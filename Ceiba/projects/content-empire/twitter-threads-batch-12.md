## THREAD 1: "I analyzed 50 AI writing samples. Here is the pattern that makes them all sound fake."

**Hook tweet:**
I analyzed 50 AI writing samples from 50 different creators.

Every single one had the same problem. Not the same words. The same structural failure.

Here is the pattern.

---

**Tweet 2:**
AI writing sounds fake because it performs confidence without earning it.

It uses words like "game-changing," "revolutionary," and "transformative" to signal importance.

But those words are borrowed authority. They say "this matters" without showing why it matters. Readers feel the difference.

---

**Tweet 3:**
The second pattern: vague enthusiasm.

"This tool will change the way you work."

Will it. How. For which tasks. Under what conditions. Compared to what.

Vague enthusiasm is the default because specificity requires having actually used the thing. Most AI writing skips that step.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The third pattern: the hedge spiral.

"This might work for some people in certain contexts depending on their specific situation."

Nobody writes like this naturally. It's AI covering itself. It reads like a terms-of-service document pretending to be advice.

Pick a lane. Say the thing.

---

**Tweet 5:**
The fourth pattern: lecture voice.

"What most people don't know is..."

This construction assumes the reader is ignorant and the writer is enlightened. It's the easiest way to make someone close the tab.

Real authority doesn't announce itself. It just says the thing and trusts the reader to recognize it.

---

**Tweet 6:**
The fifth pattern: structure without substance.

3 bullet points. Each one a general observation. Each one could apply to any topic.

Bullets feel organized. They aren't information. Most AI-generated bullet lists are five ways of saying the same thing.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The fix is not better prompting alone. It's a Voice Bible.

A Voice Bible is a document that defines exactly how you sound. Banned words. Sentence structure. Vocabulary you actually use. Platform-specific rules.

When AI writes with those constraints, it stops writing like a chatbot.

---

**Tweet 8:**
Here are the words that immediately signal AI wrote it with no editing:

- "Certainly"
- "Absolutely"
- "Of course"
- "Deep dive"
- "Excited to share"
- "Game-changing"
- "Revolutionary"

If your content has three of these, it was written by an AI that didn't know you.

---

**Tweet 9:**
The real issue is that most creators skip the identity layer.

They prompt: "Write a post about productivity."

They should prompt: "Write a post about productivity in the voice of someone who has ADHD, builds AI tools, and is skeptical of hustle culture. Use short sentences. No exclamation marks. Lead with a specific observation."

The second prompt produces something worth posting.

## THREAD 3: "How to build a Voice Bible in 30 minutes (with examples)"

**Hook tweet:**
A Voice Bible is the document that makes AI content sound like you instead of a chatbot.

It takes 30 minutes to build. Here is exactly how to do it.

---

**Tweet 2:**
Step 1: Write seven adjectives that describe your personality as a communicator.

Not what you want to be. What you actually are.

Mine: precise, calm, philosophical, honest, builder, Caribbean, understated.

Seven words. They do more work than a 10-page brand guide.

---

**Tweet 3:**
Step 2: Write a vocabulary list.

Words you use: "built," "ship," "runs," "stack," "pipeline," "honest take."

Words you avoid: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "excited to share," "deep dive."

The avoid list is more important than the use list. It removes the default AI vocabulary.

---

**Tweet 4:**
Step 3: Document your sentence structure.

My default: Short. Direct. One idea per sentence.

Rhythm: short, short, then a slightly longer sentence that carries the weight. Short again.

Write your actual rhythm. Paste in a piece of your best writing and reverse-engineer the pattern.

---

**Tweet 5:**
Step 4: Write your banned words list.

Every word that signals something false about you belongs here.

"Hustle" as a virtue. "Passive income." Exclamation marks. Rhetorical questions as hooks.

Each banned word earned its place because it carries the wrong connotation for your specific voice.

---

**Tweet 6:**
Step 5: Platform-specific calibration.

Instagram: 1-3 paragraphs, first line is the hook.
X: thread starter is a thesis, not a question.
Email: personal, one main thing, can be vulnerable.
YouTube: no intro, no "welcome back," start with the promise.

Same voice, different format rules.

---

**Tweet 7:**
Step 6: Write 3-5 sample pieces in your voice.

These become the style anchors. When you prompt AI, paste the most relevant sample and say "write in this style."

AI is good at pattern matching. Give it a pattern.

---

**Tweet 8:**
The one rule that matters most:

If the content could have been written by any AI automation brand, rewrite it.

Your content should sound like it was written by one specific person who has thought carefully about what they're building and why.

That specificity is the moat.

---

It's ready to fill in. Takes 30 minutes. Saves hours per week on editing.

The task exists in your head as a vague intention. "Work on the project." "Do the thing."

That's not a task. It's a category. Categories don't get done. Tasks get done.

The bot forces specificity before it logs anything.

**Tweet 4:**

By the time I sit down to work, the idea is already organized. I didn't have to remember it.

## THREAD 5: "Why your content sounds generic (it is not the AI's fault)"

**Hook tweet:**
Your AI-generated content sounds generic.

It is not the AI's fault.

Here is the actual problem.

---

**Tweet 2:**
The AI has no idea who you are.

It doesn't know your story. Your opinions. What you refuse to say. What words you hate. What you've actually built.

When you give it no identity to work with, it defaults to the average of everything it was trained on.

The average of everything is: generic.

---

**Tweet 3:**
The prompt "write a post about AI productivity" has been run millions of times.

The AI has pattern-matched on millions of responses to that prompt. It gives you the center of the distribution.

The center of the distribution is the most forgettable version of the idea.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The fix is not a better prompt. It is identity-first prompting.

Before you say what you want written, tell the AI who is supposed to be writing it.

Personality. Vocabulary. What they would never say. Platform they're writing for. One sample of their actual voice.

That changes the output completely.

---

**Tweet 5:**
Here is what most people actually do:

"Write me 5 tweets about AI content creation."

Here is what produces something worth posting:

"Write 5 tweets in the voice of a 22-year-old builder from Puerto Rico who is skeptical of hustle culture, builds AI tools for himself, uses short sentences, no exclamation marks, and never uses the words 'game-changing' or 'revolutionary.'"

Same request. Different universe of output.

---

**Tweet 6:**
The generic problem also has a structural cause.

Most AI content skips the editing pass.

Generate. Paste. Post.

The editing pass is where your actual voice enters the content. It's not optional. AI provides the structure. You provide the specificity.

If you skip editing, you're posting the AI's voice, not yours.

---

**Tweet 7:**
What to audit in your editing pass:

Remove every adjective that isn't specific. "Amazing" tells you nothing. "Cuts editing time by 80%" tells you something.

Remove every hedge. "Might," "could," "in some cases." Pick a lane.

Remove every word on your banned list. If you don't have a banned list, that's the first problem.

---

**Tweet 8:**
The deeper issue is that generic content is a systems failure, not a talent failure.

You don't have a bad voice. You have no documented voice.

One document fixes this. A Voice Bible. Your rules, your vocabulary, your banned words, your sentence structure.

Documented once. Applied every time.

---

**Tweet 9:**
The content that builds an audience is not the most informative content.

It's the most specific content. The most honest content. The content that could only have been written by one person.

AI can help you produce that at scale. But only if you give it the one thing it doesn't have by default: your actual identity.
