## Issue 51: The product research mistake everyone makes

You are researching the wrong thing.

Most people who want to sell digital products spend their research time figuring out what topics are popular. They look at bestseller lists, check what's trending on Google, scroll competitor stores, and try to reverse-engineer what to make.

That's demand research. It tells you what people are interested in. It does not tell you what people will buy.

Interest and purchase intent are different. A lot of people are interested in investing. Very few of them are ready to buy a guide about it today. The gap between those two groups is the mistake.

The mistake is skipping validation. Building a product based on demand research alone is a bet. You are betting that the people who are interested are also ready to pay. Sometimes that bet pays off. More often it doesn't, and you find out after spending twenty hours building something nobody buys.

The fix is validating before building. And the place to validate is not a survey or a focus group. It is the sold listings on eBay, the review count velocity on Gumroad, and the question threads on Reddit.

Sold listings tell you what people paid money for, not what they said they'd pay money for. If a product has 200 sold copies in the last 90 days on Gumroad, that is not a guess. That is evidence.

Review velocity tells you if a product is still selling or coasting. A product with 400 reviews and 2 reviews in the last month is not a good market signal. A product with 80 reviews and 30 in the last month is.

Reddit threads tell you the language people use when they are in pain. If you find three threads in a subreddit asking the same question about the same problem, the people writing those posts are buyers. They are actively looking for a solution. A product that directly answers that question will sell.

This is the logic behind the Niche Sniper. It runs this exact research process automatically. It pulls sold listing data, tracks review velocity on digital product marketplaces, and flags Reddit threads with buying intent language. The output is a scored list of product opportunities, ranked by evidence of demand.

The difference between the Niche Sniper approach and the standard approach is that you are selling before you build. You confirm buyers exist before you spend a single hour creating something.

Building a product without this validation is the equivalent of buying inventory before you know if anyone wants it. It feels like progress. It is not.

Start with evidence. Build after.

## Issue 52: What I learned from giving a product away for free

Pricing a product at zero is a strategy, not a failure.

Most people treat free products as a necessary evil. Something you give away to build a list and then forget about. A tactic. The Study Buddy went on Gumroad at $0 deliberately, and what happened next changed how I think about the entire catalog.

The free product exists to do one thing: remove the barrier to the first transaction. Not a price barrier. A trust barrier.

When someone buys their first product from you, even if it costs nothing, they become a different kind of person in relation to your work. They have made a decision. They have given you an email address and a few minutes of their attention. That transaction, even at zero dollars, is more meaningful than a follow or a like.

The Study Buddy is a structured note-taking system for students. It is simple. It is useful. It is free. The people who download it are students or people who work like students: they are learning something, they are trying to retain it, and they are looking for a system.

That profile is almost identical to the buyer profile for the Solopreneur OS, the AI Employee Guide, and the ADHD Finance template. They are all products for people who want to learn and build more effectively.

What the free product does is introduce those people to the catalog at no cost and no risk. They get value. They form a relationship with the brand. Then when they see a $9.99 or $14.99 product that solves the next problem, the decision is easier. They already know you deliver.

The data supported this. In the first month, seventy percent of the people who downloaded Study Buddy also clicked through to the main store. Eighteen percent of those purchased something else within two weeks. Without the free product acting as an entry point, those buyers would not have found the store.

The implication is that a free product is not a giveaway. It is the cheapest customer acquisition channel in the catalog.

The strategy is to make the free product genuinely useful, make sure it targets the same audience as your paid products, and make the path from free to paid obvious. One link, one recommendation, one follow-up email.

Give something away that you are proud of. The return is not immediate. It compounds.

## Issue 53: The three stages of a solopreneur

Every solopreneur is in one of three stages. Your current stage determines what you should focus on. Focusing on the wrong thing for your stage is why most people feel busy but do not move forward.

Stage one is production. You are building. Creating products, writing content, setting up systems, learning the tools. Most people start here and stay here too long. Production feels like progress because it is active. But a product nobody sees is not a business. It is a hobby with extra steps.

The exit condition for stage one is one thing: something exists that can be sold. A single product, a single service, a single offer. Not perfect. Done.

Stage two is distribution. You are putting the work in front of people. Posting consistently, building an audience, learning which content brings in buyers, testing offers. This stage is uncomfortable because it requires showing up in public with something imperfect. Most people skip this stage by going back to stage one. They improve the product instead of distributing it.

Distribution does not mean posting more. It means building a channel that reliably brings people to your work. That could be Instagram, a newsletter, YouTube, Twitter, a community. It does not matter which channel. It matters that you commit to one and run it consistently until it compounds.

The exit condition for stage two is a reliable source of attention. People are regularly discovering your work without you actively prompting each one.

Stage three is leverage. You are building systems that produce stage one and stage two outputs without requiring your direct time on every piece. Templates, automations, batched content, products that sell without ongoing involvement. Leverage means your output exceeds your hours.

Most people want stage three results while operating in stage one. That is the frustration. The system feels broken but the system is not broken. The sequence is just wrong.

Where are you right now? One clear answer to that question is worth more than a week of reading about productivity.

If you are in stage one: finish and publish. The quality will increase with the next product.
If you are in stage two: post until it works. Volume precedes consistency, consistency precedes compounding.
If you are in stage three: protect the systems. The value is in the infrastructure, not the individual piece.

The stage you are in is the stage you should be focused on entirely. Not the next one.

## Issue 54: Why the catalog beats the flagship

One product is a bet. Twenty-five products is a portfolio.

The advice most people hear when they start selling online is to focus on one thing. One flagship product. One audience. One offer. This advice is not wrong for the first product. It is wrong as a strategy past the first year.

The catalog model wins for four reasons.

First: pricing flexibility. With one product, you have one price point. You can discount it or raise it but you have one lever. With a catalog, you can test $4.99 entry points and $99 premium tiers simultaneously. You learn what different segments of your audience will pay. That information shapes every future decision.

Second: risk distribution. If your flagship product stops selling, your revenue stops. If one of twenty-five products stops selling, you have twenty-four others. The catalog does not eliminate risk. It spreads it across more positions.

Third: upsell paths. A $4.99 product and a $99 product aimed at the same audience create a natural path. Someone buys the cheap one, gets value, and sees the expensive one as the obvious next step. That path does not exist with a single product.

Fourth: audience segmentation. Different products attract different buyers. The Roblox Builder's Guide attracts a different person than the AI Employee Guide. Both of those people are in the catalog. Both can find the product for them. A flagship by definition excludes everyone who is not the ideal buyer for that one product.

The objection to the catalog model is that it takes more time to build. That is true for the first six months. After that, the systems compress the build time dramatically. The tenth product takes a fraction of the time of the first because the templates, the covers, the listing formula, and the delivery infrastructure already exist.

Build the first product to learn the process. Build the second to refine it. By the fifth, the process is efficient enough that the catalog becomes a compounding asset rather than a growing workload.

The flagship model is a simpler story to tell. The catalog model is a more resilient business.

## Issue 55: The Ceiba protocol

The most useful thing I built this year was not a product. It was a file.

The file is called primer.md. It is a plain text document that lives at the root of my project folder. It is rewritten at the end of every work session. It contains four things: what I am currently building, what just got done, what the next step is, and what is blocked.

Before I open a single project file, I read primer.md. Before I start any work, I know exactly where I am.

This sounds simple because it is. But the effect is not simple.

The problem with any creative or technical project is not the work itself. It is the cost of reorientation. Every time you sit down to work, you have to reconstruct the state of the project from whatever signals are available. Files, notes, the last thing you wrote, a half-finished task. That reconstruction takes ten to thirty minutes. Then you start working. Then you stop and go do something else. Then you come back and do it again.

The primer eliminates most of that cost. The reorientation is already done. You read the file, you know the state, you start working.

But the deeper value is not the time saved. The deeper value is that the primer forces a close at the end of every session. You cannot leave without writing the current state. That close is also a compression. You take everything that happened in the session and reduce it to a few sentences. That compression is how you know what actually matters.

Over time, the primer becomes a record. Not a journal. A functional record. Each version tells you the state of the project at that moment. When something breaks, you can trace back to when it worked. When something works, you can find the moment it started.

The system I use around the primer is what I call the Ceiba protocol: start every session by reading context, end every session by writing context. Context beats memory because memory is unreliable and context is on disk.

You do not need a complex system. You need a file, a consistent location, and the discipline to write it before you close the laptop.

Start with one sentence: what are you building right now, and what is the next step.
