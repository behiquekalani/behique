## Batch 26 / Script 2: Why Your eBay Listings Are Getting Buried
**Duration:** 50 sec
**Hook (0-3 sec):** Your listing is on page 6. Mine is on page 1. Here's the only difference.
**Body:** eBay's Cassini algorithm ranks listings based on sell-through rate. If your item has been sitting for 30 days with no watchers, it gets pushed down. Fix it by ending the listing and relisting it. Fresh listings get a visibility boost. Also, your title is probably using the wrong keywords. Go to sold listings and copy the exact phrasing buyers are already using. Not what you think sounds good. What buyers actually search. Third, free shipping moves you up. Build it into the price. $18 + free shipping beats $12 + $6 shipping every single time in search.
**CTA:** Check your oldest unsold listing today. End it, relist it with better keywords. Report back.

## Batch 26 / Script 4: How I Research a Product in 90 Seconds
**Duration:** 35 sec
**Hook (0-3 sec):** I spend 90 seconds researching before I buy anything for resale. Here's the exact process.
**Body:** Open eBay. Search the item name. Filter by Sold Items. Look at the last 30 days. I need at least 8 sales at my target price. If it's selling but slowly, I pass. If it's selling fast and the buy price is under 30% of the sold price, I buy. That's it. No spreadsheets. No tools. Just eBay's own data. The mistake most people make is checking active listings, not sold listings. Active listings tell you what people are asking. Sold listings tell you what people actually paid.
**CTA:** Practice this with one item today. Pick anything in your house.

## Batch 26 / Script 6: eBay Fees Explained Simply
**Duration:** 40 sec
**Hook (0-3 sec):** I lost $200 my first month on eBay because I didn't understand the fee structure.
**Body:** eBay takes roughly 13% of the final sale price including shipping. That's the final value fee. Then PayPal or Stripe takes another 2.9% plus 30 cents. So on a $30 sale, you're losing about $4.80 to fees before you factor in shipping cost. Build your price by working backwards. Target profit divided by 0.85. That gives you your minimum listing price. If I want $20 net, I list at minimum $23.50 and factor shipping separately or roll it in. Most new sellers set a price they like and then get surprised by the cut. Do the math first.
**CTA:** Pick one item you sold recently and calculate your actual margin.

## Batch 26 / Script 7: The Bundle Strategy That Doubled My Average Sale Price
**Duration:** 50 sec
**Hook (0-3 sec):** I stopped selling single items and my average sale price doubled. Here's what changed.
**Body:** Bundling. Instead of listing one Lego set for $12, I bought three similar sets for a total of $9 and listed them as a bundle for $38. The buyer gets perceived value. That "Lot" keyword pulls in a completely different buyer pool than a single item listing.
**CTA:** Find 3 similar items you already have and test one bundle listing this week.

## Batch 26 / Script 9: How to Write an eBay Title That Actually Converts
**Duration:** 40 sec
**Hook (0-3 sec):** Your title has 80 characters. Most sellers waste 40 of them. Here's how to use all 80.
**Body:** eBay titles are search fields, not sentences. Don't write "Beautiful vintage blue ceramic mug, perfect gift." Write "Vintage Blue Ceramic Mug 1970s Studio Pottery Handmade Coffee Tea Cup." Every word should be a search term someone would type. Include: item type, material, color, decade/era, style, and use case. Skip adjectives like beautiful, lovely, amazing. Those are not searchable. Fill the character limit. More keywords equal more search appearances. I run my draft title through eBay search itself. If my listing doesn't show up, I revise the title until it does.
**CTA:** Take one active listing and rewrite the title using only searchable keywords.

## Batch 30 / Script 3: The Expected Value Framework I Use for Decisions
**Duration:** 50 sec
**Hook (0-3 sec):** I stopped making decisions based on feelings. I use this framework instead. It takes 3 minutes.
**Body:** Expected value. Probability of a good outcome times the upside, minus probability of a bad outcome times the downside. Example: I'm considering spending $200 to source products for a new eBay category. I think there's a 60% chance I make at least $400 back in 30 days. 40% chance I sell half and break even. Expected value: 0.6 times $200 profit plus 0.4 times $0 equals $120. The expected value is positive. I take the bet. This is not a guarantee. But over many decisions made with positive expected value, you come out ahead. Prediction market traders use this exact logic on every position they take.
**CTA:** Apply EV math to one decision you're sitting on. What does the math say?

## Batch 30 / Script 6: The Calibration Practice That Made Me a Better Thinker
