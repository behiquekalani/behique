**Script 1**
**Title:** The content system that runs while you're not working
**Hook (0-3s):** I produce content five days a week. I work on content two hours a week. Here's the system.
**Body (3-45s):**
This is the full flow.

Sunday, ninety minutes: I open my ideas file, pick five concepts I want to explore, record five voice notes of two to three minutes each. Raw, unscripted, just thinking out loud.

Those go into an automated workflow. By Monday morning I have five transcribed drafts, already formatted per platform, sitting in a Notion content calendar.

Monday, thirty minutes: I review and edit each one. Not rewrite. Edit. Light passes. Then I schedule them.

The content goes out Tuesday through Saturday automatically via Buffer. No manual posting.

The system means my best thinking happens on Sunday when I have energy. The mechanical work is either automated or batched. The actual content creation is less than two hours.

The mistake most creators make is spreading the work across the week. That means context-switching every day. Batching eliminates that cost.

**Script 2**
**Title:** How to turn one idea into a week of content
**Hook (0-3s):** One idea, properly broken down, gives you seven days of content.
**Body (3-45s):**
The mistake: treating every piece of content as a separate idea you need to generate. That's exhausting and unsustainable.

The fix: one core idea, repurposed into every format your audience uses.

Here's what one idea becomes:

Day 1 (Monday): long Twitter thread, full argument, ten to fifteen tweets.
Day 2: short TikTok or Reel, the hook line from the thread, thirty-second version.
Day 3: Instagram carousel, five to seven slides, visual breakdown of the points.
Day 4: email newsletter, long-form expansion with more personal context.
Day 5: LinkedIn post, professional angle on the same idea.
Day 6: short blog post or Substack note for SEO.
Day 7: a response post addressing the best comment from Monday's thread.

Same idea. Seven formats. Seven days. You only had to think deeply once.

This is how one-person content operations scale without burning out.

**Script 3**
**Title:** Why your content feels inconsistent and how to fix it permanently
**Hook (0-3s):** Your content is inconsistent because you have a mood problem, not a creativity problem.
**Body (3-45s):**
When you create based on how you feel on any given day, you get inconsistent output. Some days you have energy and make something sharp. Other days you're tired and you skip, or you post something that doesn't represent you well.

This is normal. The solution isn't to feel consistently inspired. The solution is to decouple creation from mood.

You do this with a content vault.

Every time you have a good idea, a strong opinion, a moment of clarity, you capture it immediately. Into a running list. No effort to develop it. Just capture.

Then once a week, you open the vault and produce. You're not thinking of ideas in that session. You're pulling from a pre-built inventory. The thinking already happened in scattered moments throughout the week.

The vault means your best thinking from thirty different moments gets concentrated into one production session. Your output quality rises because you're always working from your peaks, not your averages.

**Script 4**
**Title:** The content calendar that actually gets used
**Hook (0-3s):** Most content calendars get abandoned in week three. This one doesn't.
**Body (3-45s):**
The reason content calendars fail: they're built for the optimistic version of you. The one with unlimited time and energy who will plan content three months in advance with color-coded themes.

That's not how solo creators actually work. Here's a calendar that works for how you actually are.

It has three columns: ideas queue, production this week, scheduled and done.

Ideas queue is a running list. No pressure, no dates, just capture. Feeds the other columns.

Production this week has a maximum of five items. Not twenty. Five. One per day. Achievable by a human.

Scheduled and done is your evidence file. You see the output accumulating. That visibility creates momentum.

That's it. Three columns. Weekly reset. No elaborate planning, no color coding, no project management app required unless you want one.

Simple systems survive. Complex ones don't.

**Script 5**
**Title:** How to never run out of content ideas
**Hook (0-3s):** You will never run out of content ideas once you understand where they actually come from.
**Body (3-45s):**
Ideas don't come from staring at a blank page. They come from input and collision.

Input: consuming things deliberately. Books, conversations, Twitter arguments, client questions. Every one of these is a potential idea seed.

Collision: when two unrelated inputs combine into a new perspective. That's an idea. The work is noticing the collision and capturing it before it disappears.

The practice that makes this reliable: carry a capture tool everywhere. Phone notes, voice memos, physical notebook, whatever has the least friction for you. The tool doesn't matter. The capture habit does.

The goal isn't to think up ideas on demand. It's to accumulate them passively throughout your normal life, then mine the vault when you need content.

Most creators think ideas come from inspiration. They actually come from collection. Start collecting and inspiration becomes a surplus, not a scarcity.
