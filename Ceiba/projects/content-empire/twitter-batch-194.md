# Twitter Batch 194 — The Problem With Chasing Trends

---

## Standalone Tweet 1
Chasing trends is not a strategy. It's a reaction.

Strategies are built around what you believe. Reactions are built around what you fear missing.

The fear-driven creator is always three steps behind.

Every creator who blew up chasing a trend had to find a new trend immediately after.

That's not a business. That's a treadmill.

---

## Standalone Tweet 3
The paradox of trend-chasing: the people who benefit most from trends are the ones who were already known for something else.

You need a stable platform to ride a wave.

Without one, the wave just takes you underwater.

## Standalone Tweet 4

## Thread 1: Why trend-chasing is a losing strategy for solopreneurs

**Tweet 1:**
Every few months, a new content format, a new platform, a new AI tool captures everyone's attention.

And a wave of creators pivots their entire strategy to chase it.

Here's why that almost always fails.

**Tweet 2:**
When a trend breaks, the people who benefit most are the ones who were already positioned.

MrBeast didn't blow up because he chased the right trend.

He already had a format, an identity, and an audience. He surfed the wave with existing momentum.

**Tweet 3:**
For everyone else, chasing the trend means starting from zero each time.

Zero context. Zero trust. No compounding.

You're competing with people who were there first and with people who have more resources.

**Tweet 4:**
Trend-dependent content also trains your audience to expect novelty, not insight.

They follow you for what's new, not for what you know.

That's a fragile relationship. The moment you slow down or the trend passes, they leave.

**Tweet 5:**
The cost is not just time.

Every trend you chase is a trend you're not building something real during.

Opportunity cost compounds in both directions.

**Tweet 6:**
The question to ask before chasing any trend:

"If this trend ends in 60 days, what have I built that lasts?"

If the answer is nothing, you spent 60 days running in place.

**Tweet 7:**
Trend awareness is useful.

Trend dependency is a trap.

Know the difference before you decide what to post tomorrow.

---

## Thread 2: What trend-chasing does to your positioning

**Tweet 1:**
Positioning is what comes to mind when people think of you.

Trend-chasing makes that impossible.

You can't be known for something if you're always switching to something new.

**Tweet 2:**
Strong positioning requires repetition over time.

One creator writes about deep work every week for a year.

Another writes about whatever is trending that week.

After 12 months: one is known for something specific, one is vaguely familiar.

**Tweet 3:**
The audience recognizes patterns.

If your content has no consistent thread, the pattern they recognize is "this person doesn't know what they're about."

That's not a trust signal.

**Tweet 4:**
When you stay focused on a specific domain, something interesting happens.

You become the person people tag when someone asks about that topic.

That referral network is invisible until you have it. And you only get it through consistency.

**Tweet 5:**
Chasing trends costs you that referral network.

Every pivot resets the pattern recognition process.

You're not building on prior work. You're starting over.

**Tweet 6:**
The fix is not to ignore trends.

The fix is to filter trends through your existing positioning.

"Does this trend relate to what I'm already known for? Can I cover it from my perspective without abandoning my thread?"

If yes, cover it. If not, skip it.

## Thread 3: The psychological cost of trend-chasing

**Tweet 1:**
Trend-chasing is exhausting.

Not physically. Cognitively.

You're constantly scanning, comparing, deciding, pivoting.

That's energy that could go into building something.

**Tweet 2:**
The creator who always chases trends is also always comparing.

"Did that post do as well as theirs?"

"Am I growing fast enough?"

"Should I be on that platform too?"

The comparison is endless because the reference point keeps changing.

**Tweet 3:**
Stability in your content strategy creates stability in your psychology.

When you know what you're building and why, you can ignore most of what's happening around you.

That clarity is itself an advantage.

**Tweet 4:**
Most burned-out creators burned out chasing volume and trends, not doing deep work.

Depth is sustainable.

Trend-chasing at volume is not.

**Tweet 5:**
The feeling of "I should be doing more" is almost always triggered by comparison to trend-chasers.

But you're not comparing equally.

You're seeing their output without seeing their exhaustion, their lack of direction, or their inability to convert any of it into something real.

## Thread 4: How to use trends without being controlled by them

**Tweet 1:**
This is not an anti-trend argument.

Trends are useful. They bring attention and context.

The question is how you relate to them.

**Tweet 2:**
Rule 1: Only cover a trend if you have an actual perspective on it.

Not "here's what's happening."

"Here's what this reveals about the underlying pattern I've been writing about for a year."

**Tweet 3:**
Rule 2: Stay in your lane.

If a trend is happening in a space you don't operate in, you don't need to weigh in.

Silence is not a content failure. Irrelevant content is.

**Tweet 4:**
Rule 3: Use the trend to illustrate a timeless principle.

"The rise of [trend X] is just another iteration of [principle I've been talking about]."

That framing makes you look thoughtful instead of reactive.

**Tweet 5:**
Rule 4: Never pivot your identity for a trend.

Covering a trend is fine.

Becoming a "trend content" creator to chase growth is identity destruction for short-term metrics.

**Tweet 6:**
The test: does covering this trend move you closer to what you want to be known for, or further?

Answer honestly. Then decide.

---

## Thread 5: What happens when you stop chasing trends

**Tweet 1:**
When you stop trying to keep up with everything, something uncomfortable happens first.

Your growth slows.

Trend content gets clicks. Focused content builds trust. Trust is slower.

**Tweet 2:**
Then something interesting happens.

The people who stayed start to trust you more.

Your engagement quality goes up even if your engagement volume goes down.

**Tweet 3:**
Then the referrals start.

People who find value in your specific work send others who have the same specific problem.

Those people are pre-qualified. They already trust you before they follow you.

**Tweet 4:**
Then the search traffic starts compounding.

Specific, consistent content about one topic becomes findable.

You don't need to promote it every week. It finds people.

**Tweet 5:**
The creator who focused for 2 years on one thing ends up with a business.

The creator who chased trends for 2 years ends up with a highlight reel and no customers.

Slow and specific wins. Not immediately. But permanently.
