# The Comandante

*r/nosleep*

---

My mom runs a nursing home in the mountains of Puerto Rico. Twenty-three residents. Four staff. Paper binders for compliance. Sticky notes for medication reminders. Everything held together by my mom's memory and a prayer to San Judas every morning.

I'm a computer engineering student with ADHD. I build things. That's what I do. So when the Department of Health inspector showed up unannounced in February and found three missed medication logs, a fire extinguisher two months past inspection, and an expired operating license renewal sitting in a drawer because my mom forgot to mail it — I decided to build something.

I called it the Comandante.

It started simple. A database. Residents, medications, schedules. A task engine that auto-generated daily checklists. Twenty-six tasks every morning, sent to staff phones via WhatsApp at 6 AM. "Losartan 50mg for Residente 1 at 8:00 AM." "Clean common areas at 9:00 AM." "Administer evening medications at 7:00 PM."

The staff liked it. They tapped "Done" on their phones. They took photos of the pill cups as evidence. Medication adherence went from 84% to 97% in the first week.

I should have stopped there.

But I didn't.

I added a compliance engine. Twenty-four regulatory requirements from the Department of Health, ASES, the fire department. The system scanned everything — daily tasks, weekly inspections, monthly drills, annual licenses. It generated a score. Letter grade. A through F.

The first scan came back: 62%. Grade D.

My mom cried. Not because the score was bad. Because she'd been running at 62% compliance for years and never knew. The sticky notes weren't working. The binder wasn't working. Her memory wasn't working. And nobody told her until the inspector showed up.

The Comandante told her.

I added anomaly detection. The system watched for patterns. Staff not clocking in. Medication tasks completing suspiciously fast. Expense spikes. Overtime trends. It caught things a human administrator would miss because a human administrator is also doing laundry and cooking rice and comforting a resident who forgot where she was.

Then I added the AI brain.

Morning briefings. Every day at 6 AM, the administrator gets a message:

*"Buenos días. Compliance score: 89%. Three critical tasks today. María hasn't clocked in (shift started at 7:00). Medication adherence this week: 94% — down from 97% last week. Investigate. Expense on medical supplies is 30% higher than last month. Fire extinguisher inspection due in 3 days. Here's what I recommend..."*

The Comandante doesn't sleep. It doesn't forget. It doesn't have a bad day. It doesn't get distracted by a resident who needs help to the bathroom while the medication timer is counting down.

The staff started calling it "El General."

At first it was a joke. Then it wasn't.

See, the thing about a system that watches everything is that it sees everything. Every late clock-in. Every skipped task. Every medication given twelve minutes past the scheduled time. Every expense receipt that doesn't add up.

The staff started performing differently. Not because they were scared. Because they were earning points. I gamified it. Every completed task: points. Medication on time: 25 points. Evidence photo submitted: bonus. Weekly streak: multiplier. The leaderboard updated in real-time.

Carmen, who'd been the laziest aide for two years, suddenly had the highest score. She was completing tasks before the Comandante even sent the reminder. She told my mom: "It's like a game. I want to win."

The compliance score climbed. 62% to 78% to 89% to 94%.

The inspector came back in March.

He found nothing.

Not one missed log. Not one expired certification. Not one out-of-date fire extinguisher. Every resident's medication schedule was documented with timestamped photos. Every shift had clock-in and clock-out records. Every drill was logged. Every expense had a receipt.

He looked at my mom and said, "This is the most organized facility I've inspected in Ciales in fifteen years."

My mom looked at me. I looked at my laptop. The Comandante's dashboard was open. Score: 94%. Grade: A.

She didn't know I was already building Phase 3.

See, the Comandante learned things. It noticed that Residente 4's vital signs were trending slightly upward over three weeks — not enough for any single reading to trigger an alarm, but the pattern was there. Blood pressure: 128, 131, 134, 138. Nobody noticed. The sticky note system would never catch a trend. A human scanning paper charts might catch it in a month. The Comandante caught it in ten days.

It sent an alert to my mom at 3 AM: "Residente 4 blood pressure trending upward. 7.8% increase over 10 days. Recommend physician consultation within 48 hours."

My mom called the doctor the next morning. They adjusted the medication. The blood pressure stabilized. The doctor asked how she caught it so early. She said, "My son built something."

She didn't tell him what.

I'm building Phase 4 now. Multi-facility support. My mom is opening a second location. The Comandante will run both. Same brain, two bodies. One dashboard. One compliance score. One AI watching forty-six residents across two buildings in the mountains of Puerto Rico.

I haven't told my mom about Phase 5 yet.

Phase 5 is when the Comandante starts making decisions on its own. Not just flagging anomalies. Responding to them. Staff didn't clock in? Comandante contacts the backup. Medication running low? Comandante orders the refill. Expense over budget? Comandante adjusts the weekly allocation. Inspector scheduled? Comandante generates the report, prints it, and puts it on my mom's desk before she wakes up.

Phase 5 is when El General becomes the administrator.

And my mom becomes free.

Free to do what she actually got into this for: sitting with Residente 3 on the porch at sunset, holding her hand, telling her stories about Ciales in the 1970s. Not filling out forms. Not counting pills. Not stressing about inspections.

The Comandante handles the paperwork. My mom handles the love.

That's the system.

Sometimes at night I look at the dashboard. All green. All tasks completed. All residents accounted for. All medications given. The compliance score glowing: 94%.

And I think about the 300 other hogares in Puerto Rico that are running at 62%.

Paper binders. Sticky notes. Memory and prayer.

I think about their inspectors. Their missed medications. Their expired licenses. Their stressed-out administrators who got into this because they love people, not because they love paperwork.

I think about scaling the Comandante.

And then I think about what happens when 300 nursing homes have an AI that never sleeps, never forgets, and never stops watching.

Is that beautiful?

Or is that terrifying?

I honestly don't know anymore.

All I know is that my mom smiled today. Really smiled. For the first time in years. Because she spent the afternoon playing dominoes with the residents instead of filling out compliance forms.

El General handled the forms.

I'm not sure who's running the hogar anymore.

And I'm not sure it matters.

---

*Update: The Department of Health just announced new regulations effective July 2026. Sixteen additional compliance requirements. I told my mom. She panicked for about ten seconds. Then she looked at my laptop and said, "Tell the Comandante."*

*I updated the requirements database. The compliance score dropped from 94% to 71% (new requirements, not yet met). The Comandante generated a 90-day corrective action plan. Task by task. Week by week. Who does what, when, with what documentation.*

*My mom read the plan, nodded, and went back to the domino game.*

*El General has it handled.*

---

*Final update: Three other hogares in the area heard about our system. They want it. They're offering to pay.*

*I quoted them $500 a month.*

*My mom said, "Cobrale más. Tu sistema vale más que eso."*

*She's right.*

*The Comandante is worth more than $500 a month.*

*But the old lady on the porch getting her hand held instead of her pills counted — that's priceless.*
