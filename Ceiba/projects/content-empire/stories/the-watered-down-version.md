# The Watered-Down Version

*r/conspiracy meets r/nosleep*

---

In 2009, a music video came out that shouldn't have been possible.

The animation was too fluid. The style transitions were too seamless. The way the visuals matched the emotional arc of the song — frame by frame, beat by beat — felt like something a human team couldn't coordinate in the timeline they had.

The label credited "a team of forty animators working for eight months."

I believed that for fifteen years.

I don't anymore.

---

I work in machine learning. Not at a big company. I'm independent. I train models on consumer hardware — a Mac, a Linux box with a gaming GPU, nothing fancy. In 2026, I can generate a 30-second animation clip in about four hours using open-source tools. The quality is decent. Not Hollywood. But decent.

Here's what keeps me up at night: the gap.

The gap between what's publicly available and what's privately funded.

In AI, the public timeline goes like this:
- 2014: GANs invented (Ian Goodfellow, academic paper)
- 2017: Transformers invented (Google, academic paper)
- 2020: GPT-3 (OpenAI, limited API access)
- 2022: Stable Diffusion (open source image generation)
- 2023: ChatGPT goes mainstream
- 2024: Video generation (Sora, Runway, limited access)
- 2025: Real-time AI video, AI music, AI everything

That's the PUBLIC timeline.

Now here's what I know about DARPA, In-Q-Tel, and private defense research:

They operate 10-20 years ahead of public technology. This isn't conspiracy. This is documented. The internet existed in ARPANET form in 1969. The public got it in 1995. GPS was military-only for decades before your phone had it. Stealth aircraft flew classified for years before anyone knew they existed.

So if transformers were "invented" in 2017, when were they actually invented?

If GANs were "published" in 2014, when did someone with a $100 million budget first generate an image from text?

If I can generate animation on a $2,000 computer in 2026, what could someone generate on a $200 million compute cluster in 2006?

---

Let me tell you about a music video from 2008.

The artist was A-list. The label was one of the Big Three. The video had a "CGI" sequence that lasted forty-five seconds. In it, the artist's face morphed through dozens of styles — oil painting, watercolor, pixel art, photorealistic, back to real footage. Seamlessly. In 2008.

The VFX studio credited had twelve employees.

I looked them up recently. The studio dissolved in 2011. No portfolio. No website archive. No LinkedIn profiles for the founders. Twelve people who apparently created one of the most visually innovative sequences of the decade, then vanished.

I've worked with VFX artists. Twelve people cannot produce forty-five seconds of style-transfer animation in 2008 using the publicly available tools of 2008. After Effects couldn't do it. Maya couldn't do it. Nothing could do it.

Unless they had something we didn't.

---

In 2012, a different artist released a video where virtual dancers performed choreography that was described as "motion captured and then stylized." The movement was too good. Not uncanny-valley good. Actually good. The kind of good that in 2026 we achieve by training a model on thousands of hours of dance footage and then generating new sequences.

In 2012, publicly available motion capture required a studio full of infrared cameras and a person in a ping-pong-ball suit. The output was robotic. Wooden. Obviously captured.

This video's movement was organic. Creative. It improvised. The dancers did things no choreographer planned because — I believe — no choreographer was involved. A system generated movement that looked human because it had learned from humans. Thousands of hours of humans.

The credits listed a "proprietary motion system developed in-house."

In-house where? By whom? With what training data?

---

Here's the theory. I can't prove it. But the math works.

In 2004-2006, a small number of entities — defense contractors, intelligence agencies, and the entertainment divisions of companies with deep government ties — had access to early neural network architectures that could generate visual content. Not well. Not in real-time. But given enough compute and enough time, they could produce sequences that would take human artists months.

The entertainment industry was the perfect cover. Nobody questions why a music video looks innovative. Innovation is expected. "Cutting-edge CGI" is a phrase that explains anything. You don't need to explain the tool. You just credit the VFX studio, make sure the VFX studio can't be investigated, and move on.

The artists didn't know. Most of them. They were shown the final product by their label. "We used a new CGI technique." The artist says "cool" and posts it. They don't ask what software. They don't visit the render farm. They don't meet the twelve people at the VFX studio that doesn't exist anymore.

The ones who did know — the ones who were in the room — signed NDAs that make a SEAL's clearance look casual.

---

Now let's talk about 2026.

I can generate images with Stable Diffusion. I can generate video with open-source models. I can generate music with AI. I can clone a voice with 30 seconds of audio.

This is the watered-down version.

What do I mean by watered-down?

Every open-source AI model is a distillation of something larger. Llama is a distillation of Meta's internal research. Stable Diffusion was trained on a subset of what LAION had, which was a subset of what was available. Whisper is a distillation of OpenAI's internal speech model.

We get the version they're comfortable releasing. The version that's "good enough" to create a market but not good enough to threaten anything.

The version I have can generate a face that looks almost real.

The version they have generates a face that IS real. To any observer. Under any scrutiny. In real-time. In 2026.

But that's the 2026 version of what they have.

What did they have in 2006? Something that could generate a forty-five-second style-transfer sequence and credit it to twelve animators who never existed.

What do they have now that we'll get in 2046?

I don't want to think about that.

---

Last week I was generating images for a product cover. My model produced a face. Not a real person. A generated face. But it had this quality — this warmth behind the eyes — that made me stop.

I've seen that warmth before. In a music video from 2010. A close-up of a dancer's face during a slow-motion sequence that the director said was "practical effects with digital enhancement."

The warmth was the same.

Not similar. The same.

Like they were produced by the same architecture. Fifteen years apart. One in a classified lab. One on my laptop.

The watered-down version.

---

I'm not saying Katy Perry had AI in 2009.

I'm saying someone had AI in 2009. And the easiest place to hide its output is in plain sight. In a music video. In a movie. In a commercial. In entertainment.

Because nobody audits entertainment for technical impossibilities.

Nobody asks "how did they actually make that?"

We just watch it and say "wow, great CGI."

---

*How far are you from the truth?*

The 10-20 year military-to-civilian gap is documented fact. DARPA's AI funding in the early 2000s is public record. The specific claim about music videos is unprovable — which is exactly how it would be designed. The most effective secret isn't one you hide. It's one you show everyone and label something else.

You're not wrong about the gap. You're not wrong that what we have is a watered-down version. The question isn't whether the gap exists. It's how wide it is.

And that — nobody outside the room knows.
