**Script 3**
**Title:** What I use instead of a project manager
**Hook (0-3s):** I run multiple projects simultaneously with no project manager and no chaos. Here's how.
**Body (3-45s):**
The tool: Notion. The setup: simple.

Each project has one page. That page has: the goal, the current status, the next three actions, and a log of decisions made.

That's it. No Gantt charts. No sprint planning ceremonies. No status update meetings with yourself.

The daily practice: every morning, open each project page, read the next three actions, pick one to do first. Update the status before you close the tab.

The weekly practice: every Sunday, review all project pages, update next actions based on what actually happened, note any blockers.

This takes twenty minutes daily and forty minutes weekly. It keeps everything moving without needing a person whose job is to track other people's work.

At the one-person level, the tool isn't the problem. The habit of updating it is. Make the updates take less than two minutes and you'll actually do them.

**Script 4**
**Title:** The tool that replaced a customer service rep for my products
**Hook (0-3s):** Zero customer service hires. Every buyer gets a response within minutes. Here's the setup.
**Body (3-45s):**
The most common support tickets for a digital product business are predictable. Didn't receive the product. File won't open. Refund request. Question about whether the product covers X.

Because they're predictable, they're automatable.

Setup: tag every support email by type in Gmail using filters. Each tag has a template response attached. For Gumroad products, the delivery confirmation is automatic and includes a troubleshooting FAQ that answers the top five questions before they're asked.

The result: the buyer gets a response in under two minutes. The response is accurate and warm. I never saw the ticket.

The only emails that require my attention are the ones outside the template set. About one in twenty. I handle those once a day in a ten-minute batch.

This isn't about being unavailable. It's about routing routine work to systems and reserving your judgment for decisions that actually need it.

**Script 5**
**Title:** The finance tool that replaced a bookkeeper (at first)
**Hook (0-3s):** You don't need a bookkeeper in year one. You need this instead.
**Body (3-45s):**
Early stage solo business finance comes down to three things: knowing what came in, knowing what went out, and knowing what you owe at tax time. That's it.

For that, you don't need a $300 a month bookkeeper. You need a spreadsheet and a habit.

One Google Sheet. Four columns: date, description, amount, category. Every transaction, categorized simply: income, tools, marketing, education, other.

Monthly review: total each category. Know your margin. Note any unexpected expenses.

Tax prep: at end of year, filter by category. Your accountant has everything they need. No scrambling, no missed deductions.

When this becomes too time-consuming because the volume is high, move to Wave or QuickBooks Simple. Both under $30 a month. Still not a bookkeeper.

Hire the bookkeeper when your complexity actually justifies it, not before. Most solo operators don't reach that point until year two or three.
