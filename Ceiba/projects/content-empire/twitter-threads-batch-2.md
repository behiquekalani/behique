## THREAD 2: The Prompt That Makes AI Actually Useful
**Account:** @behikeai
**Hook:** "Most AI prompts are requests. The ones that work are contracts."

---

Most AI prompts are requests.

The ones that work are contracts.

Here's the difference.

---

A request: "Write me a product description."

A contract: "You are a direct-response copywriter. Write a 150-word product description for [product]. Use PAS format: pain in sentence 1-2, agitate in 3-4, solve in 5-7. No fluff. No exclamation marks. End with a clear next step."

Same tool. Different output quality.

---

The 4 elements of a contract prompt:

1. Role. Tell the AI who it is. "You are a tax accountant reviewing this for someone filing as self-employed in Puerto Rico."

2. Constraint. Limit the output. "Under 200 words." "In bullet points." "No jargon."

3. Format. Specify the structure. "PAS format." "Numbered list." "One sentence per point."

4. Negative space. Tell it what not to do. "No generic advice." "Don't use the word 'utilize.'" "Don't start with 'Certainly.'"

---

The test: if you gave the same prompt to three different AI tools and got three nearly identical responses, it's a good prompt.

If each tool gives a different answer with different quality, the prompt is doing too little work.

## THREAD 4: Why Most People Never Ship Anything
**Account:** @kalaniandrez
**Hook:** "The real reason most builders never ship. It's not what you think."

---

The real reason most builders never ship.

It's not laziness.

---

It's optimization loops.

You pick a product to build. You research it for three days. Then you find a better idea. Research that for two days. Find another. The loop repeats.

You have 12 half-researched ideas and zero shipped products.

---

What's actually happening: research feels like work.

Your brain releases the same "progress" feeling for reading about a thing as for building it. Cheaper effort, same reward.

So you optimize for research. It's easier. Lower risk of failure. No public judgment.

---

The cure is not "just start." That's useless advice.

The cure is a decision rule.

Mine: if I have spent more than 4 hours researching something and have not written a single line of content, I am in an optimization loop. I stop researching and build a minimum version in 24 hours.

---

Minimum version means:
- One product, described in 3 sentences
- Listed for sale somewhere
- Publicly available

Not perfect. Not ready. Available.

Ship it. Then improve it.
