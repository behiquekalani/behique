Most people use Claude like a search engine.

Shallow use:
"Write me a caption."
"Give me 10 ideas."
"Summarize this article."

You get generic output because you gave generic input.

**Slide 3:**
Deep use:
Give it context first. Your brand, your audience, your voice.
Give it constraints. Word count, tone, platform, goal.
Give it a role. "Act as a direct-response copywriter."

Now the output is actually useful.

The system prompt changes everything:
"You are a copywriter for @behikeai, a brand that teaches solopreneurs to build digital income. Our tone is direct, never hype, no exclamation marks. Write for people who are already working hard and need the practical next step."

That one paragraph changes every response.

What Claude can do that most people miss:
- Break down a complex business problem
- Review a strategy and poke holes in it
- Rewrite your copy in three different tones
- Build a content plan from a single idea
- Act as the skeptical customer before you launch

**Slide 6:**
The tool is only as good as how you use it.
@behikeai has prompt templates that actually work.

**Caption:**
The gap between people who call AI tools useless and people who call them indispensable is almost entirely in how they structure their prompts. Generic input gives you generic output. That is not a limitation of the tool. That is a limitation of the input.

The people getting real leverage from Claude are treating it like a skilled consultant who needs a proper brief before they can do useful work. They are providing context, role, constraints, and examples. They are iterating on the output instead of taking the first draft.

A well-configured Claude session with a solid system prompt is a different tool than the one most people open in a new tab and type a lazy question into. The former produces strategies, frameworks, and content that would have taken hours. The latter produces something you throw away.

## Post 3 — The 20-Minute Content Day (With AI)

**Hook:** You do not need four hours to produce a week of content.

**Slide 1:**
You do not need four hours to produce a week of content.
You need 20 minutes and the right system.

**Slide 2:**
Minute 1-5: Capture the idea
One voice memo. One raw thought. One core insight you want to share.
Do not write an outline. Just talk.

**Slide 3:**
Minute 6-10: Transcribe and expand
Paste the transcript into Claude with this prompt:
"Turn this raw idea into a structured Instagram carousel (5 slides) and a 200-word caption. Voice: direct, no hype, no exclamation marks. Brand: @behikeai."

Output in under 60 seconds.

**Slide 4:**
Minute 11-15: Repurpose
Same output, different prompt:
"Now turn this into a Twitter thread."
"Now write a 3-sentence LinkedIn post from this."
"Now give me a TikTok hook."

One idea. Four platforms. Still inside 15 minutes.

**Slide 5:**
Minute 16-20: Schedule
Paste into Buffer, Later, or Metricool.
Set your posting times. Done.

You just batched a full week of content in one 20-minute session.

**Slide 6:**
The system matters more than the effort.
@behikeai — build your content system once, run it every week.

**Caption:**
Content consistency is not about discipline. It is about systems. The people who post every day are not more motivated than you. They built a process that makes posting easy. Once the process exists, discipline barely matters.

The 20-minute content day works because it removes every friction point. You do not sit down to write. You talk. Talking is easier than writing for most people, especially when the idea is still forming. The AI does the structural work. You review and approve.

That shift, from writer to editor, changes the energy of the whole process. Editing is lower resistance than creating from scratch. And the output is still yours because it came from your raw idea and your voice.

Build this process once. Run it three times a week. At the end of a month, you have more content published than most people produce in a year. Volume is leverage. Consistency builds audiences. Systems make both possible.

## Post 5 — AI Tools That Are Actually Worth Paying For (And the Ones to Skip)

**Hook:** Not every AI tool deserves a spot in your stack.

**Slide 1:**
Most AI tools are not worth paying for.
A few of them are essential.

Here is the honest breakdown.

**Slide 2:**
Worth paying for:

Claude Pro ($20/month)
For strategy, writing, analysis, and long-context work. The thinking tool. Not optional if you are building a content or product business.

**Slide 3:**
Worth paying for:

n8n (self-host, ~$5/month on Railway)
Automation backbone. Connects everything. Saves hours weekly. Pays for itself with one workflow.

ChatGPT Plus ($20/month) — situational
Worth it if you use it for code generation, image creation (DALL-E), or o1 reasoning. Skip if you already have Claude Pro.

**Slide 4:**
Skip or use free tier:

Jasper, Copy.ai, Writesonic
These are wrappers around GPT with marketing copy templates. Claude does this better. Not worth the $50/month price tag.

Most dedicated AI writing tools in the $30-100 range
The underlying model is the same as what you can access directly for $20.

**Slide 5:**
The rule:
Pay for tools that access the base model directly or that do something no base model does natively.

Do not pay for a pretty interface around a tool you already have.

**Slide 6:**
A lean AI stack at $25/month can outperform a bloated one at $300/month.
@behikeai — build the stack that actually works.

**Caption:**
There is a category of AI tool that exists purely to charge you a premium for access to the same underlying model you could use directly for a fraction of the cost. These tools have good landing pages, strong copywriting, and an impressive-looking feature list. The actual output is the same as what you get from a $20/month Claude or ChatGPT subscription.

The AI tool market is full of this right now. Solopreneurs end up spending $200/month on five subscriptions that overlap in function and do not collectively outperform two well-chosen tools.

The framework for deciding: does this tool access the base model directly, or does it provide automation, integrations, or workflows that would take significant time to build yourself? If yes, it might be worth paying. If it is just a wrapper with templates, use the base model and write your own prompts.

A tight, well-understood stack of two to three tools will always outperform a wide collection of partially-used subscriptions.
