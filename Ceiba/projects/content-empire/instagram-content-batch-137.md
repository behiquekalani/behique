## Post 1 — The Landing Page Truth Nobody Tells You

**Hook:** Your landing page is not converting because you are describing your product instead of diagnosing your buyer.

**Slide 1:** Your landing page is not converting because you are describing your product instead of diagnosing your buyer.

**Slide 2:** Every weak landing page follows the same structure:
- Here is what it is
- Here is what is inside
- Here is the price
- Buy now

That is a product sheet. Not a sales page.

**Slide 3:** A converting landing page follows a different logic:
- Here is the problem you are living with right now
- Here is why it keeps happening
- Here is what changes when it stops
- Here is the exact tool that makes that happen

**Slide 4:** The first structure talks about the product.
The second structure talks about the person.

Buyers do not care about your product. They care about their situation.

**Slide 5:** Rewrite your landing page from the buyer's perspective.
Start with their reality. End with your offer.
That is the sequence.

**Slide 6:** Build pages that sell while you sleep.

**Caption:** Most solopreneurs write landing pages the way a manufacturer writes a spec sheet. Features. Contents. Price. But nobody buys from a spec sheet.

People buy when they see their exact situation reflected back at them with enough accuracy that they think "this person understands what I am dealing with." Then they buy when that person offers a clear path out.

The landing page is not a brochure. It is a conversation. And the conversation has to start with the buyer, not the product.

Before you rewrite your page, ask one question: does this page describe my product or diagnose my buyer? The answer tells you everything about why it is or is not converting.

## Post 2 — The 5 Sections Every Converting Page Needs

**Hook:** A converting landing page is not a creative project. It is a structure problem.

**Slide 1:** A converting landing page is not a creative project. It is a structure problem.

**Slide 2:** Section 1: The Hook Headline
One sentence that names the exact outcome your buyer wants.
Not clever. Specific.
"30 Reels a Month. One Batch Day. No Studio."

**Slide 3:** Section 2: The Problem Block
Name 3 things your buyer is experiencing right now that are painful, true, and specific.
Do not soften them. Say them plainly.

**Slide 4:** Section 3: The Mechanism
Explain the simple reason the problem exists.
Not their fault. A missing piece.
Then introduce your product as that piece.

**Slide 5:** Section 4: The Proof
Testimonials, results, specifics.
Not "I loved this." But "I went from X to Y in Z time."
One real result beats five generic ones.

Section 5: The Offer Block
What they get. What it costs. What to do next.
Make the CTA a single obvious action.

**Slide 6:** Build this structure once. Sell on repeat.
Find more at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** When a landing page does not convert, the instinct is to redesign it. New colors, new fonts, new layout. But the problem is almost never visual.

The problem is structure. The page either skips the problem block entirely, jumps to features before building desire, or buries the CTA in a way that requires the buyer to make too many micro-decisions.

These five sections are not arbitrary. They follow the psychological sequence of every purchase decision: awareness, resonance, understanding, belief, action. When your page mirrors that sequence, the conversion rate goes up. When it skips steps, people leave.

Map your current page to these five sections. Identify the gap. Fix the gap. Test it.

That is the entire process.

## Post 3 — Write a Headline That Stops Them Cold

**Hook:** You have 3 seconds to stop someone from leaving your page. Your headline is the only thing that does it.

**Slide 1:** You have 3 seconds to stop someone from leaving your page. Your headline is the only thing that does it.

**Slide 2:** Most landing page headlines fail for the same reason:
They describe what the product is instead of what the buyer gets.

"The Ultimate Content Creation System" = who cares.
"30 Reels a Month. One Batch Day." = now I am reading.

**Slide 3:** A high-converting headline formula:

[Specific outcome] + [Time frame or constraint]

Remove the fluff. Every word has to earn its place.

**Slide 4:** Test your headline with this question:
"Does this immediately tell someone who this is for and what they will get?"

If the answer is no, rewrite it.

**Slide 5:** The headline is not where you show off your writing.
It is where you earn the next three seconds of attention.
Keep it plain. Make it specific. Make it fast.

**Slide 6:** Write landing pages that do the selling for you.
Start at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** David Ogilvy said it in 1963 and it is still true: when you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents of your advertising dollar.

The headline does not have to be brilliant. It has to be clear. It needs to do one thing: make the right person want to read the next sentence.

Vague headlines kill pages. "Transform your business with AI" converts nobody. "Build a 5-figure info product business with one laptop and no team" converts the person who is exactly in that situation.

Specificity is not limiting. It is targeting. The more specific your headline, the more the right person feels spoken to, and the more the wrong person self-selects out. Both outcomes are correct.

Rewrite your headline before you touch anything else on your page.

## Post 4 — The Proof Section Most Pages Get Wrong

**Hook:** Your testimonials are not building trust. They are being ignored. Here is why.

**Slide 1:** Your testimonials are not building trust. They are being ignored. Here is why.

**Slide 2:** Bad testimonial:
"This product changed my life. Highly recommend."

Nobody reads this. It says nothing about the actual problem or outcome.

**Slide 3:** Good testimonial:
"I was posting daily for 6 months with zero sales. After applying the content system, I made $800 in my first week of selling."

That is specific. That is believable. That converts.

**Slide 4:** The anatomy of a testimonial that works:
Before state (where they were) + Action taken + After state (specific result)

Three parts. Every single time.

**Slide 5:** If you do not have testimonials yet, use your own results or walk through a case study of the exact process.
Specificity is the thing. Not the name or the face.

**Slide 6:** Build a proof section people actually read.
More at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** Social proof is one of the most powerful conversion tools on a landing page, and most people waste it completely.

The problem is not the absence of testimonials. It is the type of testimonials being used. Generic praise does not build trust because it does not tell the reader anything concrete. "Amazing product" could apply to anything. It gives the brain nothing to hold onto.

The testimonials that convert are the ones that describe a specific before and after. The reader recognizes the before state as their own, sees the after as possible, and then believes that your product is the bridge. That sequence only works when the testimonial is specific enough to be real.

Collect testimonials with a simple prompt: "What was your situation before, what did you do, and what changed?" That prompt alone will give you testimonials that convert.

## Post 5 — The CTA That Actually Gets Clicked

**Hook:** Your call to action is losing sales because it is asking for commitment before it has earned it.

**Slide 1:** Your call to action is losing sales because it is asking for commitment before it has earned it.

**Slide 2:** Weak CTA: "Buy Now"
Nobody wants to buy. They want the result.

Strong CTA: "Get the 30-Day Content System"
That is the result. That is what gets clicked.

**Slide 3:** The CTA should describe what happens after the click.
Not the transaction. The outcome.

"Start Your Automation System" beats "Checkout" every time.

**Slide 4:** CTA placement mistakes:
- Only at the bottom (they leave before they get there)
- Too many CTAs (creates choice paralysis)
- Buried in the design (they miss it)

Put one CTA above the fold. Repeat it once after each major section.

**Slide 5:** The CTA is not the close. It is the handoff.
Your page did the convincing. The CTA just opens the door.
Make the door obvious.

**Slide 6:** Every element of your page should lead here.
Build pages that convert at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** A landing page with a weak CTA is like a great sales conversation where you forget to ask for the sale. Everything worked and then nothing happened.

The CTA is not an afterthought. It is the architectural endpoint of your entire page. Every headline, every problem statement, every testimonial is building toward this one moment. If the CTA is vague, hidden, or framed as a financial transaction instead of an outcome, the conversion collapses.

The fix is straightforward: write the CTA in terms of what the buyer receives, not what they spend. Then place it where they are ready to act. Above the fold before they scroll, and again after each section of value you have delivered.

One CTA. Clear outcome language. Strategic placement. That combination alone will improve almost any landing page.
