### Script 2 — AI for Research Papers (The Right Way)
**Hook (0-3s):** Stop using AI to write your papers. Use it to read them faster instead.
**Setup (3-10s):** There's a legitimate and genuinely useful way to use AI for academic research. Nobody teaches this in class.
**Content (10-55s):**
1. Upload a dense research paper to Claude or ChatGPT and ask it to explain the methodology in plain language.
2. Ask it: "What are the three weakest points in this paper's argument?"
3. Ask it to summarize the findings in the context of your specific research question.
4. Use it to generate counter-arguments you should address in your own work.
5. Use it to find gaps: "What did this paper not address that would be relevant to my topic?"
6. You still write the paper. But you understand the literature 10x faster.
7. That's the difference between using AI as a crutch and using it as a lever.

### Script 4 — How to Use AI to Actually Retain What You Study
**Hook (0-3s):** Reading your notes again is the worst way to study. Here's what works instead.
**Setup (3-10s):** Passive review feels productive but doesn't build long-term retention. AI changes how you practice retrieval.
**Content (10-55s):**
1. After studying a topic, open Claude and write: "Quiz me on [topic] with 10 questions. Mix conceptual and application."
2. Answer without looking at your notes. Treat it like the real exam.
3. After answering, ask Claude to explain which answers were wrong and why.
4. Ask it to give you a harder version of any question you got right.
5. This is active recall with immediate feedback. It's what tutors charge for.
6. Do this for 20 minutes instead of re-reading for an hour. Retention is dramatically better.

**Hook (0-3s):** The students who use AI well are going to be the professionals everyone wants to hire.
**Setup (3-10s):** There's a difference between using AI to skip learning and using it to accelerate learning. Here's the accelerator approach.
**Content (10-55s):**
1. Use AI to get feedback on your work, not to produce it. Paste your code, your essay, your design brief and ask where it's weak.
2. Use AI to compress the feedback loop. You can get critique in seconds instead of waiting for a professor's office hours.
3. Use AI to explore paths: "If I want to work in X field, what skills do I need and in what order?"

5. The skill you're building isn't the AI prompt. It's the judgment to know when the output is right.
6. That judgment only comes from doing the work yourself first.
