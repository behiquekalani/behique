## POST 3
**Title:** "The AI tools I used to build 5 products in 7 days"
**Post in series:** 3 of 10
**Week:** Week 1

---

### CAROUSEL SLIDES

**Slide 1 (Cover)**
> 5 products.
> 7 days.
> 3 tools.
>
> Here's the exact stack.

**Slide 2**
> Tool 1: Claude (claude.ai)
>
> Used for: writing, structuring, rewriting, editing.
>
> I don't use Claude like a search engine.
> I give it source material, ask it to organize, then edit the output.
> The result sounds like me because I'm still in the loop.
> The first draft just takes 20 minutes instead of 3 days.

**Slide 3**
> What I actually sent to Claude.
>
> "Here are my notes on ADHD finance management. Structure this as a practical guide for 18-25 year olds. Keep it short. Focus on systems, not motivation."
>
> That kind of input. Specific. Not open-ended.
> Open-ended prompts get generic output.

**Slide 4**
> Tool 2: Canva
>
> Used for: product covers.
>
> The cover is the thing people see before they buy. It matters.
> I used Canva's dark templates and added consistent fonts and colors.
> Total time per cover: about 25 minutes.
>
> Same color palette across all products. That's what makes a brand look like a brand.

**Slide 5**
> Tool 3: Gumroad
>
> Used for: hosting, payments, delivery.
>
> Free to use. They take a fee per sale.
> Upload the PDF. Set the price. Get a link.
> That's the whole setup.
>
> No payment processor accounts. No Stripe setup. No landing page required.
> It handles everything.

**Slide 6**
> What I didn't use.
>
> No Notion AI. No Jasper. No $50/month writing tools.
> No design software beyond Canva free.
> No paid hosting.
>
> The tools cost nothing for this phase.

**Slide 7**
> The actual workflow.
>
> 1. Pick a topic I know and can explain clearly
> 2. Write rough notes by hand or voice
> 3. Send notes to Claude with a specific prompt
> 4. Edit the Claude output aggressively
> 5. Format in Google Docs, export as PDF
> 6. Build cover in Canva
> 7. Upload to Gumroad
>
> That's the pipeline. Repeatable. Fast.

**Slide 8**
> What makes the output not feel like AI.
>
> Specificity. Details from real experience.
> If Claude writes "use budgeting apps," I replace it with "I use a specific spreadsheet, here's the formula."
> That's the edit pass. It's the most important step.

**Slide 9 (Final)**
> Post 3 of 10.
>
> Next post: why I chose digital products over eBay dropshipping.
> That decision was less obvious than it sounds.
>
> Follow @behikeai.

---

### CAPTION

Three tools. Five products. Seven days.

Claude for writing. Canva for covers. Gumroad for selling. That's the entire stack, and none of it cost me anything in week one.

The part people miss: AI doesn't replace your knowledge. It replaces the blank page. You still need to know something worth teaching. You still need to edit. The AI just gets you from zero to a draft in a fraction of the time.

The specific prompt matters more than the tool. I'll post examples in a future slide.

Post 4 explains why I went with digital products instead of dropshipping. It's a strategic decision and the reasoning is worth laying out.

## POST 4
**Title:** "Why I chose digital products over eBay dropshipping first"
**Post in series:** 4 of 10
**Week:** Week 2

---

### CAROUSEL SLIDES

**Slide 1 (Cover)**
> I had two options.
>
> eBay dropshipping.
> Or digital products.
>
> Here's why I chose digital products first.

**Slide 2**
> The eBay option.
>
> I had already built a product research pipeline for eBay.
> Google Trends scraper. Price comparison tools. A list of validated niches.
>
> The infrastructure was ready.
> The path was tested.
>
> I chose the other one anyway.

**Slide 3**
> The problem with physical products right now.
>
> Margins are thin. Shipping is unpredictable. Returns exist.
> Every sale requires sourcing, listing, packaging, tracking.
>
> It's a real business. It takes real time.
> I don't have unlimited time right now.

**Slide 4**
> The case for digital products.
>
> Build once. Sell indefinitely.
> No inventory. No shipping. No returns.
> Instant delivery. Zero marginal cost.
>
> The math is different. The risk is different.
> I build it once and the product exists forever.

**Slide 5**
> The honest tradeoff.
>
> Digital products are easier to scale.
> But they're harder to start.
>
> Nobody buys a PDF from an account with 0 followers.
> eBay has built-in traffic. Gumroad does not.
>
> The upside of digital products only kicks in once you have an audience.
> So I'm building the audience at the same time as the products.

**Slide 6**
> Why I'm keeping eBay in the plan.
>
> eBay is the fallback and the validator.
> If a product concept fails as a PDF, I can test a physical version.
> If a physical product sells well, that's a digital product topic.
>
> They feed each other. The pipeline still exists.
> Digital products just go first.

**Slide 7**
> The actual reason.
>
> I know AI deeply. I've built with it for a year.
> People around me are confused by it and asking me questions constantly.
>
> The most natural first product to build is the thing you already know.
> I'm not forcing a niche. I'm just packaging what I already have.

**Slide 8 (Final)**
> Post 4 of 10.
>
> Next post: the money I spent in month 1. Every dollar.
>
> Follow @behikeai.

---

### CAPTION

eBay dropshipping was the obvious choice. I had the tools, the research, and a validated list of products to test. The pipeline was already built.

I chose digital products anyway.

The reason is simple: I know AI the way most people don't, because I've spent a year building with it. Teaching that is the path of least resistance. Not because it's easy, but because the source material is already there.

eBay stays in the plan. It will probably become the revenue while the digital product audience grows. But digital products come first because the potential ceiling is higher and the overhead is lower.

Month 1 spending breakdown in post 5. It's a short list.

## POST 7
**Title:** "The thing nobody tells you about building in public"
**Post in series:** 7 of 10
**Week:** Week 3

---

### CAROUSEL SLIDES

**Slide 1 (Cover)**
> The thing nobody tells you
> about building in public.
>
> It's not about accountability.

**Slide 2**
> What building in public looks like from the outside.
>
> Regular updates. Honest numbers. Engagement.
> An audience rooting for you.
>
> That's the pitch.

**Slide 3**
> What it actually looks like.
>
> You post about a slow week and get 12 likes.
> You post a win and get 200.
>
> The algorithm doesn't care about your honesty.
> It rewards outcomes. Not process.
>
> That's the first thing nobody says.

**Slide 4**
> The second thing.
>
> Building in public changes what you build.
>
> You start making decisions partially for the story.
> "This will be a better post" is not the same as "this is the right next step."
>
> You have to be careful about that drift.

**Slide 5**
> The third thing.
>
> Silence from the audience doesn't mean the work is wrong.
>
> Most people watch without saying anything.
> The ones who buy your product often never comment.
> The ones who comment most often never buy.
>
> Don't optimize for the comment section.

**Slide 6**
> What building in public is actually good for.
>
> Discipline. Momentum. A record of what you did.
>
> The act of posting forces clarity.
> You can't write "I built X this week" without having built X.
> The audience is almost secondary to that.

**Slide 7**
> Why I'm still doing it anyway.
>
> Because this series is a year-one record.
> Not just for the audience. For me.
>
> When month 6 is hard, I'll be able to look back at post 2.
> "Zero products. Zero sales." And then the thing I built from there.
>
> That's worth something.

**Slide 8 (Final)**
> Post 7 of 10.
>
> Month 2 plan drops next.
> Content, services, and the next chapter.
>
> Follow @behikeai.

---

### CAPTION

Building in public is not what it's sold as.

The "accountability from your audience" thing is mostly fiction. Nobody is watching close enough to hold you accountable. The real accountability is the gap between what you posted last week and what you're posting now.

The thing that actually works: posting forces you to be honest with yourself. You can't say "I built X this week" if you didn't. The record is the point.

The audience is real. The engagement is real. But the primary beneficiary of building in public is the person building. Not the people watching.

Still worth doing. Just for different reasons than most people say.
