# Twitter Batch 182 — Building in Public Without Oversharing

---

## Standalone Tweets

**Tweet 1**
Building in public is a distribution strategy, not a confessional. Share the thinking, the framework, the lesson. Not the anxiety, the chaos, or the numbers you're embarrassed by.

**Tweet 2**
The rule for building in public: share what's useful, not what's raw. Process a thing fully before you post about it. Reflection is content. Unprocessed emotion is noise.

**Tweet 3**
Oversharing in public looks like vulnerability. It often isn't. Real vulnerability is building something even when no one is watching, not posting your low moments for engagement.

**Tweet 4**
You can be honest without being transparent about everything. Honesty means what you say is true. Transparency means sharing all of it. One builds trust. The other builds drama.

**Tweet 5**
Building in public works because it creates accountability and attracts an audience. Both of those require consistency, not constant emotional updates about how hard the journey is.

---

## Threads

**Thread 1: The difference between building in public and performing struggle**

1/ Building in public became a trend because it worked. A few people shared their journey, built an audience while building a business, and won. Then everyone copied it wrong.

2/ What made it work: sharing specific insights, frameworks, lessons, and decision-making processes in real time. The audience learned something. That learning built trust.

3/ What most people copied: sharing the emotional arc. The hard days, the self-doubt, the "I almost quit" posts. This builds sympathy, not trust. Sympathy doesn't buy products.

4/ The test: would this post be useful to someone who doesn't know you and doesn't care about your journey? If yes, post it. If it only makes sense to people already invested in your story, it's performance.

5/ Build in public by sharing the thinking. Not the feeling. Your frameworks, experiments, observations, and decisions are useful. Your anxiety spiral on a Tuesday is not.

---

**Thread 2: What to share vs. what to keep private**

1/ Not everything that happens while building belongs online. Part of building well is developing a private practice, a space where you can think and fail without an audience watching every move.

2/ Share: what you learned from a failure. Keep private: the failure as it's happening, before you understand it.

3/ Share: how you made a decision. Keep private: the indecision, the second-guessing, the 3 a.m. spiral that preceded the decision.

4/ Share: the product you're building and why. Keep private: the revenue numbers until you have context for them and a reason to share that serves your audience, not just your ego.

5/ The filter is simple: does sharing this serve my audience or does it serve my need for validation right now? One is content strategy. The other is emotional outsourcing.

---

**Thread 3: How building in public actually builds an audience**

1/ Building in public builds an audience through a specific mechanism: people follow builders because they want to learn, be inspired, or see how someone thinks. None of those require oversharing.

2/ The content that works in build-in-public: contrarian takes, systems you've tested, mistakes with clear lessons, predictions with your reasoning, frameworks that make something easier to understand.

3/ The content that underperforms long-term: raw emotional updates, vague "the journey is hard" posts, screenshots of revenue without context, follower count milestones that don't teach anything.

4/ Audience growth from building in public is sustainable when the content has utility. Utility means: after reading this, the person knows something they didn't before or can do something they couldn't before.

5/ Share the building. Share the thinking. Share the lessons. Keep the processing for your journal. The audience is here to learn, not to therapize you.

---

**Thread 4: The strategic logic of selective sharing**

1/ Every time you post, you're making a positioning decision. You're telling the world what kind of thinker and builder you are. Oversharing positions you as someone who needs external validation to keep going.

2/ Selective sharing positions you as someone who processes before publishing, who thinks before speaking, who has perspective. That's rare. That's what authority is built on.

3/ The constraint of "only share what's useful" forces you to develop actual insight before posting. This makes your content better. Better content attracts better people. Better people become better customers.

4/ You don't need to share everything to be perceived as authentic. You need everything you share to be true. Authenticity is not the same as transparency. One is about integrity. The other is about exposure.

5/ The most respected builders in public are not the most transparent. They're the most consistently insightful. Insight is the currency. Transparency is just one way to earn it, and not the only one.

---

**Thread 5: Building a framework for what to share**

1/ If you're going to build in public, you need a filter. Without one, you'll post whatever you're feeling and optimize for short-term engagement instead of long-term trust.

2/ Framework: before posting, ask three questions. One: is this true? Two: is this useful to someone who doesn't know me? Three: would I be comfortable with this post existing in five years?

3/ If the answer to all three is yes, post it. If any answer is no, either revise it until it passes all three, or keep it in a private journal where it belongs.

4/ The five-year test is the most important. In five years, you want your public build log to read like a thoughtful document of how a builder thinks. Not like a real-time anxiety diary.

5/ Build the kind of archive you'd be proud to hand to a potential client or partner as evidence of how you think. That standard, applied consistently, will make your public building more valuable than almost anyone else doing it.
