**Thread 201-T1: Why micro-niches command premium prices**

1/ The specificity premium is real.
Here's why a focused creator always out-earns a generalist at the same skill level.

2/ When you're specific, comparison shopping disappears.
Nobody googles "best life coach" and buys the first result.
But "business coach for introverted freelancers" has zero obvious competitors.

3/ Specificity also signals depth.
If you've gone narrow, the market assumes you've gone deep.
Deep expertise justifies higher prices. Always.

4/ The math works differently too.
1,000 loyal buyers who see themselves in your work beat 100,000 passive followers who don't feel spoken to.
Conversion rates in a micro-niche can be 5-10x higher.

5/ How to find your micro-niche:
- Pick the intersection of what you know, who you've been, and what they'll pay for
- Ask: what's the most specific version of the problem I solve?
- Go one level more specific than feels comfortable

6/ The fear is that you'll run out of audience.
The reality is you'll finally have one.
Broad positioning doesn't attract everyone. It attracts no one specific enough to buy.

**Thread 201-T2: The specificity test**

1/ Here's a fast test to see if your positioning is specific enough.

2/ Read your bio to a stranger.
Can they immediately picture the exact person you help?
If they need clarification, you're too broad.

3/ Try this filter:
"I help [specific person] who [has this specific problem] achieve [specific outcome] without [specific obstacle]."
Every word is doing work. If you can swap out any word without changing the meaning, go deeper.

4/ Example before:
"I help entrepreneurs grow their business online."
Example after:
"I help first-time course creators get to $3K/month before quitting their job."
The second one finds its people. The first floats by everyone.

5/ Specificity isn't permanent.
Start narrow. Earn trust. Expand later from a position of authority.
You can always go broad after you win a niche. You can't shortcut your way to authority.

**Thread 201-T3: What the specificity premium actually looks like in practice**

1/ I've watched creators at the same skill level charge $97 and $997 for roughly equivalent work.
The difference is almost never quality. It's positioning.

2/ The $97 person: "online business coach, helping you grow."
The $997 person: "I help burned-out nurses build a consulting practice so they can leave bedside care in 12 months."
Same skills. Different specificity. Different price ceiling.

3/ The $997 person also has better testimonials.
When you solve a specific problem for a specific person, the transformation is measurable.
Specific outcomes produce specific social proof.

4/ And specific social proof attracts more specific buyers.
The cycle compounds.
Generalists attract skeptics. Specialists attract believers.

5/ You don't need to be the best in the world.
You need to be the most relevant option for your exact audience.
Relevance beats quality every time in a crowded market.

**Thread 201-T4: How to niche down without panic**

1/ Most people don't niche down because they're afraid they'll be wrong.
Here's the reframe: niching down is an experiment, not a life sentence.

2/ Start with what you already know deeply.
Not what you want to learn. Not what sounds impressive.
What do people already come to you for advice about?

3/ Layer on: who specifically is asking?
Not "entrepreneurs." Not "professionals."
What kind of person, at what stage, with what exact pain?

4/ Test the niche with content before you build anything.
Write 10 posts aimed directly at that specific person.
If the right people respond, you've found something real.

5/ Adjust as you learn.
The goal isn't to find the perfect niche in week one.
The goal is to be specific enough that someone feels seen by what you make.

6/ That feeling is the foundation of every loyal audience.
You don't build trust through reach. You build it through relevance.

---

**Thread 201-T5: The price ceiling of generalism**

1/ There's a ceiling on what generalists can charge.
It's set by the market, not by their skill.

2/ Generalist price ceiling: whatever the lowest-cost competitor charges.
Because you're interchangeable.

3/ Specialist price ceiling: whatever the outcome is worth to the buyer.
Because you're irreplaceable.

4/ "Irreplaceable" sounds impossible.
It isn't. It means being specific enough that alternatives feel wrong.
A nurse who wants out of bedside care doesn't want a generic business coach.
They want someone who's been in their shoes or has helped others out of them.

5/ Specificity creates that feeling.
And that feeling is the entire business model.
Charge for relevance. The market pays a premium for it every time.
