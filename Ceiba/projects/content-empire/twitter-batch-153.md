**Tweet 4 (3-Tweet Thread)**
The business model that fits you:

Most people pick a business model, then try to fit themselves into it.

"I'll do agency work." "I'll build SaaS." "I'll do info products."

Then they grind for a year and burn out because the model requires strengths they don't have.

/2

The better approach: audit your natural outputs.

What do you produce effortlessly that others struggle with?
What do people ask you for before you offer it?
What could you talk about for 3 hours without checking the time?

Your business model lives inside those answers.

/3

Strengths-based businesses have an unfair advantage: they don't feel like work to the person running them.

That's not a lifestyle benefit. It's a competitive moat.

Competitors copying your model will always be working harder than you are. That gap compounds over time.

**Tweet 7 (8-Tweet Thread)**
How to reverse-engineer a business model from your strengths:

Most business advice starts with the market.
Find a niche. Validate demand. Build the offer.

That framework works. But it ignores the other side: your side.

Here's a process that starts with you.

/2

Step 1: List your natural outputs.

Not your degrees. Not your job titles. Your natural outputs.

What do you produce that feels automatic? Writing, systems, explaining, selling, designing, analyzing, connecting people?

List ten. Then circle the three that feel most natural.

/3

Step 2: List problems those outputs solve.

For each strength, ask: who has a painful problem that this output would solve?

A person who writes clearly can solve the "I have something to say but can't communicate it" problem for founders, executives, technical experts.

Find the match.

/4

Step 3: Find the audience with money and urgency.

A good problem + the right audience = a viable business.

The right audience has: money to pay, a deadline driving urgency, and a track record of investing in solutions.

B2B audiences often clear all three. Consumers are harder.

/5

Step 4: Design the delivery mechanism.

Now you choose the model. Not before.

Can this strength best be delivered as a service, a course, a product, an audience?

The delivery mechanism should match how you actually want to work, not how you think you should work.

/6

Step 5: Price based on outcome, not time.

Once you know what outcome your strength produces, price the outcome.

Not your hours. The transformation.

A person who eliminates $10K of wasted spend per month for a client can charge $3K/month. That's not a high price. It's a fair one.

/7

Step 6: Build a minimum viable proof.

One case study. One testimonial. One real result.

That's all you need to start charging professional rates.

Credentials, portfolios, and websites come after the first paying client. Not before.

/8

The whole framework:

Natural output -> Problem solved -> Right audience -> Delivery mechanism -> Outcome-based price -> Proof.

That's it. Most people skip the first step and wonder why the rest doesn't work.

**Tweet 10 (3-Tweet Thread)**
Three questions to stress-test your business model:

1. Would you still want to do this work if it took 3 years to be profitable?
2. Does the model require you to do things you hate on a daily basis?
3. If you had to teach someone else to do this, would you lose the thing that makes it work?

/2

Question 3 is the one most people skip.

If your business only works because of your specific strength, that's either a problem (hard to scale) or a feature (hard to copy).

Know which one it is before you try to grow.

/3

The best businesses are ones where the founder's strengths ARE the product.

Not because of vanity.
Because it creates a genuine competitive moat that no amount of funding or copying can replicate.

Build toward that.
