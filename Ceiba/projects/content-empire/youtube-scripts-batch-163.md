## Script 1: Why Your Digital Product Is Underpriced (And It's Costing You More Than Money)

**Hook (0-30s):**
Most creators price their digital products by asking "what would I pay for this?" That's the wrong question. Price is not about your perception of value. It's about the outcome the buyer gets. The moment you understand that, everything changes.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Here's a pattern I see constantly. Someone builds a solid digital product. An ebook, a course, a template system. They spend weeks on it. Then they price it at $9 or $19 because they're afraid no one will buy it at $47. So they launch. They get a few sales. The math doesn't work. They burn out. They quit.

The irony is that the low price didn't help them sell more. It just made the business unsustainable and, paradoxically, made the product feel less valuable to buyers.

Pricing psychology is real. And most creators are working against themselves without knowing it. This video is about fixing that. Not with theory, but with a framework you can apply today to any digital product you're building or have already built.

**Main Content:**

### The Outcome Pricing Framework
The only question that matters when pricing a digital product is: what is the specific outcome the buyer gets, and what is that outcome worth to them?

Not what it took you to create it. Not what similar products charge. Not what feels comfortable. What is the result worth?

A template that saves a freelancer 3 hours per week is worth hundreds of dollars a year to that person. A guide that helps someone land a $5,000 client is worth at least $500. A system that reduces a creator's content production time by 40% is worth thousands.

When you price at $17, you're either saying the outcome is worth $17, or you don't believe in your own product. Buyers notice both.

Start by writing down the specific, measurable outcome your product delivers. Then ask: what would a rational person pay for that outcome, knowing nothing about you or your product?

### Why Cheap Prices Attract the Wrong Buyers
This is counterintuitive but well-documented. Low prices filter in buyers who are price-sensitive and filter out buyers who are outcome-focused. The buyer who pays $9 for your course is often looking for information. The buyer who pays $197 is looking for transformation.

Outcome-focused buyers ask fewer refund questions. They implement more. They leave better reviews. They buy your next product.

This doesn't mean you should always charge the maximum. It means you should charge enough that the buyer has skin in the game. When someone pays $197 for something, they open it. They work through it. They get results. Those results become your testimonials and your referrals.

Price is a commitment device. Use it.

### The Three-Tier Pricing Model for Digital Products
Rather than one product at one price, think in three tiers.

Tier one is your entry-level offer. Low barrier, high volume. This is a $17-$37 product. Its job is not to make you rich. Its job is to convert cold traffic into buyers and prove you can deliver value. Think checklists, templates, short guides.

Tier two is your core offer. This is where most of your revenue comes from. $97-$297 range. A course, a system, a comprehensive guide with implementation support. This product does one complete job.

Tier three is your premium offer. $497 and up. This could be a workshop, a coaching add-on, a done-with-you program. Not everyone buys this. That's fine.

The mistake most creators make is staying at tier one forever. Or jumping to tier three before they've validated tier two. Build in sequence.

### Testing Price Before You Lock It In
You do not need to guess at price. You can test it.

Method one: anchor test. Price your product at what feels right. Then create a version of your sales page that pitches a higher price with a "current offer" discount to your tested price. Track conversion rates. This tells you the psychological ceiling.

Method two: direct ask. Survey your email list or followers. "I'm building X product. At what price would this feel like a no-brainer?" Most people will anchor low, but the distribution tells you where the resistance is.

Method three: cohort test. If you have enough traffic, run two versions of your checkout at different prices for two weeks. Compare conversion rates and total revenue. Revenue, not conversion rate, is what matters.

Price is not a set-and-forget decision. It's a hypothesis. Treat it like one.

**Conclusion:**
Here's your one action: take your current digital product, or the one you're building, and write down the specific outcome it delivers. Put a dollar value on that outcome. If your current price is less than 10% of that value, you're underpriced. Adjust it this week and document what happens to conversions.

Pricing is a skill. You get better at it by doing it, not by waiting until you feel confident. If this helped, subscribe. I publish videos every week on building digital businesses without burning out.

## Script 2: The Price Anchoring Playbook for Digital Products

**Hook (0-30s):**
There is a pricing technique used by every major SaaS company, every premium course creator, and every consulting firm that knows what it's doing. It's called price anchoring. It's not manipulation. It's communication. And you can use it starting today.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Price anchoring is the practice of presenting a higher reference price before your actual offer price. When someone sees $497 crossed out next to $197, their brain doesn't just see a discount. It recalibrates what $197 means.

This is not a trick. It's how human cognition works. We don't evaluate price in a vacuum. We evaluate it relative to context. Your job as a creator is to set the right context.

But most creators use anchoring wrong, or don't use it at all. They just post a price and hope people think it's fair. In this video, I'm going to show you the right way to anchor your digital product pricing, with real examples and a structure you can implement immediately.

**Main Content:**

### How Price Anchoring Works Psychologically
When someone sees a price, the first thing their brain does is search for reference points. What did I pay for something like this before? What does the creator say this is usually worth? What would it cost to get this result another way?

Price anchoring works by giving the brain a reference point before it has to search for one. You are controlling the first comparison.

The most common form is crossed-out pricing: "Was $497, now $197." But anchoring is broader than that. It includes comparison anchoring ("hiring a consultant for this would cost $2,000"), outcome anchoring ("the result this delivers is worth X per year"), and tier anchoring (showing a more expensive option first).

All of these work. What matters is that you use at least one of them and that the anchor is believable. An unbelievable anchor destroys trust.

### The Three Legitimate Anchoring Methods
Method one is comparison anchoring. You compare your product to an alternative the buyer already understands. "A one-hour consultation on this topic runs $300. This course covers everything in that consultation plus 8 weeks of implementation." Now $197 feels like a bargain.

The key is that the comparison has to be real. If you compare to something your audience has never paid for or would never pay for, the anchor doesn't land.

Method two is outcome anchoring. You state the financial value of the result. "If this system helps you land one additional $1,000 client per month, that's $12,000 per year. This costs $247." At that ratio, the price is trivial.

This works best when the outcome is measurable and the math is honest. Don't inflate the outcome. Use conservative numbers. Conservative math is more convincing than optimistic math.

Method three is tier anchoring. You present a higher-priced tier first. Your $497 course with coaching is shown before your $197 course alone. Now the $197 course looks accessible by comparison. This is why every SaaS pricing page shows the enterprise plan on the left.

### Building Your Sales Page Around Anchoring
Your sales page should establish an anchor in the first third. Before you reveal your price, the reader should already have a reference point in their head.

Structure it this way. Open with the problem and the outcome. Establish what it would cost to solve this problem through alternative means. Make the alternative real and specific. Then introduce your product as the better path. Then reveal your price after the anchor is set.

Most creators reveal their price too early. They put it above the fold because they're afraid of wasting the buyer's time. But a buyer who sees a price before they understand the value will always think it's too high. Sequence matters.

### What Not to Do With Anchoring
Three mistakes to avoid.

First: fake anchors. "Normally $997, but today only $47" is not an anchor. It's a credibility destroyer. If your product has never sold at $997, do not claim it has. Buyers are not as naive as some marketers assume.

Second: anchoring without proof. If you say the outcome is worth $10,000 per year, you need to be able to explain that math. If you can't, the claim weakens rather than strengthens your positioning.

Third: over-anchoring. Using three different anchors on the same page creates confusion and looks desperate. Pick the one that fits your product best and use it cleanly.

**Conclusion:**
This week, audit your product's sales page or store listing. Find where the price appears. Ask yourself: what anchor does the reader have before they see this number? If the answer is nothing, fix that first. Add one comparison, one outcome statement, or one tier display. Test it for two weeks and watch what happens to your conversion rate.

Pricing is architecture. Every element on your page either builds or erodes the case for your price. If you want more frameworks like this one, subscribe.

## Script 3: Stop Discounting. Do This Instead.

**Hook (0-30s):**
Every time you discount your digital product, you are training your audience to wait. You're telling them the price you originally charged was not the real price. And once you've done that twice, the real price stops existing.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Discounting feels like a growth strategy. Run a sale, spike revenue, feel good. But if you zoom out, it's one of the most expensive habits a digital product creator can develop.

Not expensive in the direct cost. Expensive in what it does to your positioning, your audience's behavior, and your own relationship with your pricing.

I'm not saying never run a promotion. I'm saying that discounting, as a default revenue strategy, is a trap. And there are better ways to create urgency, reward loyalty, and drive sales spikes that don't train your audience to wait for the price to drop.

This video is about those alternatives.

**Main Content:**

### What Discounting Actually Does to Your Business
When you discount regularly, a few things happen.

First, your evergreen price becomes fiction. Buyers start to feel manipulated when they buy at full price and see a 50% off sale three weeks later. This creates resentment, not loyalty.

Second, you attract discount buyers. The segment of your audience that buys at $47 during a sale is a different segment than the people who would have paid $97. Discount buyers have lower commitment. They're less likely to implement. They're more likely to request refunds.

Third, you erode your own confidence in your pricing. Every time you run a deep discount, you're implicitly agreeing that your full price was too high. That belief compounds. It becomes harder and harder to raise prices or hold them.

There is a version of promotional pricing that works. But it's structured differently.

### Value-Add Promotions Instead of Price Cuts
Instead of reducing the price, increase what's included for a limited time. This is a value-add promotion.

Your $197 course stays at $197. But for this week, buyers also get a bonus workshop, a template pack, or a one-time Q&A call. The price doesn't move. The value goes up.

This approach does several things. It creates urgency without training buyers to wait for cheaper prices. It rewards buyers who act quickly without penalizing buyers who acted earlier. And it gives you a clear reason to message your audience without a sale being the whole hook.

Value-add promotions work best when the bonus is directly related to the core product and when it's genuinely limited. Don't offer a "bonus" you'll always include.

### Launch Windows as a Structural Urgency Tool
Launch windows are a pricing architecture decision. Your product is available at its regular price during launch. After the launch window closes, it either goes up in price or off sale entirely.

This is not about creating fake scarcity. It's about making a real promise that has real consequences. "I'm teaching this live cohort once. After the cohort, the recorded version will cost more." That's honest and creates genuine urgency.

The mechanism matters. Closing a cart or raising a price after launch only works if you actually do it. If you always reopen, the urgency was a lie and your audience learns that.

### When Discounting Is Justified
There are three scenarios where discounting makes sense.

One: you are clearing a product from your lineup. You're discontinuing it, and you want to move existing copies. One-time, stated reason, done.

Two: you are running an affiliate promotion where a partner's audience gets a partner-specific code. The discount is scoped to a relationship, not broadcast to your whole audience.

Three: you are doing an explicit experiment with price. You're testing whether a lower price point changes total revenue. You document it. You learn. You make a decision.

Outside of these scenarios, hold your price.

**Conclusion:**
Before your next promotional moment, decide whether you're going to cut the price or add value. If the honest answer is that your product needs a lower price to sell, the problem is not price. The problem is positioning or targeting. Fix those. Don't mask them with a discount.

## Script 4: How to Price a Digital Product Bundle

**Hook (0-30s):**
A digital product bundle is one of the highest-leverage revenue moves a creator can make. You combine products you've already built, price the bundle at a discount relative to buying each separately, and create a new entry point that moves more volume than any single product. But most creators bundle wrong. Here's how to do it right.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Bundling is not just about combining products. It's about creating a new value narrative. The bundle should tell a story that none of the individual products tell alone. "Here are three products" is not a bundle. "Here is a complete system for doing X" is a bundle.

The difference is the buyer's mental model. When you buy individual products, you're buying tools. When you buy a bundle with a clear through-line, you're buying a result. Buyers pay more for results than for tools.

This video breaks down exactly how to structure, price, and position a digital product bundle in a way that feels like a natural offer rather than a clearance sale.

**Main Content:**

### Choosing What Goes in the Bundle
Not every combination of products makes a good bundle. The products need to share a logical progression or a common user problem.

A good bundle test: can you describe all the products in a single sentence that references one goal? "Everything you need to go from idea to published digital product in 30 days" is a bundle. "My three most popular products" is not.

The bundle should have a lead product, the thing most buyers would buy anyway if they only picked one. Then supporting products that accelerate or extend the core result. Optional: a bonus component that's exclusive to the bundle, something that doesn't exist as a standalone purchase. That exclusive element significantly increases perceived value.

### The Pricing Formula for Bundles
There is a simple formula that works. Add up the individual prices of all included products. Price the bundle at 60-70% of that total. Make sure that total and the bundle price are both visible on the page.

Example: three products at $97, $47, and $37. Total value: $181. Bundle price: $127. The buyer saves $54 and gets the complete system. You move more volume and increase your average order value compared to selling the lowest-priced product alone.

The key is that the individual prices must be real. Buyers can check. If your $97 product is actually listed for $97 on your store, the bundle math holds. If you're inflating individual prices to manufacture a discount, you will get caught and it will cost you trust.

### Positioning the Bundle vs. Individual Products
Your bundle should live at a different place in your funnel than your individual products. It should not replace them. It should serve buyers who have already decided they want the result and want the fastest path.

Individual products serve buyers who are still evaluating. Bundles serve buyers who have decided. Make sure both pathways exist.

On your store, show both. Let buyers choose their own entry point. Some will start with the $37 product and upgrade later. Some will see the bundle and buy it immediately. Both are valid. Your store architecture should support both without one cannibalizing the other.

### When to Launch a Bundle
Bundles work best at specific moments. Product launch, when you're bringing a new product to market and want to reward existing customers with a complete package. End of year, when buyers are in a purchasing mindset and want comprehensive solutions. Channel expansion, when you're entering a new platform and want a flagship offer that demonstrates the depth of your work.

Don't launch a bundle just because sales are slow. A bundle launched from a position of desperation will be priced and positioned from that same energy. Build your bundle when you have strong individual products and a clear narrative that connects them.

**Conclusion:**
Look at the products you've already built. Is there a bundle in there? Identify the through-line. Write one sentence that describes the result someone would get from buying all of them together. If that sentence is clear and compelling, you have a bundle. Price it, page it, and add it to your store this week.

## Script 5: The Psychology Behind Why People Buy Digital Products

**Hook (0-30s):**
People do not buy digital products for the information. They buy them because they believe the product will change their situation faster than figuring it out themselves. Your job is not to pack more information in. Your job is to make that belief feel justified before they buy.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
We tend to think that if we just add more value to a product, more modules, more templates, more bonuses, buyers will be more convinced. But that's not how purchase decisions work.

Purchase decisions are made in seconds. Not from a rational analysis of content volume, but from an emotional response to whether the product matches a belief the buyer already holds about their problem and the solution.

Understanding this changes everything about how you price, position, and sell digital products.

**Main Content:**

### The Three Buying Triggers for Digital Products
Every purchase of a digital product is triggered by one of three things.

The first trigger is pain recognition. The buyer is actively experiencing a problem and is looking for a solution right now. They found your product while searching for answers. This buyer is already motivated. Your job is to demonstrate that your product solves the specific pain they have, not pain in general.

The second trigger is aspiration. The buyer is not in pain but wants to reach a goal. They see your product as a path to a better version of their situation. This buyer needs more convincing than the pain buyer because the urgency is lower.

The third trigger is social proof. The buyer saw someone they respect buy or recommend your product. The purchase is partly about the outcome and partly about affiliation. They want to do what the people they admire do.

Most creators write sales copy aimed at the aspiration buyer. But the pain buyer converts at a much higher rate. If you can identify the specific, acute pain your product solves, lead with that.

### Why People Don't Buy Even When They Want To
There are four reasons a buyer who is interested does not purchase.

One: they don't believe the product will work. You haven't provided enough evidence that it delivers the outcome. Case studies, testimonials, and before-and-after stories solve this.

Two: they don't believe it will work for them specifically. "This worked for someone else but I'm different." Solve this by anticipating the objection and addressing it directly on the page.

Three: the timing feels wrong. "I'll come back to this." Address this with real urgency, not fake countdown timers.

Four: the price feels unjustified. Not too high. Unjustified. There's a difference. If you've established the outcome clearly and the anchor is set, the same price that felt high now feels reasonable.

### Matching Price to the Buyer's Belief About the Problem
Here is the practical implication. Your price should match how seriously the buyer takes their problem.

A buyer who thinks their problem is minor will not pay $497 to solve it. Not because they don't have the money. Because the math doesn't compute for them. You either need to help them see the problem as more serious, or price your product for where they currently are.

This is why problem framing in your sales copy is so important. Before you can ask someone to spend $197, you need them to agree that the problem costs them more than $197. In time, in opportunity, in frustration. Make that math visible before you reveal the price.

**Conclusion:**
Reread your current product's sales page or description. Find where you first acknowledge the buyer's pain or aspiration. Is it specific? Is it the pain they actually feel, or the general category of pain you think they feel? The more specific and accurate your pain framing, the more justified your price will feel.
