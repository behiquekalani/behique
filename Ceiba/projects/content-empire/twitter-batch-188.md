## Thread 1: The 3-Day Validation Method

1/ Before you build anything, spend 3 days on this sequence. It will save you months.

2/ Day 1: Write the landing page for the thing you want to build. No product. Just the page. If you cannot write the pitch, the idea is not clear enough yet.

3/ Day 2: Send that page to 20 people who match your ideal customer profile. Not friends. Real potential buyers. Ask for $10 to get on the early list.

4/ Day 3: Count the responses. If 3 or more people pay, you have signal. If zero pay, you have information. Both outcomes are valuable.

5/ The point is not to get rich in 3 days. The point is to test demand before you invest months of your life.

6/ Most people skip this because building feels like progress. Validation feels uncomfortable. That discomfort is protecting you from wasted effort.

7/ Validate first. Always.

---

## Thread 2: Why "I'll Build It Then Validate" Is Backwards

1/ The logic sounds reasonable: finish the product, then test if it works. Here is why it is backwards.

2/ When you have a finished product, you are emotionally attached. You spent months on it. You cannot see it clearly anymore.

3/ Feedback at that stage hits differently. You defend instead of listen. You tweak instead of pivot. You ship anyway and wonder why it does not sell.

4/ Early validation separates your ego from the idea. The idea is just a hypothesis. You are testing the hypothesis, not yourself.

5/ When you validate early and something fails, nothing is lost. When you validate late, everything is lost: time, money, and often the willingness to try again.

6/ Treat every idea as a hypothesis. Every hypothesis needs a test before it gets resources. That is the discipline that separates builders who compound from builders who burn out.

---

## Thread 3: The Validation Questions to Ask Your Audience

1/ Most people do validation surveys wrong. They ask: "Would you use this?" Everyone says yes. Nobody buys.

2/ The right questions reveal commitment, not interest. Interest is free. Commitment costs something.

3/ Question 1: "What is the biggest challenge you have with [problem area]?" Let them define the problem in their words, not yours.

4/ Question 2: "What have you already tried to solve it?" This shows you what does not work and what they were willing to invest in.

5/ Question 3: "If a solution existed that did X, what would you pay for it?" Now you have pricing data before you build.

6/ Question 4: "Can I send you a beta version in exchange for a 15-minute call?" Anyone who says yes is a pre-qualified customer.

7/ Four questions. Run them with 15 people. You will know more about your market than most businesses ever figure out.

---

## Thread 4: The Minimum Viable Offer

1/ You do not need a product. You need an offer. The offer is the commitment. The product is how you fulfill it.

2/ A minimum viable offer has three parts: the transformation (what changes for the buyer), the timeline (how long it takes), and the price (what it costs).

3/ "I will help you write a content system that generates one sales inquiry per week, in 30 days, for $200." That is a minimum viable offer.

4/ You can fulfill that with calls, documents, templates, or any combination. The delivery method comes after the sale.

5/ When you structure an offer first, you force yourself to think about outcomes, not features. Buyers pay for outcomes.

6/ Write the offer before you build. If you cannot write it clearly, the product is not ready yet either.

---

## Thread 5: What Happens When Validation Fails

1/ You run the test. Nobody pays. Here is what that actually means, and what to do next.

2/ First, separate the three possible failure points: wrong audience, wrong problem, wrong offer. They require different fixes.

3/ Wrong audience: the right idea, presented to people who do not have the problem. Fix: find the people who actually feel this problem daily.

4/ Wrong problem: people have a different version of the problem than you assumed. Fix: go back to the discovery questions, listen more carefully.

5/ Wrong offer: the transformation is unclear, the price is off, or the trust is not there yet. Fix: rewrite the pitch with more specificity.

6/ Failure in validation is not failure. It is information that would have cost you months to get any other way. Treat it that way.

7/ Run another test. Iterate the hypothesis. Keep going until you find the thing people will actually pay for.
