**Tweet 6:**
Drift is the category nobody talks about.

It's the money that left your account without a clear reason. Coffee you don't remember buying. An app you forgot you subscribed to. Three impulse purchases that felt small individually.

Drift is usually $100-$300/month for most people.

Not daily. Daily fails within two weeks for ADHD brains. Weekly builds a habit that holds.

## Thread 5 | Why Most AI Content Sounds Like AI (And How to Fix It)

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
AI content sounds like AI for one specific reason.

It's not the tool. It's the prompt. And fixing it takes 10 minutes of setup.

Here's what actually causes the problem.

---

**Tweet 2:**
AI defaults to the most statistically average version of whatever you ask for.

Average tone. Average structure. Average vocabulary.

That's what "write me a LinkedIn post" produces. The mean of a million LinkedIn posts.

---

**Tweet 3:**
The fix is not a better AI. It's a more specific prompt.

"Write me a LinkedIn post" versus "Write a 150-word LinkedIn post in the tone of [paste 3 samples of your own writing]. One insight. No bullet points. End with one direct line."

The second prompt gets a different output.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The 3-sample trick is the most useful thing I know about AI writing.

Paste 3 pieces you've already written that you like. Tell the AI to match the voice.

It can't fully replicate you. But it gets close enough that your edit pass takes 5 minutes instead of 30.

---

**Tweet 5:**
The words that immediately signal AI writing.

Certainly. Absolutely. It is important to note. Phrases that signal formality the writer didn't earn.

None of those are how real people write. They're patterns the model learned from formal documents.

Ban them explicitly in your prompt.

---

**Tweet 6:**
The structure problem is separate from the word problem.

AI defaults to: hook, three points, conclusion. Every time.

Real writing doesn't follow that structure. It follows the logic of the idea. Sometimes one point. Sometimes six. Never perfectly symmetrical.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The edit pass is not optional.

AI gives you a draft. You make it yours. That pass is where your actual voice enters the piece.

If you're publishing AI output without an edit pass, the content sounds like AI because it is AI.

## Thread 7 | What Deliberate Practice Actually Means (Not the Pop Version)

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
"10,000 hours" became a self-help meme.

The actual research behind it says something different. And the difference is why most practice doesn't produce real improvement.

Here's what Ericsson actually found.

---

**Tweet 2:**
The pop version: do something for 10,000 hours and become an expert.

The actual finding: only specific types of practice produce expert-level performance. Most practice doesn't qualify.

Ericsson called the qualifying type "deliberate practice."

---

**Tweet 3:**
Deliberate practice has three requirements.

One: it has to push just beyond your current ability. Two: it requires your full attention. Three: you need immediate feedback on whether you did it correctly.

Most practice fails all three.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The "just beyond current ability" requirement is where most people fail.

Comfortable repetition feels productive. It's not. Playing piano songs you already know for 2 hours is not deliberate practice. Playing the part you can't nail yet, slowly, repeatedly, is.

---

**Tweet 5:**
The full attention requirement is harder than it sounds.

You can't be deliberate while distracted. Listening to a podcast while running doesn't count. Reviewing code while watching a video doesn't count.

Deliberate practice requires presence. That's expensive.

---

**Tweet 6:**
The feedback requirement is why most self-teaching fails.

If you don't know whether you did the thing correctly, you can't adjust. You might be reinforcing bad form for 100 hours straight and not know it.

A coach solves this. So does a structured benchmark.

---

**Tweet 7:**
Applied to building: deliberate practice for a developer is not shipping projects.

It's finding the specific function or concept you can't do yet, building a minimal example of only that thing, and getting feedback on whether it works.

Most builders skip this.

---

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
This is why the people who improve fastest at building aren't necessarily the ones working the most hours.

They're the ones working at the edge of their ability, with attention, with feedback.

The hours matter less than the quality of the hour.

## Thread 9 | The YouTube Niche Selection Framework That Prevents Quitting in Month 2

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
Most YouTube channels die in month 2.

Not because the creator ran out of ideas. Because they picked a niche they couldn't sustain.

Here's the framework I use to avoid that.

---

**Tweet 2:**
The standard advice is "find a niche with high demand and low competition."

That's true but incomplete. A niche you can't make content in for two years will fail regardless of demand.

The missing variable is your sustainable output capacity.

---

**Tweet 3:**
The three-axis test.

Axis 1: Do you have something specific to say about this topic that isn't already said better somewhere else?

Axis 2: Can you produce 40+ videos on this topic without running out of material by month 3?

Axis 3: Does an audience with purchasing power exist for this topic?

---

**Tweet 4:**
Axis 1 is the differentiation check.

If your answer to "why would someone watch your channel instead of someone else's" is "I explain it more clearly," that's not a real answer.

Clarity is table stakes. The differentiation needs to be specific.

---

**Tweet 5:**
Axis 2 is where most people cheat themselves.

List 40 video ideas before you commit to a niche. If you can't list 40, you'll run out at video 15 and quit.

The list doesn't have to be perfect. It has to exist.

---

**Tweet 6:**
Axis 3 is the monetization filter.

A large audience that doesn't buy anything is hard to build a business on.

Check: is there a product, service, or affiliate opportunity attached to this topic? If the answer is no, the channel works as a hobby but not as income.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The niche selection mistake I see most.

Picking based on what's trending right now, not what will still be relevant in 18 months.

Trend-based niches attract fast growth and fast declines. Topic-based niches grow slower and hold longer.

---
