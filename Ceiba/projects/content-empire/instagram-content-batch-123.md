## Post 2 — The 48-Hour Framework
**Hook:** You can have a product live and selling within 48 hours. Here is how.
**Slide 1:** You can have a product live and selling within 48 hours. Here is how.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Hour 1-4: Define the one specific problem you are solving. Not five problems. One. Write one sentence that describes who has it and what they need.
- Slide 3: Hour 5-12: Write the content. A guide, a framework, a checklist, a template. The format does not matter. The clarity does. Use Google Docs.
- Slide 4: Hour 13-24: Convert to PDF. Build a simple listing on Gumroad. Write a short description that names the pain and the outcome. Set a price.
- Slide 5: Hour 25-48: Post about it. Not a polished launch. A direct honest post: "I built this because I kept getting asked about it. Here is the link."
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. Day 1 beats day never.
**Caption:** The 48-hour timeline is not a challenge designed to make a point. It is a practical ceiling on the amount of time a first product needs. Every hour past 48 that you spend without shipping is an hour spent adding friction that does not add value. The product does not need to be perfect. It needs to be clear. Clear problem, clear solution, clear price, live link. The feedback you get from one real buyer who pays money and uses the product is worth more than three weeks of refining something privately. Ship the minimum honest version. Learn from who buys and why. Make the next version better.

## Post 3 — The Right Format for Your Skill
**Hook:** Not every skill becomes a course. Most should become something smaller.
**Slide 1:** Not every skill becomes a course. Most should become something smaller.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Course: comprehensive, high-price, high-effort to make and sell. Best for skills with a long learning curve.
- Slide 3: Guide or PDF: solves one specific problem, low price, fast to make, fast to sell. Best for skills that can produce a clear outcome quickly.
- Slide 4: Template or toolkit: a pre-built version of the thing your buyer would have to build themselves. Best for process-heavy skills.
- Slide 5: The fastest path to first revenue is always the format with the shortest gap between your skill and a product the buyer can use immediately.
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. Match the format to the skill.
**Caption:** The mistake most first-time product creators make is choosing the format that feels most impressive rather than the format that best serves the skill. A comprehensive course is the hardest thing to make, the hardest thing to sell, and the thing most likely to never ship. A focused PDF guide that solves one specific problem takes a day to write, hours to format, and can sell immediately. The goal of your first product is not to be your best product. It is to prove that people will pay you for what you know. The simplest format that honestly solves a real problem is almost always the right first product.

## Post 4 — Pricing Your First Product
**Hook:** Underpricing your first product is not humility. It is a business mistake.
**Slide 1:** Underpricing your first product is not humility. It is a business mistake.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: A product priced too low attracts buyers who expect free-level quality. It also attracts buyers who never use what they buy.
- Slide 3: The right price for a first product is the minimum amount that would make the buyer take it seriously enough to actually use it.
- Slide 4: For PDF guides and focused frameworks: $9 to $27 is the range where buyers treat it as a real resource, not a freebie.
- Slide 5: Your second version can be more expensive. Your first version should be priced for credibility, not profit.
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. Price it right from the start.
**Caption:** There is a psychological threshold below which a paid product starts to behave like a free one. When something is priced so low that it feels like nothing to spend, the buyer does not weight it the same way. They download it, forget about it, and do not build the outcome the product was designed to help them build. That means no transformation, no testimonial, no word of mouth. Your first product's price is not just about revenue. It is about who you attract and what they do with what they buy. Price it at a level where the buyer feels invested enough to actually use it.

## Post 5 — The Launch That Does Not Require a Launch
**Hook:** You do not need a launch. You need a link and a reason.
**Slide 1:** You do not need a launch. You need a link and a reason.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: A traditional product launch has a countdown, an email sequence, a promotional period, and a deadline. You do not need any of this for a first product.
- Slide 3: What you need: one honest post that explains why you made it, who it is for, and what it solves. With a direct link.
- Slide 4: The post is not marketing. It is an honest statement: "I kept getting asked about this, so I built it. Here is what it covers. Here is what it costs."
- Slide 5: First sales come from people who already trust you. You do not have to persuade them. You just have to tell them it exists.
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. Ship it. Then tell people it exists.
**Caption:** The mythology around product launches keeps more products from shipping than anything else. The idea that you need a proper launch to sell something means that everything that comes before the launch becomes a reason to delay. You do not need a waitlist, a presale period, an affiliate program, or a launch week. You need to make the thing, price it correctly, put it on a platform where money can change hands, and tell people it exists with enough clarity that the right person immediately knows it is for them. The rest is refinement. But none of the refinement matters until the product is real and available.
