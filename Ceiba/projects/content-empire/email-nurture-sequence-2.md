I launched a $49 product and a $9 product around the same time.

It wasn't.

The $9 product got three times the questions. Three times the replies. Three times the engagement. People who bought it actually read it, used it, and came back to tell me what happened.

The $9 product turned more people into buyers than the expensive one ever could. And buyers who get value become repeat customers. The math is better than it looks.

## Email 7: What I Told Myself Before Shipping

**Send:** Week 2, Thursday
**Subject line:** What I told myself before shipping my first thing
**From:** Kalani from Behike

---

Before I put the first product up for sale, I sat on it for two weeks.

I had it ready. I just kept finding reasons not to publish it. The design wasn't perfect. The price felt off. Maybe I should add one more section. Maybe I should wait until I had more credibility.

The real reason was simpler. I was afraid people would see it and think it was bad.

Not just the product. Me. That they'd conclude from one PDF that I didn't know what I was doing.

I eventually published it on a Thursday night. I checked the link three times to make sure it worked, then I closed my laptop and went to bed.

Nothing happened.

Not in a bad way. In a literal way. Nobody mocked it. Nobody wrote me to say it was garbage. The world did not respond with judgment. A few people bought it. A few said thank you. Most people never even saw it.

That's the thing nobody tells you. The audience in your head, the one that's watching and waiting to criticize, does not exist. Real people are busy. They're thinking about their own problems. If your thing isn't useful to them, they scroll past. That's it.

The fear of being judged kept me from shipping for two weeks. When I finally did, I learned that the judgment I was afraid of was entirely imaginary.

What are you waiting to ship?

Reply and tell me. I'm not going to give you a pep talk. I just want to know what it is.

Kalani

## Email 8: 3 Tools I Deleted Last Month

**Send:** Week 3, Monday
**Subject line:** 3 tools I deleted last month
**From:** Kalani from Behike

---

I deleted three tools last month. Here's what they were and why they went.

**Notion.** I had it set up with a full system. Linked databases. Templates. Views for every project. It took me longer to maintain the system than to do the actual work. Every time I wanted to capture something, I had to decide which database it belonged in. That friction was killing momentum.

**A project manager I won't name.** It required me to think in a specific way to use it correctly. My tasks had to fit its structure. That's backwards. The tool is supposed to fit your work, not the other way around.

**A productivity app.** One of those apps that gamifies your habits and sends you streaks. I spent more time thinking about the streak than about what I was actually building.

What replaced them: a plain text file for daily tasks, voice notes to BehiqueBot when I have an idea, and a single spreadsheet for anything money-related.

The rule I landed on: if I need a tutorial to start using it, it's too complex. The best tools are the ones you can explain in one sentence.

This applies to your personal operations too. Not just software. If your morning routine requires a checklist of forty steps to execute, it won't survive contact with a hard week.

Simple systems outlast complex ones. Always.

If you're looking for something that handles the "personal operations" layer without requiring you to become a Notion architect, the Solopreneur OS is what I use. One system. No tutorials needed to start.

## Email 9: A Framework I Learned From Building in Public

**Send:** Week 3, Thursday
**Subject line:** A framework I learned from building in public
**From:** Kalani from Behike

---

When I started sharing my work publicly, I noticed something.

The people who built sustainable things weren't just building products. They had a structure underneath. Five layers that worked together. I started calling it OPB, the One-Person Business framework.

Here's how I think about it:

**Vision.** The long answer to "why are you doing this." Not the business version. The real one. What life are you building, and for who. Without this layer, everything else eventually loses its direction.

**Domain.** The specific space you operate in. Your corner of the internet. The problem you solve for a specific kind of person. Broad domains are impossible to compete in. Narrow domains are where traction actually happens.

**Content.** How you show your thinking publicly. This is the trust layer. People don't buy from strangers. Content turns you from a stranger into someone they recognize and trust before you ever ask for anything.

**Monetization.** The products, services, and offers that convert trust into revenue. This layer is almost always simpler than people make it. Three solid offers beat thirty mediocre ones.

**Daily Ops.** How you actually execute every day. The systems, routines, and tools that make the other four layers run without burning you out. Most people skip this layer until they collapse.

The order matters. Vision first. Ops last. Most people try to do it backwards, starting with monetization before they know their domain or have built any trust.

I wrote the complete framework as a guide. Everything in it is based on what I've actually used, not theory pulled from a podcast.
