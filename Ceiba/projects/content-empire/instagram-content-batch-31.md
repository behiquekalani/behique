**Topic:** Welcome sequence structure

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
The first 5 emails you send someone decide if they buy from you.

Most people wing it.
Here is the structure that works.

---

**Slide 2:**
Email 1: Deliver what you promised.
Immediately. No delay.

If they signed up for a free guide, send the guide.
If you make them wait, they forget who you are.

---

**Slide 3:**
Email 2: Your story.
Not your full life story. The relevant one.

Why you built this, what you figured out, what you were wrong about before.
People buy from people they understand.

---

**Slide 4:**
Email 3: The belief shift.
Identify the biggest misconception in your niche and correct it.

This positions you as someone who thinks differently.
It earns trust faster than credentials do.

---

**Slide 5:**
Email 4: Social proof or case study.
Show a result. Yours or someone else's.

Concrete numbers beat vague claims every time.
"I made $X in Y days" beats "my method works."

---

**Slide 6:**
Email 5: The soft offer.
Introduce your paid product or service.
Make it feel like a natural next step, not a pitch.

Frame it as: "If you want to go further, here is what I built."

---

**Slide 7:**
Space these out over 5-7 days.
Consistency matters more than perfection.

A mediocre welcome sequence sent consistently beats a perfect one that never gets written.

---

**Slide 8:**
You do not need fancy software.
You need the right structure.

Most platforms (Beehiiv, MailerLite, ConvertKit) support automation sequences on free plans.

## POST 3 — CAROUSEL
**Label:** Carousel 03
**Post Time:** Saturday 10:00 AM
**Topic:** Subject lines that convert

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
Your email could be perfect.
If the subject line fails, no one reads it.

7 subject line formats that increase open rates.

---

**Slide 2:**
Format 1: The direct subject.
"How I got 500 subscribers in 30 days"

No tricks. No mystery. Just what is inside.
Works because most people are too clever with their subject lines.

---

**Slide 3:**
Format 2: The question.
"Are you making this email mistake?"

Questions create an open loop. The brain wants to close it.
Keep the question specific to your reader's actual problem.

---

**Slide 4:**
Format 3: The curiosity gap.
"The email habit that changed my open rates"

Tease the information without giving it away.
The gap between what they know and what they want to know pulls them in.

---

**Slide 5:**
Format 4: The contrarian take.
"Why posting more on social is hurting your sales"

Challenge a common belief.
If it is genuinely surprising, they open just to see if you are right.

---

**Slide 6:**
Format 5: The number.
"3 subject line mistakes costing you opens"

Specific numbers signal organized, digestible content.
People know they can read it fast.

---

**Slide 7:**
Format 6: The personal angle.
"I was wrong about this for 2 years"

Vulnerability earns attention.
It also signals honesty, which builds trust over time.

---

**Slide 8:**
Format 7: The urgency framing.
"This closes tomorrow"

Use sparingly. When overused, it trains people to ignore you.
Real deadlines only.

---

**Slide 9:**
Test your subject lines.
Most email platforms have A/B testing built in.
Test one variable at a time.

Better subject lines compound over months.

---

**Slide 10:**
The best subject line is the one your specific audience opens.
Start with these formats, then learn from your own data.

**Topic:** Email frequency

The question I get most about email marketing: how often should I send?

Here is the honest answer. More than you think.

Most people underestimate how little their audience thinks about them between emails.

You are not annoying them by showing up. You are staying relevant.

The people who unsubscribe when you email more often were never going to buy anyway. They were occupying space on your list.

The people who actually want what you offer will not unsubscribe because you email twice a week.

Start with once a week. Get consistent there first. Then test twice a week after 90 days.

Consistency at lower frequency beats inconsistency at high frequency every time.

## POST 7 — TEXT POST
**Label:** Text 01
**Post Time:** Tuesday 12:00 PM
**Topic:** List segmentation

Most people treat their email list like a single group.
Everyone gets the same email at the same time.

That is fine when you are starting out.
But once you have a few hundred subscribers, segmentation changes everything.

Segmentation means grouping subscribers based on what they care about, what they clicked on, or what stage they are at.

Someone who just subscribed needs different emails than someone who has been on your list for 6 months.

Someone interested in your free content is not ready for the same message as someone who clicked your sales page three times.

The goal is not to send more emails.
The goal is to send the right email to the right person.

You do not need complex software for this.
Even basic tags in most email platforms let you start simple segmentation.

Tag new subscribers. Tag people who click product links. Tag people who open every email.

Then write to those groups differently.

Relevance is what makes people stay on your list for years.
