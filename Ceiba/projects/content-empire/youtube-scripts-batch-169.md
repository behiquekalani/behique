## Script 6: Why Your Content Is Not Getting Distributed (And What to Fix)

**Hook (0-30s):**
You have been posting consistently. The content is good. The views are not coming. Before you blame the platform, look at these three specific things. One of them is almost certainly the problem.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Distribution failure is almost always a diagnosis problem before it is a strategy problem. Most creators jump to tactics -- posting more, changing formats, switching platforms -- before they understand why the content they already have is not spreading.

There is a checklist I run through when a creator's content is underperforming. It is not complicated. But it requires being honest about things most creators avoid. This video walks through that checklist and gives you the exact questions to ask yourself about your own content.

**Main Content:**

### Retention Rate: The One Number That Matters Most

Pull up your analytics right now. Look at average view duration as a percentage of total video length. If it is under 40%, that is the problem. The platform is accurately reading that your content is not holding attention. It will not push content that people leave.

Watch your own video with fresh eyes. Where do you lose interest? That is where your viewer is leaving too. The fix is almost always in the first 90 seconds. Most videos bury the value too deep. Move the most interesting part earlier.

A practical rule: whatever you plan to say in minute five, say a version of it in the first 45 seconds. Tease the payoff. Give people a reason to stay.

### The Hook-Value Mismatch

The second distribution killer is when the hook promises something the content does not deliver. You write an intriguing title. The viewer clicks. The video does not match the promise. They leave in 30 seconds and the algorithm marks this content as bait.

This is harder to diagnose because it requires you to read your own title as if you are a stranger. Does your video actually deliver what that title implies? Not a version of it. Exactly it.

If your title is "How I Made $10K in 30 Days With One Product," your video better be a specific walkthrough of exactly that. Not a general guide to digital products. Not "here are five ways to make money." The exact thing you promised.

### Niche Drift and Audience Confusion

The third issue is niche drift. When a channel covers multiple unrelated topics, the algorithm has trouble knowing who to recommend it to. A viewer who comes for financial content does not want fashion content next. They leave. The algorithm stops pushing.

The fix is not always to delete old content. It is to make a decision going forward: what is this channel actually about? One clear answer. Every video for the next three months should fit that answer precisely.

### The Share Test

Ask yourself this about every piece of content you make: who would send this to someone else, and why? If you cannot answer that specifically, the content will not spread organically. The share is the highest signal the algorithm can receive. Content worth sharing gets pushed.

The content most likely to be shared is: content that says something true in a way the reader has never heard, content that solves a specific problem someone already has, or content that makes someone look smart or generous for sharing it.

**Conclusion:**
Check your retention rate. Audit your hook-content match. Decide what your channel is about and commit to it. Ask the share question before you publish. Fix these four things and distribution will follow. Subscribe for more tactical breakdowns like this.

## Script 7: The Sales Page Framework Every Creator Needs

**Hook (0-30s):**
There is a structural reason why most sales pages do not convert. It is not the product. It is not the price. It is that the page is written in the wrong order. Here is the order that works.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
A sales page is a conversation. Specifically, it is the conversation your ideal customer is already having inside their head about the problem your product solves. Your job as the writer is to join that conversation at exactly the right moment and show that you understand it better than they do.

When you write a sales page without understanding that conversation, you end up with a page that talks about your product instead of your buyer's problem. That is why it does not convert. This framework fixes that.

**Main Content:**

### Start With the Conversation Already Happening

Your ideal customer is not thinking about your product. They are thinking about a problem they have, an outcome they want, or a frustration they keep running into. Your opening sentence should reflect that thought back at them.

Not: "Introducing the Digital Business Accelerator." But: "If you have tried three different online business models and none of them worked, here is what they all had in common." That second sentence enters the conversation. The first one announces a product to someone who was not asking.

Map the inner conversation before you write a word of copy. Write down: what is my buyer thinking about at 10pm? What do they Google when they have this problem? What have they tried before? What made it fail? Your opening should echo the answers to those questions.

### The Credibility Bridge

After the opening, you need to establish why you are the person to solve this problem. Not with a formal bio. With a paragraph that shows you have been where they are.

"I spent two years trying to get traction with content before I understood the one thing missing. Not tactics. Not a better niche. A system for turning attention into sales." That paragraph does two things: establishes shared experience and positions the solution as something specific and learnable.

The credibility bridge is not about credentials. It is about demonstrating that you understand the problem from the inside.

### The Offer Stack

Once you have established the problem and your credibility, you introduce the product as the solution to the specific problem you have been describing. Not all problems. That problem.

Then you build the offer stack. What is included. What each piece does. What it would cost to get each piece separately elsewhere. The goal is not to inflate perceived value with fake numbers. It is to show that every element of the product has a reason to exist and serves the transformation you are promising.

End the offer stack with the price positioned as obviously fair given what has been described. Not cheap. Not expensive. Obviously fair.

### The Close

The close is where most people either push too hard or not hard enough. A strong close does three things: restates the transformation one more time, addresses the final objection (usually "is this right for me"), and gives a clear, friction-free call to action.

Do not add urgency unless it is real. Fake countdown timers destroy trust. If there is a genuine reason to act now -- a price increase, a cohort deadline, limited spots -- use it. If there is not, just close clearly and let the product do the work.

**Conclusion:**
Join the conversation. Build the credibility bridge. Stack the offer around the transformation. Close clearly. That is the framework. It works because it follows the logic of how people make buying decisions, not how writers prefer to present products. Subscribe for more on writing and selling digital products.

## Script 8: Side Business First Principles

**Hook (0-30s):**
Before you build anything, you need to answer three questions. Get these wrong and you can work for a year and end up with nothing. Get them right and the path to your first $1,000 is shorter than you think.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Most side business advice starts with tactics. Pick a niche. Build an audience. Create a product. But tactics without principles are just a list of things to do without knowing why you are doing them.

First principles are the answers to the questions that do not change regardless of platform or trend. Who is this for. What do they actually want. Why would they buy it from you specifically. If you can answer those three questions clearly and honestly, the tactics become obvious. If you cannot, no amount of tactics will help.

This video is first-principles thinking applied to building a side business while employed.

**Main Content:**

### Who Is This For

The biggest mistake in side business building is defining the audience too broadly. "People who want to make money online" is not an audience. "25-to-35-year-old professionals with a marketable skill who want to build a digital product business without quitting their jobs" is an audience.

The more specific your audience definition, the easier every other decision becomes. What to create. Where to find them. What words to use. What price point makes sense. Specificity is not a limitation. It is a precision tool.

Write a one-paragraph description of the specific person your business serves. Not a category. A person. Their job, their frustration, their goal, what they have tried before. Make it so specific that if you met that person, you would immediately recognize them.

### What Do They Actually Want

This is different from what you think they need. What they want is the outcome they would describe to a friend. Not the technical process. The result.

They do not want "a 12-module video course on content creation." They want to be the kind of person who makes money creating content. The course is just the mechanism. Lead with the want, not the mechanism.

The way to know what they actually want is to talk to them. Not survey them. Talk to them. Ask what they are trying to accomplish. Ask what they have tried. Ask what stopped working. The language they use in those conversations is the language that should appear in your marketing.

### Why You Specifically

This is the question most people skip because it feels uncomfortable. Why would someone buy this from you and not from the thousands of other people creating content in your space?

The answer is almost never credentials. It is almost always point of view. The way you think about the problem is different. Your specific combination of experience is unique. The way you explain things connects with a particular type of person.

Your job is to make that point of view explicit and consistent across everything you create. Over time, people will not just buy your products. They will buy your perspective. That is the foundation of a brand that compounds.

### The Minimum Viable Business

Once you can answer all three questions, the minimum viable business is: one product, one platform, one clear audience. Not a suite of products, an optimized funnel, and a team. One thing that works.

Get that one thing to $1,000 in revenue. Then $3,000. Then $5,000. The complexity can come later. The foundation has to be simple enough that you can actually build it in the time you have available.

**Conclusion:**
Answer the three questions. Write the specific audience paragraph. Lead with the want. Make your point of view explicit. Build the minimum viable business before you build anything else. Subscribe for the next step.

## Script 9: Membership Site Mistakes That Kill Retention

**Hook (0-30s):**
Most membership sites that fail do not fail at launch. They fail at month three. Here is why that happens and exactly what you need to build before your first member ever signs up.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
I want to talk about the gap between membership site launch and membership site sustainability. Because those are two completely different problems and most people only plan for the first one.

Launching a membership is relatively easy. You create a compelling offer, set up a payment page, bring in founding members at a lower price, and feel great for about six weeks. Then cancellations start. Not because people are unhappy with what they got. Because the value of staying is no longer obvious.

This video is about the retention problem. What causes it. What prevents it. And what you need to have in place before you ever open the doors.

**Main Content:**

### The Transformation Gap

The reason people stay in a membership is the same reason they joined: they believe it will help them get a result. When that belief weakens, they cancel. Your retention strategy is essentially a belief maintenance system.

Every month, your members should be able to point to something specific that moved them closer to the result they came for. Not "I got access to a lot of good content." Specific progress. "I launched my first product." "I hit 500 subscribers." "I landed my first client."

If your membership does not have clear milestones or progress indicators, you are relying on ambient value to retain members. Ambient value does not retain anyone.

### What Month Three Actually Looks Like

Month one: members are excited. They consume everything. They participate in the community. They feel like they made a good decision.

Month two: the initial energy fades. They start to assess. Is this keeping its promise? Is the new content worth the continued payment?

Month three: the calculation happens. "What did I actually get from this that I could not get elsewhere for less money?" If they cannot answer that question clearly, they cancel.

Your job is to make that question easy to answer. Make the wins visible. Celebrate progress publicly. Create structured programs that give members a reason to keep showing up for the next 30 days.

### The Content Trap

The most common mistake in memberships is treating them as content subscriptions. "You get new videos every week" is a promise that works for Netflix. It does not work for a $49/month professional membership because the question is always "are these videos worth $49 this month."

The better promise is transformation over time. "Every month you get the tools, community, and accountability to make measurable progress on your business." That is harder to cancel because the value is cumulative, not episodic.

The content is still there. But it serves the transformation, not the other way around.

### Community as the Retention Engine

The single most effective retention mechanism in a membership is a community where members know each other by name. Not a Slack workspace with 200 silent lurkers. An active space where relationships form.

Relationships are sticky. Content is not. When a member has three people in your community they consider peers, their cancellation decision becomes social as well as financial. That changes the math significantly.

Build the community intentionally. Weekly calls. Member spotlights. Collaborative challenges. Make showing up rewarding in ways that content alone cannot replicate.

**Conclusion:**
Fix the transformation gap. Prepare for month three before you launch. Stop selling content and start selling progress. Build real community. These four things separate memberships that compound from memberships that collapse. Subscribe for more on building recurring revenue businesses.

## Script 10: The Solopreneur Operating System

**Hook (0-30s):**
Most solopreneurs are not actually running a business. They are freelancing with extra steps. The difference is whether you have a system that runs without you or a set of tasks that only happen when you push them. Here is how to build the system.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
I want to define the solopreneur operating system clearly because most people treat it as a productivity question when it is actually an architecture question. It is not about being more organized or working more hours. It is about designing a business structure that does certain things automatically so you can spend your energy on the work that cannot be automated.

The businesses that scale to $10K per month as a solopreneur have a few things in common: they have traffic coming in without requiring daily manual effort, they have a product catalog that serves multiple stages of the buyer journey, and they have systems that handle the repetitive parts of marketing and delivery. This video is about how to build each of those.

**Main Content:**

### The Traffic Layer

Sustainable solopreneur businesses have at least one source of traffic that does not require you to show up every day. That means either organic search (blog posts, YouTube videos, Pinterest pins that rank and send traffic for years), email referral (an audience that forwards your content to others), or SEO-driven content that compounds over time.

The traffic layer takes the longest to build but pays the most long-term dividends. A blog post written in year one can still bring buyers in year five. A YouTube video with 50,000 views can generate sales every day without you touching it.

Most solopreneurs underinvest in the traffic layer because the returns are slow and invisible. They chase social media metrics instead. Social media gives you reach. Search and email give you compounding leverage.

### The Product Ladder

Your product catalog should have at least three layers: a low-cost entry product ($10-47), a mid-tier primary product ($97-297), and a high-end option ($500+). Each serves a different buyer at a different stage of trust.

The entry product converts curious followers into paying customers. The mid-tier product is where most of your revenue lives. The high-end option serves buyers who want more support and are willing to pay for it.

Most solopreneurs build one product and wonder why revenue is volatile. The product ladder creates multiple conversion points and makes revenue from any given audience segment significantly higher.

### The Delivery System

Digital products are inherently scalable. But the delivery experience still requires attention. A product that is hard to navigate, poorly organized, or unsupported feels low quality even if the content is excellent.

Set up automated onboarding sequences. Send new buyers a welcome email immediately. Give them a clear first step. Create a quick-win moment in the first 10 minutes of consuming your product. The customer who gets a win fast becomes a repeat buyer and a referral source.

### The Weekly Operating Rhythm

One piece of content per week minimum. One email to your list per week minimum. One product improvement per month. One new experiment per quarter. That is the operating rhythm of a sustainable solopreneur business.

Not daily posting across six platforms. Not 20 videos per month. One high-quality piece of content that compounds, distributed consistently over a long time period.

**Conclusion:**
Build the traffic layer even when it feels slow. Build the product ladder before you need it. Make delivery automatic and excellent. Keep the operating rhythm sustainable. That is the system. Subscribe for more on building a one-person business that runs without burning you out.
