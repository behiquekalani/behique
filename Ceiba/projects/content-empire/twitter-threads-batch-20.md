## Thread 2: "I tracked my emotional state every morning for 90 days. Here is what I found."

1/ I tracked my emotional state every morning for 90 days.

Not moods. Operational state. Here is what I found. (thread)

2/ The format was simple:

One number, 1 to 10. Written before I opened any app.

Not "how do I feel" but "what am I capable of today."

3/ The pattern that showed up in the first two weeks:

Monday and Tuesday were reliably high.
Thursday was the worst day of the week, almost without exception.

I had no idea until I had the data.

4/ What Thursday had in common:

It was the day decisions had been deferred to all week.

Everything I avoided Monday through Wednesday landed on Thursday.

The state was not random. It was accumulated weight.

5/ What changed after I saw the pattern:

I started clearing decisions on Monday.

Not acting on everything. Just deciding. Even "I am not doing this" is a decision.

Unresolved decisions have a cost that shows up in your body before your mind.

6/ The second finding:

Output quality did not correlate with state as much as I expected.

Some of my best work happened on 4/10 days.

The difference: on high-state days I started more. On low-state days I finished more.

7/ Both are necessary. Finishing on low-state days is a skill.

You do not need to feel ready to ship. Readiness is not a prerequisite for output.

## Thread 4: "How prediction markets work and why they beat polls by a large margin"

1/ How prediction markets work and why they beat polls by a large margin.

Not theory. Mechanism. (thread)

2/ A poll asks: what do you think will happen?

A prediction market asks: what will you bet on?

That difference changes everything about the quality of the answer.

3/ Why polls fail:

They have no cost for being wrong.

Someone can tell a pollster they believe something while privately thinking the opposite.

There is no penalty. The signal is corrupted by the absence of stakes.

4/ Why prediction markets work:

Every dollar you put in is a vote with consequences.

If you are wrong, you lose money.

That incentive filters out noise. People who do not actually know stop betting.

5/ The mechanism:

You buy shares of an outcome.
If the outcome happens, each share pays out $1.
The current price of a share is the market's implied probability.

Share at $0.73 = 73% probability in the eyes of the market.

6/ The historical accuracy:

Prediction markets beat polls on election outcomes by significant margins.

They also called COVID spread, tech stock movements, and policy decisions faster than traditional forecasts.

7/ Where the opportunity is:

Most people treat these markets as gambling.

The people making consistent returns treat them as research with a payoff attached.

Knowing more than the market average is the edge.
