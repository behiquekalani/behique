## Post 2 — The Compound Curve in Content

**Hook:** Your growth curve in content does not look like a straight line. It looks like nothing, then a cliff.

**Slide 1:** Your growth curve in content does not look like a straight line. It looks like nothing, then a cliff.

**Slide 2:** Month 1-6: Flat. Almost nothing. You question everything.
Month 7-12: Slow, uneven growth. Occasional spikes. Back to baseline.
Month 13-18: The baseline starts rising. Spikes become more frequent. Old posts keep bringing in traffic.
Month 18+: The cliff. Growth becomes self-sustaining.

**Slide 3:** The flat phase is the most dangerous. It is where most people quit.
They do not know they are 6 months from the cliff. They just see flatline and assume failure.
The flat phase is not failure. It is loading.

**Slide 4:** What is actually happening in the flat phase:
- Your content library is building mass
- The algorithm is cataloging your consistency
- Your audience is learning to trust you
- Your skills are compounding silently
None of it is visible in the metrics until it suddenly is.

**Slide 5:** The creators who hit the cliff are not the ones with the best content in month 3.
They are the ones still posting in month 12.
The cliff belongs to the people who did not quit during the flat phase.

**Slide 6:** Understand the curve. Stay through the flat. @behikeai documents the whole thing. Follow.

**Caption:** The compound curve in content creation is real, predictable, and almost nobody actually believes it until they experience it.

Here is what makes it hard to believe: the flat phase feels like evidence that the curve is not coming. You look at your metrics every day. You see single-digit saves and 40 views on a post you spent two hours on. That feels like a verdict.

It is not. It is phase one of the curve.

The physics of compound growth require a base before the curve bends. You are building the base in the flat phase. Every post adds to the library. Every day of consistency adds to the algorithm's trust in you. Every piece of content that does not perform is still a data point, still a skill rep, still a layer in the foundation.

The cliff is the moment the base is large enough that growth becomes self-sustaining. New posts surface old posts. Old posts send people to your profile. Your profile converts them to followers. Followers share content. The loop closes.

You cannot skip to the cliff. You have to build the base first.

Stay flat. The cliff is coming.

## Post 3 — What Consistent Creators Do Differently Every Week

**Hook:** Consistent creators are not more disciplined than you. They have better systems than you.

**Slide 1:** Consistent creators are not more disciplined than you. They have better systems than you.

**Slide 2:** The myth: consistent creators are motivated all the time.
The reality: they have systems that remove the decision of whether to post.
The decision was made when the system was built. On post day, the system executes. There is no debate.

**Slide 3:** What those systems look like in practice:
- A fixed content block on the calendar (non-negotiable, same time each week)
- A running idea capture system (nothing is lost, nothing has to be recalled from memory)
- A content format template (so creation is editing, not blank-page starting)
- A batching habit (create 3-5 posts in one session, not one at a time)

**Slide 4:** Without systems, consistency requires willpower every single day. Willpower runs out.
With systems, consistency is the default. You have to actively break it, which takes more effort than continuing.

**Slide 5:** Build the system once. Follow it indefinitely.
The system compounds. The willpower does not.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai posts the systems behind the content. Follow for the infrastructure, not just the output.

**Caption:** Discipline gets too much credit. Systems do not get enough.

When you look at a creator who has published 500 posts, you might attribute it to exceptional discipline. Sometimes that is true. More often, the real explanation is that they built a system that made quitting harder than continuing.

They batch-create content on Sunday afternoons. That time slot has been blocked for 18 months. Missing it creates friction in their week. The system creates a pull toward production rather than a push.

Their idea capture is a simple running note in their phone. Every time they think of a content angle, a question from a comment, or a problem they solved that week, it goes into the note. By Sunday, they have 8-12 raw ideas to work from. They never stare at a blank page.

Their format is templated. Hook. Problem. Insight. Proof. CTA. Every carousel follows this spine. They are not reinventing the structure each time. They are filling it with new content.

These are not complex systems. They are simple, specific, and consistent.

Build the system this week. It takes two hours. It pays for itself for years.

## Post 5 — The Niche Depth Advantage

**Hook:** Being the most consistent person in a small niche beats being inconsistent in a big one every time.

**Slide 1:** Being the most consistent person in a small niche beats being inconsistent in a big one every time.

**Slide 2:** The logic of going wide: more people in the audience means more potential followers.
The reality of going wide: more competition, diluted authority, no clear reason for anyone to follow you specifically.
The logic of going deep: less competition, faster authority, a specific audience that actively seeks you out.

**Slide 3:** Consistency plus niche specificity compounds faster than broad content with sporadic posting.
Example: 3 posts a week on "AI tools for freelancers" for 12 months builds a specific, valuable audience faster than 1 post a week on "digital business" for 3 years.

**Slide 4:** When you are consistent in a specific niche long enough, something shifts.
People in that niche stop finding you accidentally. They seek you out.
They look for your take on new developments. They share your content within their niche communities. You become the default reference point.
That status is not achievable through broad content.

**Slide 5:** Authority requires specificity. Consistency requires sustainability.
The intersection of a specific niche you can talk about indefinitely and a posting cadence you can sustain indefinitely is where compound authority lives.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. Niche: AI tools and digital business for independent builders. Consistent since day one. Follow.

**Caption:** The fastest path to authority in content is not the widest path. It is the deepest one.

A creator who posts 4 times a week on a specific topic for 12 months will have dramatically more authority in that space than a creator who posts once a week on anything that feels relevant.

The first creator has 208 pieces of content on one topic. The second has 52 pieces on 20 different topics. The algorithm can categorize the first creator immediately. The audience can describe the first creator immediately. That clarity compounds.

Niche depth also solves one of the hardest problems in content: what to post about. When your niche is specific, ideas are everywhere in your daily life. Every tool you try, every problem you solve, every insight you have in your domain is potential content. You never stare at a blank page wondering what to say.

Broad content requires constantly generating ideas from scratch. Deep niche content requires paying attention to your own experience in a domain you know well.

The compound advantage of consistent, specific content is not theoretical. It is the mechanism behind every authority account you admire.

Go deep. Stay consistent. Watch the authority compound.
