## Threads

---

**Thread 203-T1: Information asymmetry as a business model**

1/ Every successful creator, consultant, and educator is in the same business.
They know something their audience doesn't.
The product is closing that gap.

2/ This sounds obvious until you realize most people give away their asymmetry for free.
They post the answer, the shortcut, the framework on social media.
Then wonder why nobody buys.

3/ The free content is bait. It proves you know things.
The paid product is the complete system.
The distinction matters.
Give enough to be believed. Charge for the part that actually transforms.

4/ The question to ask yourself: what do I know that takes most people years to figure out?
What mistakes did I make that I could help someone skip?
What sequence did I figure out through trial and error?

5/ That's your product.
Not a course about everything you know.
A specific shortcut through a specific painful problem.

---

**Thread 203-T2: How to find your information asymmetry**

1/ Most people don't know what their asymmetry is because they're too close to it.
Here's how to find it.

2/ Method 1: Look at what you get asked.
What questions do people in your network ask you that feel completely obvious to answer?
That's a signal. The obvious answer to you is non-obvious to them.

3/ Method 2: Look at what you got wrong before you got it right.
The path between "wrong" and "right" is a product.
People pay to skip the wrong part.

4/ Method 3: Look at what took you the longest.
If something took you 18 months to figure out, someone would pay to get there in 6.
You're selling time compression, not just information.

5/ Method 4: Look at what people in your industry get consistently wrong.
You see the pattern. They're inside it.
Outside perspective on an inside problem is some of the most valuable information that exists.

---

**Thread 203-T3: Why "everyone already knows this" is almost always false**

1/ The biggest mistake smart people make: assuming their knowledge is common.
It isn't.

2/ The curse of knowledge is real.
Once you understand something deeply, it's nearly impossible to remember not knowing it.
You assume others have the same baseline. They don't.

3/ Example: a developer who's been building SaaS for 8 years assumes everyone knows how to think about database schema.
They don't.
An 8-year shortcut is sitting there unmonetized.

4/ The test is simple: go find a forum, subreddit, or comment section full of people at an earlier stage than you.
Look at what questions repeat.
Those questions represent problems people are willing to pay to solve.

5/ Your knowledge looks ordinary from the inside.
From the outside, it's a map through territory they've never been in.
Sell the map.

---

**Thread 203-T4: The information product that actually sells**

1/ Not all information products are equal.
Here's the difference between the ones that sell and the ones that sit at zero downloads.

2/ The one that doesn't sell: comprehensive knowledge dump.
"Everything I know about X."
People don't want everything. They want the specific part that solves their specific problem.

3/ The one that sells: specific outcome, specific person, specific timeframe.
"How I went from $0 to $3K/month selling digital products in 90 days, and the exact steps in sequence."
That's a story with a beginning, middle, and actionable end.

4/ Add the element of skipping pain.
"Without cold outreach. Without an existing audience. Without a course platform subscription."
The "without" is often as important as the "with."
People buy relief from specific fears as much as they buy specific outcomes.

5/ Structure your asymmetry as: here's where you are, here's where you want to be, here's the gap I crossed, here's how you cross it faster.
That's the product. Everything else is packaging.

---

**Thread 203-T5: Pricing information correctly**

1/ Most people underprice information products.
Not because they lack confidence, but because they price based on what it cost them to learn, not what it's worth to the buyer.

2/ It cost you time and mistakes to learn what you know.
Time and mistakes have no market value.
What has market value: the outcome the buyer gets, the time they save, the pain they avoid.

3/ Price based on outcome value, not on your effort.
A $27 guide that saves someone 6 months of wrong turns is worth $27.
A $297 course that helps someone land a $5K client is worth $297.
The ratio is what matters.

4/ Rule of thumb: price at 1-10% of the outcome's value.
If the outcome is worth $10K to the buyer, $97-$997 is defensible.
If the outcome is worth $500, price accordingly.

5/ Most creators price based on what they'd pay.
Buyers price based on what they want.
The gap between those two perceptions is where underprice happens.
Ask what the outcome is worth. Start from there.
