## Post 1 — The Audience-First Strategy is Keeping You Broke

**Hook:** Building an audience before you have a product is not a strategy. It's a delay mechanism.

**Slide 1:** Building an audience before you have a product is not a strategy. It's a delay mechanism.

**Slide 2:** The conventional advice:
"Grow your audience first. Then monetize."

Who this works for:
Media companies with investors and runway.

Who this doesn't work for:
Solo builders who need revenue in 90 days.

**Slide 3:** The real sequence:
1. Identify a problem you've already solved
2. Build a small product that solves it for others
3. Attract an audience through content about that problem
4. Sell the product as the natural next step

**Slide 4:** Notice the order.
Product before audience.
Not because the product is more important.
Because the product gives your content a destination.

Without a destination, your content is just entertainment.
Entertainment doesn't pay the bills.

**Slide 5:** The creator with 100 followers and a product will always out-earn the creator with 10,000 followers and no offer.

Small, engaged, product-aware audience beats large, passive, no-offer audience every time.

**Slide 6:** Build the product. Then grow the audience around it.
That's the sequence.

## Post 3 — The "Product Validation Before Audience" Method

**Hook:** You don't need an audience to validate a product. You need five honest conversations.

**Slide 1:** You don't need an audience to validate a product. You need five honest conversations.

**Slide 2:** Pre-sale validation in 7 days:
Day 1-2: Write a one-paragraph description of the product
Day 3-4: DM 10 people in your target market
Day 5-6: Have 5 honest conversations about the problem
Day 7: Decide to build or pivot

**Slide 3:** What to say in the DM:
"Hey, I'm building something for [type of person]. I think the main problem they have is [X]. Does that match your experience?"

You're not selling. You're listening.

**Slide 4:** The three responses to listen for:
1. "That's exactly the problem I have." (Build it.)
2. "The real problem is actually Y." (Pivot to Y.)
3. "I don't really have that problem." (Wrong audience.)

**Slide 5:** The validation test before launch:
Write a pre-sale page.
Post it to a small group (10-50 people).
Offer a discount for early buyers.
If 3 people pay: you have a product.
If 0 people pay: you have research.

**Slide 6:** Validate before you build the audience.
The product tells you who the audience is.
behike.gumroad.com | Link in bio.

**Caption:** Most creators wait until they have an audience before they try to validate a product. This is backwards because an audience tells you what they like, not what they'll pay for.

The people who tell you your post is great are not the same people who will pay $29 for the product version of that post. The overlap is smaller than you think.

Real validation is a transaction. Pre-orders, beta purchases, paid pilots. Until money moves, you don't know if the product idea is real.

Five honest conversations and one pre-sale page will tell you more about your product's viability than six months of audience building and engagement tracking.

Validate the product first. Build the content around the validated idea. Grow the audience that already has the problem your product solves.

The sequence is backwards from what most people teach. But it's the sequence that produces revenue instead of followers.

## Post 4 — Small Audience, Big Revenue. The Math Nobody Talks About.

**Hook:** 1,000 true fans is too many. You only need 100.

**Slide 1:** 1,000 true fans is too many. You only need 100.

**Slide 2:** Kevin Kelly said 1,000 true fans = a sustainable business.
That math is based on $100/year per fan.

But if you sell a $49 product:
100 fans x $49 = $4,900
That's 5 sales a month to 20 different fans each year.

**Slide 3:** The smaller audience, higher product math:
50 followers with a $97 product = $4,850
200 followers with a $29 product = $5,800
The follower count is almost irrelevant.
What matters is the match between the offer and the person.

**Slide 4:** Why most creators chase the wrong metric:
Follower counts are visible and public.
Revenue is private.
So we optimize for the thing people can see.

The business doesn't care about what's visible. It cares about what converts.

**Slide 5:** The signal that your 100 fans are the right 100:
- They comment with specific, detailed responses
- They DM you about the exact problem your product solves
- They share your content with people who have the same problem

If you have 100 followers doing this, you have a business.

**Slide 6:** Stop chasing followers. Start finding the right 100.

A creator with 500 followers and a product that perfectly matches their audience can out-earn a creator with 50,000 followers and no offer. This is not rare. It happens all the time. The data is just private.

The reason nobody talks about it is that there's no vanity metric for revenue. You can't see another creator's Gumroad dashboard. You can see their follower count. So the social proof all points toward growth, not toward product-market fit.

Product-market fit with a small audience is the most underrated position in the creator economy. You know exactly who your buyer is. You can talk to them. You can build exactly what they need. And you don't need anyone's algorithm to cooperate.

## Post 5 — The One Question That Fixes Your Content Strategy

**Hook:** One question will fix your entire content strategy. Most creators have never asked it.

**Slide 1:** One question will fix your entire content strategy. Most creators have never asked it.

**Slide 2:** The question:
"What does someone need to believe to buy my product?"

Not: "What content performs best?"
Not: "What does the algorithm reward?"
Not: "What do I want to talk about?"

**Slide 3:** Why this question matters:
Every piece of content you publish should move someone toward a belief that makes your product obvious.

If your product is a writing system, the belief is: "My current writing process is inefficient and replaceable."

Content that doesn't move toward that belief is noise.

**Slide 4:** How to reverse-engineer your content from this question:
1. List the 5 beliefs someone needs to buy your product
2. Make each belief the thesis of a content series
3. Publish until those beliefs are planted
4. Introduce the product as the logical conclusion

**Slide 5:** This is not manipulation.
Manipulation is changing what people believe to serve you.
This is alignment.
You're finding people who already suspect the problem exists and showing them you've solved it.

**Slide 6:** Build content with a destination.
The product is the destination.
behike.gumroad.com | Link in bio.

**Caption:** Content strategy without a product at the center is just editorial planning. It looks like a strategy. It has calendars and themes and posting cadences. But it has no commercial logic connecting it to any outcome.

The question "what does someone need to believe to buy my product" is the most important strategic question in the solopreneur toolkit. It forces you to think backward from the sale.

Every belief becomes a content series. Every content series moves a specific reader from awareness to conviction. By the time you mention the product, it doesn't feel like a pitch. It feels like the obvious answer to a problem they've been thinking about for weeks.

This is how small audiences produce disproportionate revenue. The content is targeted with surgical precision. Every post does work. There's no filler.

Map out the five beliefs your buyer needs to have. Build a content series around each one. Watch the difference.
