The lesson is still relevant. The audience that needs it hasn't found it yet. The content upgrade is the practice of taking your best old ideas and making them findable, shareable, and current again. ## Script 2: The Four Ways to Upgrade Old Content
**Hook (0-3s):** "There are four ways to take one old piece of content and make it work harder. You've probably done zero of them."
**Body:** One: update the hook. The idea is good, the opening line is weak. Rewrite it. Two: change the format. A text post becomes a video. A video becomes a carousel. Three: add current context. Reference something that happened recently that makes the old idea more relevant now. Four: extend it. Take the first point from an old post and make it its own full piece. One old post becomes four pieces of new content. You don't need more ideas. You need more ways to package the ideas you already have.
**CTA:** Save this and try it on your best old post this week.
**On-screen text:**
- "Update hook / change format / add context / extend"
- "One old post = four new pieces"

- "Repackage before you create new"

## Script 3: The Evergreen Test
**Hook (0-3s):** "Not all old content is worth upgrading. Here's a 30-second test to find which pieces are."
**Body:** Ask three questions. One: does the core insight still hold true? If the advice changed, don't upgrade it. Two: is the problem it addresses still relevant? If nobody has that problem anymore, skip it. Three: did it perform reasonably well or have you simply not distributed it properly? If it resonated with anyone, it can resonate with more people. Content that passes all three questions is worth an upgrade. Content that fails any of them gets archived. Be selective. Upgrade the best 20%, not everything.
**CTA:** Go check your analytics right now. Find the top three posts from six months ago.
**On-screen text:**
- "Still true? Still relevant? Under-distributed?"
- "Pass all 3 = worth upgrading"
- "Upgrade the top 20%, archive the rest"
- "30 seconds of analysis before you create"

## Script 4: How to Make Content Evergreen From the Start
**Hook (0-3s):** "Most creators make content that expires in 72 hours. The ones who win build content designed to last."
**Body:** Evergreen content is not about trends or news. It addresses permanent human problems. People will always want to earn more money. People will always want better health. People will always want clearer thinking and stronger relationships. If your content addresses a timeless problem, it never fully expires. The principle: write the date out of your content. Avoid phrases like "right now" or "in 2024." Speak to the problem, not the moment. The moment passes. The problem doesn't.
**CTA:** Follow to build a content system that compounds over time.
**On-screen text:**
- "Timeless problems = evergreen content"
- "Write the date out of your content"
- "The moment passes. The problem stays."
- "Compound content vs. disposable content"

## Script 5: Repurposing Is Not Copy-Paste
**Hook (0-3s):** "Repurposing does not mean posting the same thing in five places. That's not a strategy."
**Body:** Real repurposing means understanding what each platform rewards and translating the core idea into that format. A long essay becomes a thread. The thread becomes a short video. The video becomes a carousel. The carousel becomes an email. The email becomes a chapter. The core idea is the same. The format, hook, and delivery method are completely different. Each version serves a different audience at a different attention level. Most people copy-paste and wonder why it doesn't work. The translation is the work.
**CTA:** Save this for your next repurposing session.
**On-screen text:**
- "Same idea. Different translation per platform."
- "Format = the work, not an afterthought"
- "Copy-paste is not repurposing"
- "Each platform has its own language"

## Script 6: The Best Content You've Already Written
**Hook (0-3s):** "Your best piece of content is probably sitting in your drafts, unfinished, from eight months ago."
**Body:** We have a bias toward new ideas. The half-finished post from last year feels stale to us. To a reader seeing it for the first time, it doesn't. Go through your drafts. Your notes app. Your saved documents. Find the idea you started and didn't finish because it felt "too big" or "not perfect yet." That's usually the best one. The ones we protect most are the ones we care about most. Protecting them indefinitely means the world never gets them. Imperfect and published beats perfect and invisible.
**CTA:** Go open your drafts right now. Post the one you've been avoiding.

## Script 7: The Longtail Advantage of Old Content
**Hook (0-3s):** "A piece of content you made 18 months ago can bring you a customer today. Do you know which one it is?"

They buy. This doesn't happen with trending content. It happens with specific, searchable, timeless content. The more specific and thorough your old content is, the more likely it continues working long after you posted it. Most short-form content is designed to last 48 hours. Embed specific, searchable language into it and it lasts years.

## Script 8: The Content Upgrade System (Run It Monthly)
**Hook (0-3s):** "Spend 20% of your content time upgrading old work. Watch your output double without doubling your effort."
**Body:** One day per month. Pull your top 10 pieces from six to twelve months ago. Run the evergreen test. Pick three to upgrade. Update the hook. Add a current reference. Change the format. Redistribute to two new places. That's it. Ten pieces reviewed. Three upgraded. Six new versions created. Distributed across three platforms. For about three to four hours of work. This system compounds. The more content you produce, the larger your archive, the more upgrade material you have each month. Your library becomes your competitive advantage.
