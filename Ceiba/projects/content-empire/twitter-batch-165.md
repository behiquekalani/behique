**Tweet 4 (3-tweet thread)**
The 3-week digital product launch framework. Built for solopreneurs with a small audience who want real results, not just activity.

1/3

---

Week 1: Problem amplification. Post about the exact problem your product solves. Not your product yet. Just the problem. You want people to recognize themselves in your content and start following you because of it.

Every post this week should end with: "next week I'll share how I solved this."

2/3

---

Week 2: Solution teasing. Show your process. Behind-the-scenes. Partial answers. Frameworks that don't fully land without the product. The goal is to make people feel the gap between where they are and where your product takes them.

Week 3: Direct offer. Clear sales post, FAQ post, testimonial post (even if it's a beta reader), and a deadline. Close the loop you opened in week 1.

3/3

---

**Tweet 5 (single)**
The thing that separates a $0 launch from a $2,000 launch is almost never the product quality. It's whether you built a warm audience before you asked them to buy. Warmth is the asset. The product is just the offer.

**Tweet 6 (6-tweet thread)**
Complete digital product launch checklist. Bookmark this.

1/6

---

Before you build:
- Validate with a presale or a paid waitlist
- Confirm the problem with at least 5 direct conversations or detailed comments
- Write one sales page draft before you start building (forces clarity)
- Set a ship date and work backward

2/6

---

While you build:
- Document the process. Screenshots, notes, results. These become your launch content.
- Share your progress publicly (even vague updates build anticipation)
- Collect potential testimonials from beta users or early readers
- Keep the first version smaller than you planned. Ship, then expand.

3/6

---

Pre-launch (7 days out):
- Final sales page written and reviewed
- Email sequence ready (minimum 3: launch, mid-launch, close)
- Price set with a rationale (not randomly)
- Cart open date communicated at least twice
- Affiliate or referral system ready if applicable

4/6

---

Launch week:
- Day 1: open cart, explain the transformation
- Day 2: answer the top objection
- Day 3: case study or process walkthrough
- Day 4: re-explain who it's for and who it's NOT for
- Day 5: urgency (deadline, price increase, or bonus removal)
- Day 6: final close

5/6

---

Post-launch:
- Thank everyone who bought personally if the volume allows it
- Collect and document testimonials immediately
- Note what questions came up most (future FAQ and content)
- Rest for 48 hours before touching the next build
- Analyze: what worked, what didn't, what you'd change for version 2

The launch is the test. The second launch is where you actually scale.

6/6

**Tweet 7 (single)**
Your sales page is the most important document in your product business. Write it before you build the product. If you can't explain the transformation clearly before you build it, you definitely won't explain it clearly after.

---

**Tweet 8 (single)**
The price question: most first-time creators underprice because they're afraid. Price based on the transformation, not the time it took you to create it. A PDF that saves someone 6 months of mistakes is worth more than the 4 hours it took to write.

---

**Tweet 9 (3-tweet thread)**
The one launch mistake that costs more than anything else: launching to a cold audience.

1/3

---

Cold audience: people who have never engaged with you, don't know what problem you solve, and have no context for why your product is relevant to them.

Selling to a cold audience requires a lot of ad spend or a lot of volume. Neither is available to most solopreneurs at launch.

2/3

---

The fix is simple but takes time: create content that speaks directly to the problem your product solves, consistently, for at least 4-6 weeks before launch.

By the time you open the cart, a portion of your audience has already decided they want this. The launch is just the signal.

3/3

---

**Tweet 10 (single)**
The second launch is always better than the first. Not because the product improved, but because you improved. You know your buyer now. You know the objections. You know the copy that converts. Launch once to learn. Launch twice to earn.
