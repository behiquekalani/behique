## Issue 50 — The FL Studio Opportunity

**Subject line:** 30 million users, almost no AI tools

Hey,

There are 30 million people using FL Studio right now. I have spent the last few days looking at what the AI plugin market looks like for that platform specifically, and the answer is: almost nothing. A few generative melody tools. One or two mixing assistants. That is it. Compare that to the size of the community and the gap becomes obvious pretty quickly.

This matters to me because I have been thinking about where to build next. When I look at the AI tools market for music production, I keep running into the same pattern. The tools that exist are built for Ableton users or are DAW-agnostic in a way that makes them feel generic. FL Studio has 30 million users who are younger, more technically curious, and more willing to try new tools than most other communities. That is a specific audience with a specific need, and right now it is underserved.

The opportunity is not "make an AI that writes music for you." That framing does not work because no serious producer wants that. The opportunity is in the workflow layer. Things that save time on repetitive tasks. Things that help you catch mix problems earlier. Things that turn a vague chord idea into something you can actually drop into a pattern and build from.

FL Studio also has a Python scripting API that almost no one talks about. Image-Line added it quietly and barely documented it. If you know Python, you can write scripts that interact directly with the mixer, the pattern editor, and MIDI in a way that gives you real control. That is the technical foundation for building something real, and it is sitting there underused.

I am not announcing a product here. I am just naming what I see. The gap is real, the audience is there, and the technical path is clearer than it looks from the outside. If you are a producer or a developer reading this, you already see it too.

More on this next week.

Kalani

## Issue 51 — Three Types of AI Setup Users

**Subject line:** Who actually buys AI setup guides

Hey,

I have been watching who actually purchases AI setup guides and trying to categorize them. Not by demographics, but by what they are trying to solve. I keep seeing the same three types show up.

The first type is the person who wants to replace a specific tool they are already paying for. They are paying $30 a month for something that now has a free AI alternative, and they want someone to tell them how to make the switch without losing their workflow. These people buy fast. They are motivated by a specific pain and they already know what they want. The guide just needs to confirm that the switch is possible and show the steps.

The second type is the person who wants to look like they have their setup figured out. They follow the AI space. They post about tools on Twitter. They have three or four AI subscriptions and use none of them consistently. They buy setup guides because the guide itself feels like progress. These people are not a bad customer, but they are more likely to ask for a refund or leave a vague review because the guide was never really the thing they needed.

The third type is the one worth building for. This person has a specific output goal, a job or project they are trying to get done, and they want AI to help them do it faster. They are not excited about AI for its own sake. They want to finish something. They will read the guide end to end, implement it, and if it works, they will buy the next thing you put out. They are the customer that compounds.

When I write setup guides, I am writing for the third type and trying to make the first type feel seen. The second type will find you regardless. The goal is to not optimize for them.

This came up for me while reviewing the AI employee guide I put out. I went back and reread it with this lens and it was mostly written for the third type, which is good, but the intro was speaking to the second type. I am rewriting the first two pages.

More next week.

Kalani

## Issue 52 — The Content Waterfall in Practice

**Subject line:** One idea, eight pieces of content, real time

Hey,

I want to show you something concrete this week. Not a framework, not a principle. An actual example of how one idea becomes eight pieces of content and how long it actually takes.

The idea was simple. FL Studio has 30 million users and almost no AI plugins exist yet. That is the whole seed. Here is what came out of it and how long each piece took.

First I wrote a newsletter issue about the opportunity. That took about 45 minutes. I was processing the idea out loud, so the writing came fast. That is this format working the way it is supposed to.

From that same idea I wrote eight Instagram captions. Each one took a specific angle: the opportunity gap, the Python API nobody knows about, what AI can actually do versus what is hype, how to build your first plugin without a music degree. Total writing time was about 90 minutes for all eight.

Next I have two YouTube script outlines drafted from the same core insight. One is a tutorial angle, showing the Python API and how to use it. The other is a market analysis angle, the business case for building FL Studio plugins in 2026. Those outlines took maybe 30 minutes combined because the thinking was already done.

From the newsletter specifically I can pull three short-form posts for Twitter. The core argument, the Python API point, and the three-types-of-buyer framing from the last issue. Each one is already in there. I just need to trim it down.

Total content pieces from one idea: 8 Instagram captions, 3 newsletter issues (this being the third), 2 YouTube outlines, 3 Twitter posts. That is 16 pieces from about 4 hours of work spread across a few days.

The thing that makes this work is not the system. It is starting with an idea that is actually interesting to you. If I was forcing myself to write about something I did not care about, none of this would hold together. The waterfall only works when the source is real.

Start with something you noticed. Write the newsletter first. Everything else follows from that.

Kalani
