### Post 2 — TEXT
**Hook:** I charged $300 for my first AI automation project. Here is the breakdown of what actually happened.

The client was a small e-commerce brand, three people, selling handmade goods. Their problem: customer DMs across Instagram and WhatsApp were piling up unread. Two to three hours a day of manual response work, most of it answering the same six questions.

I built a Telegram bot connected to a small language model with their FAQ embedded in the context. Responses in under 30 seconds. Escalation to a human when the question fell outside scope. Setup time: one afternoon. Testing and refinement: another half day.

The $300 number came from my estimate of what they would pay a part-time assistant for two weeks of that work. I underbid. I know that now. But the project did three things the money did not capture.

It gave me a repeatable template. Every customer service automation I have built since uses the same architecture with different context. The first project was the design work. Everything after was configuration.

It gave me a case study I could describe in concrete terms. Not "I build AI chatbots" but "I reduced a client's customer response time from four hours to 30 seconds and freed up 15 hours a week of their team's time." Specific numbers travel differently than category descriptions.

It gave me the first signal that this was a real business and not a hobby with a LinkedIn bio.

The $300 was not the point. The proof of mechanism was the point.

### Post 3 — SHORT
**Hook:** The difference between an AI tool and an AI system is whether it runs without you.

A tool requires input every time. You prompt it, it responds, the loop closes when you stop.

A system runs on triggers. Something happens, the system does something, a result is produced. You built the system once. You are not required to be present.

Most people using AI are building tool habits. The leverage comes from building system habits. The distinction sounds small. The output difference is large.

### Post 5 — TEXT
**Hook:** Having ADHD while building a business is not the obstacle people assume it is. It is a different set of constraints that require different systems.

The standard advice for building a business assumes linear progress. Set goals, break them into tasks, execute in order, track completion. That model does not describe how my brain works. It took me longer than it should have to stop treating that as a personal failure.

The ADHD brain is not bad at work. It is bad at work that lacks immediate relevance or feedback. The same brain that cannot maintain attention on a routine task for 45 minutes can go six hours without stopping on something that feels genuinely important. The capacity is there. The activation conditions are different.

What changed when I stopped fighting the constraint and started designing around it.

I stopped making long to-do lists. A long list with everything on it is a map to nowhere specific. I replaced it with a single next action per active project. One thing. That is it. When it is done, I pick the next one.

I built systems that give me feedback in hours, not weeks. Digital products show you sales data immediately. Content shows you engagement immediately. eBay listings show you watchers and offers immediately. The feedback loop has to be short enough for the brain to care.

I use voice memos instead of forcing myself to write ideas down. Speaking is faster and the capture rate is much higher. I transcribe later.

None of this is a workaround. It is how the work actually gets done.

### Post 7 — TEXT
**Hook:** I run my entire business across three computers, all named after Taino mythology. Here is the actual infrastructure breakdown and why it matters.

The names are Ceiba, Cobo, and Hutia. Ceiba is the sacred tree that connects all three worlds in Taino cosmology. Cobo is the conch shell, used to call people together. Hutia is a small native mammal, quiet and persistent. The names matter because they assign identity, and identity makes it easier to know what belongs where.

Ceiba is the MacBook M4. Primary development machine. Claude Code runs here. All product work, content drafts, and project management live here. When I need to think, I am on Ceiba.

Cobo is the desktop with the GTX 1080 Ti. GPU-heavy work goes here. Local language model inference, image generation, anything that needs the hardware. When I need to process, I am on Cobo.

Hutia is dedicated infrastructure. It runs the servers. BehiqueBot lives here. The Shopify store backend, the deployment pipelines, the things that have to stay running while I am doing other work. When I need things to just keep working, Hutia handles it.

The three-machine setup is not about having more computers. It is about having clear separation between types of work. When I sit down at Ceiba, I know what kind of work I am doing. That clarity removes a decision I would otherwise make dozens of times a day.

The constraint of not having infinite resources forced a design. The design turned out to be better than having everything mixed together.

### Post 10 — TEXT
**Hook:** What I would tell someone in their first month of building an AI services business, based on the first months of mine.

Do not start with the tools. Start with a specific problem you have already solved for yourself.

The AI services market is crowded with people who learned a tool and are looking for a problem to match it. The clients who pay consistently are not buying a tool. They are buying a solution to something concrete that is costing them right now. If you build from tools, you will spend most of your time pitching to people who do not understand why they need what you are selling. If you build from problems, the pitch is a short conversation.

The second thing: charge for the outcome, not the hours. The client does not care how long it took you to build the automation. They care whether the problem is solved and whether it keeps running. If you price by hours, you are capping your income at your available time. If you price by outcome, the ceiling is the value of the problem.

The third thing: document everything you build, even when the client does not require it. The documentation becomes the product. If you can package what you built and sell it to ten other businesses with the same problem, you have turned a service into a product. That is the transition that makes the business scalable.

The fourth thing: your first client does not have to pay well. They have to prove the mechanism. Once you have a working solution and a concrete result you can describe, the next client conversation is completely different.

Start small. Build proof. Raise prices. Repeat.
