# Twitter/X Batch 138
## Topic: The minimum viable product approach to digital goods

## Tweet 1
The first version of your digital product should embarrass you slightly.

If it doesn't, you waited too long.

## Tweet 2
An MVP digital product isn't a bad product. It's a complete product with a narrow scope.

One problem. One solution. One audience. Shipped.

## Tweet 3
1/
The digital product MVP formula:

2/
One specific problem your target reader has right now.
One framework that solves it.
One format that respects their time (PDF, template, checklist, guide).
One price that doesn't require a long think.

3/
That's the whole product. Launch it.

## Tweet 4
The biggest digital product mistake: building something for 3 months before getting a single dollar of validation.

Your first buyer is your real product spec.

## Tweet 5
1/
Stages of a digital product:

2/
v1: Proves someone will pay. Format doesn't matter. Polish doesn't matter.
v2: Based on what buyers actually do with it. More targeted.
v3: Based on results buyers got. Now you have proof.

3/
Most people never ship v1. They're still redesigning their v3 in their head.

## Tweet 6
A $17 PDF that solves a specific problem for a specific person will outsell a $97 course that tries to help everyone.

Narrow scope is a feature, not a limitation.

## Tweet 7
1/
Why "I'm not ready to launch yet" usually means:

2/
You're not afraid it's not good enough.
You're afraid it IS good enough and it still doesn't sell.

3/
Ship the thing. Get the data. The story you're telling yourself about readiness is delaying the feedback loop you actually need.

## Tweet 8
Minimum viable digital product checklist:
- Does it solve one specific problem?
- Can someone use it in under an hour?
- Is the transformation clear from the title?
- Can you describe it in one sentence?

If yes to all four, ship it today.

## Tweet 9
1/
The best digital product creators think like journalists, not professors.

2/
Journalists find what people need to know right now and deliver it fast.
Professors build complete curricula and teach it over semesters.

3/
The market doesn't want your curriculum. It wants the article.

## Tweet 10
You don't need 100 products. You need one product that converts, and a second that upsells the buyer of the first.

Start there. Build the catalog after.
