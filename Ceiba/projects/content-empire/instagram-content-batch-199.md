## Post 2 — 5 Things That Kill Year One Creators

**Hook:** Five specific things cause most year one content creators to quit. Most of them are avoidable.

**Slide 1:** Five specific things cause most year one content creators to quit. Most of them are avoidable.

**Slide 2:** 1. Comparing month 3 of your account to month 36 of someone else's.
You see their reach. You do not see the three years of posting that produced it.
Fix: Only compare yourself to your own previous month.

2. Posting irregularly and wondering why the algorithm does not reward you.
Algorithms reward predictability. Irregular posting looks like unreliability.
Fix: Post at minimum 3x per week on a fixed schedule.

**Slide 3:** 3. Changing niche every six weeks because nothing is "working."
Working takes longer than six weeks. Niche-hopping resets the clock.
Fix: Commit to one niche for at least six months before evaluating.

4. Measuring success by your worst-performing posts.
Every account has low performers. One bad week is not a trend.
Fix: Measure success by your top 20% of posts and track whether that baseline rises over time.

**Slide 4:** 5. Not having a content system, only a content intention.
Intention without infrastructure collapses under stress.
Fix: Build a batching habit and a fixed weekly creation block before you need discipline to show up.

**Slide 5:** None of these are talent problems. All of them are systems and mindset problems. All of them are fixable this week.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai covers content systems, not just content ideas. Follow for the infrastructure.

**Caption:** Most year one content creators do not quit because they ran out of ideas. They quit because of one of these five specific, preventable patterns.

The good news: all five are diagnosable before they become terminal.

The comparison trap is the most common. You open the app to post and instead spend 20 minutes consuming other creators in your niche. You see the reach on their posts. You see the comments. You close the app and do not post. This cycle repeats until you forget you were ever trying.

The fix is concrete: schedule your creation time before your consumption time. Never open the app to consume until after you have posted.

The niche-hopping pattern is the second most common. A post does not perform, so the niche must be wrong. The next post is from a slightly different angle in a slightly different niche. The algorithm never categorizes you. The audience never builds. Six months in, you have content on six topics and authority in none.

Fix: pick one specific thing and commit to it for 90 days minimum.

These patterns are recognizable. The creators who make it are the ones who recognize them early and correct.

## Post 3 — How to Handle Dead Weeks Without Quitting

**Hook:** Every creator has dead weeks. The ones who last learn to treat dead weeks as data, not verdicts.

**Slide 1:** Every creator has dead weeks. The ones who last learn to treat dead weeks as data, not verdicts.

**Slide 2:** A dead week: you post your normal content. Every post underperforms. Your usual reach is cut in half. Comments are quiet. Saves are low.
This will happen multiple times in year one. Possibly every 6-8 weeks.

**Slide 3:** What a dead week is not:
- Evidence that your niche is wrong
- Evidence that you should quit
- Evidence that the algorithm hates you
- Evidence that your content quality has dropped

**Slide 4:** What a dead week usually is:
- A platform algorithm shift you have no control over
- A topic cluster that needs rotating
- Audience fatigue on a format (easy fix: switch format for 2-3 posts)
- A signal that your hooks need refreshing, not your entire strategy

**Slide 5:** The protocol for a dead week:
1. Do not post less. Post the same.
2. Swap format for one week. If you do carousels, try single images.
3. Review your top 5 performing posts. Make 3 variations of the best one.
4. Do not refresh analytics hourly. Check once per day.
5. Keep going.

**Slide 6:** Dead weeks end. Accounts that survive them compound. @behikeai. Follow for the honest playbook.

**Caption:** Nobody talks about dead weeks because dead weeks are not inspiring content.

But every creator who has been at this for more than three months has had one. And the way you handle it determines whether you make it to month 12.

The dangerous response to a dead week is to post less or stop posting while you "figure out the strategy." The problem with this response is that the algorithm interprets reduced posting as reduced commitment. Your recovery period extends because you gave the algorithm a gap.

The correct response is to keep showing up at exactly the same cadence while making small, targeted adjustments.

One of the most reliable adjustments: go back to your best-performing content. Not to repost it, but to understand what it did that your recent posts did not. Was the hook more specific? Was the topic more emotionally resonant? Did it solve a concrete problem?

Take those answers and apply them to three new posts.

Dead weeks are diagnostic opportunities. The creators who survive them leave with better content instincts than they had going in.

Stay on schedule. Adjust the inputs. Keep going.

## Post 4 — The Energy Budget Problem Nobody Talks About

**Hook:** Content creation fails most often not from lack of ideas, but from depleted creative energy.

**Slide 1:** Content creation fails most often not from lack of ideas, but from depleted creative energy.

**Slide 2:** Year one is full of energy drains that nobody warns you about:
- Creating every post from scratch instead of using templates
- Overthinking every caption before publishing
- Spending 3 hours on a post that could take 45 minutes
- Consuming competitor content daily (comparison is exhausting)
- Treating every low-performer as a personal failure that requires analysis

**Slide 3:** If every post costs you 3 hours and you need to post 4 times a week, that is 12 hours per week on content. That is unsustainable for a solo builder with a full course load, a job, or a life.

**Slide 4:** The energy budget fix:
Build templates for every content format you use. A carousel template takes 20 minutes to fill instead of 90. Batch content in single sessions. Set a time limit per post: if it is not done in 60 minutes, it goes out anyway. Perfect is expensive. Consistent is valuable.

**Slide 5:** Year one teaches you this: sustainable beats optimal.
A mediocre post published beats a perfect post still in drafts.
Your energy budget is a real constraint. Build your content practice inside of it, not in defiance of it.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai covers the systems behind sustainable content. Follow.

**Caption:** The reason most creators burn out by month 4 is not lack of passion. It is an energy budget problem.

They treat every post like a feature film. Two hours of writing. Forty-five minutes of design. Twenty minutes second-guessing the caption. Then they do that four times a week and wonder why they are exhausted.

Creative energy is finite. It refills, but it needs management.

The creators who last are the ones who build their process around their energy reality, not around an ideal posting scenario.

Batch creation is the single highest-leverage habit for energy management. Instead of switching in and out of creative mode four separate times per week, you enter creative mode once, stay in it for two hours, and produce four posts. The context switching cost alone saves you an hour per week.

Templates remove blank-page energy drain. When you sit down to create, you are not inventing the format. You are filling it. That is a completely different cognitive task, and it is far less exhausting.

Manage the energy. The output will take care of itself.

## Post 5 — The Year One Report Card

**Hook:** If you finish year one with these 5 things, you have won, regardless of your follower count.

**Slide 1:** If you finish year one with these 5 things, you have won, regardless of your follower count.

**Slide 2:** 1. A publishing record.
You posted consistently through the entire year. Not perfectly. Consistently.
This is the hardest thing to build and the most valuable thing to have.

2. A voice.
Your writing and content design has a recognizable quality that is distinctly yours. Someone who has read 20 of your posts would recognize your 21st from the first sentence.

**Slide 3:** 3. A content library with at least 150 pieces.
This library is an asset. It works permanently. It builds SEO. It surfaces in search. It is the foundation year two compounds on.

4. Pattern recognition.
You know which of your content categories performs best. You know which hooks resonate. You have tested enough formats to have real data.

**Slide 4:** 5. A lower cost per post than at month 1.
You are creating more efficiently than you were at the start. The process is faster. The decision-making is faster. The quality is higher despite the reduced time.

**Slide 5:** These five things are available to every creator regardless of niche, following size, or starting point.
They are earned through volume and time.
If you have them at month 12, the growth in year two is a near-certainty.

**Slide 6:** Year one is the foundation. @behikeai is building in public. Follow the year.

**Caption:** Stop measuring year one by follower count. Here is the actual report card.

Follower count at month 12 is a consequence of the five things listed in this post. It is not a direct measure of your effort, your skill, or your potential.

Two creators can post the same number of times in year one and have wildly different follower counts at month 12. The difference is usually timing, niche competition, platform algorithm factors, and luck. None of those are things you fully control.

What you do control: the five things above.

A publishing record is proof of character. A voice is proof of craft. A library is proof of investment. Pattern recognition is proof of learning. Efficiency is proof of growth.

When you have all five of these at month 12, the compounding in year two is positioned to happen fast. The algorithm has 12 months of consistent data on you. The audience has had enough touchpoints to trust you. The library has enough mass to generate inbound traffic.

Grade yourself on what you can control.
