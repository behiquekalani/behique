**Tweet 4 (3-Tweet Thread)**
Why most creators measure content ROI wrong:

They track: views, likes, follower growth.

These are all lagging indicators of the wrong thing.

What actually drives revenue from content is not reach. It's the depth of trust with a specific type of person.

/2

A creator with 2,000 followers and a hyper-specific audience can out-earn a creator with 200,000 followers and a general one.

Because the 2,000 are the exact people who need exactly what's being sold.

Trust at depth beats reach at scale in most business models.

/3

The real ROI question is not "how many people saw this?"
It's "did this make the right people trust me more?"

If yes, the business impact will follow. It cannot be measured in a day. But it compounds in ways that viral metrics never do.

**Tweet 7 (8-Tweet Thread)**
A framework for thinking about content ROI that actually maps to business outcomes:

There are four types of content return.
Most people only optimize for one.

/2

Type 1: Reach return.

This is the one everyone chases. Views, impressions, shares.

Reach return is real but it's the least valuable on its own. Reach without trust is noise.

Use reach to get in front of new people. Then convert them with every other type of content.

/3

Type 2: Trust return.

This is the content that makes someone think "this person gets it."

It's usually your most specific content. Counterintuitive takes. Deep dives. Honest accounts of failure.

Trust return is slow. It's also the most valuable because trust is what converts.

/4

Type 3: Search return.

Content that gets found months or years later.

This is SEO content, YouTube, Pinterest, long-form articles. It works on a different timeline but the return compounds indefinitely.

A post from 2 years ago can still be your best acquisition channel today.

/5

Type 4: Direct return.

Content where the CTA converts directly to revenue.

Sales emails. Product posts. Offer announcements.

This is where the trust you built gets cashed in. It only works if the other three are in place first.

/6

Most creators live in Type 1 and Type 4 only.

They alternate between chasing reach and pitching products.

The audience never builds enough trust for the pitch to land. So the reach never converts. So they chase more reach. The cycle continues.

/7

The fix: build a content stack with all four types.

Week one: one trust-building deep dive.
Week two: one search-optimized how-to.
Week three: one reach-focused take or thread.
Week four: one direct offer tied to the trust you've built.

Repeat for a year.

/8

ROI compounds across this stack in ways that are impossible to measure per post.

The person who bought your offer in month 11 read your trust content in month 2, found you through search in month 6, and saw your offer post in month 10.

No single post drove that sale. The whole system did.
Measure the system.

**Tweet 9 (3-Tweet Thread)**
The content metrics actually worth tracking:

Not vanity metrics. These:

1. Reply rate: are people engaging at depth or just liking?
2. DM rate: are the right people reaching out?
3. Email list conversion: is your audience converting to a more committed channel?

/2

Email list conversion is the most underrated metric in content.

Social followers are rented. Email subscribers are owned.

Every piece of content should have a path to email. Not aggressively. But always there.

Your email list is the only audience asset you actually control.

/3

Long-term: track revenue per email subscriber.

If your list converts at $5 per subscriber per year and you have 500 subscribers, you have a $2,500/year content asset.

Build to 5,000 subscribers at the same conversion rate: $25K/year.

That's what content ROI looks like when you measure the right thing.
