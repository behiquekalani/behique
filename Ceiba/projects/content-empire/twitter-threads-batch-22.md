## Thread 1: Study Buddy — 30 Days of Tracked Distractions

1/
I tracked every distraction for 30 days with Study Buddy.
Here is what I found. (thread)

2/
Study Buddy is an app I built that logs what breaks your focus sessions.
You tap a button when you get distracted. You tag the type. It builds a pattern over time.

3/
Month 1. 30 days. 847 logged distractions.
That number surprised me. That is 28 per day.

4/
Top distraction category: internal interruptions.
Not phone notifications. Not noise. My own brain generating a thought and pulling my attention to it.

5/
62% of my distractions were self-generated.
That data broke a belief I had been carrying for years: that my environment was the problem.

6/
The environment was only 38% of the problem.
The bigger issue was that I had no system for handling internal interrupts without losing the session.

7/
The fix: a capture system next to my work.
When a thought fires, I write it in two words and keep going.
The thought is captured. The session continues.

8/
Distraction rate after implementing the capture system: down 41% in the second month.
No phone changes. No environment changes. Just a place to put the thoughts that were competing for attention.

9/
Second finding: distraction spikes happened in the first 7 minutes and after the 22-minute mark.
The first 7 minutes: the ramp-up cost. The 22-minute dip: energy lag before the second wind.

10/
Knowing the spike times changed how I structured sessions.
I do not check anything in the first 7 minutes. I expect a dip at 22 and push through it.

11/
Study Buddy is a web app. Free to use.
The data from your own sessions will tell you things about how you work that you cannot see from the inside.

## Thread 2: How to Pick Your First Digital Product Niche

1/
How to pick your first digital product niche.
The decision framework I use. (thread)

2/
Most people pick a niche based on passion or trend.
Both of these fail. Here is why.

3/
Passion-based niche: you love it, but the audience is not buyers.
Trend-based niche: by the time you build the product, the trend has moved.

4/
The framework I use has three questions:
1. Do people in this niche already buy solutions to this problem?
2. Can you reach them for under $50?
3. Can you create a credible product in under 30 days?

5/
Question 1 is the most important.
If there are no existing products in the niche, the problem is usually that people want a free solution.
No existing market means you are not a pioneer. You are just early to something that will not work.

6/
Question 2 filters for distribution.
If the audience lives on Reddit, you can reach them. If they live on a conference circuit, you cannot.
Pick niches with accessible audiences.

7/
Question 3 is about your execution window.
You need to ship before you lose momentum.
A product you can build in 30 days gets built. A product you plan for 6 months usually does not.

8/
The secondary filter: can you build a second product in the same niche?
One product is a gamble. Two products is a store.
If you cannot see the second product, the niche is probably too narrow.

9/
Where to look for niches that pass all three tests:
Reddit communities with paid resources in the sidebar.
Gumroad browse pages sorted by top sellers.
Amazon "also bought" chains in nonfiction.

10/
The niche does not need to be original.
It needs to be one where you can solve a specific problem better than the existing options.
That is enough to start.

## Thread 3: Audience vs Community — Why It Matters for Revenue

1/
The difference between an audience and a community.
And why it matters for revenue. (thread)

2/
An audience watches you.
A community participates with you.

3/
Audience metrics: follower count, reach, impressions.
Community metrics: replies, questions, referrals, user-generated content.

4/
Revenue per audience member is low.
Revenue per community member is 4 to 10 times higher, depending on the niche.

5/
Here is why: an audience buys once, when you make an offer.
A community buys repeatedly, because they are invested in where you are going.

6/
You can have 500 community members and out-earn someone with 50,000 followers.
This is not a hypothetical. It is a common pattern in creator economics.

7/
The transition from audience to community requires two things:
First, a place for members to talk to each other, not just to you.
Second, you responding to people in public. Every response signals that you see them.

8/
Platforms that support community: Discord, Circle, Telegram, Twitter replies.
Platforms that support audience: Instagram broadcasts, YouTube uploads, newsletters.

9/
Most creators optimize for audience because the metrics are visible.
Follower count is public. Community depth is not.
This is why most creators are building something that does not compound.

10/
The question to ask: if I stopped posting for 30 days, would people in my space still be talking to each other?
If yes, you have a community.
If no, you have an audience that will move on.

11/
Start building community now, before you think you need it.
The time to build relationships is before you have something to sell.

## Thread 4: 25 Product Launches — What I Learned About Product-Market Fit

1/
What 25 product launches taught me about product-market fit. (thread)

2/
I have shipped 25 digital products in the last 6 months.
Not all of them worked. Here is the pattern I found.

3/
Products that sold immediately had one thing in common:
The audience already had a name for the problem.
If they could describe it before I showed them the solution, it sold.

4/
Products that required explanation sold slowly or not at all.
This is a filter. If you need more than one sentence to explain the problem, go back and find the customer's language for it.

5/
The second pattern: price resistance correlated with outcome clarity, not price point.
A $99 product with a specific promised outcome sold faster than a $14.99 product with a vague value prop.

6/
People do not resist spending money.
They resist spending money when they cannot calculate what they are getting.
Be specific. Concrete. Measurable.

7/
Third pattern: the products built from real conversations sold better than the ones I invented.
When someone asks me how I do X, and I build a product around that question, it converts.
When I build something I thought they wanted, I am usually wrong.

8/
This is the simplest product research method:
Look at what people already ask you. Build that.

9/
Fourth pattern: bundles de-risked trying new niches.
When I added a new product to the existing bundle, it got exposure without needing its own launch.
The existing buyers validated it before I promoted it separately.

10/
Fifth pattern: the products I almost did not ship because they felt too simple were the most popular ones.
Your expert curse makes you underestimate what is obvious to you and valuable to someone else.

## Thread 5: The Overnight Machine — 5 Pre-Researched eBay Listings Every Morning

1/
The overnight machine: I wake up to 5 pre-researched eBay listings every morning. (thread)

2/
Here is what runs while I sleep:
A Python script pulls trending search data, filters for resale margin, and generates structured listing drafts.
I wake up and review. Takes me 15 minutes.

3/
The input: Google Trends data for product categories, filtered by search velocity and geographic demand.
The output: product names, estimated sell price, source price range, and a listing draft.

4/
Why eBay: the feedback loop is fast.
You list, you see demand, you adjust.
It is the best place to validate product ideas before moving them to Shopify.

5/
The research step used to take 2 hours per product.
The script compresses it to about 2 minutes of review per listing.
Time savings: roughly 90%.

6/
What the script does not do: buy inventory.
That is still a human decision.
The script finds the opportunity. I decide whether to act on it.

7/
The rate limiting on Google Trends was the hardest part to solve.
Too many requests too fast and you get blocked.
Solution: randomized delays, proxy rotation, and a queue system that spreads requests over 6 hours.

8/
Current state: the script runs nightly via cron.
Results land in a markdown file I review over coffee.
The whole research operation now costs me 15 minutes of attention per day.

9/
This is what I mean by building systems instead of doing work.
The system does the repetitive part. I do the judgment part.
