Not the one I started with. The one I built after the first version failed. (thread)

2/ Version 1 of my store: all products at $9.99.

Logic: low price, high volume.

Reality: low price, also low volume. No urgency. No perceived value.

3/ The shift:

Price is not about cost. Price is about what the buyer believes they are getting.

$4.99 says "free but not free." $49.99 says "this person took this seriously."

4/ The pricing ladder I run now:

- Free lead magnets (builds trust, captures email)
- $4.99 to $9.99 (entry point, low friction)
- $14.99 to $29.99 (core products, most revenue)
- $49.99 to $99 (premium, low volume, high margin)
- $149.99 bundle (everything, best perceived value)

5/ What the bundle does psychologically:

The buyer does math. $507 in individual products for $149.99.

They do not care if they use all 25. They feel like they won.

6/ What the free tier does:

It answers the question "can I trust this person" before anyone spends anything.

No trust, no sale. The free tier is not charity. It is sales infrastructure.

7/ The real insight:

Most one-person stores fail because they have one price point.

One price means one type of buyer. A ladder means you capture the whole market.
