## Thread 77-1: Validate Before You Build

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Most people build the product first, then look for customers.

That's why most products fail.

**Tweet 2:**
The correct order:

1. Find a painful problem
2. Talk to people who have it
3. Describe the solution
4. Sell the description
5. Then build what sold

Skipping steps 1-4 is how you waste 6 months building something nobody wanted.

**Tweet 3:**
Validation means someone gives you money, attention, or explicit commitment before the product exists.

A waitlist is weak validation.
Pre-orders are strong validation.
People paying for a manual version of your solution is the strongest.

**Tweet 4:**
The fastest validation test:

Write a landing page describing the product and its outcome.
Drive 100 people to it.
Ask for email sign-ups or pre-payment.

If 10%+ sign up, you have signal. If less than 2%, the offer is wrong. Fix the offer before you build.

**Tweet 5:**
What to look for in validation:

- Do people forward your post about the problem to others?
- Do people DM you asking for the solution?
- Do people pre-pay without seeing the finished product?

These are real signals. "Sounds interesting" is not.

**Tweet 6:**
The hardest part of validation is accepting negative signals early.

Most builders rationalize: "They just don't understand it yet."

No. If they don't understand it, the positioning is broken. Fix that before building.

**Tweet 7:**
The goal of validation is to eliminate the biggest risk in building:

Building something nobody pays for.

One week of validation is worth 3 months of building.

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
Save this before your next build. Most people skip validation and blame the market. The market told them the truth. They just didn't ask.

## Thread 77-2: Talk to 10 People First

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
You don't need market research reports. You need 10 conversations with real people who have the problem.

**Tweet 2:**
Here's what to ask in those 10 conversations:

1. Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem]
2. What did you try to fix it?
3. What happened?
4. What did that cost you?
5. If a perfect solution existed, what would it look like?

Don't pitch. Just listen.

**Tweet 3:**
What you're listening for:

- Specific language (steal their words for your copy)
- Frustration intensity (high frustration = real problem)
- Failed alternatives (gaps in the market)
- What they'd pay to solve it (price anchoring)

Ten conversations gives you more than any survey.

**Tweet 4:**
The mistake most builders make in these conversations:

They talk more than they listen. They pitch the solution they already have in their head. They ask leading questions.

Shut up. Ask open questions. Let the customer tell you what to build.

**Tweet 5:**
After 10 conversations, patterns emerge.

Three people mention the same frustration. Four people tried the same workaround and it failed. Two people said they'd pay right now if the solution existed.

That's your product brief. No MBA required.

**Tweet 6:**
The rule: if you can't name 5 people who would buy your product today, you don't have a validated idea.

Not "might buy." Not "would be interested." Would buy. Today.

Start there. Find those 5. Talk to them.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
Ten conversations before any code. Ten conversations before any design. Ten conversations before any marketing.

Follow for the full framework on customer discovery and product validation.

## Thread 77-3: The Pre-Sale Method

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
The cleanest way to validate a product: sell it before it exists.

This sounds wrong until you see it work.

**Tweet 2:**
Pre-selling works because:

- It forces you to articulate the outcome clearly
- It creates real financial commitment (not just interest)
- It gives you funding to build the product
- It proves the market before you invest months building

The downside is you have to actually deliver. That's a feature, not a bug.

**Tweet 3:**
How to run a pre-sale:

1. Write a sales page with a clear outcome
2. Describe what's included and what you'll build
3. Set a pre-sale price (lower than launch)
4. Set a deadline or seat cap
5. Drive traffic and take payments
6. Build only what sold

**Tweet 4:**
Pre-sales work best when:

- You have an existing audience (even small)
- You've been writing about the problem publicly
- The outcome is specific and believable
- The price is low enough to reduce risk for early buyers

$27 pre-sale. 20 buyers. $540 in revenue before writing a word.

**Tweet 5:**
What to say to people who want it finished first:

"I'm building this based on demand. Early buyers get the best price and direct input on what's included. If you wait for the finished version, the price goes up."

Most will buy. The ones who don't weren't your customers anyway.

**Tweet 6:**
Real pre-sale signals:

- 10+ buyers at a low price point: green light
- 5-9 buyers: maybe. Examine the pricing and positioning
- 0-4 buyers: red flag. Stop and diagnose before building

Numbers don't lie. Assumptions do.

**Tweet 7:**
The pre-sale is not a trick. It's honest.

You're building something. You're sharing the vision early. Early buyers get better access and pricing for their trust.

Everyone wins. Build in public. Pre-sell in public. Deliver in public.

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
You can run a pre-sale for any digital product this week. No product required. Just a clear outcome and a payment link.

Follow for more on building without risk.

## Thread 77-4: The Problem-First Principle

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Most builders fall in love with their solution.

The best builders fall in love with the problem.

This distinction determines everything.

**Tweet 2:**
When you fall in love with the solution:

- You build features people didn't ask for
- You ignore feedback that contradicts your vision
- You spend money on the wrong things
- You wonder why no one buys

**Tweet 3:**
When you fall in love with the problem:

- You obsess over understanding it
- You talk to customers constantly
- You're willing to throw out your solution if a better one exists
- You build what people actually need

**Tweet 4:**
How to find real problems worth solving:

1. Look for things people complain about repeatedly
2. Find workarounds people have built for themselves
3. Find expensive, slow, or frustrating existing solutions
4. Find communities venting about the same issue

Any of these is a starting point.

**Tweet 5:**
The best problems share three traits:

1. Painful enough to pay to solve
2. Frequent enough to be top of mind
3. Clear enough that people can articulate it

If someone struggles to explain the problem to you, it's probably not urgent enough.

**Tweet 6:**
Once you find the problem, ask: has anyone else tried to solve it?

If yes: that's proof the market exists. Now build a better solution.
If no: that's suspicious. Either it's genuinely new (rare) or it's not really a problem.

Be honest about which one you're in.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
Pick one problem you're deeply familiar with. Not because you want to build a product, but because you lived the frustration.

That's where your best product idea lives.

Follow to learn how to turn that into something that sells.

## Thread 77-5: Build the Manual Version First

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Before you automate anything, do it manually.

This is the fastest way to validate and the fastest way to learn what actually matters.

**Tweet 2:**
The manual version principle:

Do for 10 customers by hand exactly what your product will eventually do automatically.

- If you're building a research tool, do the research manually first
- If you're building a content scheduler, write and schedule manually first
- If you're building a matchmaking platform, make matches manually first

**Tweet 3:**
Why this works:

- You deliver value immediately without building anything
- You learn exactly where the real friction is
- You understand the problem from the inside
- Customers pay for outcomes, not technology

Most people automate the wrong thing because they never did it manually.

**Tweet 4:**
The manual version is also your proof of concept.

10 customers get results manually. Those results become testimonials. Testimonials validate the product before it's built.

You now have a product page, a proven process, and social proof.

**Tweet 5:**
The concierge approach in practice:

Stripe's founders manually processed payments for their first users.
Airbnb's founders manually photographed listings and managed bookings.
Zappos started by manually buying shoes and shipping them before building any warehouse.

Start manual. Scale later.

**Tweet 6:**
When to stop being manual:

When you're doing the same task for the tenth time in the same way.

That's the signal. That pattern is what you automate. You now know exactly what to build because you've done it manually enough to understand it.

**Tweet 7:**
The mistake: jumping straight to building the automated version.

You optimize for speed when you should be optimizing for learning. Manual is slow. But the learning is 10x faster.

Slow down to speed up.

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
What can you do manually this week to test your product idea?

Not build. Not design. Not code.

Do the thing by hand. Learn first. Build second.
