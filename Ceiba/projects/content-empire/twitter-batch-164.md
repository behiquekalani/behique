**Tweet 4 (3-tweet thread)**
The paradox of niching down: the more specific you get, the more people feel like you're speaking directly to them.

1/3

---

This is counterintuitive. "If I narrow my audience, I reach fewer people." That's the math. It's also wrong.

Specificity isn't about excluding people. It's about signaling so clearly to the right people that they stop scrolling.

2/3

---

A post titled "productivity tips" reaches everyone and resonates with no one.

A post titled "productivity system for college students with ADHD who work part-time" reaches fewer people, but every single one of them reads the whole thing and shares it.

Narrow the language. The right people find you faster.

3/3

**Tweet 5 (single)**

**Tweet 6 (6-tweet thread)**
Your audience is not as niche as you think. Here's why I believe that and why it should change how you create content.

Thread.

1/6

---

The term "niche" gets misused constantly. Creators treat it as a restriction. "I can only talk about X." That's not what a niche is.

A niche is a specific intersection of problem, person, and perspective. You can have a deep niche and still cover a wide range of topics.

2/6

---

Example: Dan Koe writes about productivity, philosophy, business, creativity, and personal development. His niche is the "multi-passionate creator who wants to build a one-person business." Everything he writes connects to that one person.

The niche is the audience, not the topic.

3/6

---

The real question isn't "is my topic too narrow?" It's "is my ideal reader too vague?"

If you can't describe your reader in one sentence with a specific problem and a specific context, you haven't niched down enough. Not in topic. In person.

4/6

---

Test: write a post with a very specific person in mind. A 28-year-old freelancer who's been doing client work for two years and wants to productize. Every word you write, you're writing to that person.

The conversion rate on that post will be higher than anything you've written "for everyone."

5/6

---

Summary:
- Your topic can be broad if your person is specific
- Specificity in language signals belonging to the right reader
- The fear is excluding people. The reality is that vagueness does that better than specificity ever could
- Define the person first. The content direction follows automatically.

6/6

---

**Tweet 7 (single)**
The most common feedback I see on content that isn't growing: "it's not specific enough." Not the topic. The person. Who specifically are you helping and what specifically are they struggling with right now.

**Tweet 9 (3-tweet thread)**
Why "write for everyone" is the fastest way to reach no one.

1/3

---

The human brain is pattern-matching for relevance at about 200ms. In that window, your reader asks: is this for me?

Generic content fails that test. It doesn't signal clearly enough. The brain marks it irrelevant and moves on before the first sentence is finished.

2/3

---

Specific content passes the test instantly. "Freelancer who wants to stop trading time for money." The right person reads that and their brain says: that's me.

You've done the hardest part of marketing before the first word of content.

3/3

---

**Tweet 10 (single)**
Solopreneurs who struggle with audience growth are almost always targeting "people interested in X" instead of "people with this specific pain who are at this specific moment in their journey." That second version is a person. The first version is a demographic. Markets buy. Audiences follow. Persons act.
