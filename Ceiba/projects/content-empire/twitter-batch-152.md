**Tweet 4 (3-Tweet Thread)**
Why "I'm not ready" is a lie you tell yourself:

Readiness is not a state you arrive at.
It's a feeling you earn by shipping.

The first 10 things you make will be bad. That is not a flaw in the plan. That IS the plan.

/2

Every creator you follow shipped bad work before they shipped good work.

They didn't get good, then publish.
They published, got feedback, got embarrassed, got better.

In that order. Every time.

/3

The question is not "is this ready?"
The question is "will waiting longer actually make this better, or will it just delay the feedback I need?"

Publish. Adjust. Repeat.
That is the entire curriculum.

**Tweet 7 (6-Tweet Thread)**
The cost of waiting until you're ready:

I've watched creators sit on the same course for 18 months.
"Just a few more tweaks."
"The design isn't right yet."
"I need a bigger audience first."

Meanwhile, someone with a Google Doc and a Gumroad link is pulling in $3K a month.

Here's what waiting actually costs you.

/2

You lose the compounding.

Content compounds. A post from 6 months ago is still finding new people today.

Every week you don't publish is a week of compounding you can never recover. The gap between you and the people who started when you were still preparing keeps growing.

/3

You lose the learning signal.

Your imagined audience will always respond differently than your real one.

The piece you think will flop goes viral. The one you spent 3 weeks on gets 4 likes.

You cannot predict this from the inside. Only shipping gives you real data.

/4

You train yourself to need more preparation.

The longer you wait, the more "ready" starts to feel like a higher bar.

Six months of waiting turns a normal post into something that has to justify six months. Now you need it to be perfect or it feels like failure.

The bar rises with the delay.

/5

You lose the iteration window.

Products, content, and businesses improve through versions. V2 is always better than V1.

But V2 requires that V1 exists.

Every week you delay V1 is a week V10 is further away.

/6

The antidote is not courage.
It's reframing what "ready" means.

Ready means: does this solve a real problem or say something true?
If yes, publish it.
Everything else is refinement you can do after.

**Tweet 9 (3-Tweet Thread)**
How to publish before you're ready without embarrassing yourself:

Set a minimum bar, not a perfection bar.

The minimum bar: Is this true? Does it help someone? Would I be okay if this was the only thing someone read from me?

If yes to all three, publish.

/2

Separate "this needs more time" from "I am afraid."

Real quality issues have a checklist. You can say: this needs a stronger opening, a clearer CTA, a proofread.

Fear has no checklist. It says things like "it's just not quite right" and "maybe next week."

Know the difference.

/3

Build the habit of shipping, not the habit of quality.

Quality is an output of consistent shipping over time.
You cannot build quality in isolation.

Ship on a schedule. Let the quality improve as a byproduct.
The calendar forces the decision so fear cannot.
