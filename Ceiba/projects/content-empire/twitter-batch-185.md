## Threads

**Thread 1: The actual math of a one-person business**

1/ Most people who want to build a one-person business never sit down and do the actual math. They think in vague terms like "enough to replace my salary" without running the specific numbers. Run the numbers.

2/ Let's say your target is $5,000/month. That's $60,000/year. Achievable with: 200 customers at a $25/month subscription, or 1,200 customers buying a $50 product once per year, or 120 customers buying a $500 product once per year.

3/ The key question for each model: how hard is it to acquire and retain that customer count? A subscription model with 200 customers is very different work from a product model with 1,200 one-time buyers.

4/ There's no universally right answer. But there is a right answer for your specific situation, skills, and audience size. The math tells you which model fits where you actually are right now.

5/ Do the math before you choose the model. Most people choose a model based on what they see others doing, then get frustrated when the numbers don't work for their specific situation.

---

**Thread 2: Why the one-person business has structural advantages most people ignore**

1/ A one-person business has a cost structure that no company with employees can match. You have no payroll, no benefits, no office lease, no manager tax. Every dollar of revenue hits differently.

2/ A company that makes $500K with 10 employees might net $100K after all costs. A one-person business that makes $200K with digital products might net $180K. The revenue number is smaller but the math is completely different.

3/ The leverage in a one-person business is in the margin, not the revenue. You can build a $150K one-person business that gives you more real financial freedom than a $500K company that requires 60-hour weeks and a team to manage.

4/ This doesn't mean staying small forever. It means understanding the game you're actually in. You're not competing with companies on revenue. You're building a machine that generates income with the fewest possible inputs.

5/ The metric that matters for a one-person business is not revenue. It's revenue per hour of your time, minus your total costs. Maximize that. Everything else is vanity.

---

**Thread 3: The product pricing math most creators get wrong**

1/ Most creators underprice because they think about what they'd pay for their product, not what it's worth to the person with the specific problem they solve. These are very different numbers.

2/ A template that saves a freelancer 3 hours of work per week is worth $156/month to them if they value their time at $13/hour. A $27 one-time price is a massive undercharge.

3/ The pricing test: what is the cost to the buyer of not having this? Time, stress, money, missed opportunity. Price relative to that cost, not relative to how long it took you to make it.

4/ The math of underpricing: if you sell 100 units of a $27 product, you make $2,700. If you price it at $97, you make $9,700 on the same 100 units. The same amount of work. The same audience. Completely different outcome.

5/ Yes, a higher price means fewer buyers. But not as many fewer as you think. And the ones who buy at a higher price are better customers. They implement more, get better results, and become better word-of-mouth sources.

**Thread 4: Building to the right number, not the biggest number**

1/ There's a number that represents genuine financial freedom for you. Not the Forbes 400. Your specific number. The number at which your basic needs, your family's needs, and a margin of safety are all covered.

2/ Most people have never calculated this number. They default to "more" as the goal, which means they're always running toward a horizon that keeps moving. The math never resolves. The anxiety never ends.

3/ Calculate your number. Monthly expenses times 12 for your annual cost of living. Add a safety margin of 30 percent. Add the investment contributions that matter to you. That's your real target revenue.

4/ For most people reading this, the number is between $60K and $120K per year. That is very achievable with a one-person business and a focused product strategy. It's not a stretch goal. It's a reasonable 2-3 year target.

5/ Build to your number first. Then decide if you want to build beyond it, and for what reason. Building beyond your number for its own sake is not a strategy. It's avoidance of the question of what you actually want.

---

**Thread 5: The timeline math nobody talks about**

1/ People dramatically overestimate what they can build in three months and dramatically underestimate what they can build in three years. The one-person business is a three-year game minimum.

2/ Year one: building the foundation. Product-market fit, first real customers, content rhythm, first $1,000 months. This phase is slow and feels like it's not working. It's working.

3/ Year two: the compounding starts. Your content has history. Your product has reviews. Your name has association with a specific topic. Inbound starts. Revenue starts climbing without proportional effort increase.

4/ Year three: you're working smarter than almost everyone. You have systems. You have an audience that trusts you. You have multiple products. The math that seemed impossible in year one is now just math.

5/ The three-year frame is not demotivating. It's clarifying. It means this month's results are not the verdict. They're just the first data point in a long experiment. Stay in the game. The math always catches up eventually.
