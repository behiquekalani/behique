## Post 1 — Why You Are Underpricing Everything

**Hook:** You are not underpriced because your product is not good enough. You are underpriced because you are pricing for your own wallet, not your buyer's problem.

**Slide 1:** You are not underpriced because your product is not good enough. You are underpriced because you are pricing for your own wallet, not your buyer's problem.

**Slide 2:** When you set a price, you make a subconscious calculation:
"Would I pay this much for this?"

The problem: you built it. You know how it works. You are not the buyer.

**Slide 3:** Your buyer is making a different calculation:
"How much would I pay to solve this problem?"

Those are completely different numbers.

**Slide 4:** A $29 guide that eliminates a $300/mo inefficiency is not expensive.
It is a 10x return on day one.
Price relative to the problem, not the PDF.

**Slide 5:** Low prices do not build trust. They signal low value.
The buyer is not comparing your price to zero.
They are comparing your price to alternatives.
Know what the alternatives cost.

**Slide 6:** Raise your price. Then write the value that justifies it.
Behike Method at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** Underpricing is one of the most common and quietly destructive mistakes solopreneurs make. And it almost never comes from the product being weak. It comes from the founder being afraid.

When you price too low, a few things happen simultaneously. Your margins shrink. You need more sales to hit the same revenue. You attract buyers who are optimizing for cheap rather than for transformation. And you send a signal that the thing you built is not particularly valuable.

Price is a positioning statement as much as it is a number. A $9 product and a $97 product are in completely different psychological categories for the buyer, even if the content is identical.

Start by calculating the value of the outcome your product delivers. Then price at a fraction of that. You will almost certainly find that the right price is higher than what you are charging now.

## Post 2 — The Psychology Behind Why People Pay More

**Hook:** Buyers do not make rational decisions. They make emotional ones and then justify them rationally.

**Slide 1:** Buyers do not make rational decisions. They make emotional ones and then justify them rationally.

**Slide 2:** This means your price is not the first objection.
The first objection is: "Can I trust this person?"
The second: "Does this actually work for someone like me?"
The price comes third.

**Slide 3:** Which means: the solution to price resistance is not lowering the price.
It is building more trust and showing more evidence before the buyer reaches the price.

**Slide 4:** What builds trust before the price:
- Specific, accurate problem diagnosis
- Before and after evidence
- Your own story applied to the same problem
- Testimonials with named outcomes

**Slide 5:** When the trust is built, the price becomes secondary.
A buyer who fully trusts you and believes in the result will pay more.
A buyer who does not trust you will resist any price.

**Slide 6:** Build trust first. Then price with confidence.
Start at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** Every conversation about pricing eventually comes back to one thing: does the buyer believe the result is real and that you are the person to deliver it?

If they believe both, the price is almost irrelevant. People pay thousands for coaching programs, masterminds, and premium products not because they cannot find cheaper alternatives, but because they trust the person selling them.

The implication for solopreneurs is significant. Instead of competing on price, compete on trust. Build trust through content that accurately diagnoses the problem. Build it through evidence that shows real outcomes. Build it through consistency and specificity.

Once trust is at a certain level, your price becomes a confirmation that the product is serious, not a barrier to entry.

Price psychology starts with the relationship, not the number.

## Post 3 — The Anchor Price Strategy

**Hook:** The first number your buyer sees determines how they feel about every number after it.

**Slide 1:** The first number your buyer sees determines how they feel about every number after it.

**Slide 2:** This is price anchoring.
It is one of the most well-documented effects in behavioral economics.
And most solopreneurs are not using it.

**Slide 3:** How anchoring works in practice:
Show a higher-tier offer first ($197).
Then show your core offer ($47).
The $47 now feels like a deal relative to the $197.

Without the anchor, $47 is just $47.

**Slide 4:** You can anchor in your copy, not just your pricing table.
"Hiring a consultant for this would cost $500/hr. This system delivers the same framework for $37."
That sentence is an anchor.

**Slide 5:** Use anchoring in:
- Pricing tables (show the expensive option first)
- Product descriptions (compare to the cost of the problem)
- Sales calls (name the alternative before naming your price)

**Slide 6:** Price smarter. Sell more.
Behike Method at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** Anchoring is not manipulation. It is context. And without context, numbers have no meaning.

When a buyer sees $47 in isolation, their brain searches for a reference point: is this cheap, expensive, fair? If you give them no anchor, they create their own from memory, which might be a competitor, a subscription, or a random purchase they made recently.

When you give them the anchor, you control the comparison. You decide what $47 is being measured against. A $197 tier. A $500 consultant. A $300/mo SaaS subscription. Any of those comparisons makes your price look rational and even generous.

This is a copywriting technique, not a trick. It works because it gives the buyer the information they need to make a decision with confidence.

Use it on every pricing page you build.

## Post 4 — Why $27 Converts Better Than $25

**Hook:** Pricing is not math. It is perception. And odd numbers outperform round ones.

**Slide 1:** Pricing is not math. It is perception. And odd numbers outperform round ones.

**Slide 2:** The psychology behind $27:
Round numbers like $25 or $30 feel estimated.
Odd numbers like $27 or $37 feel calculated.
Calculated = you arrived at this for a reason.
That signals value, not discount.

**Slide 3:** The left digit effect:
$29 and $30 are $1 apart.
But $29 is processed as "in the $20 range."
$30 is processed as "thirty dollars."
One dollar changes the mental category.

**Slide 4:** Common high-converting price points for digital products:
$7 (impulse), $17 (low commitment), $27, $37, $47 (core), $97, $147, $197 (premium)

These work because they sit in psychological sweet spots established by years of buyer behavior.

**Slide 5:** The price you choose is not just a transaction.
It is a signal about category, seriousness, and value.
Test two prices. The difference will surprise you.

**Slide 6:** Price with intention, not intuition.
More at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** Behavioral pricing research has been consistent for decades: people do not process prices the way you would think. They do not compare $27 to $30 as a $3 difference. They process them as different categories entirely.

The charm pricing effect (ending in 7 or 9) is one of the most replicated findings in consumer psychology. It is not magic. It is the way the human brain processes numbers quickly under low deliberation.

For solopreneurs selling digital products, this matters directly. The difference between $25 and $27 is the difference between something that feels like a round estimate and something that feels like a deliberate price for a specific value. The latter almost always converts better.

Test your pricing. Run two versions for two weeks. Let data make the decision.

## Post 5 — The Conversation About Price You Need to Stop Having

**Hook:** If you are defending your price, you have already lost the sale.

**Slide 1:** If you are defending your price, you have already lost the sale.

**Slide 2:** Price defense happens when:
The buyer reached the price before they reached trust.
The solution: restructure the page, not the price.

**Slide 3:** The buyer should arrive at your price after:
- Understanding their problem clearly
- Believing a solution exists
- Trusting that you are the right person to provide it

If all three are true, $47 is not expensive.

**Slide 4:** The conversation to have instead:
Not "my price is fair because..."
But "here is what your situation costs you right now."

Frame the problem in cost terms first. Then the price is always smaller than the alternative.

**Slide 5:** The solopreneur who defends their price is focused on the money.
The solopreneur who sells confidently is focused on the transformation.
One of these is more persuasive.

**Slide 6:** Build confidence into the page before you build the buy button.
Behike Method at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** Price objections are symptom, not cause. When a buyer says your product is too expensive, they are usually saying one of three things: I do not believe it works, I do not believe it works for me, or I do not trust you enough yet.

The worst response is to lower the price or explain why the price is justified. Both responses miss the actual objection entirely. Explaining your costs to a buyer who does not yet trust the outcome does not move them toward buying. It moves them toward politely exiting.

The correct response is to address the underlying trust or belief gap first. More evidence. A stronger guarantee. A more specific result claim. A case study that looks like them.

Once those gaps are closed, the price conversation almost never comes up. Confident buyers do not ask for discounts on things they want.

Price confidence is built before the page. Write accordingly.
