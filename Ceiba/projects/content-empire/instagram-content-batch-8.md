## POST 2 . I Tracked 50 Roblox Games for 30 Days (Text Post)
**Account:** @kalaniandrez
**Format:** Long-form text post

---

I tracked 50 Roblox games for 30 days. Concurrent players, session length, return rate.

The finding was not graphics. Not genre. Not even how polished the game was.

It was retention mechanics. Specifically: daily rewards plus a visible progress bar plus a checkpoint system.

Games that had all three kept players 4x longer than games that had none of them. Games that had two out of three were somewhere in the middle. The pattern was consistent across different genres.

The daily reward creates a reason to come back tomorrow. The progress bar makes the current session feel meaningful. The checkpoint system removes the cost of quitting, so players leave without frustration and come back more easily.

None of these are hard to build. They are just rarely prioritized by first-time developers who are focused on the game mechanic itself.

The games with the most players were not the most technically impressive. They were the ones that understood the loop. Start. Progress. Stop. Return.

That loop is what you design for. Everything else is decoration.

**Slide 2 . The Flat Template System:**
Every piece of Roblox clothing starts as a flat 585 x 559 pixel template.

The front panel, back panel, and sleeve panels are all mapped on a single image. When the platform renders a character, it wraps that flat image onto a 3D avatar body.

This means every shirt, every jacket, every costume is technically a 2D texture file that gets projected onto a 3D shape.

You design it flat. Roblox makes it look 3D.

**Slide 3 . The Math:**
The math on clothing sales:

Sell 10,000 shirts at 20 Robux each.
That is 200,000 Robux gross.
After Roblox's 30% cut, you have 140,000 Robux.
At the DevEx rate of roughly $0.0035 per Robux, that converts to about $490 USD.

Small shirts, small margins. The volume game requires consistent output and trend awareness.

## POST 5 . The 3D Fashion Pipeline VTubers Pay $500 to $3000 For (Carousel)
**Account:** @behikeai
**Format:** Carousel (5 slides)

**Hook:** "These outfits start as 2D fabric panels."

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
VTubers pay $500 to $3,000 for custom 3D outfits.

These outfits start as 2D fabric panels.

Five steps from flat cloth to game-ready mesh.

**Slide 2 . Marvelous Designer:**
Step 1: Marvelous Designer.

You draw 2D fabric panels the same way a tailor would. Hem lines, seam allowances, panel shapes.

The software runs a physics simulation and drapes the fabric onto your 3D avatar body. Gravity, stiffness, collision, all calculated in real time.

The result is cloth that looks like it was physically sewn and worn. Not modeled by hand.

**Slide 3 . ZBrush:**
Step 2: ZBrush.

The Marvelous Designer output is high-polygon. Now you add the fine details.

Stitching along seam lines. Button holes. Collar creases. Surface texture variations where fabric folds under stress.

ZBrush handles this with sculpting tools built for exactly this kind of surface work. Nothing in this step is mechanical. It is craft.

**Slide 4 . Retopology:**
Step 3: Retopology.

The sculpted mesh has too many polygons for a real-time avatar. You rebuild the mesh manually with clean edge flow and a polygon count the game engine can handle.

This step determines whether the garment deforms correctly when the character moves. Bad retopology shows immediately the moment someone starts animating.

Clean topology is the difference between a professional delivery and a mesh that breaks in motion.

**Slide 5 . Substance Painter and FBX Export:**
Step 4: Substance Painter.

Physical textures are added here. Fabric weave patterns, color variation, wear marks, roughness maps. Substance Painter uses layer-based workflows similar to Photoshop but outputs texture maps specifically designed for 3D engines.

Step 5: FBX export.

Final mesh plus texture maps exported in FBX format. Ready to import into Unity, Unreal, VRChat, or any other target platform.

The whole pipeline from fabric panels to FBX. That is what clients are paying for.
