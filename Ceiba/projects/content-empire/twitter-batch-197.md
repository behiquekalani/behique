## Threads

**Thread 1 — The complete digital product launch checklist**

1/ After watching a lot of digital product launches succeed and fail, the pattern is clear. Here is the checklist that separates them.

2/ 30 days out: Content shift. Start talking about the problem your product solves, not the product. Build the case. Establish why this matters. Your audience should be nodding before they see a price.

3/ 21 days out: Waitlist open. Even 50 people on a waitlist changes the psychology of your launch. You have proof of interest. You have an audience to communicate with directly.

4/ 14 days out: Email sequence begins. Three to five emails that go deeper on the problem and start introducing your solution indirectly. Not selling yet. Building context.

5/ 7 days out: Social proof and specifics. Case studies, testimonials from beta testers, behind-the-scenes. Make the product feel real and proven before it is available.

6/ Launch day: Email plus social. Send the offer to your list first. They have been warming for 30 days. Give them first access. Then push to social.

7/ Days 2-7: Follow-up sequence. Address objections. Share results if buyers are already reporting them. Deadline creates urgency but only if the product is genuinely worth it.

**Thread 2 — Why most first launches fail**

1/ The first digital product launch almost always underperforms. Here is why, and how to fix it before it happens to you.

2/ The most common mistake: launching to the wrong audience. You have 500 followers who found you through memes or random posts, not through content about the problem your product solves. Of course they will not buy.

3/ Second mistake: pricing guesswork. Most people under-price because they are afraid. Under-pricing signals low value to buyers and attracts people who would not have bought anyway.

4/ Third mistake: no launch sequence. Dropping a product with one post and hoping the algorithm delivers customers is not a launch strategy. It is a prayer.

5/ Fourth mistake: no deadline. A product that is always available creates no urgency. Cart close, price increase, or bonus expiry. Pick one and stick to it.

6/ The fix for all of these is the same: build the audience first, then build the product. Everything else is a downstream consequence of whether you have the right people watching.

**Thread 3 — The sales page formula that works**

1/ Most digital product sales pages are just feature lists with a buy button. Here is what actually converts.

2/ Open with the cost of the problem. Not your solution. Not your credentials. The cost of the problem the reader is living with right now. Dollar cost, time cost, emotional cost.

3/ Agitate it. Make them feel the weight of not solving this. Be specific. Vague pain does not move people. "Wasted Sundays trying to fix your pricing and still not knowing if it is right" is specific pain.

4/ Introduce your mechanism, not your product. The mechanism is the specific process or framework your product delivers. It should sound different from everything else they have seen.

5/ Proof. Screenshots, results, quotes from beta readers. The more specific the proof, the stronger it hits. "I made $2,300 in 11 days following this system" beats "life-changing."

6/ The offer stack. What they get, the value of each piece, the total value, your price. Make the math obvious. If your product is $97 and the value stack is $400+, show that.

7/ One call to action, repeated three times on the page. No multiple offers. No confusion about what to click. One decision, made easy.

**Thread 4 — Pricing your digital product correctly**

1/ Pricing a digital product is where most creators lose before they even launch. Here is the framework I would use.

2/ First principle: price to the outcome, not to the effort. You did not spend 40 hours building a course. You built a system that produces a specific result. Price the result.

3/ Compare to the alternative. If hiring someone to do what your product teaches costs $3,000, your $297 product is not expensive. It is the cheapest version of the solution.

4/ Test anchoring. Put your full price product next to a tier that is clearly too expensive for most people. The middle option looks reasonable by comparison.

5/ Never price based on what you would personally pay. You built it. You know the shortcuts. The person who needs it does not have that context and will value it differently.

6/ Start higher than feels comfortable. You can run sales. You cannot easily raise prices without damaging trust. Your first public price sets the anchor.

**Thread 5 — The post-launch sequence no one builds**

1/ Most creators stop communicating after launch week ends. That is leaving 20-30% of potential revenue on the table.

2/ Days 8-14 post-launch are for buyers. Ask them what they are learning. Feature their progress publicly. This creates social proof that converts people still on the fence.

3/ Days 15-21 are for the fence-sitters. Re-open the cart with a case study from an early buyer. Send one email focused entirely on the most common objection you heard during launch.

4/ Days 22-30 are for the cold audience. The people who saw your launch but had no context. A longer-form piece of content that explains the full problem and solution. No sales pitch. Just depth.

5/ Month two: a case study post. One detailed breakdown of a buyer's result. This is your most powerful piece of launch content for the next launch cycle.

6/ The creators who grow consistently are the ones who treat every launch as a content system, not an event. The event thinking is why most people launch once and get stuck.
