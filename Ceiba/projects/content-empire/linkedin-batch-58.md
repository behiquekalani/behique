# LinkedIn Batch 58 — The MVP Approach to Digital Products

## Post 1
The most expensive mistake in digital product creation is building the wrong thing perfectly.

It happens constantly. Someone spends three months building a comprehensive course on a topic, launches it, and gets twelve sales. Not twelve thousand. Twelve. The problem was not the quality. The problem was that the assumption about what people would pay for was never tested. The product was built on a hypothesis, not evidence.

The MVP approach inverts this. You test the hypothesis before investing in the build. A pre-sale page. A low-cost workshop. A Google Doc that delivers the core idea. You put it in front of real people with real money and see what happens. If they buy, you build the full version knowing it works. If they do not, you saved three months of work on something nobody wanted.

This is not about lowering your standards. It is about directing your effort at the right thing. A great product built on a validated idea beats a perfect product built on an assumption every time.

What assumption about your audience are you currently building on without testing?

## Post 2
Speed is a business strategy that most product creators underestimate.

Two creators start building products on the same day. One prioritizes getting to market quickly. They ship a simple, focused product in three weeks. The other prioritizes comprehensiveness. They launch twelve weeks later with a much bigger product. The first creator has already collected feedback, made improvements, earned revenue, and built testimonials by the time the second creator launches.

The market does not reward the most comprehensive product. It rewards the first good product. "Good enough" shipped beats "perfect" delayed. Not because quality does not matter, but because real feedback from real customers improves quality faster than any amount of solo refinement. Your instinct about what to include is less accurate than your first customer's complaint about what was missing.

Ship faster. Iterate in public. The feedback loop is the quality mechanism.

## Post 3
One outcome. One product. This is the rule that changes conversion rates.

Most first-time digital product creators make the same mistake: they try to cover everything they know about a topic. The result is a product that promises ten outcomes and delivers all of them at a surface level. The buyer wanted one thing solved deeply. They got ten things addressed shallowly. Refund requested.

The products that convert well and generate lasting testimonials solve one specific problem completely. They do not cover the entire landscape of a topic. They pick the most painful, most common problem in their niche and solve it so thoroughly that the buyer cannot imagine needing anything else for that specific issue.

This also makes marketing dramatically easier. "The complete guide to online business" is impossible to market. "How to get your first 50 email subscribers without a following" is simple to market. The buyer knows if they are the person. They know what they are getting. They know what problem it solves.

What is the one most specific problem you could build a product around this week?

## Post 4
The first version of your product is research. Treat it accordingly.

Your first 20-50 customers are not just buyers. They are the research team for your second version. They will tell you exactly what was missing, what was confusing, what they needed more of, and what they skipped entirely. That information is worth far more than the revenue from those first sales. It is the blueprint for a better product that will convert at a higher rate and command a higher price.

Most creators are too proud to embrace this. They want the first version to be definitive. So they either delay the launch waiting for perfection, or they launch and ignore the feedback to protect their ego. Both paths are expensive. The ones who compound fastest treat their early product as version one, explicitly ask for feedback, and build version two based on what they learn. V2 always converts better than V1. But you only get to V2 by shipping V1.

Send three questions to your last five customers today. What did they need that was not there?

---

## Post 5
Validation before creation is not just a tactical decision. It is a respect for your own time.

You have a finite number of hours to build things. Every hour spent building something nobody buys is an hour you cannot recover. This is not about fear of failure. Failure is fine. Untested assumptions are the problem. Testing an assumption costs a week of effort. Building a product based on an untested assumption costs three months.

The validation process is simple. Describe the outcome of your product clearly. State the price. Put it in front of the people who might want it and ask them to commit. Not "would you buy this?" That question tells you nothing. Actually ask them to pay. Pre-orders, deposits, founding member pricing. Real commitment reveals real demand in a way that survey responses never will.

If they pay, you build. If they do not, you revise the offer until they do. The revision is faster than the build.
