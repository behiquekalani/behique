## Thread 3: Lumina vs f.lux — Why I Built My Own

1/
Lumina vs f.lux: why I built my own when f.lux already exists. (thread)

2/
f.lux is excellent. I used it for years.
This is not a take-down. It is an explanation of a different design decision.

3/
f.lux controls your display hardware.
It requires install. It runs as a background process. On some machines, it conflicts with color-managed apps.

4/
I wanted something that works in a browser tab.
No install. No permissions. Open it, and it works.

5/
The second difference: f.lux is primarily a blue-light filter.
Lumina adds the circadian schedule layer.
Not just "warmer light at night" but "specific light cues at specific biological times."

6/
The third difference: f.lux is built for your screen.
Lumina is built for your schedule.
It tells you when to expose yourself to bright light in the morning, not just when to dim your screen at night.

7/
I also wanted to learn.
Building it taught me more about Web Audio API, browser color spaces, and geolocation than any tutorial would have.

8/
The output is also a product.
Lumina is on Gumroad at $4.99. The product teaches you how to use circadian cues deliberately.

9/
f.lux solves one problem well.
I wanted to solve an adjacent problem and build something I could own.
That is a valid reason to build.

10/
If you just want blue light control: use f.lux. It is free and it works.
If you want to understand the full circadian system and apply it: Lumina.

## Thread 4: The Ghost Writing Kit — Scaling Content Without Losing Your Voice

1/
The ghost writing kit: how to scale content without losing what makes your content yours. (thread)

2/
The problem with hiring ghost writers or using AI for content:
it sounds like you, but it does not feel like you.
Readers notice. Engagement drops.

3/
The fix is not writing everything yourself.
The fix is capturing your voice so precisely that someone else can write in it.

4/
What goes into a voice document:
- Sentence length patterns (do you write short or long)
- Words you use constantly
- Words you never use
- Topics you always circle back to
- Your argument structure (do you start with the claim or build to it)

5/
One addition most people skip: capture your opening lines.
Your first line pattern is the most distinctive thing about your voice.
If the ghost writer gets the first line wrong, the whole thread is off.

6/
Second thing: build a "bad examples" document.
Not what sounds like you. What sounds like a parody of you.
This is harder to articulate but more useful than the good examples.

7/
Third: record yourself talking before you write.
Voice-first captures the rhythm. Text-first captures the structure.
You need both.

8/
AI application: feed the voice document to a Claude or GPT-4 system prompt.
Tell it to write in this voice. Check the output against the bad examples list.
Iterate until it passes your filter.

9/
This is what the Ghost Writer Kit teaches.
It is not an AI writing tool. It is a system for extracting your voice so the tools can use it.

## Thread 5: 6 Months of Building in Public — What Changed and What I Got Wrong

1/
6 months of building in public.
What changed and what I got wrong. (thread)

2/
What changed: I stopped thinking about audience and started thinking about documentation.
The mindset shift made every post easier to write.

3/
Building in public is not performance.
It is a log. You are logging what you built, what broke, and what you learned.
When you treat it as performance, you get writer's block. When you treat it as logging, you always have something to say.

4/
What I got wrong at the start: I posted outcomes.
"Shipped X. Revenue Y. Milestone Z."
Nobody cares about your outcomes unless they have watched the process.

5/
The posts that got the most response were the ones where something went wrong.
A launch that flopped. An assumption that was wrong. A week where nothing shipped.

6/
Honesty is the only differentiator in this space.
Everyone is showing highlights. The person showing the actual build gets followed.

7/
Second thing I got wrong: I tried to post consistently before I had a system.
Consistency is a byproduct of systems, not willpower.
I built the batch system (30 posts at once) and the consistency problem disappeared.

8/
What I would tell someone starting: do not optimize for growth.
Optimize for being honest about what you are building.
Growth follows the honest account. It does not follow the polished one.

9/
Third thing I got wrong: I thought I needed more products before I could post.
You need one product. Then you document building the second one.
The content is the build, not the launch.

10/
6 months in: one store, 25 products, 4 social platforms, consistent weekly revenue.
None of it happened because I was smart.
It happened because I kept logging.
