# Twitter Threads — Batch 102
## Topic: How to Write Hooks That Stop the Scroll on Any Platform

---

### Thread 1: The first line is the whole game. Here is how to write it.

Most people write their post and then add a hook at the end.

That is backwards.

The hook is the post. Everything else is just evidence.

1/ The first line of any post is the only line most people will read. On LinkedIn, posts get cut off after 2-3 lines. On X, the scroll is instant. On Instagram, the caption is an afterthought. The hook determines whether anyone reads what comes after.

2/ A good hook does one thing: creates enough tension or curiosity that stopping feels like losing. Not cheap curiosity. Specific, earned curiosity based on something real the reader cares about.

3/ There are 5 patterns that work consistently across every platform. They do not require genius. They require specificity and honesty.

4/ The counterintuitive statement works because it creates cognitive friction. "Posting more is why you are not growing" forces the reader to stop and either disagree or want to know more. Either reaction earns the click.

5/ The specific number works because it signals real data. "I tracked 90 days of LinkedIn posts and the results changed how I think about content" is more believable than any vague claim about strategy.

6/ The direct address works because people respond to being seen. "If you have under 1,000 followers and you are building something, read this" tells the right person that this was written for them specifically.

Most hooks fail because they are generic. Specificity is the fix.

### Thread 2: 5 hook formulas that work on every platform. With actual examples.

Write 100 hooks. 80 of them will use one of these 5 patterns.

Here they are:

1/ The counterintuitive statement.
State what contradicts the conventional belief.
Example: "Consistency without strategy is just noise at scale."
Example: "The people with the most followers are not making the most money."

Rule: it has to be defensible. You are not being contrarian for engagement. You are introducing an idea you will actually back up.

2/ The specific number.
Numbers signal real data and credibility.
Example: "I got 3 client inquiries from one LinkedIn post. Here is what made it work."
Example: "This one change doubled my comment rate in 2 weeks."

Rule: the number has to be real. Fabricated specificity is worse than vagueness.

3/ The direct address.
Call out the exact reader.
Example: "This is for the solopreneur building something on the side while working full-time."
Example: "Freelancers who are not on LinkedIn are leaving money on the table."

Rule: be specific about who you are talking to. The narrower the address, the stronger the response from the right people.

4/ The bold claim.
State what you believe clearly.
Example: "Most LinkedIn advice is written for corporate employees. This is for builders."
Example: "Your profile is losing you clients every day and you do not know it."

Rule: do not hedge. "Maybe" and "sometimes" are death to this formula.

5/ The story opening.
Drop the reader into a specific moment.
Example: "Six months ago I had 200 followers and no clients. Last week I turned down a project."
Example: "A stranger DM'd me because of a post I wrote 8 months ago."

Rule: start in the middle of the story, not at the beginning. Skip the context-setting.

### Thread 3: Why your hooks are not working. The 4 mistakes most creators make.

The hook formula is simple. Most people still get it wrong.

Here is what is actually happening:

1/ Too vague. "Some thoughts on content strategy" tells no one anything. A hook needs to make a specific promise or create specific tension. Vague hooks produce vague results: low opens, low clicks, low reads.

2/ Too long. A hook that runs 3 sentences before getting to the point has already lost most readers. The first sentence should do the whole job. The second sentence should deepen it, not explain it.

3/ Burying the lead. Starting with context instead of the point is the most common mistake. "I have been thinking about LinkedIn lately and I noticed something interesting" is 14 words that say nothing. Start with the interesting thing.

4/ No tension. A hook without tension is just a sentence. "Here is what I learned about email marketing" creates zero tension. "Why most email sequences die after the second open" creates the question the reader needs answered.

The test: read only your first sentence. Are you more curious or less curious than before you read it? If neutral or less, rewrite.

### Thread 4: The scroll stop test. How to know if your hook actually works before you post.

Before you post anything, run this test.

1/ Read only the first sentence. Cover the rest. Ask: does this sentence make me want to read the second sentence? If the answer is no or maybe, it is not ready.

2/ Replace your name with a stranger's name. Would you stop scrolling on a feed if a person you had never heard of posted this first line? If not, the hook is relying on existing trust you have built rather than earning new attention.

3/ Say it out loud. This is the fastest filter. If the first sentence sounds weird when spoken, it reads weird. Conversational hooks perform better than written-for-the-screen hooks on every platform.

4/ Check the specificity level. Highlight every specific noun, number, or concrete reference in your first two sentences. If there are none, the hook is probably too abstract. Add one specific detail.

5/ Ask: what question does this hook create? Every good hook implies a question the reader now needs answered. If you cannot name the question, the hook is not working.

Most creators skip all of this and wonder why their content does not get read.

### Thread 5: Platform-specific hook adjustments. Same formula, different calibration.

The five formulas work everywhere. The calibration changes by platform.

1/ X (Twitter): the hook is the whole tweet or the first line of the thread. It needs to work in under 15 words. Cut ruthlessly. "Why most creators never reach $5K/month" is enough. Do not add explanation before the number or context.

2/ LinkedIn: 2-3 sentences work better than 1 because the reader is in a longer-form mindset. The first sentence should still be punchy, but you have more room before the "see more" cut. Use that room to add one specific detail.

3/ Instagram: the hook is competing with visual content. It needs to be more direct and more personal than other platforms. "I did not believe this until it happened to me" works better on Instagram than on LinkedIn because the emotional hook lands differently there.

4/ Newsletter subject line: this is the highest-stakes hook in your content system. It determines open rate. The counterintuitive statement and the specific number are the two strongest formulas for email. Avoid questions. Questions feel like homework.

5/ YouTube title: titles are hooks. The bold claim and the direct address work best. "What I would do if I started my channel today with zero subscribers" works because it is specific and it directly addresses the viewer's actual situation.

Same foundation. Different length, different register, different emotional calibration.
