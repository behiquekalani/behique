# Twitter/X Batch 148 — Why Most Online Courses Fail Their Students

---

**Tweet 1 (punchy single)**
Most online courses fail students not because the information is bad.

Because the information is delivered in a way that assumes the student will do the hardest part on their own.

They won't.

---

**Tweet 2 (punchy single)**
A course is not a school.

No deadline. No social pressure. No grade. No one watching.

The completion rate is 3-10% across the industry. If that doesn't tell you something about the format, nothing will.

---

**Tweet 3 — 3-tweet thread**

Tweet 1/3:
The real reason courses fail students: they're designed around delivering knowledge, not around producing behavior change.

Knowledge is cheap. The internet is full of it.

Behavior change is expensive and rare. Almost no one is building for it.

Tweet 2/3:
You can watch every cooking video on YouTube and still not know how to cook.

Information delivery and skill development are not the same.

Most course creators confuse the two and call it teaching.

Tweet 3/3:
The best courses I've seen have one thing in common: they make it impossible to stay passive.

Assignments. Community accountability. Real feedback loops.

Not more video. More doing.

---

**Tweet 4 (punchy single)**
The dirty secret of the course industry: the creator gets paid whether you finish or not.

That incentive structure produces a lot of beautiful production with very little actual transformation.

---

**Tweet 5 (punchy single)**
Every course that fails a student fails in one of three places:
1. Unclear outcome from the start
2. Too much theory, not enough reps
3. No system for the student to stay accountable

Rarely is the information the problem.

---

**Tweet 6 — 7-tweet thread**

Tweet 1/7:
I want to talk about why online courses have a systemic failure problem.

This matters if you're building one, buying one, or wondering why the $297 thing on your hard drive isn't working.

Tweet 2/7:
The fundamental design flaw: most courses are built around the creator's knowledge, not the student's transformation.

The creator asks: "What do I know that I should include?"

The right question is: "What does the student need to do differently when this is over? And how does every module move them toward that?"

Different question. Completely different course.

Tweet 3/7:
The information problem.

Course creators over-teach because depth feels like value. More modules. More bonus lessons. More hours of content.

But information overload is the enemy of action.

A student who finishes a 3-hour course and does something with it got more value than one who buys an 80-hour course and never touches it.

Tweet 4/7:
The accountability vacuum.

School works (to whatever degree it works) partly because you have deadlines, peers, grades, social consequences for falling behind.

A self-paced course has none of that.

Yet most courses are built like self-study textbooks and then act surprised when no one finishes.

Tweet 5/7:
The onboarding problem.

Most courses dump you into a portal and say "start from module 1."

No orientation to the transformation. No clarity on what the student should expect to feel or struggle with. No promise of what they're walking into.

The first 15 minutes of a course determine whether someone finishes it.

Almost no one optimizes those 15 minutes.

Tweet 6/7:
What actually works:

- A clear before/after framing at the start ("You're going from X to Y")
- Short actionable modules instead of long comprehensive ones
- Assignments that require the student to produce something
- A community or check-in loop to create social pressure
- A graduation condition, not just a completion button

Not harder. Just built around behavior, not information.

Tweet 7/7:
If you're building a course:

The test isn't "is everything in here?"
The test is "can a normal person go from module 1 to a real result?"

If the answer requires the student to be unusually motivated and disciplined, you've built a reference document, not a course.

Fix the course. Don't blame the student.

**Tweet 7 (punchy single)**
The completion rate for most online courses is 3-10%.

If a restaurant had a 90-95% "food returned uneaten" rate, we'd call it a bad restaurant.

We don't apply that logic to education. We should.

---

**Tweet 8 (punchy single)**
A course that promises transformation but delivers information isn't a bad product.

It's a mislabeled one.

---

**Tweet 9 — 3-tweet thread**

Tweet 1/3:
If you bought a course and didn't finish it, don't automatically blame yourself.

Ask: was the course built in a way that made finishing likely?

Most aren't. That's a design failure, not a willpower failure.

Tweet 2/3:
That said, here's what gives students the best chance at finishing any course:

1. Decide the outcome before you start.
2. Block time. Not "free time" -- a specific block.
3. Tell someone you're doing it.
4. Complete the first action on day 1.

Structure what the course won't.

Tweet 3/3:
The best investment in your own learning isn't finding better courses.

It's building better personal systems around whatever you're learning from.

The course is the material. You're still the student.

---

**Tweet 10 (punchy single)**
Courses that produce results ask: "What does the student need to do?"

Courses that fail ask: "What does the student need to know?"

Build around doing.
