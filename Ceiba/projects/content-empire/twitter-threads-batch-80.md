## Thread 80-1: Vanity Metrics Are Killing Your Strategy

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
You're optimizing for likes.

But likes don't pay rent.

**Tweet 2:**
Most content creators track:

- Views
- Likes
- Followers
- Comments
- Reach

These are vanity metrics. They feel good. They're easy to track. They're almost completely disconnected from revenue.

**Tweet 3:**
The metrics that actually matter for a business:

1. Click-through rate to your offer
2. Email opt-in conversion rate
3. Email-to-sale conversion rate
4. Revenue per subscriber
5. Returning buyer rate

Notice: none of these show up in the native analytics of any social platform.

**Tweet 4:**
Why creators confuse vanity and value metrics:

Platforms show you reach and likes because those are the metrics that make you feel good and post more.

More posts = more platform growth.

The platform's incentive is not your revenue. Your incentive should be.

**Tweet 5:**
A post with 500 views and 30 email sign-ups is more valuable than a post with 50,000 views and 20 sign-ups.

One built a business asset. One built a number.

Stop optimizing for the number.

**Tweet 6:**
The minimum viable analytics stack:

- Traffic source (where are buyers coming from?)
- Email list growth rate
- Conversion rate on your sales page
- Revenue per email sent

These four tell you more about your business than all the vanity metrics combined.

**Tweet 7:**
Hard truth: if your follower count is going up but your revenue isn't, your content strategy is broken.

Growth and revenue should correlate over time. If they don't, diagnose the disconnect before you create more content.

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
Pull up your last 30 days of analytics.

What drove email sign-ups? What drove sales?

If you can't answer that, you don't have a content strategy. You have a posting habit.

## Thread 80-2: The Three Metrics That Actually Move Revenue

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
After everything, three numbers tell you whether your content is working.

Everything else is noise.

**Tweet 2:**
Metric 1: Email list growth rate.

How many new subscribers are you adding per week?

This is the clearest measure of whether your content is attracting the right people and converting them into owned audience members.

If this number is flat or declining, your top of funnel is broken.

**Tweet 3:**
Metric 2: Email-to-sale conversion rate.

What percentage of your email list buys something within 90 days?

This tells you if your list is engaged and if your offers match your audience's needs.

Industry average: 1-3%. Behike target: 3-5%.

**Tweet 4:**
Metric 3: Content-to-DM conversion rate.

How many of your posts generate an inbound message?

DMs are the highest signal of interest in content marketing. Someone stopped, processed, and reached out.

Track which posts generate DMs. Double down on those formats.

**Tweet 5:**
Why these three and not others:

- Email growth: measures audience quality
- Email conversion: measures offer-market fit
- DM rate: measures content resonance

Each one is a different layer of the funnel. You need all three to diagnose where the problem is.

**Tweet 6:**
How to use these numbers:

Low email growth but high engagement: your CTA to subscribe is weak. Fix the signup offer.

High email growth but low conversion: your offer doesn't match your audience. Fix the product.

Low DM rate: your content isn't hitting pain points hard enough. Fix the message.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
Three numbers. One month of tracking. Clear diagnosis.

Save this so you know what to look for next time you review your content performance.

Follow for more on content that actually converts.

## Thread 80-3: What a "Good" Piece of Content Actually Looks Like

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Most creators judge their content by how it performs.

The better filter: does it do the job it was designed to do?

**Tweet 2:**
Every piece of content should have a job.

- Awareness content: reach new people who've never heard of you
- Trust content: deepen the relationship with existing followers
- Conversion content: move warm readers toward buying something

A post without a clear job is just noise.

**Tweet 3:**
Awareness content metrics:

- Reach (this is the ONE context where reach matters)
- Shares and reposts
- New followers gained

Optimize these for top-of-funnel impact. Use broad, relatable pain points.

**Tweet 4:**
Trust content metrics:

- Comment depth (are people sharing real thoughts?)
- Saves
- DMs and replies
- Email replies

Optimize these by going deeper, being more specific, and being more personal.

**Tweet 5:**
Conversion content metrics:

- Click-through rate on the link
- Sales page conversion rate
- Revenue generated

Optimize these with clear offers, specific outcomes, and strong CTAs.

One post can only serve one master. Know which one before you write.

**Tweet 6:**
The content audit:

Look at your last 20 posts. For each one, answer:

1. What was the job of this post?
2. Did it do that job?
3. If not, what would need to change?

Most people discover they've been posting awareness content when their business needs conversion content.

**Tweet 7:**
The ratio that works for a solo creator building a business:

- 50% trust content
- 30% awareness content
- 20% conversion content

You're building relationships first. Revenue follows relationships.

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
What job is your most recent post doing?

If you're not sure, it doesn't have one.

Follow for more on building a content strategy with intent behind every post.

## Thread 80-4: Stop A/B Testing, Start Pattern Matching

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Most creators test variations of the same type of content.

The better move is to test fundamentally different approaches, then pattern match what works.

**Tweet 2:**
The problem with most content testing:

"Should I post at 9am or 10am?"
"Should I use a hook question or a bold statement?"

These are micro-optimizations on content that might be wrong at a fundamental level.

Fix the message before you optimize the delivery.

**Tweet 3:**
Pattern matching over testing:

Look at your 10 best performing posts over 3 months.

What do they have in common?

- Topic area
- Format (list, story, argument, how-to)
- Tone (personal, instructional, provocative)
- CTA type

That's your pattern. Write more like your best posts.

**Tweet 4:**
The content audit that takes 30 minutes:

1. Export your last 60 days of posts
2. Sort by the metric that matters (clicks, not likes)
3. Look at the top 10 and bottom 10
4. Describe the pattern in writing

You'll see it immediately. Most people are creating more of the wrong thing without realizing it.

**Tweet 5:**
What pattern matching reveals:

Most creators have 2-3 content archetypes that consistently outperform. They rarely know what they are until they look.

Once you know, you stop guessing and start producing on purpose.

**Tweet 6:**
The efficiency gain from pattern matching:

Instead of 20 mediocre posts per month, you write 10 strong posts that match your top patterns.

Half the output. Better results. More time to build the product.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
What does your top-performing content actually have in common?

If you don't know, pull the data and find out.

Follow for more on building content systems that work without burning you out.

## Thread 80-5: The Content Metric That Predicts Business Growth

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
There's one content metric that predicts long-term business growth better than any other.

Most creators don't track it.

**Tweet 2:**
That metric: content-driven email subscribers per week.

Not total subscribers. Not open rates. The weekly rate at which your content converts readers into email list members.

This is the bridge between attention and ownership.

**Tweet 3:**
Why this metric matters:

Social reach is borrowed. Email subscribers are owned.

Every piece of content either adds to your owned audience or doesn't. The ones that do have a clear pipeline: content → page → opt-in → email → product.

**Tweet 4:**
How to track it:

- Set up UTM parameters on your link-in-bio and all content links
- Connect your email platform (Beehiiv, ConvertKit) to a dashboard
- Tag every new subscriber with the source: which post, which platform, which week

Now you can see exactly which content drives your most valuable metric.

**Tweet 5:**
What the numbers tell you:

If LinkedIn drives 80% of your subscribers and Instagram drives 4%, redistribute your content effort accordingly.

Data-driven reallocation compounds. Most creators ignore this and spread effort evenly across all platforms.

**Tweet 6:**
The secondary metric that supports this:

Weekly active reader rate on your email list.

If subscribers stop opening, the pipeline is leaking. Fix the newsletter before you try to grow it.

Plugging the hole matters more than pouring more water in.

**Tweet 7:**
The content system that makes this trackable:

1. Every post ends with a CTA to a landing page
2. Every landing page has one opt-in
3. Every opt-in is tagged with the source
4. Every week you review which content drove which subs

Simple. Repeatable. Reveals the truth.

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
Pull last week's data. How many new email subscribers did your content generate?

If you can't answer that question, you don't know if your content is working.

Save this thread. Build the tracking system this week.

Follow for more on content that builds a business, not just an audience.
