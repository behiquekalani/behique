## Post 1: The 7 Post Types (Complete Reference)

This is batch 40 of LinkedIn content from the Behike content engine.

After analyzing hundreds of posts across different accounts, niches, and audience sizes, there are seven post formats that consistently generate engagement regardless of niche.

Here they are.

1. The personal story with a professional lesson. Specific incident. What you learned. Why it matters.
2. The counter-intuitive take. Start with a claim that challenges the received wisdom. Back it with evidence or experience.
3. The numbered framework. "Five things I do every week" or "Three frameworks for X." Structure creates saves.
4. The data post. A specific number, measurement, or tracked result that reveals something non-obvious.
5. The failure story. What went wrong. Why. What you changed. Failure stories build more trust than success stories because they are rarer and harder to fake.
6. The curation post. "The best resource I found this month on X" with a specific explanation of why it is worth reading.
7. The direct opinion. A clear, short take on a topic in your niche with no hedging. "I think X. Here is why."

Every post that performs well falls into one of these categories.

When your content stalls, it is almost always because you have drifted away from all seven. Pick one. Write it. Post it.

## Post 2: Why the Failure Story Is Underused

Success stories are everywhere on LinkedIn.

Every founder has a "we almost failed but we made it" narrative. Every consultant has a "the client was struggling until we did X" case study.

Failure stories are different. Not the failure you recovered from with a triumphant pivot. The failure you have not fully figured out yet.

"I built a product last year that never found customers. I still do not know exactly why. Here is what I have ruled out."

That post will outperform most success stories you can write. Not because people enjoy watching others fail. Because the willingness to be honest about unresolved failure is rare enough to be remarkable.

The professional audience on LinkedIn has developed a strong immunity to polished success narratives. They have read thousands of them. They know the format. The pattern is too predictable to be interesting.

An honest, specific, unresolved failure post creates genuine curiosity. People want to help. They want to share their version. They want to know what happened.

That is the comment energy that drives distribution.

The failure story is underused because it is uncomfortable to write. That discomfort is the tell. Write the uncomfortable one. It is usually the truest one.

## Post 3: The Direct Opinion and Why It Builds Faster

Most professional content on LinkedIn is hedged.

"It depends. There are multiple perspectives. Different situations call for different approaches."

These posts are technically correct and completely forgettable.

The direct opinion post is the opposite. It takes a position. It does not caveat that position into meaninglessness.

"Hybrid work mandates are productivity theater. Companies that need to see employees to know they are working have a management problem, not a remote work problem."

That is a direct opinion. Some people will disagree. Some will agree strongly. Almost everyone who has a view on it will read to the end.

The rule for a direct opinion post: one clear claim, two to three sentences of evidence or reasoning, one closing line that confirms the position or invites a response.

No five paragraphs of build-up before the actual take. The take goes first.

The risk with direct opinion posts is that some people will disagree publicly. This is not a downside. Disagreement in the comments is algorithmic gold on LinkedIn.

The posts you want to avoid are the ones that provoke no reaction. A mild disagreement is better than indifference.

Have a position. State it clearly. Let the audience respond.

## Post 4: The Curation Post Done Right

Most curation posts on LinkedIn underperform because they are lazy.

"Here is a great article I read this week: [link]"

That is not curation. That is forwarding an email.

Curation that performs is editorial. The value is not the resource you found. It is your judgment about why it matters, what it is saying that others are missing, and what the reader should take from it.

The format that works:

One sentence identifying what the resource is.

Two to three sentences explaining the specific insight it contains that the reader will not find easily elsewhere.

One sentence on who specifically will benefit from it.

The resource link.

Total length: under 200 words. The brevity signals confidence. You found something good. You can explain why quickly. That is the mark of someone who reads widely and thinks clearly.

The best curation posts build authority faster than almost any other format because they demonstrate two things simultaneously: you consume quality inputs, and you can extract what matters from them.

For professionals who do not have dramatic personal stories or bold contrarian takes, curation done well is one of the most reliable audience-building formats available.

## Post 5: What 40 Batches Taught Me About LinkedIn Content

This is the fortieth batch in the Behike LinkedIn content library.

Here is what three years and hundreds of posts have confirmed.

Consistency beats optimization.

The creators who worry most about the optimal posting time, the perfect format, the exact number of hashtags, are the same ones who never build momentum. They are optimizing a car that has not started yet.

The creators who post something useful every week for twelve months, imperfect, unoptimized, occasionally too long or too short, consistently outperform the optimizers.

The second thing: the posts that felt most uncertain were almost always the ones that performed best. The uncertainty means you said something real. Something true but unpolished. Something that cost you something to say.

That is what earns trust. Not the clean take. Not the framework you have memorized. The thing you actually believe, stated directly.

LinkedIn is a long game. The returns come in year two and three, not in the first ninety days.

If you are in the first ninety days and wondering if it is working, the answer is: probably yes, but it is too early to see it. Keep going.

The forty batches exist because someone kept going. That is the only requirement.
