# LinkedIn Batch 8 — Building in Public

---

**Post 1**
**Hook:** Transparency builds trust faster than any credential you could put on your resume.

**Body:**
I've watched people with MBAs, certifications, and impressive titles struggle to attract clients while a 22-year-old with no formal background builds a six-figure business in 18 months.

The difference is not credentials. It is evidence.

When you build in public, you give people something credentials never can: a window into how you think, how you solve problems, and whether you actually know what you're talking about. That's real trust.

Credentials say "trust me." Building in public says "watch me." One is a claim. The other is proof.

The professionals who are going to win the next decade are not the ones with the longest resume. They are the ones who have the most documented track record of doing the work.

Start today. You don't need permission and you don't need to be finished.

**CTA:** What's one thing you're working on right now that you could share publicly this week?

---

**Post 2**
**Hook:** Not everything belongs online. Here's how to know the difference.

**Body:**
Building in public doesn't mean oversharing. It means strategic transparency. Most people either share nothing or share everything and neither works.

What to share: your thinking process, your frameworks, your failures after you've processed them, the specific lessons from a project, results with context, and the messy middle of something you're building.

What to keep private: client details without consent, incomplete thoughts you haven't tested yet, internal conflicts with partners or team members, financial specifics that could hurt negotiations, and anything you'd regret if your next client Googled you.

The filter is simple. Ask yourself: does sharing this help someone else and protect the people involved? If yes, it belongs online. If it's drama or still too raw, it doesn't.

The goal is to be useful and honest, not to perform vulnerability. There's a big difference.

People can tell when you're sharing for attention versus sharing because you actually want to help. One builds followers. The other builds trust.

**CTA:** Read through your last 10 posts. How many of them would you send directly to a client?

---

**Post 3**
**Hook:** The best time to build an audience was two years ago. The second best time is before you need one.

**Body:**
Most people try to build an audience when they have something to sell. That's the wrong order of operations.

When you start posting because you need clients or customers, desperation leaks through the content. You write to convert instead of to help. The audience feels it and they don't engage.

When you build an audience before you need it, you have time to figure out what resonates. You can experiment without pressure. You build real relationships instead of funnels.

The professionals I know who have the easiest time getting clients are not the ones with the best service. They are the ones who have been showing up consistently for 12 to 24 months before they ever made an offer.

By the time they launched, they had an audience that already trusted them. The sales were almost incidental.

Start building now. Not when your business is ready. Not when the product is done. Now, while the stakes are low and the learning is cheap.

**CTA:** Post something useful in your field today, no pitch attached.

---

**Post 4**
**Hook:** One post won't change your business. 500 posts might.

**Body:**
The math of consistent content is not complicated, but most people quit before it works.

In month one, your posts get 12 views. Month three, maybe 80. Month six, a few hundred. Then something shifts. One post hits. Then two. Then your baseline jumps and it doesn't come back down.

That's how compounding works. The returns are not linear. The first 80% of the results come in the last 20% of the time.

The problem is that most people look at the first six months and conclude it's not working. They stop. Then they restart three months later. Then they stop again. They never make it to the compounding phase.

Consistency is not about motivation. It's about having a system. Schedule it. Make the posts short if you have to. Use templates. Lower the standard for "good enough to post."

The algorithm doesn't care how perfect your post is. It cares how often you show up.

**CTA:** Pick a posting frequency you can maintain for six months, not the most impressive one you can imagine.

---

**Post 5**
**Hook:** I spent 90 days posting about one skill. Here's what happened to my client pipeline.

**Body:**
Six months ago I picked one topic and committed to writing about it three times a week. No broad "thought leadership." Just one specific skill, documented in real time.

Month one: 40 followers gained, zero inbound inquiries. I almost quit.

Month two: One person DM'd me saying a post I wrote solved a problem they'd been stuck on for weeks. No sale, but it confirmed I was helping someone.

Month three: A potential client reached out saying they'd been following my posts for two months and wanted to hire me specifically because of a breakdown I shared about a project failure.

The lesson is not that I was special or that my content was great. The lesson is that documenting what you actually know, consistently, in public, is one of the most underrated business development strategies available.

You don't need a viral post. You need a track record. Those are very different things and only one of them is in your control.

**CTA:** Pick one skill. Document one piece of it this week. Start the track record.
