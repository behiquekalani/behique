## POST 6: Local AI is a Business Decision, Not a Tech Decision (Question-Answer)

Why would a small business run AI models on their own hardware instead of using cloud APIs?

Three reasons that have nothing to do with being a tech enthusiast:

**Privacy.** Your customer data, financial records, internal communications, and strategic documents never leave your building. No third-party server. No terms of service that let a company train on your data. No breach notification from a provider you trusted.

**Cost.** After initial setup, the ongoing cost is electricity. Not $20/user/month. Not usage-based pricing that spikes when you actually use the tool. Electricity. For a 5-person team, that's the difference between $1,200/year in subscriptions and maybe $200/year in power costs.

**Reliability.** No API outages. No rate limits during your busiest hours. No "we're experiencing high demand" messages when you need the tool most. It runs on your machine. It's available when you need it.

The hardware requirements are lower than most people think. A modern laptop with 16GB RAM can run capable models. A desktop with a decent GPU runs them fast.

I set up local AI systems for small businesses. The hardware you already own is probably sufficient. The setup takes one afternoon. The documentation means your team can use it without me.

If you're curious whether your business could benefit from local AI, I'll tell you honestly. Sometimes cloud is the right answer. But usually, it's not.

## POST 7: What I Learned Building 25 Products in 7 Days (Story-Lesson-CTA)

In March 2026, I built 25 digital products in one week. Here are the 5 things I didn't expect to learn:

**1. Quality control is the human job.** AI can produce content at incredible speed. But it can also produce confident garbage at incredible speed. My most important role was not creating. It was catching what didn't meet the standard.

**2. Systems beat talent.** I'm not the best writer, designer, or marketer. But I built a system where AI handles the 80% that's execution, and I focus on the 20% that's judgment. The output is better than what I could produce alone and faster than what a team could produce together.

**3. Product-market fit doesn't come from building.** I built 25 products. I have no idea which ones people will actually buy. Building is the easy part. Selling is the real skill, and no amount of AI fixes that.

**4. Documentation is the moat.** Anyone can ask AI to write a guide. The difference is having documented systems, real experience, and genuine expertise behind the product. The AI wrote the words. The architecture came from years of learning.

**5. The hardest part is putting it out there.** I spent three weeks building and zero days selling. My own AI system flagged this pattern: "infrastructure building as a proxy for not starting the scary thing." The scary thing is selling.

If you're a business owner watching AI move fast and wondering how to actually use it, I get it. I'm building with this stuff every day. Happy to share what's working and what's not.
