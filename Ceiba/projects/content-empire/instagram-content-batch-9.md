## POST 1 . What 90 Days of Building in Public Actually Looks Like (Carousel)
**Account:** @kalaniandrez
**Format:** Carousel (5 slides)

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
Day 1: 0 followers. 0 products. 0 sales.

Day 90 target.

Here is what actually happened.

**Slide 2 . Week 1-4. The invisible work:**
Weeks 1-4. The build phase.

22 products built. Zero posted.

Each one felt like it needed more work before going live. Each one stayed in the folder. The audience stayed at zero.

The invisible work is real. It is also not the same thing as progress.

**Slide 3 . Week 5-8. The perfectionism trap:**
Weeks 5-8. The content wave.

90 posts drafted. Still nothing live.

The pattern: build a product, plan the content for it, find a reason to refine both.

Perfectionism does not look like obsessing over fonts. It looks like always having one more thing to do before publishing.

**Slide 4 . Week 9-12. The distribution gap:**
Weeks 9-12. Distribution phase.

First posts live. Products live.

The gap between having something ready and actually putting it in front of people is wider than most builders expect. Distribution is its own skill. It does not come automatically from having a good product.

**Slide 5 . The lesson:**
Content ready does not mean distributed.

Building is only half the job. The second half is getting it in front of people, repeatedly, without waiting until it is perfect.

The build phase is comfortable. Distribution requires a different tolerance for imperfection.

## POST 3 . ADHD and Building: The Real Version (Carousel)
**Account:** @kalaniandrez
**Format:** Carousel (4 slides)

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
ADHD content on here is either inspiration or an excuse.

This is neither.

**Slide 2 . The actual mechanic:**
Hyperfocus is real.

So is the crash after it.

If you build your schedule around hyperfocus without planning for the crash, the crash takes out your whole week. Plan for both. The productive sprint and the recovery window. They are both part of the system, not one productive and one wasted.

**Slide 3 . The research loop:**
ADHD brains optimize for researching, not finishing.

Research feels like progress. It is often a delay.

The fix is a decision rule. Four hours of research means you start building now. Whatever you know after four hours is enough to begin. The rest comes from building, not from more research.

**Slide 4 . The rule that works:**
One output per day.

Not a to-do list. Not a focus session. An output.

Something that exists after you are done. A post. A file. A page. A sent email. Something with a timestamp.

To-do lists are inputs. Outputs are what move things forward.

## POST 4 . Why I Stopped Using Notion (Long-form Text Post)
**Account:** @kalaniandrez
**Format:** Long-form text post

---

Three years of Notion.

Beautiful databases. Linked views. Custom properties. Nested pages inside nested pages. The kind of system that takes a Sunday afternoon to build and feels like real work when you step back and look at it.

Zero output from any of it.

The problem was not Notion. Notion is a fine tool. The problem was that a capture system is not an execution system. I had built something that was very good at storing ideas and very bad at turning them into finished things.

The database for content ideas had 200 rows. I posted maybe four things that year.

The switch: markdown files, a Telegram bot for quick capture, and three daily questions. What am I building today. What is the output. Did it ship.

Less organizing. More building.

What actually changed was not the tool. It was the distinction between where ideas live and where work happens. I stopped treating Notion like a workspace and started treating it like an archive. The workspace became whatever I had open right now.

## POST 5 . The One-Person Business Framework I Actually Use (Carousel)
**Account:** @behikeai
**Format:** Carousel (5 slides)

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
Not Dan Koe's OPB.

My version. Built for someone starting from zero.

**Slide 2 . The reverse value ladder:**
Most frameworks say build the audience first, then the product.

The reverse value ladder does it the other way.

Start with the cheapest product. Price it low enough that buying it requires almost no trust. Sell to the people who find you. The audience builds from product buyers, not from followers waiting for a reason to trust you.

Buyers convert to followers more reliably than followers convert to buyers.

**Slide 3 . Ship rate over quality rate:**
The second product is better than the first because you shipped the first.

Not because you planned longer. Not because you researched more. Because you went through the process once and learned what you did not know before.

Quality improves through iteration. Iteration requires shipping.

**Slide 4 . The 30-day test:**
Any new project gets 30 days.

At the end of 30 days, there is a single question: has this moved?

Moved means a concrete output exists. A product page. A first post. A draft. Something. If nothing has moved, the project gets cut or placed in a parking lot with no active attention.

Most ideas that feel big never move. The ones that survive 30 days are the ones worth building.
