# Twitter/X Batch 147 — How to Price Your Second Product (The Upgrade Logic)

---

**Tweet 1 (punchy single)**
Your first product teaches people what you do.
Your second product teaches people what's possible.

Price them differently because they're solving different problems.

---

**Tweet 2 (punchy single)**
The mistake: pricing product 2 at product 1 plus $20.

The logic: product 2 should price from the outcome it delivers, not from where product 1 landed.

---

**Tweet 3 — 3-tweet thread**

Tweet 1/3:
Your second product isn't a sequel. It's a signal.

It signals that your first product worked. That the buyer got a result. That the next step exists.

Pricing it wrong destroys that signal.

Tweet 2/3:
Product 1 sells on curiosity: "What is this person about? Can they actually help me?"
Product 2 sells on proof: "I've already seen what they can do. Now I want more."

The buyer's mental model is completely different. Your price needs to reflect that.

Tweet 3/3:
If product 1 is $27, product 2 doesn't have to be $47.
It could be $197. Or $497.
Because the buyer who buys product 2 already trusts you.
You're not convincing. You're upgrading.
Trust has a price. Charge for it.

---

**Tweet 4 (punchy single)**
Most creators underprice their second product because they're still thinking like a first-time seller.

Your second buyer isn't a first-time buyer. Stop treating them like one.

---

**Tweet 5 (punchy single)**
The upgrade path:
Product 1 = awareness + trust
Product 2 = depth + transformation
Product 3 = access + relationship

Each tier has a different price ceiling. Most people never build past tier 1.

---

**Tweet 6 — 8-tweet thread**

Tweet 1/8:
The upgrade logic is one of the most misunderstood concepts in digital products.

Most creators price their second product wrong. Not because they don't care, but because they're using the wrong model.

Here's the framework:

Tweet 2/8:
First, understand the buyer journey.

Someone who buys product 2 is not the same person who bought product 1. They've changed. They've consumed your work. They've had a result, or at least the beginning of one.

That's a fundamentally different person. Price for who they've become.

Tweet 3/8:
The wrong model: "My first product is $37, so my second should be $57 or $67."

This anchors your second product to the price of the first. It's a creativity failure. It assumes the buyer's willingness to pay grows in small linear steps.

It doesn't. Trust grows exponentially.

Tweet 4/8:
The right model: "What outcome does product 2 create? What is that outcome worth to the buyer in real terms? What would they pay to achieve it elsewhere?"

Price from the outcome, not from the product before it.

If product 2 helps someone build a complete content system for their business, the value isn't $77. It might be $297.

Tweet 5/8:
The psychological reality of the second purchase:

The first buy was a bet. "Let me see if this person is who they say they are."

The second buy is a decision. "I know what they can do. I want the next thing they built."

Decisions carry different budgets than bets.

Tweet 6/8:
Practical rule: your second product should be at minimum 3-5x the price of your first.

Why? Because the trust multiplier is real. And because underpricing product 2 tells your buyer that the depth of your work is cheap.

Price communicates value before they even open it.

Tweet 7/8:
One more thing: the gap between products 1 and 2 is where most people lose buyers.

There should be a clear logical reason why product 2 exists. It should answer the natural question product 1 leaves behind.

Product 1: how to start something.
Product 2: how to scale it.
Product 3: how to make it a real business.

Every product answers the next question.

Tweet 8/8:
Summary of the upgrade logic:

- Price from outcome, not from product 1's price
- The second buyer has more trust, charge accordingly
- Minimum 3-5x jump between product 1 and 2
- Product 2 should answer the question product 1 leaves open
- Underpricing signals shallow depth

Your second product is your most important one. Build and price it like it.

**Tweet 9 — 3-tweet thread**

Tweet 1/3:
"But what if no one buys product 2 at a higher price?"

Then either the offer is unclear or product 1 didn't deliver enough of a result.

Neither problem is solved by lowering the price.

Tweet 2/3:
A weak product 1 result means buyers don't trust the upgrade.
Lowering the price of product 2 doesn't fix that.

Improving the result from product 1 does.

The answer is almost never "charge less." The answer is usually "deliver more."

Tweet 3/3:
If buyers aren't upgrading:
1. Ask them why.
2. Look at what result they got from product 1.
3. Sharpen the bridge between 1 and 2.
4. Then adjust price if needed.

Price is almost never the real blocker. Clarity and trust usually are.
