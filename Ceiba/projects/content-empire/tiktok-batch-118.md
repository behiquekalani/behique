## Topic: The 3 Types of Digital Products and Which to Build First

## TikTok 1: There Are Only 3 Types of Digital Products

**Hook (0-2s):**
"Every digital product ever made fits into one of three categories."

**Script:**
Information. Tools. Community.

That's it.

Information products teach something. Ebooks, courses, guides, templates. You package what you know and sell access to it.

Tool products do something. Software, apps, automations, scripts. You sell capability, not knowledge.

Community products connect people. Memberships, Discord servers, masterminds. You sell belonging and accountability.

Here's why this matters.

Each type requires a different skill set to build and a different relationship with your customer to sustain.

Before you decide what to build, you need to know which category fits where you actually are right now. Not where you want to be. Where you are.

Because building the wrong type first is one of the most common ways early builders waste months of work.

## TikTok 2: Why Information Products Win First

**Hook (0-2s):**
"Your first digital product should not be software. Here's why."

**Script:**
Tools require code or no-code skills and ongoing maintenance. When something breaks, your customers are stuck and you're the one who has to fix it at 2am.

Communities require an existing audience with social gravity. If you're starting from zero, a membership is just an empty room people pay to sit in.

Information products require none of that.

You need one thing: knowledge that someone else doesn't have but wants.

You probably already have it. The framework you use to solve a specific problem. The process you figured out that took you a year. The shortcut that seems obvious to you but isn't to most people.

That's the product.

Package it in a PDF, a short video series, or a simple course. Sell it for $27 to $97 to start.

Low risk. Fast to build. Direct feedback from the market.

Start with information. Add tools and community once you have customers.

## TikTok 3: When to Build a Tool Product

**Hook (0-2s):**
"Tool products make money while you sleep. But only if you build them at the right time."

**Script:**
The mistake is building a tool before you understand the problem.

Tools solve very specific problems. The more specific, the better they sell.

Before you build any tool, you need to have seen the problem enough times to know the exact input and output your customer needs.

If you've helped ten people manually do the same process, you have enough data to automate it.

That manual process, the thing you've done by hand over and over, that's your first tool product.

You know every edge case. You know where people get confused. You know what a good result looks like.

Build the tool that does what you've been doing manually. Charge less than your hourly rate for access to it.

This is why tools built by practitioners always beat tools built by developers who never did the work themselves.

## TikTok 4: Community Products Are a Long Game

**Hook (0-2s):**
"Charging for a community is one of the hardest things to do well. Most people do it wrong."

**Script:**
The problem with most paid communities is that the creator is the only reason anyone is there.

When you show up, everyone's excited. When you don't, the community is silent.

That's not a community. That's a fan club with a monthly fee.

Real communities have peer-to-peer value. Members talk to each other, not just to you. They share wins. They help each other troubleshoot. They hold each other accountable.

You build that by being very specific about who the community is for and what problem it's organized around.

Not "people interested in business." That's too vague.

"Freelancers closing their first $5,000 month" is specific enough that members immediately recognize each other as peers.

Build community after you have a hundred customers from your information products. They are your first members. They already trust you.

## TikTok 5: The Progression That Actually Works

**Hook (0-2s):**
"Information first. Tools second. Community third. In that exact order."

**Script:**
Here's why the order matters.

Information products teach you how to communicate value. You learn what words your market responds to. You learn what problems they'll pay to solve.

That knowledge makes your tools better. You know exactly what to automate because you've already been teaching people how to do it manually.

And your community is better because you built it around buyers of your information and tools. These are people who already got results. They have things to share.

Skip the order and you build on sand.

A community of people who've never gotten results has nothing to talk about.

A tool built without understanding the problem has no buyers.

An information product comes first because it costs the least to build and teaches you the most.

Start simple. Build in order. The whole thing compounds.
