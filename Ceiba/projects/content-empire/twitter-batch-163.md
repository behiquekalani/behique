**Tweet 3 (single)**
Every build decision comes down to three questions: Does the market want this? Can I build it alone or with minimal help? Does it connect to what I already have? Two yeses is enough to start.

---

**Tweet 4 (3-tweet thread)**
The solopreneur's decision framework. Four filters. Apply them in order.

1/3

---

Filter 1: Does someone pay for this right now? Not "would they pay" — does someone already pay, somewhere, for this type of thing? If yes, the market exists. If no, you're doing market creation. That's harder.

Filter 2: Can I deliver the first version in 30 days or less? If the MVP takes longer than 30 days alone, you're building for the wrong stage of your business.

2/3

---

Filter 3: Does this build on something I already have? (Audience, skill, content, systems.) If it requires building everything from scratch, it costs twice as much.

Filter 4: Can I sell this before I build it? A presale is the real validation. Not a survey. Not a waitlist. An actual payment.

Run those four filters. If you pass three out of four, build it.

3/3

---

**Tweet 5 (single)**
The failure mode of smart solopreneurs: they analyze so thoroughly that they optimize themselves out of starting. At some point, the decision to start IS the data.

---

**Tweet 6 (6-tweet thread)**
I see solopreneurs make the same build mistake repeatedly. They pick what to build next based on excitement, not evidence. Here's what to do instead.

1/6

---

Excitement is a bad signal on its own. It tells you what you're interested in. It doesn't tell you what people will pay for. And it spikes highest for ideas that haven't been tested yet, which is the worst time to act on it.

2/6

---

The better signal: friction. What do people in your target market complain about most? What do they ask about most? What are they paying to fix badly right now?

Complaints are product roadmaps. You just have to read them.

3/6

---

Once you have a friction signal, apply the 30-day test. Can you produce a first version, sell it, and deliver it within 30 days?

If no: break it into smaller versions. Ship the smallest piece that proves the concept. Then build on that.

4/6

---

The portfolio problem: solopreneurs often have 6 ideas running at the same time and wonder why nothing gains traction.

One thing at a time. Pick the idea with the clearest friction signal, the fastest build time, and the most direct path to a sale. That's your next build.

5/6

---

Summary:
1. Look for friction, not excitement
2. Validate with presales, not surveys
3. Build the smallest deliverable version first
4. One thing at a time
5. Ship, then improve

The worst build is the one you never finished. The best build is the one you shipped ugly and iterated on.

6/6

**Tweet 9 (3-tweet thread)**
The decision I wish someone had told me to stop making: building things I would use instead of things my audience would buy.

1/3

---

Builders build for themselves by default. It's the path of least resistance. You know the problem intimately. You can imagine the solution clearly. You skip the research phase entirely.

The product is technically correct and commercially useless.

2/3

---

The reframe: build for the person you were 12 months ago. Not you today, with all your current knowledge. You a year ago, with the specific gap you had.

That person exists in your audience right now. Build for them.

3/3

---

**Tweet 10 (single)**
The question isn't "what do I want to build?" The question is "what does my audience already want to buy, that I'm positioned to build?" Those are not the same question. Most solopreneurs answer the first one and wonder why it doesn't sell.
