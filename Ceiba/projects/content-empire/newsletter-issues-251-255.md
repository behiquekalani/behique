## Issue 251: The Product Bundle Strategy That Doubles Average Order Value

**Subject line:** Why two products together outsell two products separately

**Preview text:** The psychology behind bundling is simpler than you think. And most creators miss it entirely.

---

Most people think about pricing the wrong way.

They look at their product, pick a number that feels right, and hope enough people say yes. When the conversion rate disappoints, the instinct is to lower the price.

The problem is rarely the price. It's the structure of the offer.

A single product forces a binary decision: buy or don't buy. A bundle changes that question entirely. Instead of asking "is this worth it?", the customer starts asking "which version is right for me?" That shift in framing is responsible for why bundles consistently convert better and generate higher average order values.

**The lesson this week is simple: the bundle is a positioning decision, not a pricing decision.**

Here's how to think about it practically. Your products probably solve different stages of the same problem. A guide on finding the right niche, a system for creating content in that niche, and a template for turning that content into offers, those are three separate products. But together, they solve one problem completely. When you package them that way and name the outcome rather than the contents, you're not just adding products together. You're building a system the customer can see themselves using.

The formula that works: price the bundle at 65-70% of the combined individual prices. The customer feels like they're getting more for less. You generate more revenue per transaction. Both outcomes are real.

One practical constraint worth noting: don't build the bundle and then figure out the narrative. Build the narrative first. The story of why these products belong together is what makes the bundle feel like a system instead of a pile of PDFs.

**Tool of the week:** Gumroad's bundle feature lets you package products in under five minutes with no technical setup required. If you're on Gumroad and haven't used it, this is the week to test it.

**Closing thought:** The creator who sells three products as a coherent system is not competing with the creator who sells one product. They're playing a different game. The bundle doesn't just increase revenue. It signals that you understand your customer's full journey, not just one step of it.

## Issue 252: How to Write a Welcome Email That Sells Without Selling

**Subject line:** The most-opened email you'll ever send (and how to use it)

**Preview text:** Your welcome email has a 60% open rate. Here's what to do with that window.

---

There's one email in your sequence that almost everyone reads.

Your welcome email. The one sent immediately after someone subscribes.

Open rates for welcome emails average between 50% and 80%. That's two to four times the open rate of any other email you'll send. If you're using that window to say "thanks for subscribing, here's your free guide," you're leaving the most valuable real estate in your business mostly empty.

**The lesson: your welcome email is not a receipt. It's your first impression at scale.**

Here's the structure that works.

The first paragraph answers one question: are you in the right place? Be specific. "If you're a solopreneur trying to build a product business without a team or a big audience, this is for you." That specificity filters in the right people and filters out the wrong ones. Both outcomes are good.

The second paragraph sets expectations. Not vague promises, but a concrete description of what someone can expect from being on this list. Frequency, topics, format. Specific enough to be a real commitment.

The third paragraph tells a short story. One before-and-after that's relevant to the problem your product solves. This is not your full origin story. It's the compressed version that establishes you've been where they are and found a way through.

The fourth paragraph is the only CTA: a low-commitment invitation. "Reply with your biggest challenge right now." Not a sale. Not a link to your product page. A question.

That question does three things. It gets the email out of promotions folders because a reply signals to email clients that this is a real conversation. It gives you data on what your audience actually struggles with. And it starts a dialogue that positions your eventual offer as the answer to a question they literally told you they had.

**Tool of the week:** ConvertKit's automations make it easy to trigger a welcome sequence the moment someone joins any specific list segment. If you have multiple lead magnets, make sure each one triggers its own relevant welcome, not a generic one.

**Closing thought:** A great welcome email is written once and works forever. Every new subscriber gets the same quality first impression regardless of when they join. That's what makes it worth spending real time on. Treat it like the most important piece of copy in your business. For most solo operators, it is.

## Issue 253: The Minimum Viable Content System for People With Full-Time Jobs

**Subject line:** You don't need more time to create content. You need fewer decisions.

**Preview text:** The real content bottleneck is not how many hours you have. It's how many decisions you make before you write.

---

The most common thing I hear from people who want to build a content presence is: "I just don't have the time."

That's almost never the actual problem.

The time exists. It's the system that doesn't.

Every time someone sits down to post without a system, they face the same ten micro-decisions. What should I write about? Which platform is it for? What format? Is this idea good enough? How long should it be? What's the CTA? Is this relevant to my audience? Should I post today or wait until I have something better?

Those ten decisions don't just take time. They drain the cognitive energy that was supposed to go into the actual writing. By the time the decision process is over, the energy for creation is gone.

**The lesson: the content bottleneck is cognitive overhead, not hours.**

The minimum viable content system eliminates the overhead with three decisions made once.

**Decision one: one core topic.** Not "business and productivity and mindset and entrepreneurship." One specific intersection of idea and audience. The narrower the topic, the easier every individual post becomes. You're not deciding what to write about. You're exploring depth on a topic that's already defined.

**Decision two: one primary platform.** The one where your specific audience already spends time and where the format matches your natural output. If you write well and think in long-form, LinkedIn or a newsletter. If you're visual and process-oriented, Instagram. If you're verbally strong and quick-thinking, short-form video. One platform first. Expand later, with a repurposing system, not fresh creation.

**Decision three: one fixed weekly time block.** Not "when I have time." Tuesday at 7am, every week. That block is a meeting with yourself that you don't cancel. Ninety minutes per week produces one anchor piece of content and three to four short-form derivatives. That's enough for a complete digital presence if distributed intelligently.

The system doesn't need to be complex. It needs to survive your busiest week. If it only works when things are calm, it's not a system. It's a good intention.

**Tool of the week:** Notion or a simple Google Doc as your content bank. Every idea that comes up during the week goes in one place. When you sit down for your time block, you're not brainstorming. You're executing on ideas that already exist.

**Closing thought:** The creators who show up consistently are not the ones with more time. They're the ones who made decisions once and stopped re-making them every week. The system is not a creativity constraint. It's the thing that makes creativity possible under realistic conditions.

## Issue 254: Building Your Audience on LinkedIn (The Underdog Platform)

**Subject line:** LinkedIn in 2026 is what Instagram was in 2018. Here's why that matters.

**Preview text:** There's a reason the creators who understand LinkedIn are building faster than most. The window is still open.

---

Most creators treat LinkedIn as a resume.

That's the reason they don't grow on it.

LinkedIn in 2026 is a content platform with a specific audience characteristic that no other platform has: the people using it are there with professional intent. They're not doom-scrolling. They're not looking for entertainment. They're looking for ideas, perspectives, and solutions to real problems in their work.

That changes what content works, and more importantly, who sees it.

**The lesson: LinkedIn's professional context is a feature, not a limitation.**

Here's what the platform actually rewards.

Original perspective on a specific problem. Not generic advice. Not a summary of something someone else said. A clear point of view that challenges a common assumption in your niche. "Most people think X. Here's why Y is closer to the truth, based on what I actually observed." That kind of post generates the kind of comments that expand reach, because comments from people with professional context carry weight in the algorithm.

Real process content. Not the outcome. The process. "Here's exactly what I did to go from zero to my first paid client as a digital solopreneur, including the three things that didn't work." Specific. Documented. Repeatable for the reader.

The first line is the entire game on LinkedIn. Those first 100 characters before "see more" determine whether the post gets read at all. Write the first line as a standalone claim that creates enough curiosity or tension to justify opening the rest. Not "I wanted to share something I've been thinking about." The claim, directly.

The commenting strategy is under-used. A substantial comment, four or more sentences with a real perspective, on a post from someone with a larger audience in your niche, shows your thinking to their audience. That's distribution without creating new content. Twenty minutes of meaningful commenting per week drives more discoverability in the early stages than double the posting.

**Tool of the week:** Shield Analytics for LinkedIn gives you post-level data on reach, engagement, and profile views. The free version is enough for most solo creators to identify what's working without guessing.

**Closing thought:** The window for becoming a recognized voice in a LinkedIn niche is still open. There are far fewer consistent creators here than on Instagram or TikTok, and the audience's willingness to engage with depth is significantly higher. If you have a perspective worth sharing with a professional audience, this is the platform where that perspective finds its highest-leverage home.

## Issue 255: MILESTONE — Issue 255: What the Behike Report Has Become, and What Comes Next

**Subject line:** Issue 255. Here's what I didn't expect when I started this.

**Preview text:** 255 issues. What the consistency actually built, and where it's going.

---

When I sent the first issue of the Behike Report, I wasn't sure anyone would read it.

Not because the content wasn't there. But because starting anything from zero requires a specific kind of conviction that doesn't come from evidence. It comes from commitment to the direction, even without proof that the direction is right.

255 issues later, the evidence exists. And it's not what I expected.

**What I expected:** the newsletter to grow steadily in subscribers.

**What actually happened:** the newsletter became a thinking partner. Every issue forced a level of clarity on a topic that I couldn't achieve any other way. Writing for a reader who would read it this week, not someday, creates accountability to thought that no other format creates. The newsletter didn't just communicate ideas. It built them.

The subscriber growth came. But the more valuable outcome was the compounding clarity that 255 consistent writing sessions produced. The understanding of what this audience actually struggles with, what language resonates, what ideas need more development and which ones are already obvious. That knowledge doesn't exist without the practice that creates it.

**What changed between issue 1 and issue 255:**

The voice got more specific. Early issues tried to cover too much. Recent ones go deep on one idea. That specificity is what generates the replies that say "this one felt personal."

The audience got more defined. The Behike Report reader is a solopreneur, a freelancer, or someone transitioning from employment to building something of their own. The content serves that specific person now in a way it couldn't in the early issues.

The products behind the newsletter became real. The content validated what the audience needed. The products answered that need. That loop, content to trust to product to feedback to better content, is the actual business model.

**What comes next:**

More depth on fewer topics. The newsletter has covered a wide surface area. The next chapter goes vertical.

Better integration between the newsletter and the product ecosystem. The issues should point naturally toward resources that extend the idea. Not aggressively. But as a service to the reader who wants to go further.

And more honesty about what's not working. The issues that generated the most replies weren't the ones with the cleanest frameworks. They were the ones that admitted complexity and uncertainty. More of that.

**Closing thought:** 255 issues is not the milestone. The milestone was issue two. That was the first time I could have stopped and didn't. Every issue after that was a continuation of the same decision. If you're building something in public, whatever the number, what you're building is not an archive. It's evidence of who you decided to be, one consistent action at a time. Thank you for reading. Issue 256 is already written.
