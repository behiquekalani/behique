## Script 84.1 — One Platform vs. Everywhere

**Hook (0-3s):**
Being everywhere online is a strategy for people who haven't figured out where they work.

**Body (3-50s):**
The multi-platform creator looks impressive from the outside. Active on Instagram, TikTok, Twitter, YouTube, Pinterest, LinkedIn. But look closely at the numbers on any one platform and you'll usually find they're mediocre on all of them.

[ON-SCREEN: Multi-platform = mediocre everywhere]

This happens because growth on any platform requires a feedback loop. You post. You see what works. You double down. You iterate. That loop takes time and attention to close. If you're posting on five platforms simultaneously, you're running five slow feedback loops instead of one fast one.

[ON-SCREEN: 5 slow loops vs. 1 fast loop]

The creator who posts on one platform for six months with full attention will understand that platform better than someone who has been on it casually for two years.

[ON-SCREEN: 6 months focused > 2 years casual]

The argument for being everywhere is exposure. More platforms, more reach. That's true in theory. In practice, being weak on five platforms means your content never reaches the threshold where algorithms actually distribute it. Most platforms have a minimum viable engagement rate below which your content is functionally invisible.

[ON-SCREEN: Below engagement threshold = invisible to algorithm]

One platform, done seriously, crosses that threshold. Then you have reach worth redistributing.

[ON-SCREEN: Hit threshold first. Expand after.]

## Script 84.2 — Why Depth Beats Breadth for Creators

**Hook (0-3s):**
The creators who build durable audiences go deep on one thing for longer than seems necessary.

**Body (3-50s):**
Depth is uncomfortable for two reasons. It feels like you're limiting yourself. And it takes longer to see results than breadth does.

[ON-SCREEN: Depth feels limiting and slow. It's neither.]

But here's what depth actually does. It makes you the obvious answer to a specific question. When someone has a very specific problem and they find a creator who covers exactly that problem in depth, the trust is immediate and the follow is certain.

[ON-SCREEN: Depth = you become the obvious answer]

Breadth, on the other hand, makes you one of many. If you post about productivity, health, finance, and mindset, you're competing against every general self-improvement account that exists. There's no reason to follow you specifically.

[ON-SCREEN: Breadth = you compete with everyone / Depth = you own a category]

The path to depth that works: pick one intersection of problems you understand and audiences you want to serve. Go deeper than anyone expects. Stay there until people start tagging you in conversations you didn't start.

[ON-SCREEN: Stay until people tag you in conversations you didn't start]

That's the signal that you've built depth worth expanding from. Not views. Not follower count. The organic signal that you've become the reference point.

[ON-SCREEN: Depth signal = you become the reference point]

## Script 84.3 — The Algorithm Advantage of Focus

**Hook (0-3s):**
The algorithm wants to know who you are. Posting about everything confuses it. That confusion is costing you reach.

**Body (3-50s):**
Recommendation algorithms work by building a model of your content and matching it to users who have engaged with similar content.

[ON-SCREEN: Algorithm = content model matched to user patterns]

If your content is consistent, the algorithm builds an accurate model quickly. It knows who to show your content to. Distribution is efficient.

[ON-SCREEN: Consistent content = accurate model = efficient distribution]

If your content is inconsistent, the model is noisy. The algorithm can't reliably predict who will engage. So it defaults to showing your content to a smaller, more uncertain audience.

[ON-SCREEN: Inconsistent content = noisy model = uncertain distribution]

This is why a channel about one specific thing will often outperform a more "creative" channel with wider range, even if the creative channel's content is objectively better on a per-post basis. The algorithm doesn't reward quality in isolation. It rewards predictability combined with quality.

[ON-SCREEN: Quality alone loses to quality plus predictability]

The practical implication: define a tight content category and stay inside it. The constraint isn't a creative limitation. It's a distribution advantage that compounds over every post.

[ON-SCREEN: Tight content category = compounding distribution advantage]

## Script 84.4 — The Multi-Platform Trap

**Hook (0-3s):**
The multi-platform trap feels like ambition. It's actually a form of avoidance.

**Body (3-50s):**
When you're everywhere, you never have to sit with the discomfort of being small on one platform.

[ON-SCREEN: Multi-platform = avoiding the discomfort of starting small]

You can always point to the numbers on one platform to feel like you're making progress, even if none of the platforms are growing. The busyness becomes a substitute for the slower, more uncomfortable work of mastering one.

[ON-SCREEN: Busy on 5 platforms = not growing on any]

The trap also operates financially. Setting up on multiple platforms takes time you don't have. Tools for scheduling, repurposing, and managing multiple accounts cost money. The overhead of multi-platform before you have traction is a tax on a business that hasn't earned revenue yet.

[ON-SCREEN: Multi-platform overhead = expensive for an early-stage creator]

The creators I've watched build real audiences consistently did one thing. They picked the platform that fit how they naturally communicate. Long-form thinkers went to YouTube or newsletters. Short-form natural communicators went to TikTok or Twitter. They stayed until it worked.

[ON-SCREEN: Pick the platform that fits how you communicate. Stay until it works.]

You expand to other platforms from a position of strength, not as a hedge against failure on the first one.

[ON-SCREEN: Expand from strength, not from fear]

## Script 84.5 — When to Expand to More Platforms

**Hook (0-3s):**
Most creators expand to new platforms too early. Here's the threshold that actually makes sense.

**Body (3-50s):**
Platform expansion makes sense when three conditions are true. First, your primary platform is growing consistently without requiring your full attention.

[ON-SCREEN: Condition 1: Primary platform is self-sustaining]

If you still need to figure out what your primary platform wants from you, you're not ready to split your attention. The first platform has to be running on a system, not improvisation.

Second: you have an asset worth redistributing. A newsletter, a product, a body of content that has already proven it works.

[ON-SCREEN: Condition 2: You have a proven asset to redistribute]

You're not going to a second platform to start over. You're going there to take something that already works and put it in front of a different audience. If you don't have that asset, a second platform is just starting over twice.

Third: the new platform is adjacent to your primary one, not a completely different format requiring a completely different skill set.

[ON-SCREEN: Condition 3: The new platform is adjacent, not alien]

TikTok to Instagram Reels is adjacent. Both are short-form video. TikTok to long-form YouTube is a significant format shift that deserves its own learning period. Expand adjacently.

[ON-SCREEN: Expand adjacently first]
