## POST 1: Why Building Solo Is a Competitive Advantage

Solo building is not a compromise. It is a structural advantage most people never realize because they have only ever worked in teams.

No meetings means no alignment tax. You make a decision and it ships the same hour. A team of five spends a week deciding what a team of one does before lunch.

The common objection is that one person cannot do everything. That is true. But it misses the point. One person does not need to do everything. One person only needs to do the things that matter right now, and skip everything else. A team is not built to skip things. It is built to cover them. Those are different operating modes.

The slowest part of any organization is consensus. Remove consensus and the whole machine speeds up. That is the math behind solo building. Not heroics. Just fewer veto points.

## POST 2: ADHD and Engineering School

Engineering school is designed for sustained, linear focus. ADHD does not work that way. That tension is real and nobody talks about it honestly.

The curriculum assumes you move through material at a constant pace. Thirty minutes per topic, then the next one. What actually happens when you have ADHD is bursts. Two hours of complete absorption, then nothing. The schedule does not care.

The adaptation I found was not fighting the burst pattern. It was designing around it. Study when the focus is live, not when the calendar says. Build systems that capture work when the window opens. Stop trying to force the linear model onto a non-linear brain.

Engineering school rewards the person who shows up consistently. ADHD rewards the person who goes deep when they can. The trick is keeping your grade alive long enough for the deep sessions to count.

#adhd #engineering #studentlife

## POST 3: The Math Behind Digital Products vs. Consulting

An hour of consulting generates one hour of revenue. An hour building a digital product can generate revenue for three years.

The math is not close. A $19.99 guide sold two hundred times is $3,998. No calls. No deliverables. No client revisions. The hour spent writing it was fixed. The income from it is not.

Consulting has a real ceiling. You can raise your rate. You can add more hours. At some point both cap out. Digital products do not have that ceiling in the same way. The work compounds. Each new product compounds faster than the last because the audience already exists.

None of this means consulting is wrong. It is how most people fund the early stages. The error is treating consulting as the destination instead of the runway.

#digitalproducts #solopreneurship #onlinebusiness

## POST 4: How to Price Your First Digital Product

Most first-time creators underprice by half. The instinct is to go low to reduce resistance. The result is a product that signals low value before anyone reads a word.

Price anchoring works like this: if similar products exist at $49, a $9 version reads as low quality, not as a deal. The buyer's first question is not "is this affordable?" It is "why is this cheaper?" That question is not a good start.

For a first product, pick a price you would pay without hesitation if someone else built it. Then add twenty percent. If the work is genuinely useful, that number will not scare off the right buyer. It will filter out the wrong one.

The right buyer is not looking for the cheapest option. The right buyer is looking for the specific thing your product solves.

#pricing #digitalproducts #productlaunch

## POST 5: The Real Reason Most Side Projects Fail

It is not execution. Most people who start side projects are capable of executing. The reason they stall is specificity. The idea is too wide to build.

"An app that helps people be more productive" is not a product. It is a category. A category has no user. A product with no user has no feedback loop. No feedback loop means the builder is guessing indefinitely.

The projects that survive are the ones where the builder can name one specific person with one specific problem. Everything else is a narrowing exercise you are supposed to do before you build, not while building.

The fix is not discipline. The fix is scope reduction until the target is so specific it almost feels too narrow. It never is. The narrow version always has a clearer path.

#sideprojects #productbuilding #entrepreneurship

## POST 6: Building with Claude Code -- What Changed in My Workflow

The biggest change was not speed. It was the elimination of context switching.

Before: open file, understand the current state, figure out what to change, make the change, check the whole file again. For every edit. The mental overhead was the actual time cost.

With Claude Code embedded in the workflow, the context lives in the conversation. I describe the change once. The tool reads the file, makes the edit, and explains what changed. The cognitive load per edit dropped by more than half.

The second change was less obvious. Having a capable tool in the loop made me more willing to start things. The activation energy for a new file or a new feature went down because I knew the first draft would not be painful. That changed how many ideas actually made it to code.

It is not magic. It is a workflow upgrade. That is enough.

## POST 7: Why I Started Selling in Spanish

Most digital product creators sell in English by default. The assumption is that the biggest market speaks English. For most niches, that is true. It is also already saturated.

The Spanish-speaking market for AI and automation content is undersupplied. The buyers are there. A growing middle class in Latin America, a diaspora in the US, a tech-forward generation in Spain. The tools they want exist. The content explaining those tools in their language does not.

Building in Spanish is not a concession. It is a positioning decision. The English market has a thousand competitors for every niche. The Spanish market has twenty.

I am from Puerto Rico. This is not a strategy I adopted. It is the audience I actually belong to. Building for them first is not a limitation. It is an advantage with cultural credibility attached.

## POST 8: n8n vs. Writing Code from Scratch

The honest answer to when to use n8n versus writing it yourself: n8n when the logic is simple and the connections are the hard part, code when the logic is the hard part and the connections are secondary.

n8n is a connection layer. It is very good at moving data between services. It is not good at complex conditional logic, custom data transformations, or anything where you need more than a few lines of JavaScript inside a node. When you hit that wall inside n8n, the tool becomes a constraint, not an accelerator.

Writing a Python script that hits three APIs gives you full control of every edge case. The cost is that you own the maintenance.

For most automation tasks in a solo business, n8n wins. For the tasks where you find yourself fighting the tool, stop fighting.

#n8n #automation #solopreneurship

## POST 9: The Difference Between a Portfolio and a Catalog

A portfolio shows what you have done. A catalog sells what you have built. Most creators confuse the two and end up with something that impresses people without converting them.

A portfolio is organized around the creator. "Here are my projects. Here is my background. Here is what I am capable of." The subject is the builder.

A catalog is organized around the buyer. "Here is what this costs. Here is what you get. Here is what problem it solves." The subject is the customer's outcome.

The mistake is building the portfolio page and calling it a store. Good work displayed without a clear purchase path is just a gallery. Galleries do not pay rent.

If you have built things worth selling, build the catalog. The portfolio can live in the about page.

#digitalproducts #onlinestore #solobusiness

## POST 10: Why I Document Everything I Build

Every project I build gets a log. What I decided, why I decided it, what broke, what I changed. Not for anyone else. For the next version of me who has to work on this again in four months.

The compound effect is not obvious until it is. At month two, the logs are a reference. At month twelve, they become pattern recognition. You start to notice your own blind spots. The decisions you keep making and regretting. The problems you keep solving in the wrong order. The logs make the pattern visible.

There is a second effect that surprised me. Building in public, even partially, creates accountability that private logs do not. When someone else might read it, the standard for clarity goes up. You write for the reader and end up writing better notes for yourself.

Documentation is not the work. But done right, it makes every future piece of work faster.

#buildinpublic #documentation #solobusiness
