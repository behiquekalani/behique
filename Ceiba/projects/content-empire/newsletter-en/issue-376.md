# Why Building in Public Works (And Why Most People Do It Wrong)

The psychology behind showing your work isn't about transparency. It's about trust.

Building in public has become a common piece of creator advice. Share your journey. Document your progress. Show the messy middle. But most people who try it miss the actual mechanism, and they end up posting updates that nobody cares about.

The reason building in public works is not that people enjoy voyeurism. It is that watching someone build, struggle, and solve problems in real time creates a specific kind of trust that polished content cannot replicate.

**Why people follow builders, not just experts**

The traditional authority model says: become excellent, then teach from a position of mastery. That model still works. But it has a credibility gap. When you only see someone's finished work, you can admire it without trusting it. You don't know how they got there, what they got wrong, or whether their path is something you could walk.

Building in public collapses that gap. When someone documents the attempt, not just the outcome, they create a record that readers can verify. You watched the process. You saw the wrong turns. You understand the effort behind the result. That record builds a kind of trust that a portfolio page cannot manufacture.

The psychological mechanism is parasocial consistency. When you follow someone's build over weeks or months, you feel you know them. You root for them. When they ship something, you want it to succeed. That relationship is a distribution advantage.

**The mistake most creators make**

They post updates without tension. "Day 12 of building my product. Added 3 new features today." That is not building in public. That is a changelog nobody asked for.

Building in public requires stakes. The audience needs to care about the outcome. That means you have to communicate what you are trying to accomplish, why it matters, what is hard about it, and what happens if it doesn't work. Tension keeps people reading. Updates without tension are noise.

The other mistake is performing struggle instead of sharing it. Readers can tell the difference between someone who is genuinely working through a hard problem and someone who is manufacturing difficulty for content. Authenticity is not a style choice here. It is a requirement.

**How to do it in a way that builds an audience**

Frame every update as a specific problem and how you're approaching it. Not "I worked on marketing today" but "I'm trying to figure out why no one clicks through from my posts to my landing page. Here's what I've ruled out."

Make the reader feel like a collaborator. Ask questions. Share the decisions you're weighing. When you eventually ship, the people who followed the build feel ownership over the outcome. They become your first and most vocal advocates.

Document failures with the same frequency as wins. A creator who only shares the good moments has an audience that doesn't trust the wins. A creator who shares the full picture builds readers who believe in both.

**The long-term compounding effect**

Beyond individual product launches, building in public creates an archive. Someone who finds your work two years from now can read the whole story. That narrative is a marketing asset that runs indefinitely.

The builders who do this well eventually find that they don't need launch campaigns. Their audience has been following the build for months. The launch is just the day the thing becomes available.
