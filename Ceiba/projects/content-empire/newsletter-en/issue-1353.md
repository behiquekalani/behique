# Issue 1353 — Writing That Converts

**Subject:** The single sentence that determines if anyone reads past line one

---

The first sentence of anything you write has one job: make the second sentence impossible to skip. Not to explain what the piece is about. Not to introduce yourself. Not to provide context. Just to create enough tension or curiosity that stopping feels worse than continuing.

Most creator content fails at this. The opener is a restatement of the title, or a soft disclaimer, or an anecdote that takes three paragraphs to get anywhere. By then, half the readers are gone. The scroll culture doesn't wait for your warmup. Either the first line is a hook or the piece doesn't get read.

What makes a line a hook: it opens a loop the reader needs to close. It makes a claim they didn't expect. It names a pain so precisely that the reader feels seen. It starts a story in the middle of the action. Any of these work. The common thread is that something is unresolved, and resolution requires reading the next sentence.

**Tactical takeaway:** Go back to your last five pieces of content. Rewrite the first sentence of each using this test: if this were the only thing a stranger saw, would they want to read the next line? If not, cut everything before the first interesting sentence and start there. Most content's best first line is currently its third or fourth sentence.

The hook is not optional. It's the only thing that matters first.
