# Newsletter EN — Issues 651-655

## Issue 651: Substack vs Self-Hosted for Your Newsletter

**Subject Line:** Platform is infrastructure. Content is the business.

The Substack vs self-hosted debate has been running for years and will keep running. Most people are asking the wrong version of the question. They are asking which platform is better in the abstract. The right question is which platform is right for where you are right now.

Substack's genuine advantages are discovery and simplicity. The Substack Notes feed is a real growth channel. The reader app creates habit-forming consumption for your audience. You can publish your first issue in under an hour with no technical setup. For a creator who has not yet proven they can write consistently, removing that friction is a legitimate competitive advantage. The worst newsletter is the one you never publish.

The tradeoff is real. Substack takes 10% of paid subscription revenue. You do not have access to advanced email automation, segmentation, or drip sequences. The design flexibility is limited. And while your subscriber list is technically exportable, you are still operating on someone else's infrastructure, which means their decisions affect your reach.

Self-hosted platforms (Ghost, Beehiiv, ConvertKit) give you full control at the cost of full responsibility. Custom domain. Full data ownership. Automation and segmentation that can support a sophisticated marketing funnel. Better revenue economics at scale. The tradeoff is that discovery is entirely your problem. No algorithm, no built-in network, no reader app.

The decision tree I would use: under 500 subscribers and still proving the publishing habit, use Substack. Over 1,000 subscribers with consistent output, evaluate migration. Planning to use your newsletter as a serious business channel with automation and segmentation, self-hosted is not optional.

The thing that kills more newsletters than bad platform choice is inconsistency. Publishers who migrate platforms and stop writing for two months while they set up their new stack have made a category error. They optimized infrastructure while neglecting the actual product.

Write first. Optimize platform second. The platform serves the content, not the other way around.

## Issue 652: LinkedIn as a Serious Creator Platform

**Subject Line:** The most underrated distribution channel you're probably ignoring.

LinkedIn has an image problem. People associate it with recruiters, HR announcements, and humility-brag posts about how failure taught them resilience. That association is keeping serious builders off a platform that, in 2025, offers something almost no other major platform does: organic reach to an audience with purchasing power.

The audience profile on LinkedIn is fundamentally different from every other social platform. The people reading your posts have budgets, make decisions, and are specifically looking for professional insight they can use. When your content lands with that audience, it does not just earn a like. It earns a conversation that can turn into a client.

Organic reach on LinkedIn is genuinely different from Meta or Twitter. Posts still spread to people who do not follow you. The algorithm rewards comments over passive engagement, which means a post that generates a real discussion will outperform a post that gets a hundred silent likes.

What works on LinkedIn in 2025 is narrow and clear. Short-form professional insight. One strong thesis per post. Concrete process breakdowns. Personal stories with clear business lessons. The first line of any post is critical because it is all that shows before the "see more" cutoff. If that line is not compelling on its own, the rest does not matter.

What does not work is equally clear. Motivational quotes stripped of context. Posts that exist to signal virtue without producing insight. Anything that reads like corporate communication.

The creators building real revenue on LinkedIn are not treating it as a broadcast channel. They are treating it as trust infrastructure. They write for months. They get known in a specific lane. When a reader needs what they offer, they know exactly who to call.

If your work has any B2B application, you are leaving real money and real relationships on the table by not publishing there consistently. The competition is less intense than it looks from the outside.

## Issue 653: Micro-Courses vs Full Courses

**Subject Line:** The full course is not more legitimate. It's just more expensive to build.

There is a bias in the creator economy toward full courses. Ten modules. Eight hours of video. A comprehensive curriculum. The assumption is that more depth equals more value, and more value justifies a higher price.

This assumption is mostly wrong.

Completion rates on full courses are notoriously low. Industry averages sit somewhere between 10 and 20 percent. A student who paid $497 and completed 15% of your course did not get a transformation. They got a flash drive's worth of content they will feel guilty about forever. That guilt does not produce referrals or testimonials.

Micro-courses solve this by constraining scope. One specific problem. One clear transformation. Under two hours of content. Priced at $27-$97. The student knows exactly what they're buying, finishes it in a sitting, and either got the result or they didn't. That clarity is good for the student and good for your business.

The business case for starting with micro-courses is straightforward. They are faster to build. They are easier to validate before you invest months in production. They sell at a lower price point, which means lower buyer friction and higher conversion rates. They have higher completion rates, which means more results, more testimonials, and more word-of-mouth. And they work as a front-end offer to higher-ticket products and services.

Full courses make sense in specific conditions. The transformation is inherently complex and multi-week. The audience is highly motivated and has paid a premium to prove it. You have a completed micro-course that has already validated the market.

The validation test before building either: can you sell 10 people on the outcome in a 30-minute conversation? If not, the problem is not course length. The problem is that you have not found a problem people actually want solved badly enough to pay for it.

Build the micro-course. Sell it. Let the students tell you what they wanted more of. Build that next.

## Issue 654: Ghostwriting as a Business, Not a Side Hustle

**Subject Line:** You're writing for someone else. You're building for yourself.

Ghostwriting has a positioning problem. People frame it as a fallback option, something you do when you can't get your own work published, or a side hustle to fund your "real" creative work. That framing is both wrong and expensive.

Ghostwriting is one of the cleanest service businesses available in 2025. Low startup costs. No physical product. Genuine and growing demand. Margins that rival software. And a skill set that compounds with every piece you produce.

The demand case is not subtle. Every executive, founder, and expert who wants an audience but lacks writing time is a potential client. LinkedIn ghostwriting, newsletter ghostwriting, Twitter/X ghostwriting, book ghostwriting. These are all real, active markets where skilled writers are consistently underpaid relative to the value they generate.

The rates reflect the value. LinkedIn ghostwriting runs $2,000-$6,000/month per client. Newsletter ghostwriting runs $2,000-$8,000/month. Book ghostwriting starts at $15,000 and scales up. Two or three retainer clients at the midpoint of those ranges puts you well above median professional income as a one-person operation.

The skill most ghostwriters underestimate is voice capture. Writing well is necessary but not sufficient. The work is learning to think and write like someone else, to capture their vocabulary, their rhythm, their beliefs, their blind spots. This requires deep listening, not just good prose.

Starting without a portfolio of named work is not the obstacle it appears to be. Write sample content in the voice of a hypothetical ideal client. Show a prospective client five LinkedIn posts that sound like someone worth following. That is your proof.

The retainer model is essential. Project-based ghostwriting means you are reselling every few months. Retainers create predictable revenue and long-term relationships. Pitch retainers from the first conversation.

The ceiling on a solo ghostwriting business is real. But so is the floor. Build it deliberately and it becomes an asset, not a gig.

## Issue 655: What a Real Annual Review Looks Like

**Subject Line:** Most annual reviews are just goal-setting with extra steps.

The way most people do annual reviews: look at last year's goals, feel some combination of pride and guilt, write new goals, close the notebook. Nothing changes except the date at the top.

A real annual review is a structured audit. It surfaces what actually happened, not what you intended to happen. It identifies the gap between the two. And it gives you enough honest information to make a better decision about where to point next year.

Here is the structure I find useful.

Revenue audit. Total revenue by source. Which offers accounted for what percentage? Which clients generated the most revenue relative to the time they consumed? This is not about celebrating big numbers. It is about understanding which parts of your business actually worked.

Time audit. Where did your hours go? Not where you planned for them to go. Use your calendar, your invoices, and your honest memory. Most builders discover they spent significantly more time on low-leverage work than they realized. That gap is expensive.

Offers audit. Which of your offers are you genuinely proud of? Which ones feel like you're delivering something less than what you promised? The gap between what you sold and what you delivered is your product roadmap for the coming year.

Energy audit. Which clients and projects energized you? Which drained you? This is data. It tells you what to move toward and what to move away from, regardless of revenue.

Belief audit. What did you believe at the start of the year that you now believe was wrong? Which assumptions drove decisions that did not work out? Writing these down explicitly prevents the same expensive errors next year.

After the audit, one question: based on everything above, what is the single most important thing I could change in the next 90 days? Not a list. One thing. The clearest lever.

Goals without a clear audit behind them are wishes. The audit is what makes the goal real.
