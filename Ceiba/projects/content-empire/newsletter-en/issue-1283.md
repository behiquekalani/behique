# Issue 1283: The Digital Product Pricing Trap Most Creators Fall Into

There is a pricing trap that kills more digital product businesses than bad marketing does. It's called underpricing out of fear, then overworking to compensate.

It goes like this. You build a product. You think about who your customer is. Then, instead of thinking about the value delivered, you think about how much you'd personally pay for something like this. You price it at $17. You sell 50 copies. You make $850 and feel like you didn't move the needle. So you make another product. And another. Now you have 12 products, all priced at $17, and a customer base that expects cheap things from you.

Price communicates value before anyone reads a single word of your sales page. A $17 product signals a quick tip. A $97 product signals a system. A $297 product signals a transformation. Same information, different signal. Different customer.

The creator who prices at $97 doesn't just make more money per sale. They attract a different buyer. One who takes the material seriously, implements it, gets results, and becomes a repeat customer. The $17 buyer is hunting for deals. The $97 buyer is investing in outcomes.

There is a simple reframe that changes everything: stop pricing based on how long it took you to make the product. Start pricing based on the value of the outcome the product creates. A two-hour workshop that teaches someone to write a sales page that converts is worth $197, not $19. The outcome justifies the price.

**Tactical takeaway:** If you have a product priced under $30 that solves a real, measurable problem, raise the price to $47 minimum. Rewrite the sales page to describe the outcome, not the features. Watch conversion quality improve even if raw conversion rate dips slightly.

Price is a positioning tool. Use it like one.
