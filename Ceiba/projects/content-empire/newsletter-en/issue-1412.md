# Issue 1412: Price Your Products Like You Mean It

**Subject:** Underpricing your work doesn't make it more accessible. It makes it less credible.

---

There's a widespread belief among new creators that low prices remove buying friction. Price it low, the thinking goes, and more people will buy. You'll build an audience of customers. Then you can raise prices later.

What actually happens: low prices signal low value. They attract buyers who are optimizing for cheapness rather than transformation. Those buyers demand more support, generate more refund requests, and rarely become long-term customers. And when you try to raise prices, they leave.

Your price is a signal about what your product does. A $7 product says "this is a quick tip." A $97 product says "this will change how you work." A $297 product says "this is a system you'll use for years." The price shapes the buyer's expectation before they've opened anything.

The creators who build sustainable product businesses price for the outcome, not the effort. They ask: what is the value of the transformation this delivers? Then they price close to that value. That attracts buyers who are serious about results, which means better outcomes, better testimonials, and a customer base you can actually build a business on.

**Tactical takeaway:** Take your lowest-priced product and ask yourself honestly: if this product delivered on its full promise, what would that be worth to the buyer? Now compare that to your current price. If there's a large gap, you're underpricing. Test a 40% price increase on your next launch and watch what happens to both conversion quality and total revenue. Most creators are surprised.
