## Issue 846: Pinterest Is Not Dead, You're Just Using It Wrong
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** The Pinterest traffic strategy most creators skip
**Preview text:** It works while you sleep

---

Hey [First Name],

Most creators treat Pinterest like a visual bookmark site. That's why they get nothing from it.

Pinterest is a search engine with a 6-month lag. The content you pin today gets discovered six months from now, then again six months after that. It doesn't decay like a tweet or a Reel. It compounds.

Here's the strategy. Every piece of long-form content you create — newsletter, blog post, guide — gets turned into three Pinterest images. Not reposts. Not screenshots. Three distinct angles: one that speaks to the problem, one that frames the solution, and one that sells the transformation. Each image links to your opt-in or sales page.

Pin consistently for 90 days. At least one new pin per day. Use keyword-rich descriptions written the way people actually search: "how to grow a newsletter without paid ads" not "content strategy tips."

The boards you create matter. Don't make one generic board. Make specific boards for each audience pain point. "Email marketing for beginners." "Digital product launch strategies." "Solopreneur time management." Pinterest's algorithm is a matching engine. The more specific your boards, the better it matches.

At 90 days, audit what's getting saves. Double down on those formats and topics. Kill what isn't working.

The return is slow. The return is also permanent.

Key takeaways:
- Pinterest content compounds over months, not days. Start now
- Create three image angles per content piece: problem, solution, transformation

Until next time,

## Issue 847: The Minimal Tool Stack That Actually Works
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** Less tools, more revenue
**Preview text:** You don't need 14 subscriptions

---

Hey [First Name],

Tool stacks are the solopreneur equivalent of buying gym equipment instead of working out. The setup feels productive. The results don't follow.

Here's what a minimal, functional stack looks like for a creator at every stage.

Stage 1 (zero to first sale): One content platform. One email tool. One payment processor. That's Substack or Beehiiv for email and content. Gumroad for selling. Nothing else. No project management app. No CRM. No funnel builder. You don't have enough volume to justify the complexity.

Stage 2 (first sale to $1K/month): Add a scheduling tool for social. That's it. You still don't need a CRM. Your inbox is your CRM. You still don't need a funnel builder. Your email sequences are your funnel.

Stage 3 ($1K to $5K/month): Now you add a lightweight project management tool. You add an analytics layer so you know where buyers come from. Maybe a calendar booking tool if you're doing calls.

The pattern: every new tool requires integration time, mental overhead, and monthly cost. Add tools when the pain of not having them is concrete and measurable. Not when a YouTube video made it look cool.

The creators earning the most with the least complexity have one thing in common: they delayed adding tools until revenue justified them.

Key takeaways:
- Add tools only when the cost of not having them is concrete and measurable
- Stages 1-2 require fewer than 4 tools total

Until next time,
— The Behike Team

## Issue 848: How Small Creators Land Big Brand Deals
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** Brand deals aren't just for big accounts
**Preview text:** Niche beats scale every time

---

Hey [First Name],

Most creators believe brand deals require a large following. That's the wrong mental model.

Brands don't pay for followers. They pay for access to a specific buyer. If your 3,000 subscribers are all solopreneurs with $50K+ in annual revenue, you're worth more to a productivity SaaS company than a generic finance creator with 300,000 followers.

Here's how small creators land deals. First: get specific about your audience. Not "entrepreneurs" but "freelance copywriters transitioning to digital products." Not "people interested in wellness" but "working moms rebuilding their fitness after kids." The more specific your audience description, the more a brand can visualize return on investment.

Second: build a one-page media kit. Audience demographics, average open rates, engagement metrics, and one case study if you have it. You don't need 10,000 subscribers to have a media kit. You need clear numbers.

Third: pitch outbound before brands come to you. Find brands your audience already buys from. Go to their website. Find the marketing or partnerships contact. Send a short, specific email. "My audience of 2,800 freelance designers specifically asked me to recommend project management tools. Here's how a 3-issue feature partnership would work."

That's it. Specificity beats scale.

Key takeaways:
- Describe your audience as a buyer profile, not a size
- Pitch outbound with a specific pitch before waiting for inbound

Until next time,
— The Behike Team

## Issue 849: Premium Positioning Is Not About Price
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** Why raising prices alone doesn't work
**Preview text:** Premium is earned, not declared

---

Hey [First Name],

Every few months, a creator will tell me they raised their prices and nothing changed. Same number of buyers. Actually, fewer.

The mistake is treating premium positioning as a pricing decision. It isn't. It's a perception decision.

Premium positioning starts with scarcity of access. Not fake scarcity. Real scarcity. You don't take every client. You don't offer everything to everyone. You have a specific thing for a specific person. When someone can't get you on demand, you feel valuable.

It continues with the quality of the experience before the purchase. The emails you send. The website you have. The way you describe your work. If every touchpoint looks like something thrown together in a weekend, no price point will feel premium. People pay premium for the feeling of being in good hands before money changes hands.

It shows up in social proof selection. Don't feature every testimonial. Feature the most specific ones. "I went from $2K months to $6K months in 90 days using this system" beats "Really helpful and well written!" every time.

And it requires patience. You cannot declare yourself premium. You earn that positioning through repeated delivery and selective visibility.

Raise your prices. But also earn the right to them.

Key takeaways:
- Premium positioning is a perception decision before it's a pricing decision
- Every pre-purchase touchpoint communicates your tier

Until next time,
— The Behike Team

## Issue 850: The Solopreneur's Monday Routine
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** How productive solopreneurs start the week
**Preview text:** Your Monday sets the tone

---

Hey [First Name],

Most solopreneurs treat Monday like a fresh start. That's the problem. A fresh start means you're figuring out what to do in real time, which costs the first 90 minutes of your best creative energy.

The solopreneurs who compound productivity week over week treat Monday as a review-and-launch day, not a blank slate.

Here's the structure. Sunday night (15 minutes): write three things you want to be true by Friday. Not a task list. Outcomes. "One new product outlined. Three pieces of content scheduled. One pitch sent." Outcomes pull you. Tasks push you.

Monday morning, before you open anything: read what you wrote Sunday. Then spend 30 minutes on your most important output task, uninterrupted. That first 30 minutes is not for email, not for Slack, not for planning. It's for making something.

After that: a weekly review. What did last week produce? What stalled? What needs to move this week? Fifteen minutes max. Don't spiral into analysis. Get directional clarity and move.

The rest of Monday runs normally. But you've already made something and you know where you're headed. That's a different Monday than most solopreneurs have.

Key takeaways:
- Set outcome intentions Sunday night, not Monday morning
- Make something before you check anything on Monday

Until next time,
— The Behike Team
