# Issue 1346 — The Pricing Problem Nobody Talks About

**Subject:** You're not charging too much. You're charging too little.

---

The fear every new creator carries is that they're overcharging. That the price is why it's not selling. So they lower it. Then lower it again. And then they're selling at a price that can't fund the next step, and they wonder why the business feels like it's going nowhere.

Underpricing is a more common and more damaging problem than overpricing. It signals low confidence to buyers before they even read the description. A $7 product in a market where comparable products sell for $47 doesn't look like a deal. It looks like something is wrong with it. Price communicates perceived value before the content ever gets a chance to.

The second problem with underpricing is math. If your goal is $2,000 a month from digital products and you're selling something for $7, you need 286 customers. At $47, you need 43. At $97, you need 21. Those are very different distribution challenges, and the higher price point actually requires less volume to hit the same number. You don't need a bigger audience. You need a price that reflects what the work is actually worth.

**Tactical takeaway:** Pick your lowest-priced product. Ask yourself what problem it solves and how much that problem costs the buyer in time or money. Then price it at 10-20% of that cost. If someone saves 20 hours by using your template and their time is worth $50/hr, that product should be $100, not $15.

Price like you believe it works.
