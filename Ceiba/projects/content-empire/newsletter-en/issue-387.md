# Newsletter EN — Issue 387
## Why Your Landing Page Doesn't Convert

**Subject line:** The landing page problem isn't design. It's clarity.

**Preview text:** Most pages answer questions nobody is asking.

---

You built the page. You put in the hours. You picked the colors, wrote the copy, added a button. Then you sent traffic to it and watched the conversion rate sit at one percent.

This is not a traffic problem. This is a clarity problem.

**The real reason pages fail**

Most landing pages are written from the seller's perspective. They describe the product, list the features, explain the creator's story. But the visitor arrives with a completely different agenda. They are asking one question: "Is this for me, and does it solve my problem?"

The page answers a different question. That is the mismatch.

**The three structural failures**

Landing pages that don't convert usually share three problems.

No clear thesis in the headline. The first thing a visitor reads needs to say exactly what the offer is and who it is for. "Welcome to my world" is not a thesis. "A 30-day system for freelancers who want to close clients without cold calling" is a thesis. One of those keeps the visitor reading. The other sends them back to the feed.

Features instead of outcomes. "10 modules, 40 videos, and a private community" describes the container, not the result. What changes for the buyer after they go through this? What can they do that they couldn't do before? Lead with that. The features are supporting evidence, not the main argument.

Multiple calls to action. Every additional option you give a visitor reduces the chance they take any of them. One page. One offer. One button. The moment you add "also check out my free guide" or "follow me on Instagram," you've introduced a decision the visitor didn't come there to make.

**What a converting page actually does**

It takes a specific person with a specific problem and walks them from "maybe this could help" to "I need this." That journey requires four things in sequence: a headline that names their situation, a paragraph that makes them feel understood, proof that the outcome is real, and a single action to take.

That's it. Everything else is distraction dressed up as marketing.

**The fastest fix**

Read your page out loud. If you can't describe the offer in one sentence after reading it, your visitor can't either. Rewrite the headline until it answers: who is this for, what do they get, and why now.

Then remove everything that doesn't support that single answer.

The best landing pages feel too simple when you're building them. That simplicity is the point.
