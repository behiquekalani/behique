## Issue 596 — The One Metric That Tells the Truth About Your Business

**Subject line:** The number that matters more than revenue

---

Everyone tracks revenue.

Revenue is the number you post in screenshots. Revenue is what sounds good when someone asks how the business is going.

But revenue is a vanity metric if you are not tracking what comes after it.

The metric that actually tells you if your business is working is net profit per hour of your time.

Here is why.

A creator making $8,000/month who works 60 hours a week is earning $33 per hour before expenses. A full-time employee at a mid-level corporate job makes more than that without the risk, the uncertainty, or the customer support.

That same creator, if they cut their work hours to 20 through better systems, automation, and saying no to low-value tasks, is now earning $100 per hour from the same revenue base. That is a different business.

The goal of building an independent business is not to earn more money while working the same hours. It is to earn more money while working fewer hours on the things that actually matter.

So the question is: what is your current net profit per hour?

To calculate it: take your monthly net profit after expenses and divide it by your total working hours in that month. That number is your real hourly rate.

If that number is lower than you want, you have one of three problems.

First, your prices are too low. You are trading too much time for too little money. Raise prices, reduce volume, same or better income with less time.

Second, your processes are too slow. Tasks that could be templated, automated, or delegated are being done manually from scratch every time. Fix the process.

Third, you are doing too many things. You have offers that perform poorly and should be cut, but they eat your time and attention. Cut them. Focus on the thing that generates the best profit per hour.

Tracking net profit per hour forces clarity on what is actually worth doing. It is a hard question. It is also the right one.

This week, calculate your real hourly rate. Then identify one thing you could change to increase it by 25% in the next 90 days.

That is the only business strategy you need.

## Issue 597 — Why Most Digital Products Fail Before Launch

**Subject line:** The product isn't the problem

---

Most digital product launches fail before launch.

Not because the product is bad. Not because the creator is unqualified. Because the creator spent months building something nobody had been waiting for.

There is a pattern to this failure and it is completely avoidable.

The pattern: someone has an idea for a product, decides it is good, builds the entire thing, launches to silence, and concludes that digital products do not work.

The real problem is that they validated the idea with themselves instead of with buyers.

Self-validation sounds like: "I would have bought this." "This problem is real." "The market is huge."

Buyer validation sounds like: "Here is what I am building. Pre-order opens Friday at 50% off. I need 10 buyers to commit before I start."

The difference is not just philosophical. It is structural. Buyer validation tells you if real people will exchange real money for the specific solution you are proposing. Self-validation tells you nothing except that you are optimistic.

Here is the process for validating before building.

Write a one-paragraph description of the problem your product solves. Be specific. Name the person who has this problem. Name the exact pain point. Name the outcome they get.

Post that description to your audience with a simple question: "Is this a problem you are trying to solve right now?"

If you get responses, follow up with a presale offer. Offer the product at a discount before it exists. Tell them when you will deliver it.

If 10 people pay you, build the product.

If nobody pays, the problem was either not urgent enough, not specific enough, or you do not yet have the right audience for that offer.

None of those conclusions require you to build a product first.

The willingness to presell is the fastest way to separate ideas that seem good from products that actually sell.

Build second. Validate first. Every time.

## Issue 598 — The Compound Effect of Thinking in Public

**Subject line:** One year of posting your thinking out loud

---

In January 2024, I could not have told you what consistent content output actually looks like.

By January 2025, I had an answer: it looks like showing up daily with something you actually believe, even when the post gets 12 views.

Thinking in public has a compounding effect that most people abandon before they see it.

Here is what actually happens when you commit to it for a year.

The first 30 days: almost no engagement. You feel like you are shouting into nothing. Most people quit here.

Days 31-90: you start noticing which ideas land and which ones disappear. You refine your thesis. You find the framing that makes people stop scrolling.

Days 91-180: a small, specific audience starts to form around your clearest ideas. These people are not casual followers. They follow you because you think in a way that helps them think.

Days 181-365: the compounding begins. Old content still drives new followers. People are quoting your posts back to you. You are getting inbound interest from people who found you through content you posted months ago.

This is the part that does not happen without the first 180 days. You cannot skip to it.

The reason thinking in public works better than standard content marketing is because it is honest. You are not performing expertise. You are showing the actual work of working things out.

That honesty attracts a different kind of audience. Not a large audience. A specific one.

And specific audiences buy specific products. They do not need to be sold to. They already trust you because they have watched you think.

Three things to do if you want to start: write one honest opinion per day, post it where your audience is, and never delete the ones that underperform. The consistent signal matters more than the individual post.

Start ugly. Keep going. The compounding is real.

## Issue 599 — The Solopreneur's Hierarchy of Leverage

**Subject line:** You are probably working at the wrong level of leverage

---

There are four levels at which a solopreneur can work.

Most people work at level one. The highest earners work at level four. The difference is not talent. It is understanding how leverage compounds.

Level one: trading time for money. You do a task. You get paid for that task. Your income is directly proportional to your hours. This is the default state. It is also the ceiling.

Level two: systems. You document your process and build repeatable workflows. The same output now takes less time. You earn the same money in fewer hours. This is where most people stop.

Level three: products. You package your knowledge, system, or skill into something that can be sold without your time. Someone buys it while you sleep. Your income is no longer directly tied to your hours. This is where the leverage curve starts to bend.

Level four: distribution. You own an audience that trusts you. Every product you launch goes to people who are already primed to buy. Every new idea gets tested against a warm audience. The cost of customer acquisition drops toward zero. The compound effect of trust starts printing returns.

Most solopreneurs understand level two intellectually but live in level one because systematizing is boring and immediate revenue feels urgent.

Most solopreneurs understand level three but never finish a product because they are waiting until it is perfect.

Almost nobody operates at level four until they have spent years building it, usually by accident, while focused on one to three.

The path is sequential. You cannot skip to level four. But you can start level two and three simultaneously with what you already have.

This week, one question: what is one thing you do repeatedly that you have not yet documented or templated?

Start there. That is the entry point to every level above.

## Issue 600 — What I Know After 600 Issues

**Subject line:** 600 issues in. Here is what actually changed.

---

Six hundred issues.

I am not going to pretend I had a master plan from issue one. I did not. I had an idea, a vague sense that I had something worth saying, and enough stubbornness to keep going when the early numbers were embarrassing.

Here is what changed between issue one and issue six hundred.

The writing got faster. Not because I think less carefully, but because I stopped trying to sound like someone else. The first hundred issues have a certain careful quality to them that is mostly fear dressed up as thoroughness. The writing from the last year is direct because I stopped worrying about whether it sounded smart enough.

The audience got smaller and more valuable at the same time. I used to optimize for opens and list growth. Now I optimize for replies and purchases. The difference is enormous. A list of 500 people who respond when you write to them is a real business asset. A list of 5,000 people who never open is not.

The revenue per reader went up every time I got more specific. The issues that tried to appeal to everyone made the least money. The issues written for one person solving one problem made the most.

The single most important thing I did was stay consistent when nothing was working. There were stretches where I wrote to silence. Where the replies stopped. Where I questioned whether this was worth the time.

I stayed because I had made a rule for myself: measure the system, not the results. If I publish consistently, with quality, on a clear topic, the results will follow. That faith was tested. It held.

If you are at issue three of your newsletter and thinking about stopping, do not.

The compounding starts after the period where quitting makes sense.

Show up. Keep the standard high. Let time do the rest.

Thank you for reading.
