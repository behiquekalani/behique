## Issue 851: Build a Waitlist When You Have Nothing to Sell
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** How to build a waitlist before your product exists
**Preview text:** Demand before you build

---

Hey [First Name],

Building a product without testing demand first is one of the most common and expensive mistakes in the creator economy. A waitlist fixes that.

The principle: you don't need a product to know if people want one. You need a specific promise and a place to capture interest.

Here's how to build a waitlist from zero. Step one: write a one-sentence description of the outcome your product delivers. "A system for freelancers to raise their rates without losing clients." Not a product name. Not features. The outcome.

Step two: create a simple landing page. Not a full sales page. One headline. Three bullet points of what they'll get. A single email field. Tools: Carrd, ConvertKit, Gumroad pre-launch. You need this built in two hours, not two weeks.

Step three: post about the problem, not the product. Share a piece of content about the core challenge your product solves. At the end: "I'm building something for exactly this problem. If you want first access, link in bio."

Step four: DM everyone who engages. Every comment, every reply. Ask them what their biggest challenge is with this topic. You're doing market research and building the list at the same time.

At 50 names, you have proof someone wants this. At 100, you have a launch audience.

Key takeaways:
- The waitlist validates demand before you spend time building
- Post about the problem to attract the audience before revealing the product

## Issue 852: Getting Your First Testimonial Without Being Awkward
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** How to get testimonials when you're just starting out
**Preview text:** The ask most people avoid

---

Hey [First Name],

The testimonial problem feels like a chicken-and-egg trap. You need social proof to sell. You need sales to get social proof. Here's how to break the loop.

The fastest path to your first testimonial: give your product to three to five people for free or at a steep discount, in exchange for their honest feedback after they've used it.

Not a review. Not a rating. Feedback. This removes the pressure from both sides. You're not asking them to endorse you. You're asking them to tell you what worked and what didn't.

After they've used it, send a two-part message. "What was the most valuable part of [product]?" and "What did you accomplish or realize because of it?" Most people will answer those two questions in a way that is, functionally, a testimonial.

Then you ask: "Would you be okay if I shared this with my audience? I can include your name and what you do, or keep it anonymous if you prefer."

Ninety percent say yes.

The testimonials that convert best are specific. "I raised my first rate from $500 to $1,200 using the negotiation script in section 3." Not "Really useful, loved it!" Specific results create belief in prospective buyers.

If someone gives you a vague testimonial, ask a follow-up: "Can you tell me more about what specifically changed for you?"

Key takeaways:
- Give your product to a small beta group first to earn your first testimonials
- Ask for specific outcomes, not general feedback

Until next time,

## Issue 853: The 1-Page Business Plan That Actually Gets Used
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** Stop writing business plans you never open
**Preview text:** One page. Enough.

---

Hey [First Name],

A 30-page business plan is a document you write once and never read again. A one-page plan is a document you actually use.

Here's the format. One page, six sections.

1. The problem. One sentence. Who has it. How painful it is.
2. The solution. What you offer and to whom.
3. The revenue model. How money moves. What you sell, at what price, how often.
4. The customer. One specific person. Name them if you can. Describe their day.
5. The traction goal. Where you want to be in 90 days. One number. Revenue, subscribers, clients, whatever is meaningful.
6. The first three actions. The three things you do this week to move toward that 90-day goal.

Print it. Put it somewhere visible. Update it every 90 days.

The reason most business plans fail isn't the strategy. It's that they exist as documents rather than operating instruments. A one-pager forces you to be specific enough to act on it.

The most important section is the last one. Three actions. This week. Everything else is context. The actions are where the business happens.

Key takeaways:
- A one-page plan with clear actions beats a 30-page document with none
- Update it every 90 days as a living instrument, not a static artifact

Until next time,

## Issue 854: Hire vs. Automate: How Solopreneurs Decide
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** When to hire and when to automate
**Preview text:** The framework most skip

---

Hey [First Name],

Every solopreneur hits a ceiling where they're doing too much. The instinct is usually to hire. But hiring before you've automated is one of the most expensive mistakes you can make.

The decision framework is simple. Ask two questions.

First: is this task rule-based or judgment-based? Rule-based means you can write the instructions clearly enough that a system or a person following a checklist can do it correctly. Scheduling posts, processing orders, responding to FAQ emails, formatting documents. These are all rule-based. Automate them.

Judgment-based means the task requires contextual thinking that you'd have to train someone to replicate. Your writing voice. Client relationship management. Creative direction. These are hire-worthy tasks, but only when volume demands it.

Second: how many hours per week is this task costing you? If it's under two hours, automate it and stop thinking about it. If it's over five hours and judgment-based, that's a real hiring conversation.

The order: automate the rule-based work first. Then hire for the judgment-heavy work you can no longer avoid. If you hire before automating, you end up managing people through processes that should have been systems.

Most solopreneurs can get 10 to 15 hours back per week from automation before they ever need a first hire.

Key takeaways:
- Automate rule-based tasks before considering any hire
- Hire when volume of judgment-based work exceeds your capacity

## Issue 855: Writing Hooks That Stop Scrollers
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** The anatomy of a hook that actually works
**Preview text:** First 8 words do all the work

---

Hey [First Name],

Most creators know they need good hooks. Very few know why their hooks don't work.

The core function of a hook is not to impress. It's to create a gap. The reader's brain recognizes that there is something they don't know, and the only way to close the gap is to keep reading.

Here's what creates that gap:

Specificity. "How to grow a newsletter" creates no gap. "How a creator with 300 subscribers made $4,200 from one email" creates a gap. The brain immediately asks: how? A specific number, result, or timeframe signals that a real answer exists.

Counterintuition. Anything that violates what the reader thinks they know stops the scroll. "The worst time to launch a product is when you're ready." That breaks a mental model. The brain needs to resolve it.

Stakes. If the reader understands that they are missing something important, they pay attention. "Most creators fix the wrong thing when their content stops growing." That creates urgency. What are they fixing? What am I fixing?

The test: after you write a hook, ask yourself: would a stranger stop scrolling for this? If you can't confidently say yes, rewrite it before you post anything else.

Most people are one better hook away from doubling their content performance.

Key takeaways:
- Hooks work by creating information gaps, not by sounding clever
- Test every hook with: would a stranger stop scrolling for this?

Until next time,
— The Behike Team
