# The pricing mistake that kills most first products

Most first-time creators price their work based on how much time it took to make it. That is the wrong framework. Nobody pays for your hours. They pay for the transformation.

Here is the real problem. When you price low, you create a credibility gap. A $17 course about building a freelance business signals: "Even I don't believe this is worth much." The price becomes part of the message. And the message is doubt.

## Why low pricing hurts you

Buyers do not evaluate products in isolation. They evaluate them against everything else they've seen at that price point. At $17, they're comparing your work to PDF checklists and Notion templates from strangers on social media. At $197, they're comparing it to courses from creators they trust. Price places you in a category before anyone reads a single word of your sales page.

There's also a behavioral dimension. Research in behavioral economics consistently shows that people engage less with things they paid little for. The skin-in-the-game effect is real. Higher prices create higher completion rates, more implementation, and more testimonials. All of which you need to grow.

## How to price your first product properly

Start by defining the outcome, not the content. What does someone's life look like after they implement what you teach? What is that outcome worth to them in time saved, money earned, or pain avoided?

Price at 10-20% of the conservative estimate of that outcome's value. If your product helps someone land a $2,000/month freelance client, a $197 price point is not aggressive. It's rational.

Then test. Write the sales page. Run it past 5 people in your target audience. Watch where they hesitate. Adjust the positioning before you adjust the price.

## The action step

Write down the single most valuable outcome your product delivers. Put a dollar amount on that outcome. Then price your product at 10% of it. That number is probably higher than what you were planning to charge.

Sit with the discomfort. It usually means you're getting closer to the real value.
