# Issue 1323: Price Your Product Based on Transformation, Not Time

Every creator who underprices their work is making the same mistake. They are pricing based on how long it took to build, not on what it changes for the buyer.

A PDF that takes someone from confusion to clarity on a $200,000 business decision is not a $19 product. A course that helps someone quit their job in 90 days is not a $47 product. The price should reflect the result. Not the file size. Not the production hours. The result.

This is hard to internalize because we are conditioned to price by effort. Freelancers charge hourly. Employees charge yearly. We bring that frame to digital products and kill our own margins. A $97 template that saves someone 20 hours of work is a deal for them and a win for you. A $19 version of the same thing is just busy work.

The practical test is this: what would your buyer pay a consultant or agency to get the same outcome your product delivers? If the consultant fee is $2,000 and your product delivers the same result for the motivated self-starter, your product could reasonably sit at $197. You are not cheaper because you are worse. You are cheaper because you removed the middleman.

Raising your price also filters your buyers. Lower prices attract people who want a deal. Higher prices attract people who want a result. Which customer do you want to serve?

**Tactical takeaway:** Take your current flagship product and write out the specific transformation it delivers in one sentence. Then ask: what would someone pay a professional to achieve that outcome? Price your product at 10-20% of that number. Test for 30 days.

Price is a message. Make sure yours says something true.
