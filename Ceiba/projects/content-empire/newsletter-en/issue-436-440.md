## Issue 436

**Subject:** The reason your digital product isn't selling (it's not the marketing)

Most people who build digital products blame traffic when sales are slow. The real problem is upstream. You built something for a problem people can live with, not a problem they're actively trying to solve.

**Core insight:** A product that solves a tolerable problem will always underperform a product that solves an urgent one. Pain that people are already spending money to reduce is your market signal.

**What to actually do:**

1. Before building anything, find where your target audience is already spending money on the problem. Reddit threads, Amazon reviews, competitor sales pages.
2. Your product needs to promise a specific outcome, not a category of improvement. "More clarity" is not an outcome. "A 3-step system to decide your niche in 48 hours" is.
3. Validate with a landing page before building. If 5% of cold visitors opt in, you have something. If it's below 2%, rewrite the promise.
4. Price it at what the outcome is worth, not at what you feel comfortable charging. Discomfort in pricing is almost always a signal to charge more, not less.
5. Launch before you're ready. Perfectionism in product building is procrastination dressed up as quality control.

The fastest path to a product that sells is to find the thing people are already trying to buy, then be the clearest option in that space.

Build for the urgent problem. Price for the outcome. Launch before it's perfect.

## Issue 437

**Subject:** Why the $27 product and the $297 product contain the same information

Walk into any creator's product catalog and you'll find this pattern: a $27 PDF and a $297 course covering nearly identical content. The difference isn't depth. It's specificity of promise and context of delivery.

**Core insight:** Price is not a function of content volume. It's a function of how precisely the product matches the buyer's current situation and desired outcome.

**What this means in practice:**

1. A $27 product says "here is how to build an audience." A $297 product says "here is how a solopreneur with a full-time job builds 1,000 true fans in 90 days posting 3x per week."
2. Specificity removes the buyer's need to figure out if the product applies to them. That reduction in uncertainty is what justifies the price.
3. The context of delivery matters. A Notion doc at $27 feels appropriate. The same content as a structured course with templates and walkthroughs at $297 feels appropriate.
4. If you want to move up in price, don't add more content. Add more precision. Narrow the audience, sharpen the outcome, reduce the gap between buying and getting the result.
5. Your refund rate will tell you if the promise and the delivery are aligned. High refunds mean the buyer's expectation and the product reality don't match. That's a positioning problem, not a product problem.

Price is a signal. People use it to decide if a product was built for someone like them. Make sure it was.

Kalani, Behike

## Issue 438

**Subject:** The solopreneur operating system nobody talks about

Everyone teaches the creative side of the one-person business. The ideas, the content, the offers. Almost nobody teaches the operating side. How you actually run the thing without losing your mind.

**Core insight:** A solopreneur business without a clear operating rhythm becomes a reactive mess. You end up working on whatever feels urgent instead of whatever moves the business forward.

**The operating system that works:**

1. Weekly planning session, 30 minutes, same time every week. Review last week's output, set three priorities for the current week, and identify the one constraint blocking revenue right now.
2. One creation block per day, minimum 90 minutes, with nothing else scheduled around it. Content, product work, or writing. This block is non-negotiable.
3. A capture system for every idea, feedback, and observation. Ideas that live only in your head are not ideas. They're noise. Get them into a file you actually review.
4. Monthly offer audit. What did you sell this month. What converted. What got the most questions. This tells you what your audience actually wants, not what you think they want.
5. One hard stop per day. Work has no natural ending point when you're the only employee. The boundary has to be artificial and enforced.

The solopreneurs who last five years are not the most talented ones. They're the ones who built a rhythm they could sustain when motivation was low.

Build the system. Protect the rhythm.

Kalani, Behike

## Issue 439

**Subject:** How to build an audience that buys instead of an audience that just watches

There are two types of audiences. The first applauds you. The second pays you. Most creators optimize for the first because it feels better. It's also the thing that keeps you broke.

**Core insight:** An audience that buys is built on trust and specificity. An audience that just watches is built on entertainment. These are different value propositions that attract different people.

**How to shift from watchers to buyers:**

1. Stop creating content that is purely informational. Information is free. Perspective is rare. Share your actual stance on things, including the uncomfortable ones.
2. Talk about money, outcomes, and real results consistently. Audiences that buy are attracted to proof. Audiences that watch are attracted to inspiration.
3. Sell something early and often. Not because you need the revenue immediately, but because selling teaches you who your actual buyer is. The feedback is irreplaceable.
4. Your best buyers will come from the content where you said the thing nobody else in your space was willing to say. Write that piece. Post that thread. Make the argument.
5. Build an email list from day one. Social media audiences are borrowed. Email is owned. The buyers are almost always on the email list, not just the feed.

Attention is cheap. Trust is expensive to build and worth every investment.

Build the trust. The revenue follows.

Kalani, Behike

## Issue 440

**Subject:** The pricing mistake that is quietly capping your revenue

There is a ceiling that most solopreneurs hit and can't explain. Revenue grows to a certain point and then flatlines. The content is good. The audience is engaged. Something is wrong with the offer structure and usually it comes down to one thing: you only have one price point.

**Core insight:** A business with one product at one price is permanently capped by how many new buyers it can acquire. A business with a product ladder converts existing buyers into higher-value customers, and that changes the math entirely.

**How to build a product ladder that works:**

1. Start with a low-barrier entry product. Under $50. This is not your revenue driver. It is your trust builder. The buyer who spends $27 is proving they will act on information.
2. Your core offer should be $200 to $500 and solve one specific, high-stakes problem for your audience. This is where most of your revenue should come from.
3. A premium tier, whether that's a course, cohort, or direct access, should exist for the buyers who want to go faster and want you closer to the process.
4. The transition from one tier to the next should happen naturally. If someone buys the $27 product and gets a result, the $297 product should be the obvious next step.
5. Don't launch all three tiers at once. Validate each level before building the next. The ladder only works if each rung is proven.

One product is a bet. A ladder is a system. Build the system.

Kalani, Behike
