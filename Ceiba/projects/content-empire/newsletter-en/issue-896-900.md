## Issue 896: Niche vs. Broad Content: The Real Answer
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** Niche vs. Broad Content: The Real Answer
**Preview text:** Both camps are missing the point.

---

Hey [First Name],

The "niche down" crowd says pick a specific topic and own it. The "stay broad" crowd says do not box yourself in. Both are giving advice without understanding the actual goal.

The goal is not to be niche or broad. The goal is to be specific about who you help and flexible about how you help them. That is a completely different framing.

When you are specific about who you help, every piece of content lands. A solopreneur building a consulting business knows your newsletter is for them. A creator trying to grow on LinkedIn knows your content is relevant. They do not have to figure out if you are talking to them. You make it obvious.

When you are flexible about how you help them, you can talk about mindset, tactics, tools, stories, and frameworks without it feeling scattered. Everything connects back to the person, not the topic.

The creators who stall are the ones who niched into a topic instead of an audience. "I create content about productivity" is a topic niche. "I help solopreneurs build systems so they can work less and earn more" is an audience niche. One limits you. The other liberates you.

Pick your person. Then talk about everything that matters to that person.

- Niche by person, not by topic. One gives you depth. The other gives you a cage.
- Broad content builds reach. Specific content builds buyers. You need both for different goals.

## Issue 897: Audience Segmentation: The Move Most Creators Skip
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** Audience Segmentation: The Move Most Creators Skip
**Preview text:** Not all subscribers are equal.

---

Hey [First Name],

You have one email list. But you do not have one audience. You have beginners, intermediates, and people who have already bought from you. Sending everyone the same email is like giving the same lesson to every student regardless of where they are.

Segmentation is the practice of treating different parts of your audience differently. It is not complicated. It starts with a simple tag system in your email provider. Tag people based on what they downloaded, what they clicked, and what they bought. Then send content that matches where they are.

Here is a practical starting point. Segment into three groups. New subscribers who have not bought anything yet. Buyers who have purchased at least one product. And your most engaged subscribers who open every email. Each group gets slightly different content. New subscribers get more education and trust-building. Buyers get more advanced tactics and upsell offers. Engaged subscribers get early access and community invitations.

The revenue difference is significant. A segmented list converts two to three times better than an unsegmented one because the right offer reaches the right person at the right time.

You do not have to do all of this at once. Start with one tag. Buyers versus non-buyers. Then build from there.

- Segmentation is not complexity. It is respect for where your reader actually is.
- Your buyers are your best leads for your next product. Tag them and treat them like VIPs.

Until next time,

## Issue 898: The Creator's Profit Margin (And Why It Changes Everything)
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** The Creator's Profit Margin (And Why It Changes Everything)
**Preview text:** Your margin is your freedom.

---

Hey [First Name],

Most businesses operate on margins of 10 to 30 percent. A creator business, done right, operates on margins of 70 to 90 percent. That difference is not just financial. It is structural. It changes what decisions you can make and how fast you can make them.

When you sell a digital product, the cost to deliver is essentially zero. No inventory. No shipping. No manufacturing. Once the product exists, every sale is nearly pure revenue. Compare that to a service business, where your margin is limited by the hours you have. Or a physical product business, where margin is eaten by cost of goods, storage, and returns.

The high-margin creator business is not just about passive income. It is about optionality. When your margin is 80 percent, you can afford to experiment. You can offer a free webinar. You can drop the price for a launch. You can give away a product as a lead magnet. These decisions are easy when you know the economics are solid.

Most creators do not think about margin because they are focused on revenue. Revenue is vanity. Margin is sanity. A creator doing $5K per month with 85 percent margin is in a better position than an agency owner doing $20K with 15 percent margin.

Know your numbers. Then make decisions from there.

- Digital products are the highest-margin business model available to independent creators. Use that leverage.
- Margin gives you options. Build your business to protect it.

Until next time,

## Issue 899: Building on Rented Land (And What to Do About It)
**Type:** Story
**Subject:** Building on Rented Land (And What to Do About It)
**Preview text:** You do not own your platform.

---

Hey [First Name],

Every social media platform you use is rented land. Instagram can change the algorithm. Twitter can shadowban your account. TikTok can disappear from the app store. YouTube can demonetize your channel. None of these are hypotheticals. All of them have happened to real creators who built real businesses there.

This is not a reason to avoid social media. It is a reason to understand your relationship with it correctly. Social platforms are distribution channels. They are not your business. They are how people find you. What happens after they find you is your business.

The owned assets in a creator business are few but critical. Your email list. Your domain. Your products. These are the things that cannot be taken from you by an algorithm change or a terms of service update. If you had to rebuild from zero tomorrow, your email list and your products are what you would want to have.

The strategy is not complicated. Use social platforms to attract attention. Then convert that attention into owned assets. A follower is someone who can be taken from you. A subscriber is someone you can always reach. The goal is always to move people from platforms you rent to assets you own.

Every piece of content you create should have a path back to your email list. That is the whole strategy.

- Followers are rented. Subscribers are owned. Build toward what you can keep.
- Your email list is the one asset that survives platform changes. Prioritize it above all else.

Until next time,

## Issue 900: The Underrated Power of Boring Consistency
**Type:** Story
**Subject:** The Underrated Power of Boring Consistency
**Preview text:** Consistency beats talent. Always.

---

Hey [First Name],

Nobody talks about the person who showed up every week for two years with no viral moments. But that person often has the strongest business.

Consistency is not exciting. It does not make for a good story in the short term. But it does something nothing else can: it builds compounding trust. When your audience knows you will be in their inbox every Tuesday, they start to count on you. That reliability is rare. And rare things are valuable.

The creators chasing virality are playing a different game. They need each piece of content to perform. One bad week feels like failure. Their energy goes into the hope of the next hit instead of the process of building something real.

Boring consistency works because the audience you build this way is self-selecting. The people who stay subscribed through the ordinary weeks are the ones who genuinely care about what you do. They are not there for entertainment. They are there because you help them think. Those are the people who buy.

You do not need a viral moment. You need a publishing schedule you can keep for 24 months. That is the whole strategy. Pick a frequency that feels sustainable, not impressive. Then do it until the results show up.

They will show up.

- The person who publishes consistently for two years wins almost every time over the person chasing spikes.
- Sustainable consistency beats impressive bursts. Choose the frequency you can keep.

Until next time,
— The Behike Team
