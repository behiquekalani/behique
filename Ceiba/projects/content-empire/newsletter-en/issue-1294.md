# Issue 1294: How to Use Your Personal Story Without Making It About You

Personal storytelling in content is not about you. This confuses creators who think sharing personal experiences is a form of self-promotion.

When done right, a personal story is a container for an insight the reader needs. You tell the story to create context, not to showcase yourself. The reader should finish your story thinking about their own situation, not yours.

The structure is: setup, mistake or struggle, insight, application. The setup gives the reader enough context to follow. The mistake or struggle creates relatability. No one trusts a creator who has never failed. The insight is what you actually learned. The application tells the reader what to do with that insight in their own life.

The mistake creators make is lingering too long in the story itself. They describe the situation in detail, give background, explain context, and by the time they get to the insight, the reader has moved on. Keep the story short enough that it's clear you're using it as a vehicle, not as the destination.

The other mistake is making the story exceptional. Extraordinary events make poor teaching stories. "I sold my company for $50M and here's what I learned" is inspiring but inaccessible. "I almost quit my newsletter after 18 months and here's what stopped me" is relatable and actionable. Ordinary struggles with specific insights beat rare achievements every time.

**Tactical takeaway:** Find one mistake you made in the past six months. Write the four-part story: what you were doing, what went wrong, what you realized, what you now do differently. Keep it under 300 words. Publish it. This kind of story builds more trust than a week of advice posts.

The reader sees themselves in your struggles, not your victories.
