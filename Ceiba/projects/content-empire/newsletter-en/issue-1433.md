# Issue 1433 — Price Is a Signal, Not Just a Number

**Subject:** Why raising your price often increases sales

---

Price communicates more than cost. It communicates quality, exclusivity, and how seriously the buyer should take the product. This is well-documented in consumer psychology but almost never applied by first-time digital product creators, who almost always underprice.

The logic of underpricing is intuitive: lower price means more buyers. But for digital products, especially those solving real business problems, a low price signals low quality. A $9 course on launching a business signals that the creator isn't confident it will actually work. A $197 course on the same topic signals that someone thought carefully about the transformation being sold.

This doesn't mean charge as much as possible. It means price based on the value of the outcome, not the time it took you to produce it. A one-page framework that saves someone 20 hours of research is worth more than $7. It's worth whatever 20 hours of that person's time is worth.

The test is simple: if you feel like you should apologize for your price when you mention it, the price is too low.

**Tactical takeaway:** Take your most popular digital product. Double the price. Update the sales page to focus entirely on the outcome, not the content. Run it for 30 days. You will likely see the same or higher conversion rate with double the revenue per sale.

Price the outcome. Not the effort.
