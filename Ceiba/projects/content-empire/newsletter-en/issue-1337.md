# Issue 1337: How to Write Headlines That Do Not Sound Clickbait

Most creators are stuck between two bad options: boring headlines that nobody reads and sensational headlines that promise things the content does not deliver. There is a third option.

The headline's job is to make a specific promise to a specific reader. Not a vague promise. Not an overpromised claim. A specific, honest statement of what is inside. "How I Doubled My Email Open Rate in 14 Days" is specific. "The Email Hack That Changes Everything" is not.

The difference in engagement between these two types is real but not always in the direction people expect. Specific headlines often have lower click rates because they narrow the audience. But the people who click on specific headlines are far more likely to read, share, and buy. You trade broad curiosity for qualified attention.

The best headline formula is: specific result plus time frame plus honest context. "How I Went From 0 to 1,000 Subscribers Without Paid Ads in 90 Days" works because every element is falsifiable. Either you did it or you did not. Either it took 90 days or it did not. That kind of specificity signals credibility before the reader even reads the first paragraph.

The context element is where most people leave value behind. "Without paid ads" tells the reader something about who this is for. It signals that you are speaking to someone who is in the same boat, not someone with a media budget.

**Tactical takeaway:** Take your next five headlines and run them through the "could this be falsified" test. If the claim in the headline is not specific enough to be wrong, it is not specific enough to be compelling. Rewrite until you could theoretically be proven incorrect.

Honesty is the new clickbait. It just works slower and compounds longer.
