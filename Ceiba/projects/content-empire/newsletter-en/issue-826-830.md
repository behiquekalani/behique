## Issue 826: The YouTube Shorts Strategy Nobody Talks About
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** Why your Shorts aren't converting (and how to fix it)
**Preview text:** It's not the algorithm. It's you.

---

Hey [First Name],

Most creators treat YouTube Shorts like a lottery ticket. Post enough and maybe something goes viral. That's not a strategy. That's hope with a camera.

Here's what actually works: Shorts as a top-of-funnel machine for your long-form content. Every Short you make should answer one question from your ideal viewer, then leave them wanting the full framework. That tension is what drives clicks to your channel, your newsletter, or your product.

The mistake most creators make is treating Shorts as standalone content. They chase views without thinking about what happens after the view. A Short that gets 100K views but sends zero people down your funnel is just vanity. A Short that gets 10K views and converts 200 subscribers is a business asset.

Your Shorts strategy should have three layers. First, the hook Short, which opens a problem your audience has. Second, the proof Short, which shows a result or case study. Third, the solution Short, which teases the full answer that lives in your long-form content or product.

Map your Shorts to your content ecosystem. Every Short should connect to something bigger.

- Shorts with no destination are entertainment. Shorts with a destination are distribution.
- Three Short types: hook, proof, solution. Rotate them weekly.

## Issue 827: Five Contracts Every Solopreneur Needs Before Year Two
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** The legal gap that's costing solopreneurs thousands
**Preview text:** One document changes everything.

---

Hey [First Name],

Most solopreneurs lose money the same way. Not from bad products. Not from poor marketing. From not having the right paperwork when things go sideways.

A client dispute without a contract is a coin flip. A partnership without an agreement is a ticking clock. These are not edge cases. They happen to good people with good intentions, and they are almost always preventable.

Here are five documents you need before you take on your next serious client or partner.

A service agreement: spells out what you deliver, when, and what happens if either party walks away. A scope of work: separate from the service agreement, it defines exactly what is included and what costs extra. An intellectual property clause: who owns what you create. This matters more than people realize. A payment terms document: net 30 means nothing if you have no late fee clause or kill fee for abandoned projects. A non-disclosure agreement: not just for corporate work. Use it whenever you share a business strategy or proprietary process.

These do not require a lawyer to draft. Templates exist. What they require is the discipline to use them before trust becomes a liability.

- Every informal agreement is a future argument waiting to happen.
- Contracts protect both parties. Frame it that way when clients push back.

Until next time,
— The Behike Team

## Issue 828: The Content Repurposing System That Saves 10 Hours a Week
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** One piece of content. Seven platforms. Zero burnout.
**Preview text:** Work once. Distribute forever.

---

Hey [First Name],

Creating original content for every platform every day is not a content strategy. It is a burnout schedule dressed up as consistency.

The creators who last are not creating more. They are distributing smarter.

Here is the system: Every week, you produce one long-form anchor piece. This could be a newsletter issue, a YouTube video, a podcast episode, or a long LinkedIn post. That one piece becomes your content factory. You pull the core idea for a Twitter thread. You pull one insight for an Instagram caption. You pull one quote for a short-form video. You pull one question for a poll. You pull one framework for a Pinterest graphic.

Seven pieces of content from one idea. Two to three hours of creation becomes ten to twelve distribution touchpoints.

The key is building a repurposing workflow before you need it. Most creators try to repurpose retroactively, and it feels like extra work. When repurposing is baked into your creation process from the start, it becomes invisible.

Document your workflow. Write out every step from anchor piece to final distribution. Then systematize or delegate each step. You should be the only one writing. Everything else can be a system.

- One anchor piece per week. Everything else is derivative work.
- Systemize the derivative work. Protect your creative energy for the anchor.

Until next time,
— The Behike Team

## Issue 829: Strategic Planning for the One-Person Business
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** Why annual goals fail solopreneurs (and what works instead)
**Preview text:** Stop planning like a corporation.

---

Hey [First Name],

Annual planning was designed for teams with departments, budgets, and quarterly reviews. You are one person. Your business moves in days, not quarters. Planning like a corporation when you are a one-person operation is one of the reasons most solopreneurs feel perpetually behind.

The framework that actually works for solo builders is the 90-day sprint. Three months is long enough to build something meaningful and short enough to stay focused without losing urgency.

Each 90-day sprint should have one primary goal. Not five. Not three. One. The single outcome that, if achieved, would make the quarter a success. Everything else you do should either support that goal or be deprioritized.

Around that primary goal, you set three to five supporting objectives. These are the measurable milestones that tell you if you are on track. Each week, you identify the single most important task that moves the needle toward one of those objectives.

This is not a rigid system. Life interrupts. Markets shift. Opportunities arrive without warning. The 90-day sprint gives you enough structure to move with intention and enough flexibility to adapt.

Review weekly. Adjust monthly. Recommit quarterly.

- One primary goal per 90-day sprint. Clarity beats ambition.
- Weekly reviews keep the sprint from becoming wishful thinking.

Until next time,
— The Behike Team

## Issue 830: The Creator's Quarterly Review
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** The 30-minute review that changes your next 90 days
**Preview text:** Reflect less. Extract more.

---

Hey [First Name],

Most creators never stop to review. They finish one sprint, one launch, one content series, and immediately jump to the next thing. This feels like momentum. It is actually the fastest way to repeat your mistakes and miss your patterns.

The quarterly review is not about feeling good about what you built. It is about extracting what works and eliminating what does not.

Here is a 30-minute framework. Spend 10 minutes on wins: what worked, what surprised you, what you would double down on. Spend 10 minutes on lessons: what did not work, where you wasted time, what assumptions turned out to be wrong. Spend 10 minutes on direction: given what you now know, what is the one thing you would prioritize next quarter that you did not prioritize this one.

The goal is not a long document. The goal is three clear decisions. What to start. What to stop. What to scale.

Creators who review quarterly compound their learning. Those who skip the review stay on the same level longer than they need to.

You do not need more content. You need better feedback loops.

- Three outputs from every quarterly review: start, stop, scale.
- Schedule it now. A review that is not calendared does not happen.

Until next time,
— The Behike Team
