## Issue 796: How to Validate a Digital Product Before You Build It
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** Don't build first. Validate first. Always.
**Preview text:** Sell it before you make it.

---

Hey [First Name],

The single most expensive mistake in the creator economy is building a product nobody buys. Not because people don't want what you made, but because you never asked before you built.

Validation is the step that separates people who grow fast from people who spin their wheels for years.

Here's a validation process that works. Step one: state the problem clearly. Write one sentence that describes the specific pain your product solves and who has it. If you can't write that sentence clearly, you're not ready to build. Step two: find ten people who have that problem. Not friends. People who are actually in your target audience. Have a conversation with them. Ask about the problem. Don't pitch the product yet. Step three: describe the outcome your product delivers and ask if they'd pay for it. If fewer than three out of ten say yes, rethink the offer. If five or more say yes, you probably have something.

The cheapest form of validation is a pre-order. Write a sales page describing the product before it exists. Drive traffic to it. If people buy, you build. If nobody buys, you've saved yourself weeks of work.

Most digital products should be validated within a week before any serious building begins. Not because you're hedging your bets, but because real feedback makes the product better and the launch faster.

Build what people are already asking for, not what you assume they need.

- Presell before you produce. A presale is the most reliable form of validation.
- Five conversations with real potential customers are worth more than a month of planning.

## Issue 797: Building Your Solopreneur Marketing System
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** Marketing that runs even when you don't
**Preview text:** Stop relying on motivation to market.

---

Hey [First Name],

The problem with most solopreneur marketing isn't quality. It's that it only happens when there's time and energy left over after client work. Which means it almost never happens.

A marketing system changes that. Instead of marketing being a task you do when you remember, it becomes a process that runs on its own rhythm.

Here's the structure of a minimal effective marketing system for solopreneurs. One lead-generation channel that you own, meaning email, SEO, or a platform where you have meaningful reach. One content type that you produce consistently each week. One offer that stays live and visible at all times. One conversion mechanism, a landing page, a sales call, a direct purchase link, that turns interest into revenue without requiring a custom pitch every time.

That's the whole system. Four moving parts.

The key is choosing a lead channel that matches how you actually work. If you hate writing, don't build a blog. If you hate being on camera, don't base your marketing on video. The best marketing system is the one you'll actually run.

Once the system is built, your job is to maintain it, not reinvent it. You feed the content. The content feeds the list. The list feeds the offer. The offer feeds the business.

Marketing becomes maintenance instead of a crisis.

- A four-part system beats ad-hoc effort every time
- Choose a lead channel that matches how you actually work, not what's theoretically best

## Issue 798: Finding the Leverage Point in Your Business
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** One constraint is costing you everything. Find it.
**Preview text:** One thing limits everything.

---

Hey [First Name],

Every business has a constraint. One thing that, if you fixed it, would unlock growth in everything downstream. Most founders spend months working hard on the wrong things because they've never identified what the actual constraint is.

This concept comes from the Theory of Constraints, but you don't need the theory. You need the question: "What is the one thing that, if we fixed it tomorrow, would make the biggest difference in revenue?"

For most early solopreneurs, the constraint is either leads or conversion. Not production. Not fulfillment. Not systems. If you don't have enough money coming in, it's almost always because either not enough people are seeing your offer, or not enough of the people who see it are buying.

How to find your constraint: trace the customer journey backwards. How many people are paying you? How many reached your offer? How many heard about you? Where does the biggest drop-off happen? That's your constraint.

Once you identify it, you put disproportionate energy there. Not evenly distributed hustle across everything. Focused, deliberate work on the one bottleneck that controls everything else.

Fixing the constraint doesn't mean everything works perfectly. It means a new constraint becomes visible. That's the game. Identify, fix, repeat.

The founders who grow fastest aren't doing more. They're doing the right thing at the right place.

- Most early business problems are either a leads problem or a conversion problem
- Trace the customer journey backwards to find where the biggest drop-off is

## Issue 799: Client Attraction Content: How to Create Content That Brings Buyers
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** The content that finds clients for you
**Preview text:** Your content can close deals.

---

Hey [First Name],

There's a difference between content that builds an audience and content that attracts clients. Most creators focus on the first kind and wonder why their growing following isn't translating into revenue.

Client attraction content operates on a different principle. It isn't trying to entertain or educate broadly. It's trying to demonstrate specific competence to a specific type of buyer.

Here's what that looks like. Case study content: "Here's a real problem a client had, what we diagnosed, what we did, and what the outcome was." This content isn't glamorous. It's direct evidence that you can solve a problem that other potential clients recognize in themselves.

Process content: "Here's exactly how I approach [specific challenge]." This shows potential clients what it's like to work with you before they hire you. It builds confidence in your method.

Objection content: "The three things people usually worry about when hiring someone like me, and how I handle each one." This closes the objection before the sales conversation even starts.

Contrast content: "Why most [service providers] do X and why I do Y instead." This positions your approach as a deliberate choice, not a default.

None of this requires a huge audience. It requires the right audience, even 500 people who have the problem you solve.

Post client attraction content once a week. The other posts can build reach. These posts build pipeline.

- Client attraction content demonstrates specific competence, not general knowledge
- One client-facing post per week creates a slow but consistent flow of qualified leads

## Issue 800: The Creator's Exit Strategy
**Type:** Story
**Subject:** The question every creator avoids but shouldn't
**Preview text:** What are you building toward?

---

Hey [First Name],

Most creators never ask the exit question. Not because it's morbid, but because they're so focused on surviving the next month that thinking about where this is all going feels like a luxury.

But the exit question is actually a direction question. Not "when are you stopping?" but "what kind of asset are you building?"

Because there's a difference between building a personal brand and building a business. A personal brand is tied to you. If you stop, it stops. A business has systems, products, and audiences that don't require you in every transaction.

The creator's exit strategy is about gradually shifting from the first kind to the second.

That shift happens through a few specific moves. First: productizing. When your knowledge lives in a guide or course rather than only in your head, it creates value without your presence. Second: building an email list you own. Social platforms are borrowed land. An email list is yours regardless of what any platform does. Third: creating processes that someone else can run. Even if you never hire, this mental discipline keeps you from building a business that only works when you do.

The creators who last ten years aren't necessarily the ones who worked hardest in year one. They're the ones who built assets, not just income.

Income disappears when you stop. Assets don't.

You don't have to plan your exit. But you should know what you're building.

- The difference between a personal brand and a business is whether it works without you
- Build assets, not just income. Products, lists, and processes outlast hustle.

Until next time,
— The Behike Team
