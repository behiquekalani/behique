# Issue 1279: Why Most Online Courses Fail Their Buyers

Online courses as a format are not the problem. The business model around them usually is.

Most creators build courses to capture value: here's what I know, pay me for access. The problem is that knowledge without implementation support often stays knowledge. The buyer finishes 30% of the course, life intervenes, and nothing changes. They don't ask for a refund because they know the course isn't the issue. But they don't buy from that creator again either.

The courses that create loyal customers are built around transformation, not information. They're designed around what the buyer needs to do, not what the creator needs to say. Every module answers: what does the buyer do after this? If the answer is "listen to the next module," the design is broken.

The fix doesn't require scrapping the course. It requires adding implementation checkpoints. Clear action steps. Templates that remove friction. Optional group accountability. These elements turn information into action, which turns buyers into success stories, which turns into testimonials, which turns into more sales.

If you're building a course, ask yourself: could a buyer finish this course and have taken zero meaningful actions? If yes, redesign before you launch.

**Tactical takeaway:** Take any course or product you've built or are building. For each section, write one action the buyer takes after completing it. If you can't write the action, the section is teaching without leading. Cut or rewrite it until the action is clear.

Design for action. Not consumption.
