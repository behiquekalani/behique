# Issue 1396: The Problem With "Follow Your Passion" as Business Advice

**Subject:** A more honest framework for choosing what to build

---

"Follow your passion" sounds liberating. In practice, it is responsible for a lot of failed businesses and a lot of confused creators who turned something they loved into something they resent.

The problem is that passion alone tells you nothing about market fit, pricing power, or sustainability. You can be deeply passionate about something and still be unable to make money from it, because the market doesn't value it the same way you do, because the competition is too dense, or because the monetization model is structurally weak.

A better framework combines three questions: What are you genuinely good at? What do people pay for? What can you learn enough about to maintain genuine curiosity? The intersection of all three is where sustainable businesses live. It is not as romantic as pure passion, but it is considerably more durable. And the good news is that competence and financial validation both tend to generate passion over time. You often learn to love what you are good at and rewarded for.

**Tactical takeaway:** Run the three-question audit on your current or planned business. For each question, give yourself a score from 1-10. If your "what do people pay for" score is below 7, that is the variable to investigate before investing more time in the other two.

Build where passion and market overlap. That is the only version that lasts.
