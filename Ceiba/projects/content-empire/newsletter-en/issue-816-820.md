## Issue 816: Course vs Guide vs Template
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** Which digital product format should you build?
**Preview text:** Format shapes conversion.

---

Hey [First Name],

Not every idea should become a course. And not every course idea should stay a course.

The format of your digital product shapes who buys it, how they use it, and what they are willing to pay. Choosing the wrong format means misaligned buyers, bad reviews, and low repeat purchases.

Here is a simple framework.

A guide is for people who need to understand something. It is comprehensive and educational. It answers "what is this" and "how does this work." Guides work well for audiences who are earlier in their journey. They are fast to produce and easy to price at $9 to $29.

A template is for people who need to execute something. It eliminates the blank page. The buyer knows what to do but does not want to build the structure from scratch. Templates work well for tactical, repeatable tasks. They are the highest effort-to-value ratio for the buyer. Price range: $15 to $49.

A course is for people who need transformation. It is sequential. It takes someone from a defined starting point to a defined outcome. Courses require more production time and command higher prices, typically $97 and above. But they also require more trust to sell.

The fastest path to a first sale is usually a guide or a template. Once you have buyers who trust you, the course becomes a natural upgrade.

- Match format to where the buyer is in their journey
- Templates sell fastest because they deliver immediate utility

## Issue 817: When to Scale
**Type:** Educational
**Subject:** The signs you are ready to scale and the signs you are not
**Preview text:** Scale amplifies what is there.

---

Hey [First Name],

Scaling a broken system makes the breaks bigger. This is why most creators who try to scale too early end up more frustrated, not more successful.

There are three things that need to be true before scaling makes sense.

First, your offer converts consistently. Not occasionally. Not once in a while when conditions are perfect. Consistently, at a rate you understand and can predict. If you do not know your conversion rate, you are not ready to scale.

Second, you can fulfill the demand. If doubling your sales would break your delivery process, you need to fix fulfillment first. For digital products, this is usually not the issue. For services, it almost always is.

Third, your unit economics are positive. Meaning for every dollar you put in, you get more than a dollar out. If acquiring a customer costs more than that customer generates, scale will destroy you faster than stagnation.

When all three are true, scaling is just turning a dial. More traffic into a proven system produces more output.

The most effective scaling lever for creators is paid distribution. Not paid ads to cold audiences. Paid distribution to warm audiences through partnerships, sponsorships, or list swaps. You are borrowing trust rather than trying to manufacture it.

Build the system first. Validate the economics. Then scale.

- Scaling amplifies what already exists, good and bad
- Validate conversion rate and unit economics before investing in scale

## Issue 818: The Creator's Pricing Floor
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** How to price your work without underselling it
**Preview text:** Cheap prices repel buyers.

---

Hey [First Name],

Underpricing is not humility. It is a business mistake.

Most first-time creators set prices based on what they would pay as a consumer. That is the wrong reference point. Price based on the value the buyer receives, not the cost it took you to produce.

The pricing floor is the minimum you should charge. Anything below it signals low quality to the buyer, even when the product is exceptional. Price psychology is real. A $7 guide and a $27 guide signal completely different levels of credibility, even if the content is identical.

Here is a practical rule. For a digital guide or report, the floor is $17. For a template or toolkit, the floor is $27. For a course or program, the floor is $97. These are not arbitrary. They are the price points where buyers take the product seriously enough to actually use it, and where you take the sale seriously enough to invest in the customer relationship.

Raise your prices when you have testimonials and case studies. Every piece of social proof justifies a price increase. A proven product that has helped real people is worth more than an unproven one, and your pricing should reflect that.

Test higher prices before dropping lower ones. Most creators assume they need to cut price to increase sales. Often the opposite is true. A higher price signals confidence and attracts buyers who are ready to act.

- Price above what feels comfortable until you have data proving otherwise
- Social proof earns the right to charge more

## Issue 819: Affiliate as a Passive Stream
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** How to earn from products you already recommend
**Preview text:** Recommend what you use.

---

Hey [First Name],

You are already recommending things. Tools you use. Books that changed how you think. Courses that delivered results. The question is whether you are getting paid for those recommendations.

Affiliate income is the simplest passive stream for most creators because it requires no product creation. You promote something you genuinely use and believe in. Someone buys through your link. You earn a commission.

The key word is genuinely. Affiliate marketing breaks down the moment you recommend something to earn a commission rather than because it is actually good. Your audience can feel the difference. Weak recommendations erode trust faster than they generate revenue.

Build your affiliate stack around things you use every day. Your email marketing tool. Your design software. The hosting platform your products live on. The courses that shaped your thinking. These are natural recommendations that fit into your content without feeling forced.

The best places to embed affiliate links are in tutorial content, resource pages, and product comparison posts. Someone who just learned how to do something from you is highly primed to buy the tools you recommend to do it.

Most affiliate programs pay 20 to 40 percent on digital products. A single well-placed link in a high-traffic piece of content can generate revenue for years without any additional work.

- Only recommend what you have used and trust
- Tutorial content is the highest-converting placement for affiliate links

## Issue 820: The Solo Brand Audit
**Type:** Tactical
**Subject:** Is your brand saying what you think it is?
**Preview text:** What does your brand signal?

---

Hey [First Name],

Most creators built their brand gradually. A profile here, a color there, a bio they wrote at 11pm and never changed. The result is usually a brand that evolved without intention.

A solo brand audit takes 30 minutes and tells you whether your current brand is doing its job.

Start with the impression test. Open your profile on your main platform and look at it through the eyes of someone who has never heard of you. What do they learn in the first five seconds? What problem do you solve? Who do you solve it for? If that is not immediately clear, your bio and header need work.

Next, audit your content. Scroll through your last 20 posts. Is there a consistent theme? A consistent tone? Or does it look like five different people manage the account? Consistency signals reliability. Scattered content signals that the creator is still figuring things out.

Then check your visual identity. Same colors. Same fonts. Same type of imagery. Visual consistency is not about being rigid. It is about being recognizable. People should be able to see your content in a feed and know it is yours before they read the name.

Finally, ask yourself: if someone followed you for 30 days and then a friend asked what you are about, could they answer in one sentence? That sentence is your brand.

- First five seconds should communicate your core value proposition
- Brand consistency makes you recognizable in a noisy feed

Until next time,
— The Behike Team
