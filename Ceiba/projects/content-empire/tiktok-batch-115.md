## TikTok 1: The Broke Creator Checklist
**Hook (0-2s):**
Here's the checklist that keeps creators broke. How many do you have?

**Script:**
One. You post consistently but have no product.

Two. You measure success in followers and views, not revenue and conversions.

Three. You treat every platform as an end destination instead of a funnel.

Four. You've never written a sales page, a product description, or a price with justification.

Five. You've spent more time on content strategy than on understanding what your audience actually wants to buy.

Six. You believe that if your content is good enough, money will eventually appear without you having to ask for it.

Seven. You avoid talking about your products because it feels pushy.

That last one is the most common. The belief that selling is uncomfortable. That inserting an offer into your content somehow cheapens it.

But think about it from your audience's perspective. If they trust you, they want to buy from you. Every time you post without an offer, you're withholding from them the thing that would actually help them take action.

Selling is not pushy. Selling is service, when you have something real to offer.

## TikTok 2: The One Skill That Actually Compounds
**Hook (0-2s):**
Most skills plateau. This one doesn't.

**Script:**
Here's what skill compounding actually looks like.

You learn to write clearly. That skill makes you better at communicating your ideas. Better communication makes you better at selling. Better selling makes you more money. More money gives you more time to think. More thinking gives you better ideas to write.

The cycle feeds itself. Writing is the only skill I've found that legitimately compounds into every other area of your work and life.

But there's a condition. It has to be writing for clarity, not writing for performance. Writing to figure out what you think, not to sound impressive.

The fastest way to improve this skill is to write daily, in private, about the ideas you're trying to understand. Not to publish. Just to think.

Then take the clearest version of what you discover and publish that.

Over time, your public writing becomes sharper. Your thinking becomes faster. Your ability to see through complex problems improves. And your audience grows, because clear thinking is rare and people are drawn to it.

One skill. Everything else follows.

**CTA:**
Save this. Start a private writing practice this week. 15 minutes a day.

## TikTok 3: The Content-to-Product Pipeline
**Hook (0-2s):**
Here's the exact pipeline for turning content into a product.

**Script:**
Step one. Find the pattern in your content. What problem do you keep returning to? What question does your audience keep asking? This is your product topic.

Step two. Write the definitive version of your answer. Not the 60-second version. The complete version. Everything you know about this problem and how to solve it. Don't edit yet. Just get it all down.

Step three. Structure it. What's the logical order for someone who has never thought about this? What do they need to know first? What unlocks everything else? Build the outline from the reader's journey, not your own.

Step four. Add context and tools. What templates would save them time? What frameworks would help them apply this? What examples make it concrete?

Step five. Package it. PDF, course platform, workshop, coaching program. The format matters less than the substance. Start with the simplest format and upgrade later.

Step six. Price it honestly. What is this worth to the right person? What problem does it solve and how much does that problem cost them? Price toward that value.

Your content is already doing steps one and two. You just need to finish the pipeline.

## TikTok 4: Personal Brand vs. Idea Brand
**Hook (0-2s):**
There are two types of brand. Only one of them scales without burning you out.

**Script:**
A personal brand is built on who you are. Your story, your lifestyle, your face, your personality. It scales with your visibility. When you stop showing up, it stops growing.

An idea brand is built on what you think. Your thesis, your frameworks, your perspective on how the world works. It scales with your ideas. When you stop showing up, people still share your thinking.

Personal brands are powerful but exhausting. They require constant content production, constant visibility, constant performance of the self. And they're fragile. One off-brand post, one controversy, one long absence, and the whole thing wobbles.

Idea brands are quieter but more durable. The work speaks for itself. People share the idea even when they've forgotten your name. The brand builds in the background while you're sleeping.

For solopreneurs, the ideal is a hybrid. Put your name on ideas. But make sure the ideas can stand without you in the room.

Your face gets people to click. Your ideas make them stay.

## TikTok 5: What Growth Looks Like When You Stop Chasing It
**Hook (0-2s):**
The month I stopped optimizing for growth was the month I started growing.

**Script:**
For a long time I was obsessed with metrics. Follower count, views per post, engagement rate, reach, all of it.

I was publishing based on what might perform, not what I actually believed. I was chasing trending topics instead of building my own.

The content was fine. It was just hollow. And hollow content attracts hollow followers.

Then I stopped. I started writing and posting only what I found genuinely interesting, genuinely true, genuinely useful to the specific person I was trying to help.

The metrics got worse before they got better. Views dropped. Follower growth slowed.

But something else happened. Replies from people who said they'd been waiting for content like this. DMs from people asking to buy. New followers who immediately engaged with everything.

The audience got smaller and better at the same time.

Better audiences buy. Worse audiences just watch.

The paradox of growth: the less you chase it, the more durable it becomes.
