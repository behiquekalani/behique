## Post 1 — TEXT POST (450 words)
**Topic:** AI services for small businesses

Most small businesses don't need AI strategy. They need one problem solved.

I've talked to about a dozen small business owners over the past few months, mostly food businesses, retail shops, one logistics company. Not one of them asked me about AI transformation. They asked about specific pain points.

The restaurant owner wanted to stop copy-pasting customer feedback from Google Reviews into a spreadsheet by hand every Monday morning. That was it. One task, 45 minutes a week, completely manual.

The retail shop owner had a price-matching problem. She was manually checking three competitor websites every week to adjust her pricing. Two hours, every Friday, from her phone.

The logistics coordinator was drowning in reply emails. Same questions from customers every day: "Where is my package?" He was typing out answers manually because the automated system they had was too rigid to handle the variations in how people asked.

None of these are complex AI problems. They're workflow problems. The AI part is a tool, not the solution.

For the restaurant owner: a simple scraper that pulls Google Reviews into Airtable nightly, with a Claude prompt that categorizes feedback by theme. Total setup time: 3 hours. She got those 45 minutes back every week.

For the retail shop: an n8n workflow that pulls competitor pricing via a scraper three times a week and outputs a comparison table to a shared Google Sheet. Setup: 4 hours. No more Friday manual work.

For the logistics coordinator: a GPT-4 integration hooked into their email through Zapier. It reads the incoming message, identifies the question type, pulls the tracking info from their system, and drafts a reply for human review. He clicks send instead of writing. Setup: 5 hours.

None of this is revolutionary. It's plumbing. But for a business owner working 60-hour weeks, getting 2 hours back per week is a meaningful quality-of-life change. And it's a $500 to $1,500 service because the implementation knowledge has value even when the concept is simple.

The actual insight here is about market positioning. If you want to sell AI services to small businesses, don't lead with AI. Lead with the problem. Ask: what do you do manually every week that you wish you didn't have to do? That question opens doors that "AI automation services" doesn't.

The business owners I've talked to are not scared of AI. They're skeptical that it will work for their specific situation. Show them it works for their specific situation and the sale closes fast.

## Post 3 — TEXT POST (500 words)
**Topic:** Building in Puerto Rico

I'm building a business from Puerto Rico and I want to talk about what that actually means.

It doesn't mean I have a startup with investors and a co-working space and a product roadmap on a whiteboard. It means I'm a computer engineering student building things between classes with a laptop and a stable enough WiFi connection.

Puerto Rico gets brought up in business conversations usually in two contexts: tax incentives for wealthy transplants, or post-hurricane recovery narratives. Neither of those is my story. I'm from here. I grew up here. My family is here. My motivation is rooted here in a way that no pitch deck can capture.

What Puerto Rico actually offers as a building environment is a specific combination of constraints that I think have made me better at this, not worse.

The market is small. That means you can't rely on local volume. Everything I build has to work for people who are not in Puerto Rico. That discipline shapes every product decision I make. It forces distribution thinking from day one.

The professional network in tech is smaller. That means you can't rely on warm introductions to get clients. You have to build a public presence. Content, proof of work, a track record that speaks for itself. That's forced me into content creation and personal brand building earlier than I would have done otherwise.

The economic reality here is that most people are not spending discretionary income on digital products. My buyers are almost entirely off the island. So I learned early that the internet is the market, not the geography.

There's a work ethic embedded in Puerto Rican culture that doesn't get talked about enough in the business press. We're used to solving problems with fewer resources. Power goes out, you figure out the backup plan. Supply chains break, you find another route. Internet is slow, you download what you need in advance. These are not disadvantages in entrepreneurship. They're the exact muscle you need to build when everything doesn't work as planned.

I'm not romanticizing poverty or hurricanes or infrastructure problems. Those are real costs that real people pay. I'm saying that the constraint-solving mindset they produce is a genuine competitive advantage in a world where most builders have access to every resource and still don't ship.

The other thing worth naming: I'm building in Spanish and English simultaneously. My personal brand, my content, my customer service, my product descriptions. Bilingual is not a liability in the creator economy. The Spanish-speaking digital market is large and underserved by quality digital products. That's an opportunity, not a detail.

I don't know what the business looks like in 5 years. I know it started here, in Puerto Rico, at a kitchen table, between exams. That origin matters to me. It shapes why I'm doing it and not just what I'm doing.

## Post 5 — TEXT POST (550 words)
**Topic:** n8n automation case studies

I want to document two n8n workflows I built recently because I think they illustrate what automation actually looks like in practice for a solo operator.

Case 1: Content repurposing pipeline.

The problem: I was creating long-form content, usually a guide or a detailed post, and then manually adapting it for different platforms. TikTok script, Instagram caption, LinkedIn post, email newsletter. Same core content, four different rewrites, done manually each time.

The n8n workflow: a Google Doc trigger. When I mark a doc as "ready to repurpose" in the title, n8n picks it up. It sends the content to Claude with four different prompts, one for each platform format. It saves each output to a corresponding folder in Google Drive organized by date and platform. It also sends me a Telegram notification with a summary of what was generated.

The result: what took me 90 minutes per piece now takes about 8 minutes, most of which is me reviewing and editing the outputs. The quality is good enough that I edit maybe 20% of the output per piece.

Case 2: eBay sold item tracker.

The problem: I was manually checking my eBay sold items, copying the details into a tracking spreadsheet, and then updating my sourcing notes based on what sold and what didn't. This was about 20 minutes per day.

The n8n workflow: pulls from the eBay API every morning at 7am. Logs all new sold items to an Airtable base with: item name, sale price, category, and days listed. A second automation runs a weekly summary every Sunday: best-performing categories, average days to sell, total revenue. Sends the summary to my Telegram.

The result: I have 3 months of clean sales data now that I use to make sourcing decisions. Before this, I was making those decisions on memory and instinct. The data has changed what I buy. I'm spending more time in board games and ceramics based on actual sell-through rates I can see.

What I want people to take from these two examples is the difference between automation as a concept and automation as a practice.

As a concept, automation is about replacing human work with machine work. That framing is accurate but it undersells the real benefit. The real benefit is data accumulation over time. When a workflow runs every day for 90 days, it builds a record that you could never build manually because you would never find the time or consistency to track it manually.

Three months of daily eBay data is a competitive advantage over every seller who is running on memory and intuition. I have it because a 4-hour workflow build gave me an automatic tracker that runs without my involvement.

n8n is free to self-host. The learning curve is real but not steep. The first workflow takes a weekend. The second one takes an afternoon. By the fifth workflow you're building things in an hour that used to take a day of manual work.

If you're a solo operator doing any kind of repetitive digital work, the return on learning n8n is one of the highest I've found in the last year.

## Post 7 — TEXT POST (480 words)
**Topic:** The solopreneur economy

The solopreneur economy is real and the numbers are starting to reflect it.

One-person businesses generating $100K+ per year are not exceptional anymore. They're a documented category. And the infrastructure that makes them possible has gotten dramatically better in the last 3 years.

What changed: AI reduced the skill tax on content creation, code generation, customer service, and research. Tools like n8n and Zapier reduced the cost of building automations that previously required a developer. Platforms like Gumroad, Stripe, and Substack made payment processing and distribution accessible to individuals. Cloud computing made hosting affordable at small scale.

The result is that one person can now operate at the output level of a small team, if they're strategic about what they automate and what they do personally.

But here's the thing I want to push back on in the solopreneur content space: most of the advice is about working less. Passive income. Build once, earn forever. Four-hour workweeks.

That framing is not wrong but it's incomplete. The reality is that building a solo business requires intense focus and consistent output for at least the first 12 to 18 months. The passive income part comes after the active building phase. Skipping the building phase because you saw someone's revenue screenshot is the fastest route to building nothing.

The solo business model I'm building is built around three income streams: digital products that sell asynchronously, services I deliver directly to clients, and content that builds an audience for both. None of these is passive in year one. All three compound over time if you stay consistent.

The other thing worth naming: solo doesn't mean isolated. The best solo operators I've observed have a small network of peers they share openly with. Not competitors in a zero-sum sense. Builders who are solving adjacent problems and can give you honest feedback that a stranger on the internet won't.

For me that peer network is small. Three people. One other student builder in a different country, one freelancer who does client work similar to mine, one person further along the path than I am. We share numbers, strategies, and honest assessments of what's working. That's not a team. It's a small accountability structure.

Solo means you own everything and you answer to yourself. It doesn't mean you work alone in the dark without any external input.

The solopreneur economy is expanding and I think it's one of the most important structural shifts in how work and value creation are organized. But the version of it that actually works looks like sustained, focused building, not passive income fantasies.

If you're building something solo right now, the most useful thing you can do today is identify one person who is 12 months ahead of you and ask them what they wish they'd done differently.

## Post 10 — SHORT POST (140 words)
**Topic:** The student entrepreneur path

I used to think I needed to wait until after graduation to build something real.

Then I realized that framing was a productivity trap. There is no post-graduation moment where building becomes easier or the stakes lower or the time more available. The constraints change. The obligations change. The free window before full-time employment is narrower than it looks from inside college.

The skills that actually matter for building a business, seeing market gaps, executing without perfect information, managing your own energy and focus, are not taught in a curriculum. They're built through repetition.

The degree matters. Finish it. But the degree and the business are not competing for the same resource. They're competing for the same hours.

Decide how you want to use yours.
