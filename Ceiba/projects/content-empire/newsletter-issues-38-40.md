## ISSUE 38

**Subject line:** The 3-minute morning check-in that changed how I work

**Preheader:** Your mood is data. Here is what to do with it.

---

Hey,

There is a thing most productivity systems ignore entirely: how you actually feel before you start working.

They give you task lists, time blocks, prioritization frameworks. None of them ask whether you slept well or if something is sitting heavy in your chest this morning. They assume you are a machine that starts at the same baseline every day.

You are not. Neither am I.

---

### Why Mood Is a Productivity Variable

Here is the thesis: your emotional state is not separate from your output. It is one of the main inputs.

When I started tracking my mood every morning, the first thing I noticed was how often I was trying to do deep focused work while I was in an anxious, scattered, or depleted state. Not because I was lazy. Because I had no system for noticing the mismatch.

High-focus cognitive work done in an anxious state produces work you will rewrite tomorrow. Creative work done while exhausted produces nothing you will actually use. The wrong task in the wrong state is not productivity. It is theater.

A 3-minute morning check-in solves this by making the mismatch visible before it wastes three hours.

---

### The Actual Check-In (What I Do)

This takes three minutes. No journaling app required. Paper works fine.

**Question 1: What is my energy level right now?**
Scale of 1 to 10. One number. No explanation needed yet.

**Question 2: What is my primary emotional state?**
Not "how do I want to feel." What is actually present right now. Anxious. Calm. Excited. Heavy. Irritated. Flat. Pick the closest word.

**Question 3: What is the one thing I'm avoiding today?**
This one matters more than the other two. The thing you avoid first is usually the thing that shapes your whole day by its absence.

That is the whole check-in. Three questions, three answers, three minutes.

---

### How to Use the Data

The check-in is useless if it just produces a log. Here is the decision it should drive:

If your energy is low and your state is anxious or scattered, do not sit down and try to write or code. That is a misallocation. Do admin tasks, review work, light communication. Save the deep work for when you are actually capable of it.

If your energy is moderate and your state is calm or neutral, this is your best window for focused output. Block it. Protect it. Do not fill it with email.

If your energy is high and your emotional state is excited or clear, this is a creative window. Use it for generating, not organizing.

The one thing you are avoiding: that item goes on your short list for the day, not because you have to do it immediately, but because naming it prevents it from quietly draining your attention all day from the background.

---

### Why This Works for ADHD Specifically

If you have ADHD, your brain already struggles with transition and state management. The check-in creates an artificial pause between "woke up" and "started working" that your brain does not naturally produce on its own.

It also gives you a concrete moment to recognize that you are scattered before you have already spent 40 minutes on something unproductive. That recognition is not automatic. The ritual makes it automatic.

Three minutes is not a burden. It is less time than you spend staring at your phone after your alarm.

## ISSUE 39

**Subject line:** What 90 days of mood tracking taught me about my own brain

**Preheader:** The patterns I found were not what I expected.

---

Hey,

I tracked my mood and energy every morning for 90 days straight. I used a simple system: energy 1-10, emotional state in one word, the thing I was avoiding, and the type of work I planned to do.

Here is what the data showed.

---

### Pattern 1: My Best Work Happens in a Narrow Window

I assumed I was a morning person. The data said I am a late-morning person.

My highest-quality focused work consistently happened between 9:30 and 11:30 AM. Before that, I was present but not sharp. After that, the quality declined fast, even when I felt like I was still in flow.

If I started important work at 8 AM instead of 9:30 AM, the output looked like work but it had fundamental problems I would not catch until later. Wrong assumptions. Sloppy logic. Decisions I would reverse.

The lesson: I was wasting the early morning on the tasks that required the most from me, during the window where I was giving the least.

---

### Pattern 2: Anxiety Is a Warning, Not a Character Flaw

I tracked "anxious" as my morning emotional state 31 times out of 90 days. That is more than a third of my mornings.

For a long time I would just push through those days and try to work normally. The data showed that on anxious mornings, my task completion rate dropped significantly, and the work I did complete needed substantially more revision.

What actually helped: on anxious mornings, the first task I worked on needed to be small and completable in under 20 minutes. A quick win that lowered the nervous system activation before I tried to do anything complex.

Anxiety is not laziness. It is a signal that your system is dysregulated. The fix is regulation before output, not more pressure.

---

### Pattern 3: The Avoidance Item Predicted My Day

Out of 90 days, I tracked the one thing I was avoiding each morning. Then I tracked whether I actually did it that day.

Days when I did the avoided task before noon: above-average output, higher satisfaction at end of day, less mental fatigue.

Days when I skipped the avoided task: lower overall output, more context-switching, a consistent sense of not having finished anything real.

The thing I was avoiding was usually not difficult. It was uncomfortable. There is a difference. Difficult things take skill. Uncomfortable things take a decision to start.

The check-in made me see that difference instead of treating both as equally optional.

---

### Pattern 4: Creative and Analytical Work Live in Different Bodies

This one surprised me.

When I tracked creative output days versus analytical output days, they correlated with different emotional states more consistently than I expected.

Creative work, writing, ideation, strategy, went well when my state was "open," "curious," or "light." Even mildly excited worked well.

Analytical work, code, finance, systems, went better when I was "calm" or "focused." Not excited. Not stimulated. Just quiet and clear.

I had been scheduling creative and analytical work at random, based on what the calendar said. The data showed I was probably producing about 60-70% of what I was capable of, just by ignoring the state variable.

---

### What 90 Days Buys You

Thirty days of data is a sample. Sixty days is a trend. Ninety days is a picture of your actual brain.

You will find things in there that no productivity framework teaches you because they are specific to you. The patterns I found are not universal. Yours will be different.

But you cannot find yours without the data. That is the only point.

## ISSUE 40

**Subject line:** Calm and focused are not the same thing

**Preheader:** Different states need different tools. Here is the map.

---

Hey,

People talk about "getting into focus" like it is one thing.

It is not. There are at least two distinct productive states, and they require different environments, different inputs, and different music. Conflating them is one of the main reasons people sit at their desks for four hours and produce almost nothing.

---

### The Difference Between Calm and Focused

Calm is a low-arousal positive state. Your nervous system is settled. You feel at ease. Cognitively, you are available but not activated. The processing is gentle.

Focused is a moderate-to-high-arousal positive state. Your attention is narrowed and directed. There is a mild pressure, not stress, but intentional tension toward a target. The processing is active.

Both are productive. They are productive for different things.

Calm is better for: reviewing work, making decisions that require perspective, writing that needs nuance, having real conversations, learning complex material that requires absorption rather than speed.

Focused is better for: producing output under a deadline, building, coding, drafting at pace, getting through a high volume of tasks in sequence.

If you try to do focused work in a calm state, you will drift. If you try to do calm work in a focused state, you will rush it and miss things.

---

### How to Get to Each State on Purpose

The key word is "on purpose." Most people wait for a state to arrive. The better approach is to design for it.

**To reach calm:**

Reduce inputs before you start. No notifications, no social media, no unread messages. Give yourself 5 minutes with no stimulus at all. Slow your breathing. The goal is to lower your arousal level, not to motivate yourself.

Music: ambient or instrumental. Low tempo. No lyrics, or lyrics in a language you do not understand. Binaural beats in the delta or theta range work for some people. Brian Eno's ambient catalog is a consistent recommendation for a reason.

Environment: natural light if possible. Low background noise or genuine silence. Comfortable physical position without lying down.

**To reach focused:**

You need a slightly elevated arousal state. The tool for this is a constraint with stakes, even artificial ones. Set a 25-minute timer and name the specific deliverable before you start.

Music: mid-tempo instrumental, lo-fi, or electronic with rhythm. The rhythm matters. It serves as an external metronome for your attention. Lyrics work for some people in focused mode, but they increase cognitive load. Know which category you are in.

Environment: some people focus better with low ambient noise, the classic coffee shop effect. What you are looking for is a signal-to-noise ratio that keeps your attention mildly engaged without pulling it off task.

---

### The Third State Nobody Talks About: Regulated

There is a state that has to come before either calm or focused can work. Call it regulated.

Regulated means your nervous system is not in a stress response. If you are dysregulated, meaning anxious, depleted, or emotionally flooded, neither calm nor focused is accessible. You will try to work and produce nothing, or produce things you regret.

The fastest path to regulated is not motivation. It is physiology. Short walk. Cold water on your face. Five minutes of box breathing. Physical reset before cognitive demand.

For ADHD specifically: dysregulation is more frequent and more disruptive than it is for neurotypical people. Building a quick regulation protocol into your morning is not optional. It is foundational.

---

### The System

Map your tasks to the right state before you start the day.

Look at your task list. Mark each item: C for tasks that require calm, F for tasks that require focused. Schedule C tasks in your natural low-arousal windows. Schedule F tasks in your natural activation windows. Use the music and environment protocols accordingly.

This is not complicated. Most people have never done it because nobody told them the distinction mattered.

It matters a lot.
