If you have ADHD and you keep failing your own systems, it is probably not a motivation problem. It is a friction problem.

## Post 14 | ADHD Productivity and Systems

**Caption:**
A task list assumes you will read it, feel motivated by what is on it, and start the right thing at the right time.

For most ADHD brains, that chain breaks at step two.

Triggers work differently. A trigger is an if-then condition. If I sit down at my desk, then I open the one document for today's work before I open anything else.

The trigger does not require a decision. The decision was made when you set up the trigger. Now it is just a habit executing.

I use three. One in the morning linked to coffee. One after lunch linked to a specific playlist. One in the evening linked to closing every tab that is not tomorrow's prep.

None of this is original. It is standard habit stacking research applied to an ADHD context. The difference is knowing your brain actually needs the structure, not just the list.

## Post 19 | Building in Public

**Caption:**
Last month I deployed a version of BehiqueBot that had a bug in the voice transcription pipeline. About 15 percent of voice messages were being dropped silently. No error. No log. Just gone.

What I learned: silent failures are worse than loud ones. A crash tells you something is wrong. A silent drop just makes you think the feature is not being used.

Instrument everything you ship. Especially the parts you think are fine.

**Caption:**
Most productivity advice frames the Pomodoro technique as a focus tool. Set the timer, work for 25 minutes, stay on task.

That is half of it.

The part that actually helps ADHD brains is the back half. When the timer ends, you have permission to stop. Full stop. No guilt. No "just five more minutes."

ADHD time blindness means stopping is often harder than starting. Once you are in a task, the internal signal that tells you to take a break is unreliable. You either cannot stop or you stop too soon.

The external timer replaces that internal signal. It says stop regardless of how the task feels.

The rest is real. The start is deliberate. Neither one is optional.

If Pomodoro has not worked for you before, check whether you were actually taking the break or just resetting the timer.

## Post 23 | Claude/AI Coding

**Hook:** The best prompt for code is not a request. It is a spec.

**Caption:**
Most people prompt Claude for code by describing what they want: "write me a script that does X."

That produces code that might work. It also produces a lot of code that kind of works but breaks on the second real input.

The people who consistently get clean, working code write a spec before they write a prompt.

The spec answers four things. What is the input. What is the expected output. What are the edge cases that matter. What does failure look like and how should it be handled.

That is not more work. That is the work you were going to do anyway when the first version broke. You are just doing it in the right order.

Give Claude a real spec and the first version usually runs. Give it a vague request and you are debugging indefinitely.
