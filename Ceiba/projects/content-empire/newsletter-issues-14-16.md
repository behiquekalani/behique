## ISSUE 14

**Subject: I offered free AI audits. Here is what I found.**

---

Hey,

A few weeks ago I posted that I was offering free AI audits to small business owners. Fill out a short form, I would look at their business, tell them exactly where AI could save them time or money.

I expected maybe five responses. I got more than twenty.

Here is what I found.

---

### This Week's Build

Almost nobody had an automation problem. They had a clarity problem.

When I asked people what they wanted to automate, most of them said "everything" or "I don't know, you tell me." That sounds like an AI question. It is actually a business clarity question. You cannot automate a process you have not defined.

The businesses that got the most value from the audits were the ones that already knew where they were losing time. They just needed someone to map the AI tools to the specific bottleneck.

The pattern I kept seeing:

1. Customer intake was manual. Every business was doing something by hand that a simple form plus a trigger could handle. Jotform, Tally, or Google Forms connected to a notification system. Fifteen minutes to set up, hours per week saved.

2. Social content was reactive, not scheduled. Most people were posting when they remembered, not on a schedule. Not a creativity problem. A system problem. A basic content calendar with a two-week buffer solves this.

3. The follow-up gap was the biggest revenue leak. Most businesses were not following up with leads at all, or following up once and stopping. An email sequence with three to five touches, sent automatically after someone fills out a form, would have changed their close rate.

None of these required expensive software. Most required free tiers of tools they had already heard of.

What surprised me most: the ROI on small, boring automations is higher than the ROI on anything flashy. Nobody needed an AI employee. They needed a form that sent an email.

---

### Tool of the Week

**Tally.so** - free form builder that connects to most automation tools without needing Zapier. Simple to set up, clean interface. I used it for the audit intake form. The free plan covers most small business use cases.

Free. tally.so.

---

### The Number

**3** - the average number of hours per week recovered when a small business owner fixes their intake and follow-up process with basic automation. That number comes from the audits I ran. Not scientific. But it held consistently across different business types.

---

### What I'm Thinking About

The free audit format taught me more about what people actually need than any amount of market research.

If you are building anything in the AI tools space right now, do the audits. Talk to the people. The gap between what people think they need and what they actually need is where the product is.

## ISSUE 15

**Subject: The quiz that tells you which AI tool to start with**

---

Hey,

I kept getting the same question from different people: "I want to use AI in my business but I don't know where to start. What should I use?"

My honest answer was always the same. It depends. What does your week look like? What takes the most time? What do you hate doing?

Those questions felt like a conversation. So I built a quiz.

---

### This Week's Build

The Product Finder Quiz is now live on the site.

It asks seven questions. It takes about two minutes. At the end, it tells you which Behike product fits your situation and why.

Here is how it works and why I built it this way instead of a product page.

Product pages show you everything at once. Most people do not know which product applies to them. They scan the list, nothing clicks, they leave. The signal gets lost in the noise.

A quiz removes the scanning problem. You answer questions about your actual situation, and the quiz surfaces the one thing that fits. Less cognitive load. Clearer recommendation.

The quiz logic is built around four variables:

**Time.** How many hours per week are you losing to tasks that feel like they should not require a human? If the answer is more than five, the automation audit is the starting point.

**Type of work.** Is the friction in content creation, client management, research, or internal operations? Each category maps to a different tool set.

**Technical comfort.** Are you comfortable copying a workflow from a template, or do you want something fully built and explained? This splits the product recommendations into two paths.

**Stage.** Are you pre-revenue, early revenue, or established? The tools that matter at each stage are different. A pre-revenue solo builder needs different things than someone doing $5K a month trying to remove themselves from repetitive tasks.

The quiz ends with a specific product recommendation, a one-sentence explanation of why, and a direct link.

No email required to see your result.

---

### Tool of the Week

**Typeform** - the tool I used to build the quiz. The free plan allows one quiz with logic jumps. Logic jumps are what make the quiz feel personalized. Without them, it is just a form.

The free plan is enough for a simple product finder quiz. typeform.com.

---

### The Number

**67%** - the percentage of visitors to a product page who leave without clicking anything, based on typical e-commerce conversion data. A quiz converts better because it gives people a clear path instead of a list of options.

---

### What I'm Thinking About

Quizzes are underused in small business content. They feel like a gimmick until you build one and realize how much better the conversion math is.

The reason: a quiz asks about the person before it talks about the product. Most marketing does the opposite. Product first, person second.

Asking someone about their situation before showing them a product is just good sales process. A quiz is a way to automate that conversation at scale.

## ISSUE 16

**Subject: 100 days of building in public**

---

Hey,

One hundred days ago I started building in public. That means sharing what I was building, what was working, what was not, and the actual numbers behind it.

This is the honest version of what happened.

---

### What Got Shipped

Ten products are now live on Gumroad. That includes guides, templates, and the ADHD Budget Playbook.

Seven newsletter issues went out on schedule. Three landed late.

Two Instagram accounts are active. @behikeai went from zero to just under a thousand followers. @kalaniandrez is slower. Still growing.

The AI Audit offer ran for two weeks. Twenty-three audits completed.

One quiz is live. Built in the last two weeks.

---

### What Worked

The personal framing worked. Issues that started with a specific story from my own life performed better than issues that started with a principle or a list. The 47-category budget story was the highest-open issue so far.

Short captions on Instagram outperformed longer ones. Posts where the thesis was in the first line and the rest of the caption expanded on it got more saves than posts where the hook was vague.

Free audits generated more direct trust than any amount of content. Doing actual work for people, for free, with no pitch, moved the needle on sales faster than posting.

---

### What Failed

I planned to post every day on both accounts. That lasted about three weeks before I was running on recycled content and the quality dropped. I switched to three times a week and quality went back up.

The email list growth was slower than I projected. I expected to be at five hundred subscribers by now. The current number is lower than that. The quiz and the free audit offer are the two things moving it most consistently. Organic posting alone was not enough.

One product did not sell at all in the first month. I have not pulled it. But I have not promoted it either. That is a decision I need to make.

---

### The Honest Numbers

Products live: 10
Products with at least one sale: 7
Newsletter open rate: around 40%
Instagram followers combined: under 2,000
Email subscribers: fewer than 500

None of those numbers are impressive on their own. The direction is right. The pace is slower than the projections I made at the start.

Building in public is useful for two reasons. It creates accountability. And it forces you to be honest about what is actually happening instead of what you planned to happen.

---

### What Comes Next

The email list is the focus for the next thirty days. Every piece of content, every free resource, every audit points toward the email signup. That is the one metric I am optimizing for.

The product line is stable. No new products until the existing ones are converting consistently.
