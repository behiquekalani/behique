## Post 1 — You Are Underpricing. Here Is How to Know.

**Hook:** If pricing your product felt uncomfortable, you might have priced it right.

**Slide 1:**
Most solopreneurs underprice their products.
Not slightly. Significantly.

Here is how to tell if you are one of them.

**Slide 2:**
Signs you are underpricing:
- You based the price on what you would pay, not what the buyer will pay
- You priced it low to avoid the fear of rejection
- You are apologizing for the price in your copy
- You feel like the product is "not good enough" to charge more

**Slide 3:**
The underpricing trap:
Low price does not mean more sales.
Often it means fewer, because buyers associate low price with low quality.

A $9 product and a $47 product can require the same amount of explanation.
The $47 one attracts buyers who actually implement it.

**Slide 4:**
How to find the right price:
Ask: What is the outcome worth to my buyer?
Not: What did it cost me to make?

A guide that saves someone 10 hours of research is worth $47 even if it took you 4 hours to write.

**Slide 5:**
The confidence test:
Write out why your product is worth its price in 3 sentences.
If you cannot do this, you do not believe in the price yet.
Fix the belief before you fix the number.

**Slide 6:**
Your price is a signal. Make sure it is sending the right one.
@behikeai — pricing frameworks for digital products.

**Caption:**
Underpricing is one of the most common and most costly mistakes solopreneurs make when launching digital products. It feels like a safe choice. It feels like it removes the risk of rejection. In reality, it creates a different set of problems that are harder to see.

A low price implies low value. Buyers who find a product at $7 often assume it cannot be substantive. They buy it on impulse, implement it casually, and do not get results because they did not take it seriously. Then they do not recommend it. Your price shapes how buyers engage with your product.

Higher prices attract buyers who are more invested in solving the problem. They read more carefully. They implement more thoroughly. They get better results. Better results become testimonials, word of mouth, and referrals. Your pricing strategy affects your outcomes in ways that go far beyond the revenue per unit.

Price based on the value of the outcome, not the time it cost you to produce. A well-placed shortcut is worth more than a comprehensive textbook. Know what problem you solve and price accordingly.

## Post 2 — The Psychology of Pricing: Why $47 Outsells $20

**Hook:** The cheaper option does not always win. Here is why.

**Slide 1:**
The cheaper option does not always win.
Pricing psychology explains why.

**Slide 2:**
Perceived value vs. actual value:
Buyers cannot evaluate quality before they buy.
So they use price as a proxy for quality.

$20 = "probably basic."
$47 = "this person did real work here."

Same product. Different framing.

**Slide 3:**
The $97 anchor effect:
List a bundle at $97, then offer a single product at $47.
$47 feels like a deal.

Without the anchor, $47 feels like the full price.
With the anchor, it feels discounted.
Same number. Different psychology.

**Slide 4:**
Odd numbers outperform round numbers:
$47 converts better than $50.
$97 converts better than $100.

The theory: round numbers feel like list prices. Odd numbers feel like they have been calculated for a reason.

**Slide 5:**
The confidence premium:
Higher-priced products imply the seller believes in the product.
Buyers pick up on that signal.

If you do not believe your product is worth $47, they will sense it in your copy.
Price with confidence. Write with conviction.

**Slide 6:**
Understanding pricing psychology is part of building a real business.
@behikeai — the digital product playbook.

**Caption:**
Pricing is not just a number. It is a communication tool. The price you set sends a message to potential buyers about what to expect, how seriously they should take the product, and how much of themselves they should invest in using it.

Most people set prices by comparing to competitors and going slightly lower, or by doing simple cost-plus math without understanding how buyers actually make purchasing decisions. Both approaches leave money on the table because they treat pricing as math rather than communication.

The buyer looking at your product is not comparing it to its cost of production. They are comparing it to the size of the problem it solves. A guide that saves them five hours of research every month is not worth $9. It is worth $9 per use, compounding over time. The price you set should reflect how you understand that math.

Learn basic pricing psychology. Test different price points with small audiences before committing. Watch your conversion rate and your customer quality at each price point. The data will tell you more than any theory about what your specific audience responds to.

## Post 3 — Tiered Pricing: How to Sell to Three Buyers at Once

**Hook:** One product. Three price points. Three times the revenue opportunity.

**Slide 1:**
One product.
Three price points.
Three times the revenue opportunity.

Tiered pricing explained.

**Slide 2:**
The problem with one price:
Every buyer has a different budget and a different level of need.
A single price leaves money on the table at both ends.

Too low: loses the buyer willing to pay more.
Too high: excludes the buyer who would have paid less.

**Slide 3:**
A tiered pricing structure:

Tier 1 ($17-27): The guide alone
Digital download. Core content. Good for buyers testing the waters.

Tier 2 ($47-67): Guide + templates + bonus
Adds implementation tools. The best value tier. Most people pick this one.

Tier 3 ($97-147): Everything + direct access or a workshop recording
Adds personalization or community. For buyers who want more than information.

**Slide 4:**
Why buyers pick Tier 2:
The middle option feels smart.
Not the cheapest (risky), not the most expensive (overkill).

Designers call this the Goldilocks effect.
Price your core offer in the middle on purpose.

**Slide 5:**
How to build tiers from one product:
- Take your existing product
- Tier 1: the PDF alone
- Tier 2: add a Notion template or worksheet
- Tier 3: add a recorded walkthrough or email access

You are not building three products. You are bundling assets you already have.

**Slide 6:**
Most buyers will choose the middle tier. Design your middle tier first.
@behikeai — the product strategy that earns more from the same audience.

**Caption:**
Tiered pricing is one of the highest-leverage pricing strategies available to solopreneurs selling digital products, and it requires no additional products to implement. You are repackaging what you already have into bundles that serve buyers at different levels of commitment and budget.

The psychological mechanism behind it is well-documented. When given three options, most buyers avoid the extremes. The cheapest option signals low quality or low commitment. The most expensive option triggers spending anxiety. The middle option feels like the balanced choice.

This means your core offer, the thing you most want people to buy, should occupy the middle tier. Price your basic option below it and your premium option above it. The premium option serves a real audience segment and makes the middle option look reasonable. The basic option provides an entry point for buyers who are not yet ready to commit fully.

The practical setup on Gumroad or Payhip is simple. You are not creating three separate products. You are creating three bundles with different combinations of assets you already have. Start with what you have. Build the tiers from it.

## Post 4 — Why Free Does Not Build a Business

**Hook:** Giving away free content builds an audience. It does not build a business.

**Slide 1:**
Free content builds an audience.
It does not build a business.

Understanding the difference matters.

**Slide 2:**
What free does well:
- Attracts people to your brand
- Demonstrates your expertise
- Builds trust before asking for money
- Creates an email list

**Slide 3:**
What free does not do:
- Generate revenue
- Filter for serious buyers
- Create the sustainability to keep producing content
- Signal to buyers that your paid work is premium

**Slide 4:**
The free trap:
You post great free content for months.
You build an engaged audience.
You launch a product.
Nobody buys.

Why? You trained your audience to expect free.
You never made selling a normal part of your presence.

**Slide 5:**
The fix:
Sell from day one, even if what you are selling is cheap.
A $9 lead product teaches your audience that you sell things.
It prepares them for the $47 and $97 products coming later.

**Slide 6:**
Free is a strategy. Selling is the business.
@behikeai — build both with intention.

**Caption:**
There is a version of the "provide value first" advice that is genuinely useful. Build trust before you ask for money. Demonstrate competence before you make claims. Earn the attention before you spend it. All of that is true and important.

But there is a distorted version of that advice that keeps solopreneurs in a permanent generosity loop where they produce high-quality free content indefinitely without ever asking for anything in return. This is not generosity. It is avoidance.

Not selling is a way of avoiding the vulnerability of having your product judged. If you never launch, you never fail. But you also never build a business.

The resolution is straightforward: sell from the beginning. Not aggressively, not constantly, but consistently. If 20% of your posts mention a product, your audience knows you are a creator who sells things. When you launch something significant, they are not surprised. They expected it. They were waiting for you to build something worth buying.

Free content earns the right to sell. But you have to actually sell.

## Post 5 — Raise Your Prices. Here Is How to Do It Without Losing Buyers.

**Hook:** Raising prices is one of the best business decisions you can make. Here is how to do it right.

**Slide 1:**
Raising prices is uncomfortable.
It is also one of the best decisions you can make for your business.

Here is how to do it without losing buyers.

**Slide 2:**
When to raise prices:
- You have at least 20 sales at the current price
- The reviews or feedback are consistently positive
- You have added more value (templates, updates, bonuses) since launch
- Your audience has grown since the original price was set

Any one of these is enough. All four make it easy.

**Slide 3:**
How to announce a price increase:
"The price of [product] is going up to $[new price] on [date]. If you want it at the current price, grab it before then."

That is the whole announcement.
You do not need to justify it. You do not need to apologize.

**Slide 4:**
The urgency effect:
A price increase deadline is one of the most effective organic urgency drivers.
It is honest. It is not manufactured scarcity.
The deadline is real. The response is real.

**Slide 5:**
What happens after you raise prices:
- Revenue per sale increases immediately
- Some buyers who were on the fence buy before the deadline
- Total volume might drop slightly but revenue usually rises
- The buyer quality often improves

**Slide 6:**
You built something valuable. Charge accordingly.
@behikeai — the pricing and product strategy playbook.

**Caption:**
Most solopreneurs set a launch price and then never revisit it. The product gets better over time, the audience gets larger, the creator gets more skilled, and the price stays the same. This is a slow leak in your revenue model.

Prices should be reviewed regularly. Not arbitrarily increased, but evaluated against the value the product is delivering and the market conditions you are operating in. If you have added a template pack, updated the content, or received consistent five-star feedback, the product is worth more now than it was at launch.

The announcement of a price increase has a secondary benefit beyond the revenue impact: it signals that your products have staying power. You are not selling a one-time launch. You are building a catalog that grows in value over time. Buyers who see that you raise prices on quality products will act faster on future launches because they know the early price is the best price.

Raise your prices when you have earned it. Do it cleanly, without apology, with a clear date. Watch what happens.
