**Account:** @behikeai
**Format:** Carousel (5 slides)

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
Most people track income.

Almost nobody tracks cash flow.

Those are two different numbers. For solopreneurs, the difference is the whole game.

**Slide 2 . Income statement vs cash flow:**
An income statement tells you what you earned.

Revenue minus expenses equals profit. Simple. Most people stop here.

Cash flow tells you when money actually moves. When did the client pay. When did the bill hit. What is the balance on a given day.

You can be profitable on paper and broke in practice. That is a cash flow problem, not an income problem.

**Slide 3 . The solopreneur version of this problem:**
You closed a $2,000 client in January.

They pay net-30. Your tools renew on the 15th. Your rent is due on the 1st.

On paper: profitable. In practice: you are short on the 1st and the 15th before the $2,000 lands.

That is not a revenue problem. That is a timing problem. Cash flow tracking is how you see it coming.

**Slide 4 . What to track:**
Monthly income, line by line, with expected payment date.

Fixed costs, with exact due dates.

Variable costs, tracked weekly.

The gap between your lowest projected balance and your next income. That gap is your risk window.

Most people have no idea what that number is. Until something breaks.

## POST 3 . I Calculated My Real Hourly Rate. It Was Lower Than I Thought. (Carousel)
**Account:** @kalaniandrez
**Format:** Carousel (5 slides)

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
I calculated my real hourly rate.

It was lower than the number I had in my head. By a lot.

Here is the formula and where most people get it wrong.

**Slide 2 . The formula:**
Net income divided by hours worked.

That is it. Two numbers.

The problems are in how people calculate each one.

**Slide 3 . Mistake 1: Using gross income:**
Most people divide by their gross income. Before taxes.

That is not what you actually made. That is a number that belongs to the government.

Net income is what hits your account after taxes, fees, and platform cuts. Use that number. It is lower. It is the real one.

**Slide 4 . Mistake 2: Undercounting hours:**
People count client hours. They do not count admin hours.

The time you spent invoicing. The hour you spent fixing a technical issue. The 30 minutes you spent on emails that had nothing to do with the deliverable.

They do not count tool cost hours either. If you spent $150/month on software to serve a client, that is real cost. Convert it to hours at your effective rate and add it to the denominator.

**Slide 5 . What to do with the number:**
Once you have the real rate, three things change.

You know which clients are worth keeping. You know which services are actually profitable. You know what your minimum hourly target is before saying yes to anything.

**Account:** @behikeai
**Format:** Carousel (6 slides)

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
You can set up your 2026 finances in one afternoon.

Not perfectly. But well enough that money stops being a guessing game.

Here is the exact sequence.

**Slide 2 . Step 1: Open the sheet and set up income:**
Open the Cash Flow Dashboard.

Add every income source you had last month. One row per source. Add the expected payment date for each.

Do not estimate. Use last month's actual numbers. You are building a baseline, not a projection.

**Slide 3 . Step 2: Categorize last month's spending:**
Pull your last bank statement.

Go line by line. Put each expense into one of three buckets: fixed, variable, one-time.

Fixed: things that recur at the same amount. Variable: things that change month to month. One-time: ignore these for now.

This step takes the most time. It also gives you the most information.

**Slide 4 . Step 3: Find the number that does not look right:**
After categorizing, one number will stand out.

It is usually in variable expenses. A category where you spent more than you thought. Food, subscriptions, software, or something that does not have a name yet.

That number is why you are doing this. Write it down. That is your first target.

**Slide 5 . Step 4: Set 3 savings targets:**
Not goals. Targets. Specific numbers with specific timelines.

Emergency fund target: one month of fixed expenses. Date to reach it.

Business tool budget: what you will spend on tools this quarter. Date to review.

One personal target: whatever is next for you. Date and amount.

Three targets. Written down. Revisited monthly.

**Slide 6 . The result:**
One afternoon of setup. One number that surprises you. Three targets.
