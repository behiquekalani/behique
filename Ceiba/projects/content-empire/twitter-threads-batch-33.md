**Thread 1**
**Topic:** Why your first digital product is priced too low

Tweet 1: Your first digital product is probably priced at $7 or $9. That price will get you buyers who expect free quality, generate almost no meaningful revenue, and train your audience that your work is cheap.

Tweet 2: Low prices do not lower the barrier to purchase as much as most creators think. Someone choosing between $7 and $27 is not price-sensitive, they're trust-sensitive. They're asking: is this worth anything?

Tweet 3: A $7 product says "I'm not sure this is worth more." A $27 product says "I believe this solves a real problem." The second framing attracts better buyers and filters out people who would ask for refunds either way.

Tweet 4: The practical test: if you wouldn't pay your own price for what you're selling, your price is wrong. Not too high. Too low. You've stopped believing in your own product.

Tweet 5: Start at $27 minimum for any digital guide. $47 for something with real depth. $97 for anything that saves someone significant time or money. The market will tell you if you're wrong. Low prices cannot.

**Thread 2**
**Topic:** The math of pricing your digital product correctly

Tweet 1: Let's do simple math that most first-time digital product creators skip. At $7, you need 143 sales to make $1,000. At $47, you need 22. At $97, you need 11. Your marketing effort is roughly the same.

Tweet 2: The effort to acquire 143 buyers is not 13x the effort to acquire 11 buyers. It's closer to 10x. That means you're doing 10x the work for the same revenue outcome. Low pricing is not conservative. It's inefficient.

Tweet 3: Add in transaction fees, platform cuts, and support time for 143 buyers versus 11, and the gap gets wider. Premium pricing is not just about profit margin. It's about operational sanity as a solo operator.

Tweet 4: The audience size you need to sustain a $97 product business is genuinely small. 100 buyers a month at $97 is nearly $10,000 monthly revenue. You don't need 100,000 followers for that.

Tweet 5: Stop optimizing for sales volume when you're small. Optimize for revenue per buyer. Higher prices with a smaller, more targeted audience outperforms high volume with bargain pricing every time at this scale.

**Thread 3**
**Topic:** How to price based on the outcome, not the content

Tweet 1: You are pricing your digital product based on what's in it. Hours of content, number of pages, production quality. That's the wrong framework. Price based on what changes after someone uses it.

Tweet 2: A 10-page PDF that saves someone 20 hours of research is worth more than a 200-page guide they never finish. The value is in the outcome, not the volume. Your buyer is purchasing a result.

Tweet 3: Ask yourself: if this product works perfectly for my ideal buyer, what happens in their life? They save money, they make money, they save time, they reduce stress? That outcome has a dollar value. Price relative to it.

Tweet 4: Example: a guide on finding profitable eBay products is not competing with books. It's competing with the cost of buying the wrong inventory, which could easily be $300-$500. A $47 guide that prevents that is cheap.

Tweet 5: Lead with the outcome in your sales copy, not the content. "Stop losing money on eBay inventory" converts better than "25-page guide to product research." The buyer wants the first thing, not the second.

Tweet 6: Follow @behikeai. Outcome-based positioning is how small products generate serious revenue without big audiences.

**Thread 4**
**Topic:** The one pricing mistake that kills repeat buyers

Tweet 1: The biggest pricing mistake with digital products is not charging too much for the first sale. It's charging so little that the buyer never perceives the value, uses the product, and comes back for more.

Tweet 2: Underpriced products create discount buyers. Discount buyers are loyal to price, not to you. The moment someone else offers a cheaper option, they leave. You cannot build a business on this customer type.

Tweet 3: A buyer who pays $47 and gets real value is primed to buy your next $97 product. A buyer who paid $9 and felt neutral about it will need to be convinced again from scratch, and they'll anchor on the $9 they paid before.

Tweet 4: The lifetime value of a premium buyer is 5-10x the lifetime value of a discount buyer. You can build a sustainable business on 50 premium buyers. You cannot build one on 500 discount buyers.

Tweet 5: If you already have a cheap product live: raise the price. Don't worry about alienating existing buyers. The new buyers at the higher price will be better customers and the math will change immediately.

**Thread 5**
**Topic:** How to test your price before you commit

Tweet 1: You don't have to guess your price. You can test it. Here's a method that takes one week and gives you real data instead of anxiety-based decisions.

Tweet 2: Build a landing page for your product before it exists. No product yet, just the sales copy describing the outcome. Set the price you think it should be. Put a waitlist form at the bottom. Drive 200 people to it.

Tweet 3: Measure conversion to the waitlist, not just traffic. If 15-20% of visitors join the waitlist at your chosen price, your price is in range. If it's under 5%, either the price is wrong or the copy is wrong. Both are fixable.

Tweet 4: The waitlist also builds a launch list. By the time you finish the product, you have buyers who already mentally committed at a specific price point. First launch conversion rates are dramatically higher.

Tweet 5: You can also run a direct price test with a simple post: "Building X product, thinking of pricing it at Y. Would you buy it at that price? Reply yes/no." Raw feedback from real people beats internal assumptions every time.
