# Twitter Batch 215 — Building in Public (What to Share and What to Keep Private)

---

## Standalone Tweets

**Tweet 1**
Building in public is not oversharing. It is strategic transparency. You share the process, the lessons, and the numbers that help your audience. You protect everything else.

**Tweet 2**
The mistake in building in public is sharing outcomes before they are real. Share the attempt. Share the learning. The result can wait until it is verified.

**Tweet 3**
Your failures are your most valuable content, but only after you have extracted the lesson. Raw suffering is not content. Processed insight is.

**Tweet 4**
Building in public does not mean sharing everything. It means sharing the parts of your journey that make someone else feel less alone in theirs.

**Tweet 5**
The best build-in-public creators treat their journey like a research project. They document, analyze, and share findings. They are not performing. They are reporting.

## Threads

**Thread 1 — The framework for what to share vs. what to keep private (6 tweets)**

1/ Building in public is one of the most powerful strategies for growing an audience as a solopreneur. It is also widely misunderstood.

2/ What to share: your process, your experiments, your mistakes, your numbers (when real), your reasoning, your pivots, your lessons learned.

3/ What to keep private: your pending deals, your legal agreements, your client details, your mental health in raw form before you have processed it, your unvalidated hypotheses stated as facts.

4/ The test for whether something should be shared: does this help my audience, or does this just relieve my need to be seen? Share the first. Sit with the second.

5/ Numbers are powerful when they are honest. Sharing "$300 in revenue month one" builds more trust than vague claims about "growing fast." Specificity is what makes it real.

6/ The long game: the people who build in public for 2-3 years with honesty and consistency become the most trusted voices in their niche. Not because they were perfect, but because they were real.

---

**Thread 2 — How to share failures without destroying your credibility (5 tweets)**

1/ The fear of sharing failures is that it makes you look incompetent. The reality is the opposite, if you do it right.

2/ Share failures with framing: what happened, what you thought would happen, why it did not, and what you are doing differently. That structure turns failure into evidence of intelligence.

3/ Do not perform sadness. Do not perform frustration. Share the analytical version of the experience. Your audience is not your therapist. They are your collaborators.

4/ The creator who shares failures with honesty and clarity builds more trust than the one who only shares wins. Wins are expected. Honest failure is rare and credible.

5/ One caveat: do not share failures in real time while they are still raw. Give yourself 48 to 72 hours to process. Then write the lesson, not the emotion.

---

**Thread 3 — The numbers worth sharing in public (5 tweets)**

1/ Revenue numbers attract attention. But they are not the only numbers worth sharing, and they carry the highest risk of being misconstrued.

2/ Numbers that build trust without significant risk: email list size, follower growth rate, open rates, conversion rates, product download counts.

3/ Revenue numbers work well when they are in context. "$300 from a $27 product" is more credible and useful than "$10,000 months." Context removes the brag and adds the lesson.

4/ The numbers that hurt your credibility: inflated follower counts, unverified screenshots, income claims without expense breakdowns. Your audience is more sophisticated than you think.

5/ A good rule: share numbers that your audience can benchmark against their own reality. Numbers that are too aspirational create distance. Numbers that are real create connection.

---

**Thread 4 — The build-in-public mistake that kills trust (4 tweets)**

1/ The fastest way to destroy credibility while building in public is this: sharing outcomes before they are real.

2/ Announcing a product launch before you have even started building. Claiming a "sold out" offer that had three spots. Rounding $1,800 up to "$2k month."

3/ Your audience has a finely tuned sense for when they are being marketed to versus when they are being shown something real. They will forgive failure. They will not forgive performance.

4/ Build in public like a journalist, not a marketer. Report what happened. Let the audience draw conclusions. The trust that builds from that honesty is durable.

---

**Thread 5 — Why building in public is the most efficient marketing strategy for solopreneurs (6 tweets)**

1/ Building in public is not just a content strategy. It is the most efficient marketing strategy available to a solo creator.

2/ It creates content automatically. Every decision, every experiment, every result is a potential post. You are working and marketing at the same time.

3/ It attracts the right audience. The people who follow a creator's journey are self-selecting as people who care about that specific path. They are pre-qualified for your eventual offer.

4/ It builds accountability. Sharing your goals publicly makes you more likely to hit them. Your audience becomes a soft commitment mechanism.

5/ It generates proof over time. After 12 months of honest build-in-public content, you have a documented case study of your own journey. That is the most credible portfolio that exists.

6/ The solopreneurs who will win in the next 5 years are the ones who documented their journey from the beginning. Start now. You cannot go back and build the archive later.
