## Post 2 — PARA: The Four Folders That Organize Everything

**Hook:** Four folders. Every piece of information in your life fits into one of them.

**Slide 1:** Four folders. Every piece of information in your life fits into one of them.

**Slide 2:** P: Projects. Things with a specific outcome and a deadline. Active work. Limited in number. Everything here demands attention now.

**Slide 3:** A: Areas. Ongoing responsibilities with no end date. Health. Finances. Client relationships. Things you maintain, not things you finish.

**Slide 4:** R: Resources. Topics and reference material you might use later. Research. Templates. Saved articles. Useful but not immediately actionable.

**Slide 5:** A: Archive. Everything inactive. Old projects. Past clients. Outdated resources. Out of sight but searchable if you ever need it.

**Slide 6:** PARA is the foundation. @behikeai has the Notion template to set it up in 30 minutes.

**Caption:** Most people fail at organization because they try to organize by topic or category. The problem is that topics multiply forever. You end up with 40 folders and nothing is findable.

PARA is different because it organizes by actionability, not by subject. The question you are answering is not "what is this about?" but "what does this mean for me right now?"

Projects require action. Areas require maintenance. Resources are available when needed. Archive is done.

This structure works because it maps to how you actually use information. You do not browse your archive daily. You do not need your resources front and center. You need your active projects visible and your areas monitored. PARA makes that automatic.

The setup takes one session. After that, every new piece of information has an obvious home. The friction of organizing disappears, and so does the anxiety of things being lost or forgotten.

## Post 3 — The Second Brain Is Not a Filing System

**Hook:** Most people build a second brain that becomes a second junk drawer.

**Slide 1:** Most people build a second brain that becomes a second junk drawer.

**Slide 2:** The mistake: capturing everything with no system for processing it. You add notes, ideas, and articles constantly. Nothing ever gets used. It just piles up.

**Slide 3:** A real second brain has three functions: capture, organize, and retrieve. If any of those three is broken, the whole system fails.

**Slide 4:** Capture: quick and frictionless. One inbox where everything lands. No decisions required at capture time.

**Slide 5:** Organize: a weekly session to sort the inbox into PARA. Takes 20 minutes. Keeps the system clean and usable.

**Slide 6:** Retrieve: build for search. Name things clearly. Tag consistently. Your future self is the user. Make it easy for them.

**Caption:** The most common second brain mistake is treating it like a library you never visit. You add books constantly but never check any out.

The system only works if the information inside it gets used. That requires two habits most people skip: a regular review to process what has accumulated, and a search reflex to check the system before trying to remember things on your own.

The capture habit is usually the first to form because it feels productive. Ideas go in. That feels good. But without the organize and retrieve habits, the system becomes a landfill with good intentions.

Start with a simple capture habit. Add a weekly 20-minute sort session. Build the search reflex by checking your notes before starting any new project or piece of content. Those three habits turn a folder system into a genuine thinking partner.

The second brain is only as useful as the person maintaining it. But maintenance does not have to be heavy. It just has to be consistent.

## Post 5 — The Solopreneur Second Brain Setup in 5 Steps

**Hook:** Five steps to build a second brain that actually runs your business.

**Slide 1:** Five steps to build a second brain that actually runs your business.

**Slide 2:** Step 1: Choose one tool. Notion, Obsidian, Roam. Pick one and commit. The system matters more than the software. For most solopreneurs, Notion wins because it combines notes and databases.

**Slide 3:** Step 2: Build PARA. Four sections. Projects, Areas, Resources, Archive. Everything you add goes into one of these four categories. No exceptions.

**Slide 4:** Step 3: Create a capture inbox. One place for everything that comes in. Ideas, tasks, links, voice memos. No sorting at capture time.

**Slide 5:** Step 4: Connect your business databases. Projects, clients, content pipeline. Linked to each other. Visible from a single dashboard.

**Slide 6:** Step 5: Run a weekly review. 30 minutes. Process inbox, update projects, set priorities. Do not skip this. It is the maintenance ritual that keeps the whole system alive.

**Caption:** Building a second brain is not a one-day project. It is a progressive build. You do not need a perfect system before you start. You need a starting point and a commitment to maintain it.

The five steps above are the minimum viable second brain. They are not the most sophisticated setup. They are the one that most people can actually build, maintain, and use without burning out on their own organizational system.

The mistake most people make is building the whole thing before trusting any of it. Start with the inbox and PARA. Add the databases when you have active projects that need them. Build the weekly review ritual after you have something to review.

The second brain is not built in a day. It is maintained in small sessions over months. Each session makes the system slightly more useful. That is the model. That is how it compounds.
