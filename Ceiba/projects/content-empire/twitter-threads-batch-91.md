## Thread 91-1: Why You're Pricing Wrong

You're not undercharging because you don't know your worth.
You're undercharging because you're scared of rejection.

Here's the pricing framework that changes everything:

**Tweet 2**

That's a race to the bottom.
The person with the lowest price wins nothing except a full calendar and an empty bank account.

**Tweet 3**
2/ Price anchoring works in your favor.

Put a $997 offer next to a $297 offer.
Suddenly $297 looks like a deal.
The anchor sets the context. The context sets the decision.

**Tweet 4**
3/ Your price signals your positioning.

Low price = low confidence = low trust.
A $19 ebook and a $97 ebook send completely different messages.
One says "I made this in a weekend." The other says "I built something worth studying."

**Tweet 5**
4/ The real cost of undercharging is time.

$27 x 100 sales = $2,700
$97 x 30 sales = $2,910

You made more with 70 fewer customers.
More time. Less support. Better clients. Higher perceived value.

**Tweet 6**
5/ Raise your prices and you'll see one thing immediately.

The quality of your buyers improves.
Cheap buyers complain. Serious buyers implement.
Price is a filter. Use it.

**Tweet 7**
6/ The rule: price to the transformation, not the deliverable.

You're not selling a PDF. You're selling the outcome the PDF creates.
What is that outcome worth to the buyer?
Price there.

**Tweet 8 (CTA)**
If you've been sitting on a digital product afraid to charge what it's worth:

Raise the price today.
Not next week. Today.
The market will tell you if you're wrong. But you'll be surprised how often it doesn't.

## Thread 91-2: The Pricing Psychology Nobody Explains

**Tweet 1 (Hook)**
Buyers don't know what your product is worth.
They need you to tell them.
That's what pricing psychology is.

Here's how it actually works:

**Tweet 2**
1/ Value is perceived, not inherent.

A $10 bottle of wine and a $80 bottle of wine.
Same grapes, different story.
Your price tells the story before the product does.

**Tweet 3**
2/ Charm pricing still works but not the way you think.

$97 vs $100 isn't about saving $3.
It's about signaling "I thought about this price."
Round numbers feel arbitrary. Specific numbers feel intentional.

**Tweet 4**
3/ Free is the worst price.

Free attracts people who don't value your work.
They don't implement. They don't refer. They don't return.
Charge something, even if it's small. The act of paying creates commitment.

**Tweet 5**
4/ The pain of paying goes down when you frame value first.

Lead with the outcome, then the price.
"Get 90 days of content in one weekend" followed by "$47" lands differently than "$47 for my content system."

**Tweet 6**
5/ Scarcity without authenticity destroys trust.

"Only 3 left" on a digital product is insulting.
Buyers know.
Use real scarcity: limited cohort spots, price increasing at launch end, bonus expiring.

**Tweet 7**
6/ The confidence signal is the most underrated pricing lever.

When you say your price without a question mark in your voice, people pay.
When you hedge, they negotiate.
Own the number.

**Tweet 8 (CTA)**
Price is a message. What are you currently telling people about your work?

If the answer is "not enough," fix it.
Behike has tools that help you package and position digital products correctly.
Link in bio.

## Thread 91-3: The 3-Tier Pricing Model for Creators

**Tweet 1 (Hook)**
One price = one type of buyer.
Three prices = you capture everyone willing to pay.

The tier model every creator should use:

**Tweet 2**
1/ Tier 1: The Entry Point ($17-$47)

Low barrier. High volume potential.
This is where someone becomes a customer, not just a follower.
It should deliver one clear, fast win.

**Tweet 3**
2/ Tier 2: The Core Offer ($97-$297)

Your main product. Full system. Full transformation.
Most of your revenue lives here.
Make this the no-brainer middle option.

**Tweet 4**
3/ Tier 3: The Premium ($497-$997+)

Done-with-you or done-for-you version.
High touch. Higher margin.
This is where your best clients find you.

**Tweet 5**
4/ The math changes when you stack tiers.

20 sales at $27 = $540
5 sales at $147 = $735
1 sale at $597 = $597

All three in a month: $1,872 from the same audience.
One price never captures the full range.

**Tweet 6**
5/ The upgrade path is built in.

Entry buyer proves the work. Upgrades to core.
Core buyer trusts you. Upgrades to premium.
You don't need to find new customers. You need to build the staircase.

**Tweet 7**
6/ Start with one tier. Build the rest.

Most creators stall because they want to launch all three at once.
Pick your best offer. Price it at the middle tier.
Add above and below as you validate.

**Tweet 8 (CTA)**
Your pricing structure is your business model.
Stop treating it like an afterthought.

Build the tier. Build the path. Build the business.

## Thread 91-4: How to Raise Your Prices Without Losing Customers

**Tweet 1 (Hook)**
Raising your price is the most terrifying thing a creator can do.
Most never do it.
Most also stay broke.

Here's how to raise prices and keep your buyers:

**Tweet 2**
1/ Give existing customers a grandfather window.

"Price increases in 7 days. Current price locked in if you buy before then."
This creates urgency and rewards loyalty at the same time.

**Tweet 3**
2/ Add something before you raise.

A new bonus, a live session, updated content.
You're not just charging more, you're delivering more.
The increase feels justified because it is.

**Tweet 4**
3/ Announce it matter-of-factly.

Don't apologize. Don't over-explain.
"The price is going from $47 to $97 on Friday. Here's why the value went up."
Confidence in the announcement equals confidence in the product.

**Tweet 5**
4/ Track what happens after.

Most creators expect cancellations and complaints.
What actually happens: a small dip in volume and an increase in buyer quality.
The math usually works out in your favor.

**Tweet 6**
5/ Your audience respects growth.

When you raise your price after building something better, followers notice.
It signals momentum. It signals that you take your work seriously.
Stagnant prices signal stagnant creators.

**Tweet 7**
6/ If you're terrified of raising prices, ask one question.

Would I charge this much if I had to look my best customer in the eye and defend the value?
If yes, raise it.
If not, improve the product first, then raise it.

**Tweet 8 (CTA)**
You built something worth more than you're charging.
Stop waiting for permission to price it correctly.

## Thread 91-5: The Underpricing Trap (And How to Escape It)

**Tweet 1 (Hook)**
Underpricing feels safe.
It's actually the riskiest thing you can do to your business.

Here's the trap and how to get out:

**Tweet 2**
1/ Low prices attract high-maintenance buyers.

The less someone pays, the more they expect you to do the work for them.
Premium buyers self-select as serious. Budget buyers self-select as skeptical.
Your price filters your client quality.

**Tweet 3**
2/ Underpricing signals insecurity, not generosity.

Buyers feel it. "Why is this so cheap? What's wrong with it?"
Counterintuitive but true: a higher price increases trust before the buyer reads a single word.

**Tweet 4**
3/ The compounding effect destroys you slowly.

$27 product. 200 sales. $5,400.
Same product at $97. 60 sales needed to match it.
You'll spend less on support, less on refunds, and less of your sanity.

**Tweet 5**
4/ You can't build a real business on volume alone.

Volume requires infrastructure: ads, systems, staff.
Margin requires positioning: better product, better messaging, better clients.
Most solo creators should build for margin, not volume.

**Tweet 6**
5/ The escape plan is simple, not easy.

Pick your best product. Research what buyers actually pay for comparable outcomes.
Set the price at the upper third of that range.
Launch. See what happens.

**Tweet 7**
6/ Most creators discover the same thing after raising prices.

Their conversion rate drops slightly.
Their revenue goes up significantly.
Their customer complaints nearly disappear.
The fear was always bigger than the reality.

**Tweet 8 (CTA)**
The creator economy rewards positioning, not pity pricing.

If you've been undercharging, this is your sign.
Fix the number. Fix the outcome. Fix the business.
