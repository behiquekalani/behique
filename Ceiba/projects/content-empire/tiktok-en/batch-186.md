## Topic: Why Most Digital Products Fail to Sell

---

### Script 1

**Hook:**
Your digital product didn't fail because the market is saturated. It failed before you made it.

**Body:**
Most creators build first and sell second. That's the whole problem.

Point 1: Nobody asked for what you built. You made something you thought was valuable, not something people were already paying to solve.

Point 2: The offer isn't clear. If someone reads your sales page and can't say back what they get and why it matters in one sentence, you've already lost them.

Point 3: There's no proof of demand. Testimonials, screenshots, stories from beta users. Without social proof, you're asking strangers to trust a stranger.

Point 4: The price signals the wrong thing. Too cheap looks like a side project. Too expensive without trust looks like a scam. Price is a positioning decision, not a math problem.

### Script 2

**Hook:**
99% of digital products that fail share one thing in common. And it's not what you think.

**Body:**
The problem isn't the product. It's the positioning.

Point 1: They're solving the wrong pain. People buy solutions to embarrassing problems or urgent desires, not general improvement. "Get better at writing" is a hobby. "Land your first paid client in 30 days" is a product.

Point 2: The creator is not the authority yet. You can't sell a course on trust if nobody knows you exist. Audience comes before offer.

Point 3: There's no funnel behind the link. Dropping a Gumroad link in a bio is not a sales strategy. You need warm traffic, a good reason to click, and a reason to buy now.

Point 4: They quit after three days of no sales. Distribution takes longer than creation.

### Script 3

**Hook:**
I wasted six months building a digital product nobody wanted. Here's the one question I didn't ask.

**Body:**
The question: is this person already spending money on this problem?

Point 1: If the audience isn't already paying for something similar, the market doesn't exist yet. That means you'd have to create the demand and sell the product. Too hard for a solo creator starting out.

Point 2: Survey before you build. Even five replies to a story poll telling you what they struggle with most is more valuable than a month of building in silence.

Point 3: Sell before you build. Pre-sell a waitlist or early access. Real money from real people is the only validation that counts.

Point 4: The market tells you what to build. You just have to listen before you start typing.

### Script 4

**Hook:**
Your digital product is not the problem. Your traffic is.

**Body:**
Most creators blame the product when the product never had a real shot.

Point 1: Cold traffic doesn't buy. People need to know you, consume your content, trust your perspective before they'll put in a card number. That takes time and repetition.

Point 2: The platform matters. A $97 PDF sells differently on Instagram than it does on LinkedIn. Know where your buyer spends time.

Point 3: One post is not a launch. A real launch is a week of content that builds desire, addresses objections, and creates urgency. One announcement post is just noise.

Point 4: Most people see your offer once and forget. The algorithm is not your sales team. You have to show up consistently.

### Script 5

**Hook:**
The real reason your digital product has zero sales has nothing to do with quality.

**Body:**
Quality is the last thing that closes a sale.

Point 1: Nobody can see the quality until after they buy. What they're actually buying is your confidence, your clarity, and your story. If those three are missing, no amount of polish matters.

Point 2: You're talking features, not outcomes. Nobody buys a course because it has 12 modules. They buy because of what those 12 modules change in their life or work.

Point 3: You're not making an offer, you're making an announcement. An offer has a price, a deadline, a reason to act now, and a specific result. An announcement is just information.

Point 4: The gap between good product and good sales is a skill. And that skill is learnable.
