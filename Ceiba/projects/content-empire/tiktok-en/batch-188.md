## Topic: Pricing Your Knowledge

---

### Script 1

**Hook:**
You're charging too little. Not because you're greedy. Because you don't understand how pricing actually works.

**Body:**
Pricing is not about what something costs to make. It's about what it's worth to the buyer.

Point 1: The value of your knowledge is measured by the outcome it produces, not the hours you spent acquiring it. If your framework saves someone six months of trial and error, the price floor is far higher than you think.

Point 2: Low prices attract low-commitment buyers. People who pay very little often ask the most, complain the most, and implement the least. A higher price filters for serious people.

Point 3: Your price is a positioning statement. $27 says "useful tool." $297 says "real investment." $2,970 says "transformation." Each one attracts a different type of buyer and a different relationship.

Point 4: The fear under low pricing is usually not strategy. It's self-doubt. You're not charging less because it makes business sense. You're charging less because you're afraid nobody will pay more.

### Script 2

**Hook:**
Here's why the $9 course is actually harder to sell than the $297 one.

**Body:**
Counter-intuitive but backed by buyer psychology.

Point 1: Low prices require more volume. To make $10,000 at $9, you need 1,112 buyers. At $297, you need 34. Which customer acquisition problem would you rather have?

Point 2: High prices create higher perceived credibility. The buyer assumes that if it costs more, it must be better. Price shapes perception before anyone sees the content.

Point 3: More expensive products attract people who are ready to act. The $9 buyer is browsing. The $297 buyer has already decided they want to change something.

Point 4: You can make one expensive product work with a tiny audience. You need a huge audience to survive on cheap products.

### Script 3

**Hook:**
You have knowledge worth thousands of dollars. You're packaging it like it's worth ten.

**Body:**
The problem isn't your knowledge. It's how you've framed it.

Point 1: Package your knowledge into a specific outcome for a specific person. "Everything I know about marketing" is worth nothing. "How a freelance designer gets three clients in 60 days without cold outreach" has a price.

Point 2: Add context to increase perceived value. A PDF is cheap. A PDF plus a checklist plus a video walkthrough plus a template is a system. Same knowledge, higher perceived value.

Point 3: Anchor your price against alternatives. If the alternative to your $197 course is a $5,000 coaching engagement or three years of trial and error, $197 sounds like a deal, not an expense.

Point 4: Raise your price before you feel ready. The discomfort of raising a price is always smaller than the long-term cost of undercharging.

### Script 4

**Hook:**
The creator charging $2,000 for a course isn't 200 times better than the one charging $10. They just understand something different.

**Body:**
That something is transformation vs. information.

Point 1: Information is cheap. The internet is full of free information. What people pay for is a curated path from where they are now to a specific result, with less friction.

Point 2: High-ticket products sell a transformation. Low-ticket products sell information. Ask yourself which one you're actually selling right now.

Point 3: You can't charge $2,000 without trust. Trust comes from content, consistency, and proof. The price is downstream of the relationship.

Point 4: Raise your price in stages. You don't have to jump to $2,000. Move from $27 to $97 to $297 and let the market teach you where resistance actually lives.

### Script 5

**Hook:**
Pricing anxiety is the silent killer of every knowledge business. Here's how to kill it first.

**Body:**
The anxiety comes from tying your price to your self-worth. They're not the same thing.

Point 1: Your price is not a vote on your value as a person. It's a business decision about positioning, audience fit, and revenue model. Keep those two things separate.

Point 2: Test your price with a small audience first. Sell to 10 people. See where resistance shows up. Adjust. You don't need to figure out perfect pricing before you launch.

Point 3: Use the "what would make this a no-brainer" test. At your current price, what would someone need to believe to buy without hesitation? Now make sure your sales page communicates that.

Point 4: The right price is the one your ideal buyer sees and says "that's fair for what I'm getting." Not the one that feels comfortable to you.
