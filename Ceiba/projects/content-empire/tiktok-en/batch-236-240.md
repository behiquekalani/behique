## script-236
**Topic: The biggest mistake new creators make**

Most new creators are building for the wrong audience.

They start posting for everyone, which means they reach no one.

The mistake is not that you're posting too much or too little. The mistake is that you haven't chosen a specific person to talk to.

When you know exactly who you're writing for, the content becomes easy. You already know what they're struggling with. You already know what they want to hear.

Stop asking "what should I post?" Start asking "what does my specific person need to understand today?"

The creators who grow fast aren't more talented. They're more targeted.

One person, one problem, one solution. That's the entire strategy.

If you're not growing, it's almost always an audience definition problem, not a content quality problem.

Fix that first. Everything else becomes easier.

Most people are chasing the story, not the math.

## script-238
**Topic: How to write content when you have nothing to say**

Writer's block isn't a creativity problem. It's a constraint problem.

When you have too much freedom, you freeze. The solution is to give yourself less freedom on purpose.

Three things that work:

One. Write about what you learned this week, not what you know in general. What changed your thinking? That's the post.

Two. Find a take you disagree with. Argue against it clearly. That's another post.

Three. Pick one question your audience keeps asking. Answer it completely. That's three posts.

You don't need a new idea every day. You need a system that recycles your thinking into content.

Most of what I post started as a conversation, a frustration, or a question I couldn't stop thinking about.

Content doesn't come from inspiration. It comes from paying attention.

## script-239
**Topic: The creator's relationship with failure**

The creators who last aren't the ones who never fail. They're the ones who made failure boring.

When you post 200 times and 180 of them perform poorly, the failure becomes routine. It stops feeling like a verdict on your worth and starts feeling like data.

Most creators quit too early because they're still treating every miss as proof they don't belong.

Here's the frame shift: failure is not feedback about your identity. It's feedback about one variable. The hook. The timing. The topic. The format.

Change one variable. Try again.

The goal is to keep the surface area of your attempts large enough that something has to land.

Volume doesn't guarantee success. But quitting before volume guarantees failure.

Ship more. Expect less from each individual piece. Let the math do its job.

**Topic: Why your first 100 followers matter more than your next 10,000**

Your first 100 followers are your proof of concept.

Not because they'll buy everything you make. Because they told you something was worth paying attention to.

Most people treat 100 followers as nothing. They're waiting for 10,000 to start taking themselves seriously.

That's backwards.

The feedback you get at 100 followers is the most honest you'll ever receive. These people found you before you were obvious. They're telling you something specific resonated.

Listen to them. Ask what they want more of. Ask what problem brought them to you. That information shapes the next 10,000 followers you attract.

The creators who blow up at 10,000 did the work to understand their 100.

Your first audience is a focus group. Most people skip the research and wonder why growth stalls.

Don't skip it.
