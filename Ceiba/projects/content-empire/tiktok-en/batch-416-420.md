## Script 416 — Content Licensing Revenue

**Hook (0-3s)**
You can get paid for content you already made. Not with ads. With licensing.

**Body (4-55s)**
Most creators sell their time. A post goes up, it gets views, maybe a sponsorship, and then it's done.

Content licensing is a different model. You create something once, and you give other people or businesses permission to use it, in exchange for a fee.

Here are three ways to do this starting now.

First: license your templates, frameworks, or systems to other creators who want to skip the work of building from scratch.

Second: license your content method to small businesses that need to show up on social but don't know how to create consistently.

Third: license your visual assets, like infographics or slide decks, to newsletters, educators, or course creators who need polished materials.

You don't need a massive audience for this. You need something that works and that someone else wants to use.

The biggest difference between licensing and regular content: you stop charging per hour and start charging per use.

## Script 417 — Referral Engine for Solopreneurs

**Hook (0-3s)**
The best sales system costs nothing. It's your own clients selling for you.

**Body (4-55s)**
A referral engine is a system. Not a hope.

Most solopreneurs wait for clients to recommend them naturally. It happens, but not at a rate that builds a predictable business.

A working referral engine has three components.

The timing: you ask for a referral right after the client gets a result, not at the end of the contract. Right after the win is when motivation is highest.

The script: you give your client exactly what to say, who to send it to, and how to do it in under two minutes. If there's friction, they won't do it.

The incentive: it doesn't have to be money. Early access, a bonus session, or even public acknowledgment can work better depending on who your clients are.

When all three are in place, referrals stop being a pleasant surprise and become a predictable acquisition channel.

Most solopreneurs treat referrals like luck. The ones growing fast treat them like a process.

## Script 418 — Sponsorship Media Kit

**Hook (0-3s)**
Without a media kit, sponsors can't trust you. And without trust, there's no deal.

**Body (4-55s)**
A media kit is not a pretty PDF with your follower count. It's your sales argument.

It needs to answer three questions before the sponsor asks them.

First: who do you reach? Not just how many people, but who they are, what problem they have, and what they buy. Sponsors don't pay for followers. They pay for access to a specific kind of buyer.

Second: what results does your audience produce? Click-through rates, replies, past affiliate sales, testimonials from other sponsors. Any data that proves your audience takes action.

Third: what exactly do they get? A clear menu of what you offer, with prices or ranges, no ambiguity. If a sponsor has to ask basic questions about what they're buying, you've already lost the conversation.

With a strong media kit, you stop chasing sponsors and start attracting inbound interest.

The document doesn't close the deal. But it determines whether the conversation starts.

## Script 419 — Systems Audit

**Hook (0-3s)**
If you don't know what's broken in your business, everything feels urgent. Nothing moves.

**Body (4-55s)**
A systems audit is not complicated. It's asking three questions about every part of your business.

Question one: is this generating results or just activity? A lot of solopreneurs are busy. Posting content, answering DMs, updating tools. But activity is not progress. If you can't point to a specific outcome that this work produces, it's a candidate for elimination.

Question two: if I disappeared for two weeks, would this process keep running? If the answer is no, that process depends too much on you. That's a bottleneck, not a system.

Question three: is the time I invest here proportional to the income it generates? If 60% of your working hours goes toward something producing 10% of your revenue, that gap is your real problem.

The audit doesn't tell you what to build. It tells you what to cut.

And cutting the wrong things is almost always what allows the right things to grow.

## Script 420 — Information Product Business

**Hook (0-3s)**
An information product business is not about selling courses. It's about building an asset that works without you.

**Body (4-55s)**
There's a difference between creating content and building an information business.

Content creators produce to feed algorithms. Information businesses produce to build catalogs.

A well-positioned catalog of digital products generates revenue months or years after you built the product. Organic content almost never does that.

The model works like this. You have a specific problem you know how to solve. You document the solution in a format someone can consume without your presence. You sell it through a channel with existing traffic, whether that's search, a community, or your email list. And you improve it over time based on real data.

You don't need a team. You don't need funding. You need clarity about the problem you solve and the discipline to document it well.

The information product business is the only model where the knowledge you already have is the inventory.

Most people with valuable knowledge never package it. They keep giving it away for free in conversations, posts, and DMs. That's a choice. Just not a very strategic one.
