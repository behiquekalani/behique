# LinkedIn Batch 66 — High-Ticket vs Low-Ticket

## Post 1
The solopreneur who insists on $27 products is not being humble. They are being afraid.

Pricing is not a reflection of what the market will bear. It is a reflection of what you believe your work is worth. And most solopreneurs chronically underestimate that number because they are standing too close to their own expertise.

The math is straightforward. At $27, you need 185 customers to hit $5,000 in revenue. At $500, you need 10. Both require marketing. Both require support. One requires 18 times the transaction volume. The model you choose determines your operating reality.

There is also a filtering effect most people ignore. Every price point attracts a type of buyer. The $27 buyer is not necessarily going to become your $500 buyer. They are different people with different levels of urgency and commitment. The audience you build at each price point reflects the price, not your potential.

This is not an argument that everyone should go high-ticket immediately. It is an argument that pricing decisions should come from strategy, not fear. If you have been stuck at $27 for 12 months, the question worth asking is not "how do I sell more?" It is "why am I still here?"

What is the honest reason you have not raised your prices yet?

## Post 2
High-ticket and low-ticket are not opposites. They are tools. Most solopreneurs use the wrong one at the wrong time.

Low-ticket products serve a specific function. They create buyers. A buyer is categorically different from a follower. Buyers have overcome the psychological barrier of handing you money. They are already inside the relationship. They convert to higher offers at multiples that cold traffic never will.

High-ticket products serve a different function. They create transformation stories. One client who goes through a serious engagement and gets a documented result gives you proof that compounds across every subsequent sale. That proof closes sales that would otherwise require hours of persuasion.

The mistake is not choosing one over the other. The mistake is not understanding why you are choosing it and what it is supposed to do for your business architecture.

Most solopreneurs with low-ticket products have never surveyed their buyers. They do not know what the next problem is. They do not know what their buyers would pay to solve it. They are sitting on a buyer list with no plan to activate it.

Build the ladder before you sell the first rung.

## Post 3
The real cost of a low-price product is not the margin. It is the audience you train over time.

Pricing creates expectations. When someone finds you through a $27 product, their mental model of you is set at $27. Every future offer gets compared to that anchor. Not to the market. Not to competitors. To the first number they paid you.

Moving that anchor is possible. But it takes deliberate work. You need new content that reframes your positioning. You need new social proof at the higher price point. You need to give the existing buyer list a reason to recalibrate.

Most solopreneurs skip this work. They launch a $500 offer to a list that knows them as a $27 creator and wonder why it does not convert. The list is not wrong. The setup is wrong.

This is not a reason to avoid low-ticket. It is a reason to think about the full arc before you launch the first offer. Where does this price point sit in the bigger picture? What does it train your audience to expect? How do you plan to move them up?

Pricing strategy is audience strategy. They are the same decision made at different times.

## Post 4
The best argument for high-ticket is not the revenue. It is the feedback quality.

When someone pays $2,000 for your help, they show up differently. They do the work. They ask real questions. They tell you when something is not working. They give you direct insight into what your audience actually needs versus what you assumed they needed.

When someone pays $27, they often do not engage at all. The purchase was low enough that there was no psychological cost to abandoning it. You get no useful information about whether your system works.

The feedback loop at higher price points is one of the most underrated advantages of premium offers. Every client interaction at that level makes you better. You learn faster. You see the same problems from different angles. You develop nuance that is impossible to develop by selling to people who are not serious.

The price point you choose determines not just your revenue but your learning velocity. If you are still early in building your expertise, high-ticket gets you better faster.

Where are you generating your best professional feedback right now?

---

## Post 5
Neither high-ticket nor low-ticket is inherently better. The question is which one serves your current constraints.

If you have a large audience and strong content systems, low-ticket scales efficiently. You have the volume to make the economics work. You can automate delivery. You can run paid ads once you have data on what converts.

If you have a small audience and strong expertise, high-ticket is the rational choice. You cannot outrun your audience size with a $27 product. But you can serve 10 people extremely well at $500 and build proof that attracts the next 10.

Most solopreneurs ignore their actual constraints when making pricing decisions. They copy the model they saw someone else use without asking whether that person's starting conditions match theirs.

Conditions that matter: audience size, audience quality, your capacity for direct client work, your content output velocity, your existing proof and testimonials. These inputs determine which model makes sense right now.

The model is a function of your reality. Start there.

What constraints are you working around in your current pricing?
