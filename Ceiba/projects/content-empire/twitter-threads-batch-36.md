**Thread 1**
**Topic:** Why most advice fails before you even try it

Tweet 1: Most advice isn't wrong. It's just built for someone else's context. And context is the thing no one talks about.

Tweet 2: When a $10M founder says "just ship it," they have a team, capital, and credibility. When you're starting out, shipping the wrong thing costs you 3 months of trust you haven't built yet.

Tweet 3: Advice is compressed experience. The problem is compression destroys context. You get the conclusion without the conditions that made it true.

Tweet 4: Before applying any advice, ask: what was the situation this person was in when this worked? If you can't answer that, you're guessing.

Tweet 5: Build a filter. Every piece of advice you receive, ask: who is this for? What stage? What resources? What constraints? If it doesn't match yours, file it for later or ignore it completely.

Tweet 6: Most people fail because they follow the right advice at the wrong time. Follow @behikeai for frameworks built for solo builders, not funded teams.

**Thread 2**
**Topic:** The stage problem in advice

Tweet 1: Stage 0 advice applied at Stage 3 will destroy you. Stage 3 advice applied at Stage 0 will paralyze you. Nobody tells you which stage the advice is for.

Tweet 2: "Focus on one thing" is Stage 2 advice. At Stage 0, you need to experiment broadly because you don't know what works yet. If you focus too early, you focus on the wrong thing.

Tweet 3: "Say yes to everything" is Stage 0 advice. At Stage 2, saying yes to everything is how you become mediocre at many things and excellent at none.

Tweet 4: The reason advice forums are noisy is that everyone is at a different stage and nobody labels their posts. You're getting Stage 5 signals when you're at Stage 1.

Tweet 5: Figure out your stage first. Then filter advice ruthlessly. Only take advice from people one or two stages ahead of you, not ten stages ahead.

Tweet 6: Follow @behikeai for stage-specific frameworks for solopreneurs building from zero.

**Thread 3**
**Topic:** Why your environment breaks good advice

Tweet 1: You followed the advice exactly. It still didn't work. Here's the thing nobody says: environment is advice's silent variable.

Tweet 2: "Wake up at 5am and do deep work" works beautifully in a quiet house with no obligations until 9am. It fails if you have a baby, a loud roommate, or a job that drains you by 6pm.

Tweet 3: Environment isn't just physical. It's your relationships, your obligations, your energy cycles. Advice that ignores this is advice built in a vacuum.

Tweet 4: Before you blame yourself for not following through, ask: is this environment compatible with this advice? Sometimes the answer is no, and that's not a character flaw.

Tweet 5: Adapt the advice to your environment, not the other way around. If "morning routine" doesn't work, try "post-lunch window." The principle stays. The execution changes.

Tweet 6: Solo building is about working with your real life, not against it. Follow @behikeai for systems that actually fit.

**Thread 4**
**Topic:** The identity mismatch problem

Tweet 1: Advice only works if you believe you're the kind of person it applies to. Most people fail because of an identity mismatch, not a skill gap.

Tweet 2: If someone tells you to "cold email 50 people a day" and deep down you believe you're not a salesperson, you'll find a hundred reasons to not send those emails. The advice is fine. The identity is the blocker.

Tweet 3: Every piece of advice carries an implicit identity assumption. "Post every day" assumes you see yourself as a creator. "Charge more" assumes you see yourself as worth it.

Tweet 4: This is why self-help books don't stick. You read the strategy but never update the belief system underneath it. The behavior changes for a week and reverts.

Tweet 5: Before you change your behavior, ask what belief would make this behavior natural. Then work on the belief first. The behavior follows.

Tweet 6: Identity-first building is what separates people who sustain momentum from people who cycle. Follow @behikeai for more on this.

**Thread 5**
**Topic:** How to extract useful advice from useless advice

Tweet 1: Most advice is useless in its raw form. That doesn't mean it's worthless. It means you have to process it before consuming it.

Tweet 2: Step one: strip the advice down to its underlying principle. "Post 3 times a day" becomes "stay top of mind consistently." Now you can apply the principle in a way that fits your context.

Tweet 3: Step two: identify the constraint it was solving. Was this person solving a visibility problem? A consistency problem? A monetization problem? Different constraints, different solutions.

Tweet 4: Step three: map it to your actual situation. You're not them. What does your version of this principle look like with your time, tools, and audience?

Tweet 5: The goal isn't to copy advice. It's to borrow principles and rebuild them for your situation. This is why people who think clearly outperform people who follow precisely.

Tweet 6: Follow @behikeai for breakdowns of popular advice with the context restored.
