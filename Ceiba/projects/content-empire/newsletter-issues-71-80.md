## Issue 71: What I Learned From Building 34 Products in 6 Weeks
**Subject:** 34 products in 6 weeks. Here's what happened.
**Preview text:** The honest breakdown of what worked, what didn't, and what I'd do differently.

Six weeks ago I set a target: ship as many digital products as I could before second-guessing myself into doing nothing. The number I landed on was 34.

Not 34 polished, validated, market-tested products. Thirty-four rough, real, done-enough products. PDFs, guides, templates, mini-courses, system documents I was already using myself. Things I knew, packaged in a form someone else could use.

Here's what I actually learned.

Speed matters more than quality at the start. This goes against everything you hear in product circles. But the feedback loop on a product you haven't shipped yet is zero. The feedback loop on something live, even if it's imperfect, is real. I'd rather have 34 imperfect things and know which two get traction than have one perfect thing I'm still polishing.

You can't predict what sells. I had opinions about which products were good. My opinions were wrong about half the time. The guide I thought was too simple moved. The one I was most proud of sat untouched. The market doesn't care about your effort. It cares about whether the thing solves a real problem for a specific person right now.

Volume creates options. When you have 34 products, you have 34 shots at finding one that lands. You can bundle, test price points, run promotions. When you have one product you've been building for three months, you have one shot, and the pressure on that one thing makes it harder to see it clearly.

The work that actually matters is the listing, not the product. I could build something in two hours. Writing a listing that converted took longer than the product itself sometimes. The title, the description, the framing. That's the skill. The product is just the artifact. The positioning is the product.

Completion is a skill you have to train. Starting things is easy. I have a backlog of started things going back years. Finishing 34 of anything in six weeks required treating done as the only valid output. Not revised. Not improved. Done and shipped. That muscle, once you build it, changes how you work.

The one thing I'd do differently: I'd have set up the distribution infrastructure before building. A list. A system for where traffic goes. I built the products first and the pipeline after. That was backwards. Thirty-four products with no audience to point at them is a warehouse, not a business.

What I'm doing now is treating the 34 as a testing library. I'm watching what moves, doubling down on those angles, and letting the slow ones sit. Most products don't die. They just wait for the right moment or the right framing.

If you're sitting on knowledge you're not packaging, the question isn't whether it's good enough. It's whether you've shipped enough to have any real signal at all.

Question: What's one thing you know well enough to explain to someone else in 30 minutes? That's a product. Have you built it yet?

## Issue 72: How to Price Your First Digital Product
**Subject:** How to price your first digital product
**Preview text:** Most people get this wrong in the same direction. Here's the fix.

Most people underprice their first digital product. Not a little. By half, sometimes by 80%. And the reason isn't humility. It's fear disguised as modesty.

Here's how I think about pricing now, after going through it wrong first.

The instinct is to price low so people will buy. The logic sounds reasonable: lower price, lower barrier, more buyers. But this logic has a flaw. Low prices attract people who are price-sensitive, not people who are ready to act. The buyer who finds your $7 guide is usually someone browsing for free information and settling for cheap. The buyer who pays $47 for the same information read the page, believed it would help them, and is committed enough to their problem to pay for a solution.

Price signals perceived quality. That's not marketing spin. It's how people process value. A $7 PDF and a $47 PDF can contain identical information. The reader of the $47 one will implement it more. They have skin in the game.

The right way to price your first product is to find a comparable product in the same category and price at or above it. Not below. At or above. Then make sure your listing earns that price with specific, credible promises. Not "learn everything about X." More like: "the four decisions I made that took my eBay store from zero to $2,000/month, and the ones I'd skip if I started over."

Specificity earns price. Vagueness destroys it.

Here's my practical framework. Ask three questions.

One: What is this person's alternative? If they could hire someone to do what your product teaches, what would that cost? If your guide replaces a $500 consulting call, pricing it at $27 is not humility. It's burning value.

Two: What is the outcome worth to them? Not the information. The outcome. A guide that helps someone make an extra $500/month is worth more than one month's value of that outcome. Price reflects transformation, not page count.

Three: Can I raise it later? Yes, always. But you lose the early buyers who would have paid more and told people about it. Start higher than feels comfortable. Run a launch discount if you want to. Don't set your floor at the lowest number you think someone might pay.

One more thing: round numbers under $100 perform worse than slightly odd prices. Not because of some hack, but because $47 feels considered, $50 feels arbitrary. $29 feels like a decision was made. $30 feels like a placeholder.

Your first product is a test. Price it like it matters, because pricing it like it doesn't matter will give you data that's impossible to interpret.

What does your product actually help someone do? That answer should drive your price, not your comfort level.

## Issue 73: The AI Installation Service Business
**Subject:** What the AI service business actually looks like
**Preview text:** Not the pitch. The actual day-to-day when you're selling AI to small businesses.

I want to describe what it actually looks like to run a service business selling AI tools to small companies. Not the pitch version. The day-to-day version.

Most of the clients I've talked to don't know what they want. They know they've heard "AI" enough times that they feel behind. That anxiety is real and it creates an opening, but it also means the first conversation is rarely about tools. It's about figuring out where their time actually goes and whether automation has any business being there.

The entry point I've found that works: start with one workflow, not a full audit. People who want a "full AI audit" are shopping. People who want their email responses handled, or their content repurposed, or their client intake automated are ready to move. Specificity is what separates a real lead from someone who wants to feel like they're doing something about AI without changing anything.

The service itself is n8n workflows, mostly. Sometimes a custom GPT wrapper. Sometimes just connecting three tools they already have but aren't using together. The technical side is usually simpler than clients expect. What they're paying for is the judgment: knowing which workflow to build first, what the failure modes are, how to make it foolproof enough for a non-technical person to maintain.

Day to day, this means a lot of Loom videos explaining what I built and why. It means building things that don't break when the client accidentally disconnects an integration. It means anticipating the five ways someone will misuse a workflow and handling four of them before handing it over.

The pricing model that's worked for me is a setup fee plus an optional monthly retainer for maintenance. Not a subscription with no defined output. Clients know what they're paying for setup and they understand maintenance as a separate decision. That clarity closes faster than packages with vague deliverables.

The business challenge is not technical. The business challenge is pipeline. You can be excellent at building these systems and have zero clients because you haven't shown the work anywhere. Every workflow I've built should become a short video, a case study, a before/after breakdown. I'm still building that habit.

The thing that surprised me most: clients don't care about the tools. They don't care if it's n8n or Make or Zapier. They care that the problem they explained to you got solved and they don't have to think about it anymore. That's the product. The tools are invisible.

If you're thinking about this kind of service, the starting point isn't building your offer. It's finding one real problem in one real business and building the solution for free or cheap. The case study is worth more than the fee in the beginning.

What would you automate first if you were starting this service today?

## Issue 74: ADHD and Entrepreneurship
**Subject:** What my psychologist taught me about systems
**Preview text:** Not productivity hacks. The actual framework that changed how I build.

I was diagnosed with ADHD in my early twenties. I had already failed at enough personal productivity systems that I had started to believe the problem was effort, not structure. My psychologist disabused me of that idea pretty quickly.

What she taught me isn't what you'll find in a productivity article. It's more specific and more honest than that.

The first thing: ADHD is not a deficit of attention. It's a deficit of regulated attention. I can focus, intensely, on things that are new or high-stakes or intrinsically motivating. What I can't do reliably is choose to focus on something boring when something more interesting exists nearby. The problem isn't focus. It's direction.

This distinction matters for how you build systems. Most productivity advice is written for people who have directional control over their attention and just need help with volume or prioritization. That advice doesn't work for me because it assumes the wrong thing about what's broken.

What actually helps me:

Remove the competition. If I'm supposed to write a newsletter and my phone is on my desk, I'm competing with my phone for my own attention. That's a competition I usually lose. The fix isn't willpower. It's removing the alternative. Phone in another room. One tab open. One task named. The environment does the work that my prefrontal cortex won't.

Make the next action completely unambiguous. "Work on the store" is not a task. It's a category. "Write one product description for the AI news tracker guide, starting with the problem sentence" is a task. The more specific the next action, the less activation energy it requires. ADHD brains resist initiation, not execution. The task needs to already be started before I sit down.

Capture everything immediately and decide later. I use a Telegram bot I built for this. The idea is to separate capture from decision. If I have to evaluate every idea when it arrives, I lose the idea and the momentum. If I just capture it and decide later, I keep both. The backlog is not the enemy. The lost idea is the enemy.

Short cycles, real breaks. Not the 25-minute pomodoro as a rule, but the principle behind it: work has an endpoint. I work better knowing I stop at a defined time than knowing I should "work until it's done." Undefined endpoints are demotivating when your attention regulation is unreliable.

The entrepreneurship angle is this: building your own thing is one of the more ADHD-compatible ways to work, if you design it right. The variety, the ownership, the direct feedback loop from your actions. These are all things that work with how ADHD brains function. The risk is that without structure, the same traits that make you good at starting things will keep you from finishing them.

What's one thing in your current setup that you could redesign to remove friction instead of trying to push through it?

## Issue 75: The Tools I Can't Live Without in 2026
**Subject:** The tools I actually use, not the ones I recommend
**Preview text:** Specific apps, specific reasons. No affiliate links, no sponsorships.

I'm going to give you my actual stack. Not what I think you should use. What I use, why I use it, and what the real benefit has been for me.

Claude. I use it for thinking more than writing. I paste messy ideas and ask it to find the structure. I give it my rough drafts and ask it to identify the weakest sentence. I use it to pressure-test decisions before I commit to them. The value is not that it writes for me. The value is that it's a patient, fast, honest thinking partner that doesn't care if I change my mind.

n8n. Self-hostable workflow automation. I use it to connect tools that don't talk to each other natively and to build small pipelines that run without me. The learning curve is real but it's worth it if you're building automated income or services. The self-hosted version means I own my automations and I'm not paying per-task fees that compound as I scale.

Obsidian. My knowledge base. Everything I've learned, written, or saved lives here in markdown files on my own hard drive. No subscription dependency. No company holding my notes hostage. I can access, search, and link everything. The graph view is less useful than people say, but the linking and the local-first philosophy are worth the switch from Notion.

Cursor. AI-assisted code editor. I'm not a strong developer but I build things. Cursor bridges the gap between my ideas and my ability to implement them. I can describe what I want, see it generated, and then modify it. It's not magic but it's the closest thing to a coding co-pilot I've found that actually works.

CapCut. For video editing. Simple, fast, good enough for everything I need at this stage. I'm not trying to make film. I'm trying to make content consistently without spending four hours on a two-minute video.

Railway. Hosting for my bots and services. I deploy once and it stays up. I don't have to manage servers. For the kind of small services I run, the pricing is reasonable and the deployment workflow is close to zero friction.

Warp. Terminal with AI built in. It saves me lookup time constantly. I don't have to remember every CLI flag. I describe what I want to do and it suggests the command. Small thing, real impact over a full working day.

What I stopped using: Notion (too much structure, too many clicks), Zapier (pricing), most "productivity" apps. I stopped trusting productivity apps in general and started trusting systems I design myself.

The pattern across everything I kept: local-first where possible, low friction to use daily, does one thing well. The tools that left were the ones I was maintaining more than using.

What's one tool you're still using out of habit rather than because it's still the best option?

## Issue 76: Why I'm Building in Spanish
**Subject:** Why I'm building in Spanish
**Preview text:** It's not a niche strategy. It's a bet on a market that's been ignored.

I'm Puerto Rican. My first language is Spanish. The community I grew up in, the way I think when I'm tired, the way I speak to my family. Spanish. This is not a strategy I designed. It's just who I am.

But I want to talk about it as a strategy anyway, because the LATAM opportunity is real and I think most English-first creators are sleeping on it.

Here's the situation. The Spanish-speaking internet is huge. Over 500 million native speakers. A growing middle class in Mexico, Colombia, Peru, Argentina, Chile. A generation of young people entering the workforce who are building businesses, learning to code, figuring out how AI applies to their work. This audience is underserved in a specific way: there's a lot of Spanish-language content, but very little high-quality, practical, builder-focused content. The deficit isn't volume. It's depth.

Most Spanish content about entrepreneurship and AI falls into one of two categories. Translated versions of American content that don't account for different economic contexts, platforms, payment systems, and regulations. Or motivational content that's more energy than substance. There's a gap between those two things and it's where I'm building.

The practical advantages are real. Lower competition for SEO and discoverability. Less noise in the content space means standing out is easier. Building an audience of buyers who have fewer options creates stronger loyalty. And if you're also visible in English, you have both markets.

The challenges are also real. Payment infrastructure is harder in some countries. Platforms like Gumroad work but LATAM buyers sometimes have friction with international payment processors. Digital product purchasing behavior varies across the region. What works in Puerto Rico doesn't automatically work in Argentina.

My approach is this: I build the core product in both languages, I price for local purchasing power where I can, and I focus the content on problems that are universal enough to land across the region while specific enough to feel relevant. ADHD and productivity. AI tools that work in Spanish. Building income online with limited starting capital.

The reason I'm naming this explicitly is that I think a lot of people who could build for Spanish-speaking audiences either translate their existing content as an afterthought or avoid it because they see it as a smaller market. I don't see it that way. I see it as a market that's grown faster than the content serving it.

If you speak Spanish, or Portuguese, or any language that's underserved in the creator space: the gap is real. The English-first internet doesn't have a monopoly on knowledge worth paying for.

## Issue 77: What Polymarket Taught Me About Decisions
**Subject:** What prediction markets taught me about thinking
**Preview text:** Not investing advice. A framework for making better decisions under uncertainty.

I've been using Polymarket for a few months. For those who don't know it: it's a prediction market where you bet real money on the outcomes of real-world events. Elections, sports, economic indicators, regulatory decisions. The price of a contract reflects the crowd's collective estimate of the probability of that outcome.

I'm not writing this to tell you how to trade it. I'm writing this because using it changed how I think about uncertainty, and that's been more valuable than any return.

The first thing it taught me: my confidence is often disconnected from my accuracy. I would look at a market, have a strong opinion, and then watch the resolution and realize I had been overconfident. This isn't unique to me. It's a documented feature of human cognition. We feel more certain than the evidence supports. Polymarket gives you a place where that overconfidence has a direct cost, which means you feel it and learn from it faster than you would in regular life.

The second thing: probability is not binary. Before I started thinking in probabilities, I thought about events in outcomes. This will happen, or it won't. Polymarket forces you to think in percentages. Is this a 70% likely event or a 55% likely event? That question sounds small but it changes everything. A 70% outcome fails 30% of the time. If you treat 70% as certain and it fails, you're surprised. If you treat it as 70% and it fails, you update. The latter is much better for building accurate mental models over time.

The third thing: most markets are efficient enough that finding real edges is hard. The cases where I've done well were when I had specific local knowledge the market didn't fully price in. Not clever reasoning from public information. Actual knowledge of something others didn't know or weren't weighing correctly. This is the same principle that applies to business: generic strategy rarely beats someone with deep domain knowledge in a specific area.

What I've taken from this into how I run the business: I try to assign rough probabilities to decisions before I make them. Not formally, but approximately. I'm about 65% confident this pricing will convert. I think this content format has an 80% chance of outperforming the last one. When the outcome resolves, I compare. Over time, the calibration improves.

The practical habit I recommend: when you're about to make a decision, write down your confidence level as a percentage. Not "I think this will work" but "I think this has a 70% chance of working." After a month of doing this, look at your accuracy. Most people are surprised.

What's a decision you're making right now where you're treating a 60% probability like it's a 95% one?

## Issue 78: The Content Calendar I Actually Follow
**Subject:** My actual content calendar (not the aspirational one)
**Preview text:** What I planned vs. what I shipped. The real system underneath.

I've planned four content calendars this year. I've followed parts of all of them and the whole of none of them. This is not a confession of failure. It's a description of how a content system actually works when you're building a business at the same time as building an audience.

Here's what I've figured out actually works for me.

The aspirational calendar is for planning. The operational calendar is for executing. These are different documents and they need to be. The aspirational calendar tells me what I want to produce in a month. The operational calendar tells me what I'm making this week, broken down by platform and format. I stopped trying to make one document do both jobs.

My weekly operational structure looks like this. One newsletter issue, written on Sunday or Monday. Three to five Instagram posts, created in batches. TikTok scripts written in batches of ten when I'm in the right headspace, filmed when I have a clear two-hour block. One piece of "anchor" content per week, which is the thing I'd make even if I was only making one thing.

The anchor concept has been the most useful change I've made. When the week gets hard, I don't ask which of the ten things on my list to do. I ask: did I make the one anchor piece? If yes, the week is not a failure, regardless of what else shipped or didn't.

I keep a content backlog, not a content calendar in the traditional sense. The backlog is a running list of ideas, hooks, and angles organized by platform. When I sit down to create, I don't decide what to make. I pull from the backlog. The decision was already made. This is critical for ADHD-brained people because decision fatigue at the moment of creation kills output.

What I don't do: I don't plan content more than two weeks out. Things change. A trend appears. A product ships and changes what's relevant. Planning too far ahead creates a false obligation to content that's no longer the best use of that time slot.

The platform rhythm that's worked for me: Instagram is where I build the brand. TikTok is where I find new people. The newsletter is where I deepen the relationship. Email converts. Shorts finds. Instagram holds. That's the model.

The most honest thing I can tell you about a content calendar: the goal is not to execute it perfectly. The goal is to ship consistently enough that you build a feedback loop. Imperfect and consistent beats perfect and sporadic every time.

What's the one content piece per week you'd make even if you were only making one? That's your anchor. Start there.

## Issue 79: How I Built a Store With Zero Paid Traffic
**Subject:** Zero paid traffic, first sales. Here's how.
**Preview text:** What actually drives organic buyers when you're starting from nothing.

I haven't spent a dollar on ads for Behike. Every sale I've made has come from organic traffic. I want to be specific about how that happened because "build organic traffic" is advice that means nothing without the mechanics underneath it.

The first thing that drove traffic was content that solved specific, searchable problems. Not broad content about "AI for entrepreneurs." Specific content: how to use this tool for this task, what I learned from this experience, the exact steps I took to do this thing. Specific content ranks better, converts better, and attracts the right person. Broad content attracts volume that doesn't buy.

The second thing: I treated every piece of content as a funnel entry, not an end point. A TikTok or Instagram post that doesn't have a clear next step is a dead end. Every piece of content I make points somewhere. To the newsletter, to a free resource, to a product page. The path has to be clear or people don't take it, not because they don't want to but because they're busy and they need to be guided.

The third thing: the free products I offered were actually good. The lead magnets, the checklists, the free guides. I see a lot of creators treat free products as disposable content. If the free thing is weak, the person who downloads it doesn't trust the paid thing enough to buy it. The free thing is your first impression as a seller. It needs to be better than expected.

The fourth thing: I showed the work publicly. Not the highlight reel. The actual process. What I was building, what was working, what wasn't. People follow processes they can learn from more than they follow results they can only admire. The builder content outperformed the wins content consistently.

Specific platforms that drove meaningful traffic for me: Instagram Reels for reach, the newsletter for conversion, direct DM conversations with people who engaged with my content for initial sales. Not bots, not mass outreach. Actual replies to people who commented or messaged first.

The thing that didn't work: posting without a strategy and hoping the algorithm would surface it to buyers. Posting into a void, even consistently, doesn't build an audience. The content needs to be pointed at someone specific with a specific problem. When I got specific about who I was talking to and what problem I was solving, the content started to do actual work.

The honest caveat: organic takes time. The compounding effect is real but it's slow at the start. I have zero regrets about skipping ads in month one because the feedback from organic traffic tells you things that paid traffic hides.

What's the most specific version of your audience you could describe right now, and is your content actually pointed at them?

## Issue 80: Month 1 Numbers. Honest Revenue Report.
**Subject:** Month 1 numbers. Here's what actually happened.
**Preview text:** Real numbers, real context. No vanity metrics.

I'm going to give you the actual numbers from my first month selling digital products. Not the best month. Not a curated success story. The first month, with context.

Total revenue: under $500. I'm not going to give a precise number because the exact figure isn't the point. The point is what it means and what it tells me about what to do next.

Products sold: 4 different products moved. The other 30 in the catalog had zero sales. This is normal and I didn't panic about it, but it's important to name it honestly. Having 34 products doesn't mean 34 income streams. It means 34 bets, a few of which will hit.

Where the sales came from: three sales came from people who found me through content. Two came from direct DM conversations. One came from a bundle. None came from someone discovering the store by browsing. Every sale was downstream of a relationship or a content piece.

What the data tells me: the products that sold have one thing in common. They were specific enough that the person reading the listing could immediately see themselves as the target customer. The products that didn't sell are either too broad, aimed at too generic a person, or not priced and positioned clearly enough. The product itself is rarely the problem. The presentation almost always is.

The honest context: under $500 in month one is not the number that changes my life. But it is the number that proves the model works. Before month one, I had a hypothesis. After month one, I have data. The data says: specific products with clear positioning convert. Broad products don't. Content drives traffic. DMs convert traffic.

What I'm changing in month two: rewriting the listings for the five products closest to converting based on engagement, doubling down on the formats that drove traffic, and adding one new product in a category that showed demand based on comments and questions.

What I'm not changing: the organic-first approach, the content calendar structure, the newsletter. These are the things that built the first $500 and they're the foundations the next $5,000 will sit on.

The number that matters more than revenue in month one is evidence. Evidence that someone found what I built, read what I wrote, decided it was worth paying for, and bought it. That happened. That's the signal.

What would it mean to you to make your first $100 from something you built? Not as a ceiling. As a proof point. That first sale is what I'd focus on if I were starting today.
