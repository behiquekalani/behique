### Thread 1 — The Solopreneur Automation Stack: What to Automate First

**Tweet 1 (Hook):** The first thing to automate is not the flashiest thing. It is the task you do most often that requires the least judgment.

**Tweet 2:** Most solopreneurs automate in the wrong order. They build complex workflows before automating the basics. The basics are where the hours are.

**Tweet 3:** Tier 1 automations: things that happen on a schedule. Email delivery sequences, social media scheduling, weekly reports, invoice reminders. These should be zero-touch within 90 days of starting.

**Tweet 4:** Tier 2 automations: things triggered by an action. Buyer gets product delivery. New subscriber gets welcome sequence. Form submission creates a task. These free you from monitoring.

**Tweet 5:** Tier 3 automations: things that require judgment but follow a pattern. Lead scoring, content repurposing, research summaries. AI handles these now. Three months ago they required a human.

**Tweet 6:** The ROI calculation for any automation: how many minutes does this task take multiplied by how often it happens. If an automation saves 30 minutes per week, it saves 26 hours per year.

**Tweet 7:** 26 hours per year at your consulting rate of $100/hour is $2,600 in recovered time. Most automations cost less than $20/month to run. The math is not close.

**Tweet 8:** The mistake: spending 8 hours building an automation that saves 2 hours per year. Match automation investment to time savings. High frequency tasks first.

**Tweet 9:** Tools: n8n for workflows (self-hosted, free). Make (Integromat) for simpler setups. Zapier only if you need the premium integrations and can afford the cost.

**Tweet 10:** Summary: Automate in order of frequency, not complexity. The boring tasks compounding over a year free more time than the complex automations that feel impressive but rarely run.

### Thread 2 — Build a Content Machine That Runs Without You

**Tweet 1 (Hook):** Your content should be publishing while you sleep. If it requires you to be awake to post it, you have a content job, not a content system.

**Tweet 2:** The content machine has three stages: ideation, creation, distribution. All three can be semi-automated. The only part requiring full human attention is the original insight.

**Tweet 3:** Ideation automation: set up RSS feeds of sources you trust. Use an AI tool to summarize weekly. Your raw material arrives in your inbox. You never have to search for ideas.

**Tweet 4:** Creation: write the long-form piece manually. Then use AI to extract tweets, LinkedIn posts, carousel scripts, and email subject lines. One human input. Multiple AI outputs.

**Tweet 5:** Distribution: schedule everything through Buffer, Typefully, or n8n. Content scheduled on Monday publishes all week. You do not touch social media daily because the system does.

**Tweet 6:** The newsletter automation: write it. Hit send. The email service handles delivery, segmentation, and analytics. Beehiiv and ConvertKit do this for free or cheap at low volume.

**Tweet 7:** Repurposing automation: use a simple n8n workflow to detect when you publish a new piece and trigger a reminder to create the derivative formats. The robot tells you when to work.

**Tweet 8:** The advanced layer: AI reads your best-performing tweets, identifies patterns, suggests next week's angles. You are not guessing. You are iterating on what already works.

**Tweet 9:** What cannot be automated: your perspective, your lived experience, your specific opinions on hard questions. That is what people follow you for. Protect that time. Automate everything around it.

**Tweet 10:** Summary: Automate ideation inputs, distribution outputs, and derivative creation. Create the core content manually. The system multiplies the manual input without replacing it.

### Thread 5 — The Automation Mindset: How to See Every Task as a System

**Tweet 1 (Hook):** Automation starts before you open any tool. It starts when you begin looking at your work as a series of inputs, processes, and outputs that can be defined and repeated.

**Tweet 2:** Most people automate at the wrong level. They try to automate the output before they understand the process. You cannot automate what you have not defined.

**Tweet 3:** The first step: for any task you want to automate, write out every step as if you were giving instructions to someone who has never done it. If you cannot write it down, you cannot automate it.

**Tweet 4:** The second step: identify the trigger. What event starts this task? A form submission, a new email, a calendar event, a new follower, a completed purchase. Every automation has a trigger.

**Tweet 5:** The third step: identify the output. What does done look like? A sent email, an updated database entry, a Slack message, a scheduled post. Clear output, clear automation.

**Tweet 6:** The fourth step: choose the minimum viable tool. Do not build a complex workflow for a simple task. Zapier, Make, or even a scheduled email does most of what you actually need.

**Tweet 7:** The maintenance question: most automations break once per quarter due to API changes or tool updates. Budget 30 minutes per month to check that your automations are still running. This is not overhead. It is asset maintenance.

**Tweet 8:** The leverage mindset: every hour spent building an automation that saves two hours per month pays back in six months and then earns forever. This is the highest ROI work a solopreneur can do.

**Tweet 9:** The systems audit: once per quarter, list every task you did manually last month. Circle the ones you did more than four times. Those are your next automations.

**Tweet 10:** Summary: Automation is a way of seeing, not just a set of tools. See every repeated task as a system. Define it. Trigger it. Automate it. Recover the time. Repeat.
