## Issue 26: How to Build a Product Without an Audience

*The Behike Brief | Week 26*

---

### Opening

Everyone who tells you to "build an audience first" already has one. That advice is useless when you are starting with zero followers and need revenue to justify continuing. I did not wait for an audience. I built the product, found where the audience already existed, and got in front of them there. The cold start problem is real. It is also solvable.

---

### Main Topic: The Cold Start Problem for Digital Creators

The standard creator playbook is: post content for 6 months, build an audience, then launch a product to that audience. The math works. But most people quit before month 6 because there is no revenue to sustain the effort. The strategy is correct in theory and useless in practice for anyone who needs results before 6 months.

There is a different approach. You find where your audience already is, provide value there, and bring them back to your product. No audience required.

**Where buyers exist without following you:**

Gumroad has organic search traffic. People go to Gumroad directly looking for products. If your product title matches a real search term and your preview page is good, you will get sales from people who have never heard of you. I know this because my first few sales on Gumroad came from people who found the product through the Gumroad discovery feed. They did not know my name. They knew what problem they needed to solve.

Reddit is a buyer-rich environment when used correctly. Not spammed, not self-promoted. I am talking about genuinely answering questions in subreddits where your product's topic lives. Someone asks how to set up n8n for the first time. You write a genuinely useful 200-word answer. You mention at the bottom that you made a guide for exactly this if they want to go deeper. That is not marketing. That is providing value and offering a next step. Communities like r/Entrepreneur, r/digitalnomad, r/SideProject, and any niche-specific sub relevant to your product are all full of people actively looking for solutions.

YouTube comments are another. Find videos on your product's topic with active comment sections. The people commenting are actively engaged and interested. Useful comments that solve a problem people mention in the thread can send traffic to your profile and from there to your product.

Pinterest works particularly well for digital products with a visual component. Canva templates, study guides, productivity tools. Create pins that link directly to your Gumroad page. Pinterest has long content shelf life. A pin can generate traffic for 18 months with no additional effort.

**The niche sniper method:**

Instead of building a broad audience, pick one hyper-specific pain point and own it completely. Not "productivity for entrepreneurs." Something like: "eBay margin calculation for new resellers." Or "n8n workflows for solopreneurs who do not code."

The specificity does three things. It makes your product easier to find because the search terms are less competitive. It makes the buying decision faster because the relevance is obvious. And it makes word of mouth more likely because people share things that solve a very specific problem they struggled with, not things that are generally useful.

The niche sniper approach is: find a pain, check that people are actively searching for it, create the most specific product that solves it, and go to where those people already are.

**How to validate before building:**

Before building the product, validate demand. Post about the topic in one relevant Reddit community and measure the response. If the post gets upvotes and comments, the pain is real. If it gets ignored, either the topic is wrong or the community is wrong.

Check Gumroad for existing products in the niche. If there are products with reviews, demand exists. If the products have low ratings or outdated content, there is a gap you can fill.

Check Google Trends for the search term. Stable or rising over 12 months is good. Declining means you are entering a shrinking market.

Total validation time: about 2 hours. Do it before spending 20 hours building.

**The content-product flywheel without an audience:**

Post one piece of genuinely useful content per platform per week. Not promotional. Useful. The content solves a small version of the problem your product solves completely. At the end of the content, mention the product for anyone who wants the complete solution.

This is how you build an audience while selling to the people who find you early. You do not need to choose between building and selling. They are the same activity when the content is genuinely useful.

---

### Tool Spotlight: Niche Sniper Research Method (Gumroad + Reddit + Google Trends)

This is not one tool. It is a research sequence using three free tools to validate a product idea in under two hours.

Step 1: Go to trends.google.com. Search your product topic. Check interest over 12 months. Check related queries. The "rising" queries section often shows underserved pain points that people are searching for but not finding good answers to.

Step 2: Go to Gumroad and search the same term. Look at what exists, how many reviews the products have, and what buyers are saying in the reviews. Bad reviews on existing products are a product brief for you.

Step 3: Go to Reddit and search the term. Read the top posts. Read the comments. Notice what questions people keep asking and what existing answers are missing or incomplete.

After those three steps, you know: whether demand exists, what the competition looks like, and what gap you can fill. Total time: 90 minutes. That is the niche sniper sequence.

Verdict: it is not glamorous. It is not a tool you pay for. It is a research habit. The creators I know who consistently ship products that sell all do some version of this before building.

---

### What I Built This Week

I finished the Niche Sniper Guide, which walks through the exact research sequence above with screenshots, example searches, and a template for recording what you find. It also includes a scoring rubric for deciding whether a niche is worth entering. I used this exact method before building most of the products in my catalog. The guide documents the process so someone starting from zero can validate their first product idea without wasting weeks building something nobody wants.

---

### One Link Worth Your Time

"The Mom Test" by Rob Fitzpatrick. It is technically about customer interviews for startups but the core principle applies directly to product validation for solo creators. People will tell you what you want to hear. This book shows you how to ask questions that actually reveal whether someone would pay for what you are building. Short book, one sitting, worth rereading.

---

### Closing

You do not need an audience before your first sale. You need to find where your future buyers already exist and give them something useful there. Build a product that solves one specific problem. Go where that problem lives. The audience follows the product when the product earns it.

## Issue 27: The Bilingual Advantage Most Creators Ignore

*The Behike Brief | Week 27*

---

### Opening

I am from Puerto Rico. My first language is Spanish. For most of my online work I have defaulted to English because that is where the tutorials are, that is where the audience seems larger, and that is what most creators who I follow use. Then I looked at the actual numbers for the Spanish-language AI and tech content space. The gap is significant. The opportunity is real.

This is not about serving a smaller market. It is about being first in a market that is growing faster than the English one.

---

### Main Topic: The Spanish-Language AI Content Gap

There are approximately 500 million native Spanish speakers in the world. Latin America has among the highest rates of social media usage per capita globally. The region also has one of the fastest-growing populations of young people interested in technology, entrepreneurship, and AI tools.

Despite all of this, Spanish-language content about practical AI tools is years behind English-language content. The tutorials that exist are often machine-translated, outdated, or surface-level. There are almost no Spanish-language newsletters covering AI tools for creators and solopreneurs at the level of depth that exists in English.

That is the gap. And it is large.

**Where the opportunity actually is:**

YouTube in Spanish is the clearest signal. Channels covering AI tools in Spanish that were started in 2023 or 2024 are growing significantly faster than comparable English channels started at the same time. The competition for search terms like "herramientas de IA para creadores" or "automatizacion con n8n en espanol" is dramatically lower than the equivalent English terms. The search volume is growing.

On Instagram and TikTok, Spanish-language AI content consistently outperforms English content on a per-follower basis for engagement. Spanish-speaking audiences engage more with educational content from creators who share their background and speak directly to their context. The personal connection matters more in Latin American social culture.

For digital products, the gap is even more obvious. Go to Gumroad and search for AI guides in Spanish. Almost nothing. The guides that exist are often translated versions of English content that do not address the specific context of Spanish-speaking creators. There is almost no original Spanish-language material.

**Why most bilingual creators do not take this seriously:**

The default assumption is that English content reaches a larger total market, so Spanish is a smaller opportunity. This logic ignores two things. First, the competition for attention in English is orders of magnitude higher. Second, being one of the few trusted voices in a large underserved market is more valuable than being the 500th voice in a saturated one.

I would rather be the fourth-best Spanish-language AI content creator than the 400th best English-language one. The ceiling is the same. The starting position is completely different.

**How to do it without double the work:**

The efficient approach is to create in English, then adapt for Spanish. Not translate. Adapt. Translation is mechanical. Adaptation means changing examples, changing cultural references, and adjusting the framing for a different context.

A guide about building a Shopify store written for a US audience uses dollar amounts, American payment processors, and US shipping options. Adapted for Latin America, it uses local payment methods, regional shipping realities, and country-specific platform considerations. The core knowledge is the same. The implementation details are different. Getting those details right is what earns trust with Spanish-speaking audiences.

**Content that crosses language without needing translation:**

Some content formats work in both markets with minimal modification. Case studies about real numbers and real results translate well because numbers are numbers in any language. Process guides with clear steps translate well because the logic is universal. Opinion pieces and cultural commentary do not translate at all. They need to be written natively for each market.

The 80/20 for a bilingual content strategy: create long-form educational content in English, adapt the most practical guides for Spanish, and build a Spanish-language email list separately. The Spanish list will be smaller but will likely have higher open rates and engagement.

---

### Tool Spotlight: DeepL vs Google Translate vs ChatGPT for Content Localization

I tested all three on the same 500-word content excerpt to see which produces output I could actually use as a starting point for Spanish adaptation.

DeepL: the best pure translation output. The sentences sound natural and the vocabulary choices are closer to how people actually write in Spanish, not how they write in a textbook. The free tier handles 500 words at a time. Paid tier starts around $9/month for higher limits.

Google Translate: fast and free with no word limit. The output quality has improved significantly in the past two years. Good for quick checks and understanding foreign-language content. For anything you plan to publish, the sentences often feel slightly mechanical in a way that native speakers notice immediately.

ChatGPT: the most useful for adaptation rather than translation. When I ask it to translate and adapt for a Puerto Rican or Mexican audience with specific context notes, the output is closer to how I would actually write. It can adjust formality level, swap Americanisms for regional equivalents, and explain why it made specific choices. Slower than DeepL for pure translation but more useful for the full localization task.

My actual workflow: DeepL for a first-pass translation, then ChatGPT with a prompt specifying the target region and tone to adapt the output. Total time added per piece: 30 to 45 minutes for a 600-word article. That is a reasonable cost for reaching a second market.

Verdict: use DeepL for translation, ChatGPT for adaptation. Google Translate is fine for personal understanding but not for publication.

---

### What I Built This Week

I created a Spanish content batch of 10 Instagram posts adapted from existing English content in the catalog. The process of adapting them clarified exactly where the English content was too US-specific and needed real changes. Three of the posts had to be substantially rewritten, not just translated. The process also identified two product ideas that would serve the Spanish-language market better than anything adapted from English originals.

---

### One Link Worth Your Time

The We Are Social Digital Report (wearesocial.com/reports). Updated annually. The Latin America section shows social media usage stats, platform breakdowns, and content consumption patterns by country. The 2025 report confirms that YouTube, Instagram, and WhatsApp dominate in Latin America, and that educational content consumption is above global average. Real data for anyone planning a bilingual content strategy.

---

### Closing

English-first is a habit, not a strategy. If you speak Spanish and you are creating AI or business content, you are sitting on a real advantage that most creators in your space are not using. The Spanish-language market for this content is large, underserved, and growing. Being bilingual is not a side feature of who you are. It is a legitimate competitive edge if you use it.

## Issue 28: One-Person Business Math: What Does $10K/Month Actually Take?

*The Behike Brief | Week 28*

---

### Opening

Most people never do the actual math. They say "$10K a month" the way people say "I want to be rich." It sounds like a goal. It is not a plan. When I actually mapped out the numbers, I realized that $10K a month is achievable with fewer customers than I assumed. The math also showed me exactly where I was wasting effort and what I needed to build next.

The math is the plan. Let me show you mine.

---

### Main Topic: Realistic Math for $10K/Month as a Solo Digital Product Creator

$10,000 per month is $120,000 per year. That is a real income for one person. The question is what combination of products, prices, and customers gets you there, and what is actually feasible as a solo operator with no team.

Before I break this down, one important framing: $10K/month is not a stable plateau you reach and maintain without effort. It is an average across a range of months. Some months will be $6K. Some will be $14K. Plan for averages, not for smooth lines.

**Scenario 1: The pure digital product catalog**

If your average product price is $12 and your average margin is 70% after platform fees, each sale nets you about $8.40.

To hit $10,000 in net revenue, you need approximately 1,190 sales per month.

1,190 sales per month from a catalog of 15 products is about 79 sales per product per month. That requires consistent traffic to each product page. With Gumroad organic, social content driving traffic, and an email list, this is achievable but requires 6 to 12 months to build the traffic.

More realistic at 12 months: 300 to 500 sales per month from a catalog that is still building traction. That is $2,500 to $4,200/month. Real but not $10K.

**Scenario 2: Mixed price point catalog with a high-ticket offer**

This is where the math changes significantly. Add one $97 offer to the catalog. If you sell 20 of those per month alongside 500 lower-price product sales, the numbers look like this.

500 sales at $12 average: $4,200 net
20 sales at $97 (70% margin): $1,358 net
Total: approximately $5,558/month

Add a service offer at $500 (two clients per month):
Total: $6,558/month

Still not $10K. But you can see the path. The variables are: number of products, traffic volume, average price point, and whether you have any service income or high-ticket offer.

**Scenario 3: The fear funnel model**

The fear funnel is a specific content strategy where your content addresses fears that your audience already has, and your products solve those fears. The conversion rates on fear-based funnels are higher than educational content funnels because urgency is built in.

This model works in three layers. Free content establishes the fear as real and establishes your credibility. A $7 to $17 product provides a first solution and proves you deliver. A $47 to $97 product provides the complete system. You are selling the same audience three times at increasing price points, with each purchase building toward the next.

A working fear funnel with consistent traffic can hit 10 to 15% conversion on the entry product and 20 to 30% upsell rates to the mid-tier product. With those conversion rates and 3,000 monthly visitors to the top of the funnel, the math approaches $10K. It takes 12 to 18 months to build 3,000 consistent monthly visitors without paid ads.

**What actually gets you to $10K faster:**

The fastest path to $10K for a solo creator is a combination model. Digital products provide passive base revenue. A service offering provides high-ticket income. The service income funds the time to build the product catalog. The product catalog eventually replaces the service income.

In concrete terms: 3 to 5 product sales per day averaging $20 ($21K/year), plus 1 to 2 consulting or service clients per month at $500 to $1,000 per project. Combined, that is approximately $8,000 to $13,000 per month. That is a realistic 18-month target for someone starting from zero who ships consistently.

**The honest constraint:**

The bottleneck is not the math. It is traffic. Every scenario above assumes a specific number of monthly visitors. Building organic traffic to 3,000+ monthly visitors without paid ads takes 12 to 18 months of consistent content creation. If you want to compress that timeline, you need either paid ads, a strong partnership or referral, or a piece of content that goes viral. None of those are reliably controllable.

The business model is sound. The timeline is longer than most people want to hear.

---

### Tool Spotlight: The Revenue Spreadsheet Approach

There is no special tool for this. The most useful thing I have done for revenue planning is a simple Google Sheet with four tabs.

Tab 1: Product catalog with current price, monthly sales, and net revenue per product.
Tab 2: Traffic sources by channel with weekly visitor counts and source breakdowns.
Tab 3: Monthly revenue tracker with actual vs target comparison.
Tab 4: 12-month projection with editable assumptions for sales growth.

The spreadsheet forces you to make your assumptions explicit. When you write down "I expect 50 sales per month for this product," you have to confront whether that is realistic given your current traffic. The projection tab shows you exactly when you hit $5K, $7K, and $10K under your current assumptions.

I update it every Sunday. Takes 10 minutes. It has prevented me from building products that would not have moved the needle even if they sold well.

No paid tool does this better than a spreadsheet you build yourself, because the assumptions need to match your specific situation.

Verdict: build it yourself. Use a template if you want a starting point but make sure you understand every cell.

It also has a section for tracking service income separately from product income. Building this was more clarifying than any business planning session I have done. Seeing the actual numbers in one place answered questions I had been avoiding.

$10K a month is not a mystery. It is a math problem with known variables. Traffic, price point, conversion rate, catalog size. Run the numbers for your specific situation. You will either confirm the plan is solid or you will find the constraint that needs to change. Either outcome is better than guessing.
