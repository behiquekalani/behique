### Thread 1 — Why Your Product Is Probably Underpriced

**Tweet 1 (Hook):** If you spent more than ten hours building a digital product and you're selling it for $9, the problem is not your audience. The problem is your pricing model.

**Tweet 2:** Underpricing is not a generosity strategy. It's a fear strategy. You charge low because you're afraid someone will say it's not worth it.

**Tweet 3:** Here's what low pricing actually signals: low confidence. The buyer reads the price before they read the description. A low price primes them to look for reasons it's cheap.

**Tweet 4:** The counterintuitive reality: raising the price often increases sales when the product genuinely delivers. People buy differently at $97 than at $7. They invest more attention. They get more out of it.

**Tweet 5:** Low prices attract the highest-friction customers. They buy, they demand support, they want refunds, and they leave negative reviews because they expected more even though they paid almost nothing.

**Tweet 6:** Higher prices attract buyers who have already decided the transformation is worth pursuing. They're not testing. They're committing. They follow through. They get results. They become case studies.

**Tweet 7:** The cost of underpricing is not just revenue. It's your positioning. You train the market to see your work as entry-level. That belief is hard to correct later.

**Tweet 8:** Do the math on your time. If it took you 40 hours to create a guide and you sell 100 copies at $9, you made $900. Divide by 40 and you're at $22.50 per hour before platform fees. That is not a sustainable model.

**Tweet 9:** Price based on the value of the outcome, not the cost of production. A guide that helps someone avoid a $10,000 mistake is not a $19 product.

**Tweet 10:** Test a higher price on your next launch. Not 10% higher. Double it. Then watch the conversion rate. You may find it barely changes while your revenue doubles.

**Tweet 11:** The question to ask before setting any price: if a stranger with the exact problem this product solves saw this price, would they think "that seems fair for that outcome"? Start there.

### Thread 2 — The Pricing Psychology That Actually Works

**Tweet 1 (Hook):** The psychology of pricing is not about tricks. It's about context. Every price feels different depending on what it's next to and how it's framed.

**Tweet 2:** Anchoring is the most powerful pricing principle. The first number someone sees sets the expectation. Everything after is measured against it.

**Tweet 3:** If your product costs $97, lead with what it would cost to get the same outcome through a service. "A consultant charges $300/hour for this. This guide gives you the framework for $97." That's anchoring.

**Tweet 4:** Tiered pricing creates choice, and choice reduces the cost of the decision. When someone can pick between Standard and Pro, they stop asking "should I buy this" and start asking "which tier is right for me."

**Tweet 5:** Three tiers work better than two. The middle option gets chosen most often. The high tier makes the middle look reasonable. The low tier creates a floor that the buyer usually doesn't want to occupy.

**Tweet 6:** Round numbers versus charm pricing. $100 feels like a round, premium number. $97 feels like a sale. Neither is wrong. Match the choice to your brand positioning. Premium brands use round numbers.

**Tweet 7:** Scarcity and urgency are real levers but they only work if they're real. False countdown timers train your audience not to trust you. Genuine limited cohorts or genuine price increases work.

**Tweet 8:** Framing the investment as daily cost works for higher-ticket products. "Less than a cup of coffee per day for a year." That is not manipulation. It's a perspective shift that removes the sticker shock.

**Tweet 9:** The language around the price matters as much as the number. "Investment" rather than "cost." "Access" rather than "download." Small word changes shift the entire mental model.

**Tweet 10:** Social proof placed near the price is high-impact placement. The moment someone sees the number, they look for evidence it was worth it. Put the evidence there.

**Tweet 11:** Pricing psychology is not about getting people to buy things they shouldn't buy. It's about removing the friction from a decision that was already leaning yes.

### Thread 3 — How to Price a New Product With No Track Record

**Tweet 1 (Hook):** You don't need a track record to price confidently. You need a clear outcome and a clear audience. The price comes from those two things.

**Tweet 2:** The mistake when launching for the first time is defaulting to cheap to "get reviews." You can test a higher price and still get reviews. The price does not determine whether buyers leave feedback.

**Tweet 3:** Start with the outcome. What does the buyer have after this product that they didn't have before. How much would that outcome cost to achieve another way.

**Tweet 4:** That alternative cost is your ceiling. Price somewhere between "clearly a steal" and "obviously reasonable." You don't have to be at the ceiling. You have to be within a range that makes sense.

**Tweet 5:** Research comparable products. Not to copy their price. To understand the market's existing expectation. You can price above the market if your positioning justifies it.

**Tweet 6:** Beta pricing is a legitimate tactic. "First 20 buyers get it at $37. Price goes to $67 after launch week." This rewards early buyers, generates initial reviews, and creates momentum.

**Tweet 7:** Announce the price increase in advance. Not as a manipulation tactic. As information. Some buyers will decide based on the deadline. That's their choice with real information.

**Tweet 8:** Collect feedback from the first buyers aggressively. Not to validate the price. To understand what they valued most, what surprised them, what they would have paid for. That data shapes your next product.

**Tweet 9:** Don't let the first price be permanent. Your first price is a hypothesis. After your first 20 or 30 sales, you have real data. Adjust based on evidence.

**Tweet 10:** If nobody buys, the problem is usually the positioning or the audience, not the price. Lower the price only after you've verified that the offer itself is landing.

**Tweet 11:** Your first sale at a real price matters more psychologically than any amount of validation. It confirms that someone found it worth it. That belief compounds in how you build and price everything after.

### Thread 4 — Bundles, Upgrades, and Recurring Revenue

**Tweet 1 (Hook):** A one-time product sale is the beginning of a revenue relationship, not the end of it. Most digital product sellers leave 60% of their revenue on the table by not thinking past the first transaction.

**Tweet 2:** The person who just bought something from you is the most likely person to buy something else from you. They trusted you once. That trust is now easier to extend.

**Tweet 3:** Bundling: take two or three products that solve related problems and sell them together at a discount. The buyer gets more value. You increase average order value without creating new products.

**Tweet 4:** The bundle discount should feel real. If the individual products are $37 each and the bundle is $69, the buyer is getting something. If the bundle is $72 and the individual prices are fake, they'll feel it.

**Tweet 5:** Order bump: a small add-on offered at checkout. "Add the templates pack for $17." This captures buyers who are already committed and willing to spend more with one more click.

**Tweet 6:** The upgrade or pro tier: the same product with more access. Bonus content, community access, a live session, a worksheet pack. Price it at 1.5x to 2x the base. Some buyers will take it.

**Tweet 7:** Email sequences that offer related products after purchase. Not immediate upsells. Three to five emails that deliver value first, then introduce the next relevant product naturally.

**Tweet 8:** Recurring revenue for digital products usually comes from one of two places: a community or a new content drip. Both require ongoing maintenance. Price accordingly.

**Tweet 9:** A subscription at $19/month is fine if you can retain subscribers for six months on average. That's $114 per subscriber. Price the subscription based on what you can realistically deliver monthly.

**Tweet 10:** The highest-leverage move for a small library of products is a vault subscription. One price for access to everything you've made. Easy to explain. Easy to justify. Scales without new creation.

**Tweet 11:** Map out what a customer who buys everything from you would spend. That number is your potential LTV. Build toward it intentionally with every product you add.

### Thread 5 — When to Discount and When to Never Discount

**Tweet 1 (Hook):** Discounting is the most misused pricing tool in digital products. Done wrong, it trains your audience to wait for sales and destroys the perceived value of everything you sell.

**Tweet 2:** The rule: discount for new buyers. Never for existing buyers. Existing buyers paid full price and earned the right to feel like they made the right choice.

**Tweet 3:** Launch week pricing is not a discount. It's a reward for early action. Frame it that way. "First week pricing. Goes up after launch." That's a policy, not a markdown.

**Tweet 4:** Holiday sales work if they're infrequent. One sale per year, at a meaningful discount, with clear framing, generates real revenue without conditioning buyers to wait.

**Tweet 5:** Black Friday is the one exception most digital product sellers can use. The entire market is in "buy" mode. A 30-40% discount with a clear window is legitimate and effective.

**Tweet 6:** Do not discount because sales slowed down. That is the wrong signal to send. If sales slowed, the problem is distribution or positioning. Discounting addresses neither.

**Tweet 7:** Do not respond to individual requests for discounts by default. It devalues the product for everyone who paid full price. Have a policy and stick to it.

**Tweet 8:** If you want to offer discounts to specific groups, build it into your positioning. Student pricing. Early supporter pricing. Alumni pricing. These are structured, not reactive.

**Tweet 9:** Discounting by removing price is the lazy version. Discounting by adding value is better. "Same price, now includes the bonus module." You maintain the price floor while improving the offer.

**Tweet 10:** The brands with strong pricing power never discount defensively. They raise prices as demand increases. They discount once a year with intention. Everyone else chases the market with markdowns.

**Tweet 11:** Build your pricing policy once. Write it down. Apply it consistently. The consistency is what creates the perception of value over time. Inconsistency is what erodes it.
