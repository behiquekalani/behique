## Script 69.1 — Notion for Your Business

**Hook (0-3s)**
"Your business doesn't need more apps. It needs one connected system."

**Body (3-50s)**
Notion is not just a note-taking tool. It's the operating system for a lean business.

Point 1: Most solopreneurs manage their business across 7 to 12 apps with no central hub. Tasks in one place, ideas in another, content in a third, client info in a spreadsheet. Notion consolidates this. One workspace, everything connected.

Point 2: The most important thing to build first is your content pipeline. A simple database with columns for idea, status, platform, draft, and scheduled date. Drag and drop your content from idea to live. You'll never lose a post idea again and you'll always know what's next.

Point 3: Build your product roadmap as a second database. Each product row contains the idea, status, price point, launch date, and link to the sales page. You can see your entire business at a glance.

Point 4: The mistake people make is building an over-engineered Notion setup before they have a business to run. Start with two databases. Add more only when the problem is clear. Complexity is the enemy of use.

Notion only works if you actually open it. Keep it simple enough to open daily.

**Caption**

## Script 69.2 — LinkedIn for Solopreneurs

**Hook (0-3s)**
"LinkedIn is the most underused platform in the creator economy. I'll prove it."

**Body (3-50s)**
LinkedIn reach is higher than any other platform right now and most creators aren't there.

Point 1: The average LinkedIn post from an active creator reaches 5 to 15 times more people than the same post on Instagram. Organic reach on LinkedIn is where Instagram was in 2013. The window is open. Most people haven't noticed.

Point 2: LinkedIn audiences are buyers. The demographic skews toward professionals who have income and are actively looking for tools, information, and services to make their work easier. That's the best audience you can have.

Point 3: The content that performs on LinkedIn is slightly longer than on other platforms. 150 to 300 word posts with a clear hook, a useful insight, and a soft call to action. No hashtag stuffing, no engagement bait. Just clear thinking from a person who knows what they're talking about.

Point 4: The best use of LinkedIn for solopreneurs is as a B2B channel. If you offer any service or product that helps businesses, professionals, or companies, LinkedIn should be your first platform, not your last.

LinkedIn's moment is right now. The people who build there over the next 18 months will be very hard to compete with in two years.

## Script 69.3 — Faceless Content Strategy

**Hook (0-3s)**
"You don't need to be on camera to build an audience."

**Body (3-50s)**
Faceless content is not a workaround. For some niches, it's the superior strategy.

Point 1: Faceless content focuses attention on the ideas, not the person. In niches where information is the product, removing the personal brand element can actually increase credibility. The content speaks for itself.

Point 2: The best faceless formats are screen recordings with voiceover, text-based videos with music, animated explainers, and written carousels. All of these can build an audience and drive product sales without a single face on screen.

Point 3: The downside of faceless content is that it's harder to build deep trust at the individual level. People buy from people. The workaround is consistent voice and perspective. Your writing style, the way you frame problems, your specific opinions. These become your identity even without a face.

Point 4: AI tools have made faceless video production 80 percent faster. Voiceover, b-roll, editing, captions. The production barrier that made faceless content laborious is mostly gone now.

Choose faceless if being on camera is a genuine blocker. Don't use it as a way to stay hidden if the real issue is fear.

## Script 69.4 — Repurposing One Video Into 10 Pieces

**Hook (0-3s)**
"I make one piece of content per week. I post it 10 different ways."

**Body (3-50s)**
Content repurposing is not about being lazy. It's about being smart with your best work.

Point 1: Start with a long-form anchor. A 10-minute YouTube video, a 2,000-word newsletter, or a 60-minute podcast. This is the root. Everything else is a branch cut from that root.

Point 2: From one long-form piece, you can extract 3 to 5 short-form clips for TikTok and Reels. You can pull the top insight for a Twitter thread. You can summarize it into a LinkedIn post. You can turn the framework into a Pinterest graphic. You can send the key section as an email to your list.

Point 3: The tools to do this have gotten dramatically faster. AI can help you find the clips, write the summaries, and reformat the structure. What used to take a team can be done solo in an afternoon.

Point 4: The same idea distributed across 5 platforms reaches 5 different audiences, some of whom will then discover the rest of your catalog. One idea, multiplied. The content budget is the same. The distribution is 10x.

Create once. Distribute everywhere. The work is already done.

## Script 69.5 — Why Most Online Businesses Fail

**Hook (0-3s)**
"Most online businesses don't fail because of competition. They fail before they have any."

**Body (3-50s)**
The real reasons are less dramatic and more preventable.

Point 1: The offer is never validated. Someone builds a product for weeks, launches it to a small email list, gets no sales, and concludes the market doesn't want it. In most cases, the market never got a real chance to see it. Validation happens before you build, not after.

Point 2: The creator confuses activity with progress. Posting on social media, redesigning the website, optimizing the Gumroad page. These feel like work. They're not the work. The work is the offer, the traffic, and the conversion. Everything else is support.

Point 3: Impatience kills more businesses than competition. Results in the creator economy are distributed over 12 to 24 months. Most people evaluate at month 2 and quit. The ones who stay until month 12 rarely quit because the math has changed by then.

Point 4: There is no system. Individual effort without a system doesn't scale and doesn't survive a bad week. Systems replace willpower. The businesses that survive are the ones with repeatable processes that run even when motivation is low.

Failure in this space is almost always a strategy problem, not a talent problem. The strategy is fixable.

**Caption**
Online businesses fail from unvalidated offers, vanity work, impatience, and no system. All of these are fixable before you start. Most people don't bother.
