## Post 24-1: The Business Model That Doesn't Require a Team, Office, or Inventory

You can sell something today that costs nothing to store, nothing to ship, and nothing to deliver.

It's not a secret. But most people treat it like it is.

Digital products.

A guide, a template, a framework, a course, a system. Something you create once and sell repeatedly.

The economics are unlike anything in physical commerce.

The cost to deliver the first copy: significant. Design, writing, recording, editing.

The cost to deliver the ten-thousandth copy: near zero.

The margin on a $97 digital product with no physical inventory is 80-95%.

Compare that to most physical product businesses running at 20-40% margins after costs.

The model that makes this accessible:

You have knowledge someone else needs. You package it clearly. You find the people who have the problem it solves. You sell them the answer.

No warehouse. No team. No monthly inventory risk.

The barrier to entry is effort, not capital.

Most people who could build a profitable digital product business don't because they underestimate the value of what they already know.

They don't have a knowledge problem. They have a packaging problem.

## Post 24-2: Why Digital Products Are the Highest-Leverage Thing Most Professionals Can Build

The traditional income model for professionals: trade time for money.

You learn a skill. You perform that skill for clients. You get paid for your time.

The ceiling on this model is your available hours.

Digital products break that ceiling.

You spend time once to create a product. You spend near-zero time on every subsequent sale.

A professional who bills $150/hour can sell 5 hours of work to one client for $750.

Or they can package those same 5 hours of knowledge into a product and sell it to 500 people for the same price.

The difference: $3,750 vs. $375,000. Same underlying expertise. Different model.

This is not hyperbole. This is the math behind every successful creator, educator, and consultant who has built an asset-based business.

The shift required is not a technical one. It's a mental one.

From "I get paid for my time" to "I get paid for my thinking."

Thinking packaged into a product scales. Time does not.

The professionals who build this model early have an asymmetric advantage over those who don't.

Not because they work more. Because they build once and sell repeatedly.

## Post 24-3: How to Price a Digital Product (The Part Nobody Explains Clearly)

Most people price their digital product based on what feels comfortable to them.

That's the wrong input.

Pricing should be based on the value of the outcome to the buyer, not the effort it took to create the product.

A template that saves a marketing manager 10 hours per month is worth $200 to that manager.

If that manager bills at $75/hour, you've saved them $750 per month.

Charging $27 for that template is not humility. It's leaving money on the table while also signaling that the product isn't that valuable.

The pricing framework I use:

What problem does this solve? What is the cost of not solving it?

How much time does it save? What is that time worth to the buyer?

What would the buyer pay a professional to solve this manually?

Price at a fraction of the professional alternative and a fraction of the cost of the problem.

The other factor: price signals quality.

A product priced at $7 creates a different expectation than one priced at $97.

The buyer approaching a $7 product expects $7 quality.

Price to match the quality you've built. Then deliver more than that price promises.

## Post 24-4: The First Digital Product You Should Build (And Why It's Probably Not a Course)

The first digital product most people try to build is a course.

It's also the hardest product to build well and the hardest to sell without an existing audience.

Start smaller.

The fastest digital product to build and sell is a template or a guide that solves one specific problem.

Not a course that teaches an entire domain. One guide. One problem. One outcome.

Examples:

A freelancer's proposal template that wins 3 out of every 5 pitches.

A spreadsheet that calculates eBay profit margins including all fees in real time.

A content calendar system for solopreneurs who post across 3 platforms.

These take hours to build, not months.

They have a narrow, specific audience that immediately recognizes the value.

They price between $7 and $49, which lowers the buying decision friction.

And they teach you the most important skill in digital product businesses: how to talk about your product in a way that converts.

Once you've sold your first template or guide, you understand your audience better.

You know what language they use. What they're most worried about. What they trust you on.

Build the course then. When you know exactly who you're building it for.

## Post 24-5: The Distribution Problem Nobody Talks About with Digital Products

Creating a digital product is not the hard part.

Getting it in front of the right people is.

This is the conversation most product business advice skips.

The distribution options, ranked by speed and cost:

Paid ads: fastest, highest cost, requires testing budget and copywriting skill.

Existing audience: fastest (if you have one), lowest cost. Most people don't have one yet.

Platform marketplaces (Gumroad, Etsy, AppSumo): built-in traffic, but competitive. Discoverability varies.

SEO and content: slowest, compounding. Produces traffic 6-18 months after you start.

Partnerships and affiliates: medium speed, requires relationship building. High ROI when it works.

The honest advice: if you have no audience, you need either a budget for ads, a platform with built-in traffic, or the patience to build content-driven distribution.

The mistake: spending 80 hours building the product and 2 hours on distribution.

Flip that ratio.

Once the product is built, distribution becomes the full-time job until the product is selling consistently.

Distribution is not marketing. It's the part of the business that makes everything else matter.

Build the product. Then go to work on distribution like your business depends on it.

Because it does.
