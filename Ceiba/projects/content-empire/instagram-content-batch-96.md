## Post 2 — The Skill Audit

**Hook:** Before you build a business, you need to know what you're actually selling.

**Slide 1:**
Do a skill audit.
Most people skip this.
It's why they pick the wrong business.

**Slides 2-5:**
Slide 2:
What a skill audit is:
A 30-minute exercise.
You list everything you know how to do.
Then you rank it by market demand.

Slide 3:
The three skill categories:
Hard skills (design, code, writing, finance)
Soft skills (communication, systems, teaching)
Contextual skills (niche knowledge, lived experience)
Each can be a business.

Slide 4:
How to pick the right skill to sell:
Cross three filters.
You're good at it.
People pay for it.
You can do it for two years without hating it.
One answer to all three. That's your starting point.

Slide 5:
Common mistakes during the audit:
Picking what sounds impressive.
Picking what a mentor told you.
Picking what's trending.
None of those are filters for sustainability.

**Slide 6:**
The skill you undervalue is often the skill others will pay most for.
Run the audit. Follow @behikeai to go deeper.

**Caption:**
The people who struggle to monetize usually have the skills. What they lack is the framework to identify which skill has leverage.

A skill audit forces you to get concrete. Instead of "I'm good at marketing," you land on "I can write email sequences that convert for e-commerce brands." That specificity is what becomes a service, a product, or a course.

Most people skip this step because it feels like homework. But without it, you're building a business on a foundation you haven't actually examined.

The audit takes 30 minutes. The clarity it gives you lasts years.

Do the audit before you build anything. Then build around what survives all three filters.

## Post 3 — The One-Page Business Model

**Hook:** A business plan does not need to be 40 pages. It needs to fit on a sticky note.

**Slide 1:**
The one-page business model.
No MBA required.
Here's the whole thing.

**Slides 2-5:**
Slide 2:
Five boxes. That's the model.
1. Who you help
2. What problem you solve
3. How you deliver the solution
4. What you charge
5. How you get customers
Write one sentence per box.

Slide 3:
Example for a writing solopreneur:
1. SaaS founders
2. They can't communicate their product's value
3. Weekly email newsletters + LinkedIn posts
4. $1,500/month retainer
5. LinkedIn DMs + referrals
Five sentences. That's a real business.

Slide 4:
Why most people never finish this:
They overthink "who you help."
They try to serve everyone.
Specificity feels limiting.
It's the opposite. Specificity creates demand.

Slide 5:
When to expand beyond one page:
When you have revenue.
When you have customer data.
When you have a pattern to systematize.
Not before.

**Slide 6:**
One page. Five boxes. One sentence each.
That's all you need to start.
Follow @behikeai for the full system.

**Caption:**
Business plans are a tool built for people raising outside capital. If you're building a one-person business with your own skills and a laptop, you don't need 40 pages of projections.

You need five answers.

Who do you help. What problem do you solve. How do you deliver it. What do you charge. How do you find customers.

Every complexity beyond that is a distraction until you have paying customers. The founders who succeed fast aren't the ones with the most detailed plans. They're the ones who get to market fastest with the clearest offer.

Write the five boxes. Test the offer. Let the market tell you what to refine.

That's the whole game at this stage.

## Post 4 — The First 90 Days

**Hook:** The first 90 days of a one-person business are not about growth. They are about proof.

**Slide 1:**
What to do in your first 90 days.
Not growth.
Proof.
Here's the breakdown.

**Slides 2-5:**
Slide 2:
Days 1-30: Find the signal.
Talk to 10 potential customers.
Don't pitch. Ask questions.
What do they struggle with.
What have they already tried.
What would solving it be worth.

Slide 3:
Days 31-60: Build the proof.
Take one small client. Maybe for free or discounted.
Deliver an outsized result.
Document everything.
That case study is your first real asset.

Slide 4:
Days 61-90: Build the system.
Price your offer based on the result, not your time.
Create a repeatable delivery process.
Write two pieces of content about what you learned.
That's the foundation.

Slide 5:
What most people do instead:
Build the website.
Design the logo.
Create the Instagram.
Launch nothing.
Wonder why it's not working.

**Slide 6:**
The first 90 days are not about being seen.
They are about finding out if your offer works.
Follow @behikeai for the full 90-day framework.

**Caption:**
Most new solopreneurs spend their first 90 days building things that don't create revenue. They optimize the brand before they have a customer. They design the website before they have an offer that converts.

The first 90 days have one job: proof of concept.

Can you find a customer. Can you solve their problem. Can you get paid for it. Everything else is noise until those three things are true.

Days 1-30 are about discovery. Days 31-60 are about delivery. Days 61-90 are about systematizing the thing that worked.

After 90 days you don't have a perfect business. You have evidence. And evidence is the only real foundation.

## Post 5 — Why Solo Beats Team at the Start

**Hook:** A team does not solve the problems you have in year one. It multiplies them.

**Slide 1:**
Why you should build solo first.
Not because it's easy.
Because it forces the right lessons.

**Slides 2-5:**
Slide 2:
What building solo teaches you:
Every bottleneck is yours to diagnose.
You learn the whole business.
You find out what actually needs to be delegated.
You can't delegate your way out of a broken offer.

Slide 3:
What hiring too early actually does:
Adds coordination overhead.
Adds payroll before profit.
Adds someone who needs direction you don't have yet.
Complexity scales the wrong things.

Slide 4:
The right time to bring someone in:
When the task is defined, documented, and repeatable.
When you've done it yourself enough to evaluate quality.
When the cost of the hire is covered by existing revenue.
Not before.

Slide 5:
The solo advantage most people ignore:
You move at the speed of a decision.
No meetings. No alignment. No approval chains.
That speed is a real competitive advantage.
Use it fully before you give it up.

**Slide 6:**
Build solo until you understand every part of the business.
Then delegate from clarity, not desperation.
Follow @behikeai for the solo operator framework.

**Caption:**
There's a version of "build a team" that's actually just avoiding the hard parts of building a business.

If the offer isn't working, a team won't fix it. If the delivery is inconsistent, adding people makes it worse. If you don't understand the business yourself, you can't lead anyone else through it.

The one-person business is not a stepping stone. For many people, it's the destination. Low overhead. High margin. Full control over your time and output.

Even if you eventually build a team, the solo phase teaches you things you can't learn any other way. Every function, every bottleneck, every customer interaction runs through you. That's education you can't outsource.

Build solo first. Understand everything. Then bring people in to expand what already works.
