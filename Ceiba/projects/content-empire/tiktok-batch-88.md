## TikTok 5: How to Price Your Digital Product
**Hook:**
Most people underprice their digital products by 50%. Here's why.

**Script:**
The psychology of pricing is counterintuitive. A $7 ebook signals low effort and low value. A $47 guide signals substance. A $97 system signals expertise. The buyer doesn't know what's inside before they buy. They use the price as a proxy for value. When you price too low out of fear, you attract buyers who question everything and want refunds. When you price correctly, you attract people who are serious about the outcome and who get results. Getting results means testimonials. Testimonials mean more sales. Start at a price that makes you slightly uncomfortable. Test it. The market will tell you if it's wrong.

## TikTok 8: Write Better Hooks in 60 Seconds
**Hook:**
Your hook determines whether anyone sees your content. Here's how to write a better one.

**Script:**
A good hook does one of three things. It makes a counterintuitive statement that challenges what people believe. It identifies a specific pain point with enough precision that the viewer feels seen. Or it promises a specific outcome in a specific timeframe for a specific person. Bad hooks are vague. "Here are some tips for growing your business." Good hooks are specific. "Here's the exact email sequence that got me five sales in 48 hours with no paid ads." The gap between a good and bad hook is often just specificity. Add a number. Add a timeframe. Add a specific result. Test both and let the data tell you which one worked.

## TikTok 9: The One-Offer Business
**Hook:**
The fastest path to $5K a month is one offer done well.

**Script:**
Most people try to build multiple income streams before any single stream is actually working. The result is five things growing slowly instead of one thing growing fast. ## TikTok 10: Content Frameworks That Never Get Old
**Hook:**
There are three content frameworks that work on every platform, in every niche, forever.

**Script:**
Framework one: the mistake. "I did this wrong for two years. Here's what I learned." This works because people learn more from failure stories than success stories. Framework two: the comparison. "Most people do X. But the people winning are doing Y." This creates contrast and makes the viewer feel like they're getting an insider secret. Framework three: the counter-narrative. "Everyone says you need X to succeed. You don't. Here's what actually matters." This challenges a common belief and positions you as someone who thinks independently. These three frameworks never stop working because they tap into permanent psychological drivers: learning from failure, wanting to be in the know, and skepticism of conventional wisdom.

**CTA:**
Save these three. You now have unlimited content ideas.
