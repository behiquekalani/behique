## Tweet 1 (Single)

## Tweet 2 (Single)
The smartest thing a solopreneur can do in year one:

Build products. Not just services. Not just content.

Products decouple your income from your time. Services don't.

## Tweet 3 (3-Tweet Thread)
The goal is not to have one product that sells well.

The goal is to have ten products that each sell a little. Every day.

[1/3]

---

One product at $27 selling 3 times a day is $2,916/month.

Ten products averaging $27 selling 3 times a day combined is $29,160/month.

Same effort to build the traffic. Ten times the output because the library does the work.

[2/3]

---

Most people stop at one product because building it was hard and they want to see it succeed before building the next.

The library mindset says: the first product proved the model. Now build the second, third, fourth.

[3/3]

---

## Tweet 4 (Single)
A $7 product is not a low-value product.

It's a trust bridge. Someone who buys a $7 product from you is 10x more likely to buy your $97 product than someone who's only read your free content.

Price the entry low. Stack the value.

---

## Tweet 5 (Single)
The digital product stack framework:

- $7-$17: entry point (remove friction, build trust)
- $27-$47: core product (the thing you're known for)
- $97-$197: expanded system (deeper, more complete)
- $297+: done-with-you or bundle

Build from the bottom up.

## Tweet 6 (6-Tweet Thread)
How to build a digital product library from scratch, without an audience, without a track record, and without spending six months on your first product.

[1/6]

---

Start with what you already know.

Not what you wish you knew. Not what you think sounds impressive. What you can explain to someone tomorrow without preparation.

That knowledge has value. Most people who need it don't have it. That gap is a product.

[2/6]

---

Build the smallest useful version.

A 10-page guide that solves one specific problem is more valuable than a 200-page course that solves everything poorly.

The question is: "After reading this, can someone do one thing they couldn't do before?" If yes, it's a product.

[3/6]

---

Price it at $17-$27 and publish it.

This is not the price you'll always charge. It's the price that removes the internal debate about whether it's worth it.

Getting your first ten sales teaches you more than six months of planning ever could.

[4/6]

---

Build the second product before the first one is "successful."

The library grows by adding, not by waiting. The second product makes the first more valuable. They point at each other.

Most solopreneurs wait. The ones who build win.

[5/6]

---

After five products you'll start to see the pattern. Some sell consistently, some sell occasionally, some barely sell.

Double down on what sells. Cut what doesn't. Build adjacent products to the winners.

That's the library working. Let it.

[6/6]

## Tweet 7 (Single)
The reason most solopreneurs stay broke:

They sell their time in one-off projects instead of building assets that sell without them.

A PDF you wrote once can sell 10,000 times. A client project you completed once paid once.

## Tweet 8 (3-Tweet Thread)
Your knowledge is an asset that depreciates slower than you think.

[1/3]

---

A guide you wrote two years ago can still sell today if the problem it solves still exists.

Most problems people buy solutions for are timeless. Money, productivity, relationships, health. Those don't go out of style.

[2/3]

---

Your job is not to create content that expires.

Your job is to build a library of solutions to problems that will exist five years from now.

That library is the asset. Guard it like one.

[3/3]

## Tweet 9 (Single)
Most people think a $5,000/month income from digital products requires a massive audience.

It doesn't.

$5,000/month at $47/product = 106 sales/month = 3.5 sales per day.

3.5 sales per day is achievable with a 1,000-person email list if the product is right and the trust is there.
