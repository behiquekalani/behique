## Script 81.2 — How to Write Email Subject Lines

**Hook (0-3s):**
Nobody opens your emails because your subject lines are boring.

**Body (3-50s):**
The subject line is not a title. It's a promise.

[ON-SCREEN: Subject line = a promise, not a title]

A title describes. A promise implies a gap between where someone is and where they want to be. "Weekly Newsletter Issue 14" describes. "The thing killing your open rate" creates a gap.

[ON-SCREEN: Gap = curiosity = opens]

Three subject line frameworks that consistently outperform: the contrarian statement, the specific number, and the unfinished thought.

[ON-SCREEN: Contrarian / Specific number / Unfinished thought]

Contrarian: "Why posting daily is hurting your growth." Specific number: "5 subject lines I split-tested last month." Unfinished thought: "I was wrong about this."

[ON-SCREEN: Real examples, not theory]

The goal is not to trick people. It's to match the internal conversation they're already having. When your subject line names a problem they've been sitting with, the open is automatic.

[ON-SCREEN: Match their internal conversation]

One rule: never write a subject line that can be answered with yes or no. "Are you making this mistake?" No. "The mistake most creators make with email" is better.

[ON-SCREEN: No yes/no subject lines]

**CTA (50-60s):**
I test subject lines weekly. Follow for the patterns I'm finding.

**Caption:**
Your open rate is a subject line problem. Three frameworks that work: contrarian, specific number, unfinished thought.

## Script 81.3 — From Free to Paid: The Bridge

**Hook (0-3s):**
The gap between free content and first sale is not about price. It's about trust architecture.

**Body (3-50s):**
You can have 50,000 followers and make zero sales. It happens constantly.

[ON-SCREEN: Followers does not equal buyers]

The reason is that free content builds awareness, not trust. Trust is built through a different mechanism: repeated exposure to your thinking in a context where you're not trying to sell anything.

[ON-SCREEN: Free content builds awareness, email builds trust]

That context is your email list.

The bridge from free to paid has three stages. Stage one: someone discovers your content and finds it useful. Stage two: they join your list because you offer something specific they want. Stage three: over a series of emails, you demonstrate a level of depth that makes them think "this person knows what they're talking about."

[ON-SCREEN: Discover, join, deepen]

Most creators skip stage three. They get someone on the list and immediately pitch. That's like asking someone to marry you on the second date.

[ON-SCREEN: Don't pitch before you've built depth]

The bridge is content. Specifically, email content that solves real problems before asking for money. When someone has gotten three genuinely useful things from you for free, the fourth ask lands differently.

[ON-SCREEN: Give 3, ask 1]

**CTA (50-60s):**
I break this down step by step inside my newsletter. Subscribe with the link in bio.

**Caption:**
Going from free content to paid products is a trust problem, not a price problem. The bridge is email. Give 3, ask 1.

## Script 81.4 — The Lead Magnet That Actually Works

**Hook (0-3s):**
Most lead magnets don't work because they solve the wrong problem.

**Body (3-50s):**
A lead magnet should do one thing: make someone's most pressing problem slightly smaller.

[ON-SCREEN: One problem, slightly smaller]

The mistake most creators make is building a lead magnet that is too broad. "The ultimate guide to productivity" attracts everyone and converts no one because it doesn't speak to a specific moment of pain.

[ON-SCREEN: Broad lead magnets attract browsers, not buyers]

The lead magnets that convert are specific, fast, and useful in one sitting. A checklist. A template. A short framework with five steps. Something someone can open, use in 15 minutes, and close feeling like they made progress.

[ON-SCREEN: Specific / Fast / Usable in one sitting]

The title should make the benefit obvious. Not "The Complete Email Guide." Something like "The subject line formula I use to get 40% open rates."

[ON-SCREEN: Make the benefit undeniable in the title]

One more thing: the lead magnet should be directly connected to what you eventually sell. If you sell a course on email marketing, your lead magnet should solve a small email marketing problem. This is how you attract buyers instead of just subscribers.

[ON-SCREEN: Lead magnet must connect to your paid offer]

The lead magnet that converts solves one specific problem in 15 minutes. Broad guides attract browsers. Specific tools attract buyers.

## Script 81.5 — Email Frequency That Doesn't Annoy

**Hook (0-3s):**
Sending too many emails doesn't kill your list. Sending boring emails does.

**Body (3-50s):**
The frequency debate is a red herring.

[ON-SCREEN: Frequency is not the real problem]

People don't unsubscribe because you email them twice a week. They unsubscribe because twice a week they get an email that wastes their time. The frequency is not the issue. The quality-to-frequency ratio is.

[ON-SCREEN: Quality-to-frequency ratio is what matters]

Here's a principle that holds across lists of every size: the tolerance for high frequency increases when every email delivers something specific and useful. Daily emails work if every one of them is a dense insight the reader saves or acts on. Weekly emails fail if they feel like a newsletter for the sake of a newsletter.

[ON-SCREEN: High frequency is fine if every email earns it]

The framework I'd use: start with weekly. Establish a format. Build a reputation for delivering value in that format. Then consider increasing frequency only after people are telling you they look forward to your emails.

[ON-SCREEN: Build reputation before increasing frequency]

One signal to watch: reply rate, not open rate. If people are replying to your emails, you have permission to send more. If no one replies and your open rate is dropping, the problem is quality, and more emails will make it worse.

[ON-SCREEN: Reply rate is the real health metric]

**CTA (50-60s):**
Want to see what a value-dense weekly email looks like. Subscribe to my newsletter, link in bio.

**Caption:**
Email frequency is not the issue. Quality-to-frequency ratio is. People unsubscribe from boring emails, not frequent ones.
