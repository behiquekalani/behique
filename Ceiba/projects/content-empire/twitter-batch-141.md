# Twitter/X Batch 141: How to Write a Cold DM That Actually Gets Responses

---

## Tweet 1 (Single)
Most cold DMs fail before they're even read.

The first line is a transaction request disguised as a compliment.

"Hey, I love your work!" means nothing when the next sentence is "Can you pay me?"

---

## Tweet 2 (Single)
The cold DM template that actually works:

1. Specific observation about their work
2. Why it matters (to them, not you)
3. One question that requires no commitment to answer

That's it. No pitch. No portfolio link. No "let me know if you're interested."

---

## Tweet 3 (3-Tweet Thread)
The reason your cold DMs get ignored:

You're writing to a version of the person that doesn't exist.

[1/3]

---

You picture them sitting at their desk, free time, happy to discover a new vendor.

They're actually overwhelmed, filtering noise, and protecting their attention like it's money.

Your DM has to pass that filter.

[2/3]

---

The filter question they ask: "Does this person understand my situation, or are they just looking for a client?"

Answer that in your first line, and you're already ahead of 90% of cold outreach.

[3/3]

---

## Tweet 4 (Single)
Bad cold DM: "I help businesses grow with social media."

Good cold DM: "I noticed you post consistently but your engagement dropped since you switched to carousels in January. I have a theory about why."

Specificity is the message.

---

## Tweet 5 (Single)
People don't respond to pitches.

They respond to being seen.

Your cold DM should make them feel like you actually paid attention, not like they're entry 47 in a spreadsheet.

---

## Tweet 6 (6-Tweet Thread)
The anatomy of a cold DM that gets a response. Most people get this wrong at every step.

[1/6]

---

Step 1: The observation.

Not a compliment. An observation.

"I loved your last post" is a compliment. Easy to ignore.

"Your last post on pricing got more saves than any other post this month" is an observation. It tells them you were paying attention.

[2/6]

---

Step 2: Connect it to something they care about.

They don't care that you noticed. They care what it means for them.

"That save rate usually means the audience wants more tactical content, not inspiration."

Now you're adding value before asking for anything.

[3/6]

---

Step 3: Ask one small question.

Not: "Want to hop on a call?"
Not: "Are you open to working together?"

Ask something that proves your curiosity is genuine.

"Is that a direction you've been thinking about, or was that post more of an experiment?"

[4/6]

---

Step 4: No attachments, no links, no portfolio.

Every link you include is a reason for them to exit the conversation.

Keep it clean. The goal of a cold DM is not to close a deal. It's to open a conversation.

[5/6]

---

Step 5: Short. Under 150 words.

If you can't say it in under 150 words, you haven't thought it through enough.

Long DMs signal that you're nervous and overcompensating. Short DMs signal confidence.

The best cold DM I ever sent was 47 words. It got a same-day response.

[6/6]

---

## Tweet 7 (Single)
Your cold DM is not a cover letter.

Stop summarizing your experience and start demonstrating your thinking.

One sharp observation about their business is worth more than a paragraph of credentials.

---

## Tweet 8 (3-Tweet Thread)
Nobody taught you how to write a cold DM. They taught you how to write a sales email.

Those are two completely different things.

[1/3]

---

A sales email goes to a warm list. The reader already opted in. The relationship is established.

A cold DM goes to someone who never asked to hear from you. Every word has to earn its place.

[2/3]

---

The mindset shift: You're not sending a pitch. You're starting a conversation that might eventually become a working relationship.

Send it like that. Write like the first message, not the closing argument.

[3/3]

---

## Tweet 9 (Single)
Cold DM rule:

If they could copy and paste your DM and send it to 100 other people, it's not a cold DM. It's spam with formatting.

Write something only you could send, to only them.

---

## Tweet 10 (8-Tweet Thread)
I analyzed 200+ cold DMs I've received over the past year. Here's what the ones I responded to had in common.

Thread:

[1/8]

---

They were short.

Under 100 words. No exceptions. Every DM I responded to could fit in a tweet.

Not because I'm lazy, but because a short DM signals that the sender respected my time enough to edit.

[2/8]

---

They referenced something specific.

Not "I follow your content." That's nothing.

The ones I responded to cited a specific post, a specific idea I'd shared, or a specific thing about my work that takes more than 30 seconds to notice.

[3/8]

---

They didn't need anything from me.

The first message asked a question or made an observation. That's it.

No ask, no pitch, no "I'd love to work with you." Just curiosity.

[4/8]

---

They made me think.

The best ones introduced a perspective or a question that I hadn't considered.

That's rare. Most cold DMs confirm what you already know. The good ones challenge it.

[5/8]

---

They were clearly written by a human.

No template language. No "I hope this message finds you well." No bullet points listing their services.

Just a person talking to another person.

[6/8]

---

They had no links.

No portfolio. No website. No Calendly.

Links in a cold DM are a trust signal going the wrong direction. They say "I want to close you" before you've even had a conversation.

[7/8]

---

Summary:

- Short (under 100 words)
- Specific (cites something real)
- Asks, doesn't pitch
- Makes you think
- Sounds human
- No links

That's the whole framework. The DMs that don't follow this are the ones that get archived without a reply.

[8/8]
