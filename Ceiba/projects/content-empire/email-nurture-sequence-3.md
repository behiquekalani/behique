When people see a $4.99 product, they usually think one of two things.

Either it's cheap because it's low quality. Or whoever made it doesn't know what their work is worth.

Neither is true here. Let me explain how this actually works.

The $4.99 product exists to answer one question: can I trust this person?

That's it. Not revenue. Not scale. Trust.

When someone buys something at that price, they're not making a big bet. They're paying a small amount to see if the value is real. If it is, they now have a reference point. They know the quality. This is why the front of a product catalog matters. It's not a discount rack. It's a door. The lowest-priced thing in a catalog should be the clearest demonstration of value, something that makes the buyer think, if this is what they give me for five bucks, what does the $47 one look like?

## Email 13 (Day 29)

Send day: Day 29
Subject line: Building a business with a brain that fights you
A/B variant: ADHD and entrepreneurship: what nobody tells you
Preview text: ADHD wrecked my early attempts. Then I stopped fighting it.

---

I was diagnosed with ADHD my first year of engineering school.

By that point, I'd already figured out something was different about how I worked. Hyperfocus that let me build for six straight hours and then disappear completely for a week. Systems that worked perfectly for three days and then got abandoned because they felt boring. Ideas that came in floods and then left just as fast.

The early version of how I tried to build a business looked like this: I'd get fired up about something, work like crazy for a few days, then stall. Jump to a different idea. Come back to the first one two weeks later. Never quite finish anything. Plenty of motion, not much momentum.

The problem wasn't the ADHD. The problem was that I was trying to build systems designed for a different kind of brain.

The things that actually worked were smaller and more specific. One task at a time, not five. Deadlines tied to something real, not just a date in a calendar. Capture every idea immediately because if it doesn't get written down it disappears. Build in review points so nothing falls through the gaps.

Once I stopped trying to run on the same operating system as everyone else, things started moving. The hyperfocus became an asset instead of a liability. The scattered energy got channeled into a system that could hold it.

The ADHD Finance Guide was something I built for myself first. It's a framework for managing money and decisions when your brain doesn't naturally do consistency. Not a lecture. An actual operating system.

## Email 15 (Day 37)

Send day: Day 37
Subject line: What your knowledge is actually worth
A/B variant: You know things people will pay for. Most of it is priced at $0.
Preview text: Most people price their knowledge wrong. Here's the three-tier model.

---

The most common mistake people make with knowledge products is not charging too much.

It's charging nothing, or $5, for something someone else would charge $500 for.

This happens because of a specific mental error. You know something so well that it feels obvious to you. And because it feels obvious, you assume it's not worth much. But the person who doesn't know it yet doesn't feel that way. They'd pay real money to skip the two years you spent figuring it out.

Here's the pricing model I use, and it works for anyone selling knowledge in any format.

Tier 1 is the guide. $9 to $15. A focused, specific document that solves one problem clearly. This is the entry point. Its job is to demonstrate that you know what you're talking about and that your style is worth paying for again.

Tier 2 is the kit. $30 to $50. A system with multiple components. Not just the explanation of what to do, but the actual files, templates, or workflows to do it. The person buying this is past the "should I trust this person" stage and wants to implement, not just read.

Tier 3 is the service. $500 and up. You do the work for them, or you guide them through it directly. The price is not for information. It's for your time, judgment, and accountability. This is where most of the revenue lives.

Most people skip tier 1 and tier 2 because they think they need to start at tier 3. This is backwards. The trust that justifies a $500 service is built through the $12 guide someone bought six months ago.

## SEQUENCE NOTES

### Timing recommendations

The gaps in this sequence are intentional. The jump from day 22 to day 25 is short because email 12 follows directly from the mindset shift in email 11. After that, the gaps widen. Day 29, 33, 37. Longer gaps reduce unsubscribes and give each email more room to land.

If you have higher engagement early (strong open rates on 11 and 12), you can tighten the schedule by one day on each subsequent email. If you're seeing drop-off, extend by two days and consider rewriting the subject line on email 13 before blaming the content.

After day 37, move subscribers to a weekly broadcast or content newsletter. Do not let them go silent. Silence between emails 15 and the next touchpoint is the highest-risk moment for list churn.

### Split test suggestions

Email 11: Test subject lines on audience segment that did not buy from the welcome sequence. The "2 AI tools" variant typically outperforms the "using AI wrong" variant for warmer lists. Cold audiences may flip.

Email 12: The A/B subject line tests price psychology framing vs. curiosity framing. Track which one correlates with actual click-throughs to the catalog, not just opens.

Email 13: This is the highest-variance email in the sequence. Personal story emails either index very high (readers who connect with the narrative) or average (readers who skipped it because the topic didn't match their situation). Segment clicks by which lead magnet they downloaded. ADHD-related magnets will show much stronger performance on this one.

Email 14: The P.S. invite to reply is a deliberate deliverability move. Replies train inbox filters that you're a real sender. If you get replies, actually respond to them. The first-hand knowledge of what your subscribers are doing manually is also genuinely useful for future product ideas.

Email 15: Test the CTA split. Half the list gets the catalog link, half gets the Ghost Writer Kit specifically. The specific product link will likely convert higher for people who have already browsed the catalog. The catalog link performs better for people who haven't.

### Performance notes

Based on typical mid-funnel email behavior for a subscriber list built around AI and digital products:

Email 11 (80/20 of AI tools) tends to be the highest-open email in this set. The topic is specific and the subject line avoids the generic "AI tools" framing most newsletters use. Expect above-average open rates.

Email 12 (why $4.99 products exist) tends to have strong click-through to the catalog because it reframes browsing as intentional behavior rather than idle shopping. A reader who clicks after this email is more likely to buy.

Email 13 (ADHD and building) is a list-qualifier. It self-selects the readers who are actually building something versus those who are just interested in the topic. The reply rate on this one tends to be higher than average. Treat replies as high-intent signals.

Email 14 (first automation to build) is the most conversion-focused email in the set for the n8n product. The P.S. reply offer functions as a soft qualification step that moves the most interested subscribers toward a direct conversation.

Email 15 (pricing your knowledge) is the longest-cycle email. It tends to have lower immediate conversion but generates purchases one to two weeks later when someone is in a moment of actually making a product decision. Tag anyone who clicks but doesn't buy and follow up in 10-12 days.
