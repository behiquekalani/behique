**Tweet 6:**

**Tweet 8:**
The products that sold fastest were the ones where I had genuine experience. The ones I wrote with thin knowledge sold slowly or not at all. AI didn't cover for the knowledge gap. It amplified it.

**Tweet 9:**
Bottleneck was not speed. Bottleneck was deciding a topic was good enough to start. Every day I had a window of "is this actually worth building?" Most of the time the answer was yes. The hesitation was the cost.

## Thread 2: The AI Installation Service Business Model

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
There's a service business hiding inside the AI tools space. Most people building AI are missing it.

**Tweet 2:**
The model: you install, configure, and connect AI tools for small businesses. You charge for setup, not for the tools themselves. One-time project fee or a monthly retainer for maintenance and updates.

**Tweet 3:**
Why this works: most small business owners know AI exists. Most of them have no idea how to actually set anything up. The gap between "I've heard of ChatGPT" and "I have a working AI system in my business" is large. You charge to cross it.

**Tweet 4:**
What you're actually selling: time. A business owner who spends 40 hours learning to set up n8n + a CRM integration is spending 40 hours not running their business. You can do that setup in 6 hours. You sell the 34 hours you saved them.

**Tweet 5:**
Pricing I've seen work: $300-$800 for a basic automation setup (email sequences, lead capture, response automation). $150-$300/month retainer for monitoring and updates. Agencies charge 5-10x that.

**Tweet 6:**
What you need to know: n8n or Make for automation. A few AI APIs (OpenAI, Claude, or local models). Basic understanding of how webhooks and APIs connect. That's it. No engineering degree required.

**Tweet 7:**
The sales process is simple because the problem is obvious. "How much time do you spend on [repetitive task]?" If the answer is more than 10 hours a month, the math on paying you works immediately.

**Tweet 8:**
Target clients: solo consultants, real estate agents, small e-commerce stores, local service businesses. Not enterprise. Not startups. People who have a working business and a repetitive problem.

**Tweet 9:**
The risk: you become support for the system you built. Price the retainer accordingly. Scope every project with a change request policy. First clients will ask for extras. Have the conversation before it starts.

## Thread 3: Why Most Solopreneurs Fail at Content

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Most solopreneurs fail at content for one reason. It's not consistency. It's not quality. It's simpler.

**Tweet 2:**
They write about their products instead of writing about the problems their products solve. Every post is a soft pitch. Audiences don't follow soft pitches. They follow people who show them something useful.

**Tweet 3:**
The OPB content model is this: 50% education (here's how something works), 30% process (here's what I'm building), 20% offers (here's how to work with me or buy the thing). Most people invert this. 80% offers, 20% everything else.

**Tweet 4:**
The education posts build trust. The process posts build relationship. The offer posts convert the trust and relationship into transactions. You can't skip to the offer posts without the first two.

**Tweet 5:**
Consistency matters, but it's not the whole story. Consistent bad content is just more noise. The question is whether each piece gives someone something they didn't have before. One insight. One specific fact. One reframe.

**Tweet 6:**
Platform matters less than people think. The accounts that grow fast are usually doing something most accounts aren't: they're specific. "AI tools for solopreneurs" is specific. "Business and tech tips" is not.

**Tweet 7:**
The audience you want is not the largest audience. It's the most specific audience that overlaps with what you sell. 10,000 people who care exactly about the thing you do is more valuable than 100,000 who sort of like your content.

**Tweet 8:**
The failure mode I see most: someone posts 30 days in a row, gets mediocre results, stops. Then starts again 2 months later. The 30 days was working. The stop is what killed it. Algorithms reward the long game, not the sprint.

**Tweet 9:**
The practical fix: make content production a system, not a mood. One day a week you write 5-7 pieces. You schedule them. You post daily without the daily decision. Decision fatigue kills more content calendars than anything else.

**Tweet 10:**
Last thing: the accounts worth studying are not the ones with the most followers. They're the ones converting content into business. Find someone building a similar thing. Watch how they talk about their work. Learn from behavior, not metrics.

## Thread 5: Polymarket and Prediction Markets — How I Think About Information Bets

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Prediction markets are not gambling. They're a structured way to bet on your research quality. Here's how I think about them.

**Tweet 2:**
The core mechanic: you buy shares in an outcome. "Will X happen by Y date?" If correct, shares pay $1. If wrong, they pay $0. Price reflects market probability. A 70-cent price means the market thinks 70% chance of yes.

**Tweet 3:**
Where edge comes from: better information, better models, or better calibration than the average market participant. The question is never "do I think this will happen?" It's "do I think this more or less likely than the current price says?"

**Tweet 4:**
The research process I run: for any market I consider, I look for base rates. How often have similar situations resolved this way historically? Base rates anchor the estimate before I look at current information.

**Tweet 5:**
Then I look at what the market might be missing. Prediction markets are efficient but not perfect. They miss information in niche domains, in non-English sources, and in early-stage developments where few people are paying attention.

**Tweet 6:**
What I don't do: bet on politics or sports markets where emotional biases run high and information edges are hard to maintain. Too much noise. I prefer tech and economic markets where research is more tractable.

**Tweet 7:**
Position sizing matters more than picking winners. A 60% confidence bet on a 50-cent market warrants a small position. A 90% confidence bet on a 20-cent market warrants a much larger one. The math is Kelly Criterion, which you can look up and apply directly.

**Tweet 8:**
The failure mode: overconfidence. You do good research, you win a few bets, you start sizing larger without proportionally better confidence. This is how people blow accounts that were profitable for months.

**Tweet 9:**
One honest limitation: I have $500 to deploy. At this scale, the expected value difference between a good bet and a bad one is measured in tens of dollars. The real value right now is building the research and calibration habit, not the returns.

**Tweet 10:**
I built a monitoring tool for tracking prediction markets I'm watching. It checks price movements and flags when something shifts more than 10 points. That's usually when new information entered the market. That signal is more useful than the price itself.

**Tweet 11:**
This is not financial advice. Prediction markets involve real risk. What I'm describing is a research and thinking framework, not a guaranteed return strategy. Size accordingly.
