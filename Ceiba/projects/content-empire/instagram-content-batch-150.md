## Post 1 — 750 Posts Ago

**Hook:** 750 posts ago I did not know if anyone was paying attention.

**Slide 1:** 750 posts ago I did not know if anyone was paying attention.

**Slide 2:** I was posting into what felt like empty space. No engagement, no feedback, no proof the work was landing. Just the decision to keep going.

**Slide 3:** What kept me going was not confidence. It was a system. Show up. Write the thing. Post it. Do not check the numbers for 24 hours.

**Slide 4:** The system removed the emotional decision point. I did not have to decide whether it was worth continuing. The system continued. I just executed the system.

**Slide 5:** 750 posts later, I understand that early silence differently. The foundation was being laid. The algorithm was learning. The archive was building. None of it was visible yet.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. The foundation phase is invisible. Build it anyway.

**Caption:**
750 posts is a number that means nothing until you sit with it.

Each one of those posts was a decision. Not a big one, not a dramatic one. Just: do the thing today. Even when the numbers were flat. Even when the topic felt exhausted. Even when I wondered whether any of this was building toward something real.

The honest answer is that I did not know for a long time. That is the part nobody talks about in the creator space. The uncertainty does not go away when you start. It does not go away at 100 posts or 300 posts. At some point it becomes something you operate alongside rather than something you wait to resolve.

The system is what carried the uncertainty. When the decision is already made, doubt has less purchase. You are not asking whether to post today. The answer is already yes. You are just asking what to say.

750 posts is not a milestone about success. It is a milestone about showing up. The showing up is what eventually produces the success.

## Post 2 — What I Got Wrong in the First 100 Batches

**Hook:** The first 100 batches had one major error. Here it is.

**Slide 1:** The first 100 batches had one major error. Here it is.

**Slide 2:** I was writing for a general audience. Solopreneurs, creators, entrepreneurs, builders. Everyone and no one simultaneously.

**Slide 3:** The content was technically good but it did not pull people in the way specific content does. Because it was designed to not exclude anyone, it did not deeply include anyone.

**Slide 4:** The shift came when I stopped trying to be useful to everyone and started writing for one specific person. Someone who builds alone, thinks in systems, does not want to be a personality, wants the work to be the proof.

**Slide 5:** The engagement changed. The comments got more specific. The DMs got more personal. The content found its people.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. Specific content finds its people. General content finds nobody.

**Caption:**
There is a version of early content creation that is technically correct but emotionally absent.

The posts are useful. The information is accurate. The format is clean. But nothing in the writing risks being wrong for someone. And content that cannot be wrong for someone cannot be deeply right for anyone.

The first phase of building this account looked like that. Broad enough to be applicable to most people, specific enough to be useful, but not specific enough to be genuinely for a particular person in a particular situation.

The turn came when I started writing the way I actually think. Specific, direct, a little philosophical, not interested in performing enthusiasm. That voice has a much smaller natural audience. But the people it finds are actually here for it.

The lesson from 100 batches of getting it partly wrong: write for the person you actually want to reach. Lose the people you were trying not to exclude. The ones who stay are the right ones.

## Post 3 — The Things That Actually Moved the Numbers

**Hook:** Three things moved the numbers. One was obvious. Two were not.

**Slide 1:** Three things moved the numbers. One was obvious. Two were not.

**Slide 2:** Obvious: consistency. Posting on a schedule regardless of how a previous post performed. Every creator says this. It is true and most people still do not do it.

**Slide 3:** Not obvious 1: linking every post back to a single core theme. The account that talks about one thing builds search relevance for that thing. The algorithm learns to show it to people looking for that thing.

**Slide 4:** Not obvious 2: replying to every comment personally for the first six months. Not with a generic response. With a specific, thoughtful answer. That behavior signals genuine engagement to the algorithm and builds real relationships with early followers.

**Slide 5:** The numbers are downstream of these three. Consistency, topic coherence, genuine engagement. In that order.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. Three levers. Not fifteen.

**Caption:**
The creator space generates a lot of advice about growth. Optimal posting times, hook formulas, hashtag strategies, collaboration calendars, viral format breakdowns.

Some of that is useful. Most of it is noise that distracts from the three things that actually compound.

Consistency creates an archive. An archive creates relevance. Relevance creates search and recommendation traffic that is independent of your current posting frequency.

Topic coherence tells the algorithm what you are about. An account that has posted 600 pieces of content about one topic has a completely different search profile than an account that has posted 600 pieces about ten topics.

Genuine engagement in the early phase converts followers into something denser than followers. They become invested. They show up. They bring others.

Three levers. Applied consistently for eighteen months. That is the whole strategy.

## Post 4 — The Hardest Part Was Not Consistency

**Hook:** Consistency was not the hard part. This was.

**Slide 1:** Consistency was not the hard part. This was.

**Slide 2:** The hard part was making something and then finding out it did not land. Not from engagement data. From something someone said directly.

**Slide 3:** "This felt generic." "This could have been written by anyone." "Your earlier stuff was better."

**Slide 4:** Those moments do not have a system that catches them. You have to sit with the honest feedback, separate the useful signal from the noise, and rebuild from whatever is left.

**Slide 5:** That is the part of building a content business that does not get posted about. The contraction after a piece misses. The recalibration. The small crisis of wondering if you actually have a voice worth building.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. The recalibration is part of the build. Not a detour from it.

**Caption:**
750 posts does not mean 750 wins.

Some of those posts were wrong for the moment. Some were too polished, some too raw. Some said the right thing in the wrong tone. Some were genuinely bad and got posted anyway because the system said post.

The hard part of building anything in public is that the misses are visible. A writer who drafts badly and revises in private only shows the good version. A creator building in public shows the iteration in real time, including the versions that did not work.

I do not think that is a disadvantage, looking back. The misses are part of the record. They show that the account is not a highlight reel. The people who stick around through the misses are the ones who trust the thing you are building more than they trust the individual posts.

An account that is only wins is an account that is performing. An account that includes the recalibrations is an account that is building.
