## Post 1 — The Problem With "Pick One Thing"

**Hook:** The advice to pick one niche assumes you are one type of person.

**Slide 1:** The advice to pick one niche assumes you are one type of person.

**Slide 2:** Most niche advice comes from people who had one obsession from age 12. They cannot understand why you care about fitness, business, design, and philosophy at the same time. They treat it as a focus problem.

**Slide 3:** It is not a focus problem. Multi-passionate people have multiple genuine interests. The issue is not the interests. The issue is that most niches are built around topics, not around the person doing the work.

**Slide 4:** A topic-based niche says: "I post about nutrition." A person-based niche says: "I document building a healthy, high-output life." One is narrow and forgettable. The other is specific and expandable.

**Slide 5:** The goal is not to pick one topic. The goal is to find the thread that connects your interests and make that thread visible to the right audience.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. More on how to find your thread in the next post.

**Caption:** The "pick one niche" advice breaks down the moment you have more than one real interest. It assumes your value is topic-specific, and it is not. Your value comes from how you think, what you connect, and what problems you actually solve. The creators who last are not the ones who forced themselves into a box. They are the ones who built a box that fit them. This post is about why the standard advice fails multi-passionate people, and what the real question should be instead. The real question is not "what should my niche be?" The real question is "who am I actually trying to help, and what is the intersection of what I know and what they need?" Answer that and the niche becomes obvious.

## Post 2 — The Intersection Test

**Hook:** Your niche is not a topic. It is where your knowledge meets someone else's problem.

**Slide 1:** Your niche is not a topic. It is where your knowledge meets someone else's problem.

**Slide 2:** Most people try to find their niche by looking inward. "What am I passionate about?" is the wrong starting question. Passion is fuel. It is not a positioning strategy.

**Slide 3:** The better question is: what specific result can I help a specific type of person achieve, using the knowledge I have already accumulated across all my interests?

**Slide 4:** Example. You like fitness, productivity, and business. That sounds scattered. But if you help early-career professionals build high-energy work habits without burning out, all three interests show up. They are not competing. They are compounding.

**Slide 5:** The intersection test: list your top 3 to 5 interests. List the problems people in your life ask you about. Find where those two lists overlap. That overlap is the beginning of your niche.

**Slide 6:** Save this. The intersection test is one of the fastest ways to cut through niche paralysis. Follow @behikeai for more.

**Caption:** Niche paralysis is real, and it hits multi-passionate people hardest. You have too many options and no framework for choosing. The intersection test cuts through it. Your interests are not a liability. They are a signal about where you have accumulated rare combinations of knowledge. Most specialists know one domain deeply. You know three or four with enough depth to connect them. That combination is harder to replicate than any single domain expertise. The question is not which interest to sacrifice. The question is which problem, solved by your specific combination, creates real value for real people. Start there. The niche will follow.

## Post 3 — The Umbrella Positioning Method

**Hook:** You do not need one niche. You need one umbrella.

**Slide 1:** You do not need one niche. You need one umbrella.

**Slide 2:** An umbrella position is a core belief or philosophy that holds multiple interests together. Instead of posting about one topic, you post from one perspective that makes sense of many topics.

**Slide 3:** Dan Koe does not post about productivity or business or philosophy separately. He posts from the perspective that modern humans need a new operating system for their life. Every topic serves that perspective.

**Slide 4:** Your umbrella is not a topic sentence. It is a worldview. It is the lens through which you interpret everything you post about. When your content comes from a consistent worldview, multiple topics feel cohesive, not scattered.

**Slide 5:** To find your umbrella: write down the one belief you hold about how people should live or work that most people around you do not share. That belief is often your umbrella.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. The next post covers how to test if your umbrella is strong enough before you commit to it.

**Caption:** The creators who look most coherent to their audience are often the most multi-passionate behind the scenes. What makes them coherent is not that they narrowed their interests. It is that they found the through-line. The umbrella. One consistent perspective that frames every piece of content, regardless of topic. This is how you stay interesting across a wide range of subjects without confusing your audience. The confusion comes from posting without a perspective, not from posting about multiple topics. If every post comes from the same worldview, the audience does not need you to stay on one topic. They just need you to stay consistent in how you see things.

## Post 4 — Start Narrow, Expand Later

**Hook:** The creators who tried to be broad from day one are the ones you have never heard of.

**Slide 1:** The creators who tried to be broad from day one are the ones you have never heard of.

**Slide 2:** Starting narrow is not about limiting yourself permanently. It is about giving your first hundred followers a clear reason to follow you. A vague brand attracts vague followers who do not know why they subscribed.

**Slide 3:** Pick the most specific version of your niche to start. Not "business" but "business for first-generation entrepreneurs." Not "fitness" but "fitness for people with desk jobs and no gym access." Specificity creates resonance.

**Slide 4:** Once you have built an audience around a specific position, expansion becomes easy. Your audience already trusts your judgment. They will follow you into adjacent territory because the trust is in you, not in the topic.

**Slide 5:** The path: start with the most specific angle you can credibly hold. Build to 1,000 people who deeply identify with that angle. Expand from there with confidence.

**Slide 6:** Save this framework. Follow @behikeai for the full niche-building series.

**Caption:** Starting broad is the most common mistake multi-passionate creators make. They try to reach everyone, which means the content resonates with no one at full strength. The audience that forms around vague positioning is also vague. They do not know what they came for, so they do not know why to stay. Starting narrow does not mean staying narrow forever. The creators who dominate broad categories today started in a tight corner. They earned the right to expand by first building deep loyalty. A small audience that genuinely cares about your specific angle is worth more than a large audience that could not explain what you do. Start with the corner. Expand after the trust is built.

## Post 5 — The Commitment Test

**Hook:** You do not need to be certain about your niche. You need to be willing to commit for 90 days.

**Slide 1:** You do not need to be certain about your niche. You need to be willing to commit for 90 days.

**Slide 2:** Most people wait for certainty before they start. Certainty about a niche does not come from thinking. It comes from posting. You cannot know if an angle works until you test it with real content in front of a real audience.

**Slide 3:** The 90-day commitment test: pick the niche that feels most natural right now, not the one that feels most profitable or most strategic. Post consistently for 90 days. Measure what resonates. Adjust based on data, not anxiety.

**Slide 4:** What you are testing: Does the content feel natural to produce? Does the audience that shows up feel like the right people? Are you learning more about the topic as you post, or are you already running out of things to say?

**Slide 5:** After 90 days you will have data, not just feelings. Most people discover that their chosen niche was right and they just needed time to find their voice within it. Some discover it was wrong. Both outcomes are valuable.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. The decision is not permanent. Start and learn.

**Caption:** Niche paralysis keeps more creators offline than lack of ideas ever will. The fear that you will choose the wrong thing and waste time is itself the thing wasting time. The 90-day commitment test breaks that pattern. You are not signing a contract. You are running an experiment with a defined endpoint. If the niche is wrong, you learn that in 90 days with a body of content behind you. If it is right, you have 90 days of momentum built. Either way you are ahead of where you are right now. The niche that felt uncertain on day one rarely feels uncertain on day 91. Commit to the experiment. The certainty comes after.
