**Slide 1 (Hook):**
I use an AI safety checklist before every client project.

I'm giving it away for free.

Here is what is in it and why it matters.

**Slide 2 . What the checklist covers:**
The checklist has four sections.

Data handling. Where does the AI tool store inputs? Does it use your prompts to train future models? This is the section most businesses skip. It is the one that creates liability.

Access and permissions. What accounts, APIs, and integrations does the tool connect to? What can it read? What can it write? Minimal access is the rule.

Output review. Who reviews AI outputs before they go to a client or a customer? Is there a step in the workflow that requires human confirmation before anything goes out? If not, there should be.

Tool verification. Is the tool from a reputable provider? Has it been updated in the last six months? Is there a privacy policy that covers your use case?

**Slide 3 . Why this exists:**
I built the checklist after seeing two businesses use AI tools without reading the data policies.

One was feeding customer data into a free chatbot that stored all inputs on a third-party server. The customers did not know. The business owner did not know. That is a data handling problem, not an AI problem.

The checklist does not require you to be technical. It requires you to ask the right questions before adding a tool to your workflow.

**Slide 4 . Who needs this:**
If you use any AI tool in a client-facing workflow, this checklist applies to you.

Doesn't matter if you are a freelancer, a small agency, or a solo business owner. The questions are the same.

The checklist takes about 15 minutes to run through for each new tool you add. That 15 minutes has prevented more than one bad situation in my own work.

## POST 3 . The Business Automation Audit I Do Before Every AI Install (Carousel)
**Account:** @behikeai
**Format:** Carousel (5 slides)

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
Before I add any AI tool to a business, I run a 30-minute audit.

Here is what gets checked, how the scoring works, and how to run it yourself.

**Slide 2 . What gets checked:**
The audit covers five areas.

Process definition. Is there a documented process for the task being automated, or is it in someone's head? AI can only automate something that is already defined. If the process is not written down, the first step is writing it down.

Current time cost. How long does the task take per week, in real minutes? This is the number that determines whether automation is worth building.

Error rate. How often does the manual version of this task produce an error? High error rate tasks are high-value targets for automation. Low error rate tasks may not be worth the build time.

Tool fit. Does an AI tool exist that maps cleanly to this task? Or would using AI here require significant custom work? Custom work has a cost. Plug-and-play tools do not.

Dependency risk. If the AI tool goes down or changes its pricing, what breaks? Every automation creates a dependency. The audit scores the severity of that dependency.

**Slide 3 . The scoring system:**
Each of the five areas gets a score from 1 to 3.

1: not ready or not worth it.
2: could work with some setup.
3: clear value, worth building now.

If the total score is 10 or higher, the automation is worth building. Below 10, fix the underlying process first or skip the automation entirely.

Most businesses I audit score between 7 and 9 on their first pass. That is not a failure. It means the groundwork is not done yet.

**Slide 4 . The most common finding:**
The audit almost always finds the same thing.

The task is not actually repeatable. It looks like a repeatable task from the outside. But when you ask "does this always work the same way," the answer is no.

That variability is what kills automations. You build the workflow, it handles 80% of cases correctly, and then the 20% of edge cases require more manual intervention than the original manual process did.

The audit catches this before you build. That is the point.

**Account:** @kalaniandrez
**Format:** Single Image with caption

---

**Caption:**
I give things away for free before I sell anything.

Not because I am generous. Because of how the math works.

When someone downloads a free resource and it is actually useful, two things happen. They remember where it came from. And when I eventually have something to sell, they already trust the source.

Selling to a cold audience is hard. Selling to someone who already got value from you is easy.

The free-first model is not charity. It is the most efficient form of marketing I have found for a one-person business.

Here is how it works in practice.

A free checklist or template costs me maybe two hours to build. It gets downloaded by some number of people over time. A percentage of those people end up on the email list. A percentage of those people eventually buy something.

Every paid customer I have had came from somewhere. The ones who came from a free resource converted faster and with less friction than the ones who found a paid product first.

The economics of free: you give something useful, you build trust, you create a relationship before there is a transaction. The transaction comes later. It comes easier.

I am not giving away the best work for free forever. I am giving away the best entry point for free, so the people who are a good fit can find me without risk.
