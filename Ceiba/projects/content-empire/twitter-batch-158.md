**Tweet 4 (3-tweet thread)**
Tweet 1:
Most solopreneurs price based on what they think people will pay.

That's the wrong question.

Tweet 2:
The right question: what is the outcome worth to the client?

If your work saves them $50,000 a year, charging $2,000 is almost an insult.

Price against the value of the outcome, not the hours you put in.

Tweet 3:
Hours are a cost.
Outcomes are a value.

As long as you price by the hour, you'll be treated like a cost. Not like a value.

Make the shift. It changes everything about how clients treat you.

**Tweet 7 (8-tweet thread)**
Tweet 1:
How to raise your prices without losing all your clients.

A practical breakdown for solopreneurs who know they're undercharging but don't know how to change it.

Tweet 2:
First, understand why you're undercharging.

It's not because you don't know your value.
It's because you're afraid of the no.

That fear of rejection is more expensive than any client you'll ever lose.

Tweet 3:
Step 1: Raise prices for new clients only.

Don't disturb existing relationships yet.
Just start quoting the new number to every new prospect.

You'll find that more people say yes than you expected.

Tweet 4:
Step 2: Document results obsessively.

Every case study, every metric, every client win.

This is your pricing evidence. The more concrete your outcomes, the harder it is for anyone to argue against your rate.

Tweet 5:
Step 3: Reframe the offer, not just the number.

"$500 for a landing page" feels expensive.
"$500 for a landing page that converts traffic into buyers" feels cheap.

The price didn't change. The frame did.

Tweet 6:
Step 4: Raise existing client prices at renewal.

Give 60 days' notice. Explain the new rate. Offer continuity at a grandfather rate for 3-6 months if you want to soften the transition.

Most clients who are worth keeping will stay.

Tweet 7:
Step 5: Stop offering discounts.

Discounts train clients to wait for the sale and devalue your work in their mind.

If someone pushes back on price, offer less scope, not a lower price.

Tweet 8:
The goal isn't to charge more for the same thing.

It's to deliver so much value that the price becomes the least interesting part of the conversation.

Get there, and you'll never worry about pricing again.
