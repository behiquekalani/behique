**Hook:** You don't need 10 apps. You need one inbox.

**Slide 1:** You don't need 10 apps. You need one inbox.

**Slide 2:** One place where everything lands. Links, ideas, tasks, observations, voice notes, screenshots. Everything.

**Slide 3:** The rule: when something is worth keeping, it goes to the inbox immediately. No sorting. No deciding. Just in.

**Slide 4:** In Notion, this is a simple page called Inbox. One click to open. One click to add. That's all the friction there can be.

**Slide 5:** The inbox is not storage. It's a transit zone. Things come in, get processed once a week, and move to the right place.

**Slide 6:** Save this post. You're going to reference it when you build.

**Caption:**
The simplest fix for information overload is also the one most people skip: build a single inbox.

Not a folder. Not a notebook. Not a category. An inbox. One page where everything goes the moment you want to capture it. No decisions. No organization. No friction.

The critical design principle here is zero friction on capture and zero attachment to the inbox staying clean. Your inbox will fill up. That's good. That means capture is working.

Once a week, you process it. You read through what came in. You move useful things to the right place in your system. You delete what was noise. You close the week with a clean inbox and a system that reflects reality.

Most people try to organize as they capture. That's why they stop capturing. Remove the organization step from capture entirely. Let the inbox hold the mess and process it separately.

Build this first. Everything else comes after.

## Post 4 — The Routing Map: Where Information Goes After the Inbox

**Hook:** Capture is the easy part. Routing is where most systems break.

**Slide 1:** Capture is the easy part. Routing is where most systems break.

**Slide 2:** When you process your inbox, every item needs to go somewhere. If you don't have a map, you freeze. Then you stop processing. Then you stop capturing.

**Slide 3:** The routing map is simple. Ask three questions: Is this actionable now? Is this reference material? Is this an idea I want to develop?

**Slide 4:** Actionable now goes to your task list or project page. Reference material goes to your Resources section. Ideas go to a dedicated Idea Capture page.

**Slide 5:** That's the whole map. Three questions. Three destinations. Done.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. Tomorrow's post is the full system tied together.

**Caption:**
The reason most second brain systems collapse is not a capture problem. It's a routing problem.

You open the inbox, see 30 items, and have no clear sense of where each one belongs. The decision cost is too high. You close the inbox without processing. The pile grows. Eventually you stop trusting the system.

A routing map eliminates the decision cost. You're not deciding where something goes. You're answering one of three fixed questions and following the answer.

Is this something I need to act on? Send it to tasks or the relevant project. Is this reference I'll use later? Send it to resources. Is this an idea I want to develop? Send it to idea capture.

The whole processing session becomes mechanical. You move through the inbox quickly because you're not making creative decisions. You're running a simple filter.

Build the map once. Use it every week. The system stays functional indefinitely.
