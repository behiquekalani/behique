## POST 1: What 25 Products Taught Me About Product-Market Fit

Building twenty-five products is not a strategy. It started as a catalog experiment and became an education.

The first ten were mostly guesses. I built what seemed useful and waited to see what resonated. The feedback was slow and hard to read. The eleventh product I built after a conversation with someone who had a specific, named problem. It sold faster than the previous ten combined.

Product-market fit is not about the quality of the product. It is about the specificity of the problem. A good product solving a vague problem will underperform a decent product solving a precise one. Every time.

The catalog now has shape because I stopped building from inspiration and started building from friction. Find the friction. Build the fix. That is the pattern.

## POST 2: Why Most AI Tools Feel Useless

The tools are not the problem. The framing is.

Most people approach AI tools with a vague task. "Help me write better." "Summarize this." "Make this cleaner." Those prompts produce generic output. Then the user concludes the tool is not useful. The tool gave back exactly what it received.

The tools that feel useful are the ones attached to a specific, repeatable task. Not "help me write" but "rewrite this paragraph in my brand voice, which sounds like X, and remove any word that could have been written by a template." That is a task with a target. The output is actually usable.

The gap is not the tool. It is the investment in defining the task. Most people skip that step and blame the tool for the shortcut.

## POST 3: The Pomodoro Timer Actually Works -- Here Is Why

Not because of the 25 minutes. Because of the break.

The 25-minute work block does one thing: it makes starting feel low-stakes. You are not committing to solving the problem. You are committing to looking at it for 25 minutes. That is a different cognitive contract. Much easier to accept.

The 5-minute break does the other thing: it interrupts the spiral. When you are stuck, the worst thing you can do is stay stuck. The break forces a reset. Most of the time the problem looks different when you come back.

For ADHD specifically, the structure does something extra. It creates predictable transitions. The brain knows the work will end soon. That makes starting it less threatening.

The tool is not sophisticated. That is the point.

## POST 4: Revenue Transparency -- What My First Digital Product Made in Its First Week

The first product made $47 in the first week. Three sales.

That number is not impressive. What it did was prove the mechanism. Someone who did not know me found the product, decided it was worth their money, and paid for it. That is the whole system working. The number can grow. The mechanism had to be proven first.

The second week it made $23. The third week, $91 after a post that referenced it once in passing. The correlation between content and sales became visible immediately.

Most creators do not share early numbers because they are small. Sharing them would have saved me months of wondering whether any of this could work. It can. The early numbers are just calibration data.

## POST 5: Building a Store vs. Building a Business

A store is a collection of products with a checkout page. A business is a system that brings buyers to that page and keeps them coming back.

Most solo creators build a great store and then wonder why nothing sells. The store is the easiest part. The audience is the hard part. The audience has to exist, trust you, and have a reason to return.

The mental model shift is from "I have products to sell" to "I am building an audience that has a problem I know how to solve." The store is downstream of that. Without the audience, the store is just a well-organized folder nobody visits.

The implication is clear: content before catalog. Audience before store. Trust before transaction.

## POST 6: How I Use Claude as a Second Brain, Not a Content Generator

The wrong use is asking it to write things for you. The right use is asking it to think with you.

The difference in practice: instead of "write me a LinkedIn post about X," I say "here is the argument I want to make, here are the pieces I have, what is the clearest way to structure this?" Then I write the post. The output is mine. The structure came from a conversation.

The second brain function is more valuable than the content generation function because it compounds. Every conversation that helps me think more clearly makes the next one faster. I am not outsourcing the thinking. I am augmenting it.

Content generators produce content. Second brains produce better thinking. Better thinking produces better content and better decisions and better products. The leverage is different.

## POST 7: The Self-Hosted Store vs. Gumroad Decision

Gumroad is the right answer for most people starting out. It handles payments, delivery, and discoverability. The fees are real but the cost of building infrastructure to replace it is higher in the early stages.

The self-hosted argument makes sense when three things are true: you have consistent traffic you own, you want full control of the buyer relationship, and the transaction volume makes the platform fees significant. None of those conditions exist at the start.

The error is optimizing for ownership before you have the volume that makes ownership matter. Self-hosting a store with no traffic is an infrastructure project dressed up as a business decision.

Start on Gumroad. Move when the math says move. The migration is not complicated.

#gumroad #digitalproducts #onlinestore

## POST 8: Puerto Rico as a Base for Building Online

Puerto Rico gets talked about mostly as a tax destination. That framing misses what it actually is as a place to build.

The timezone sits between US and European work hours. The internet infrastructure is solid. The cost of living is lower than any major US city while remaining legally domestic for US-based banking and payment processing. No currency conversion, no international banking friction.

The honest tradeoff is infrastructure uncertainty after storms and a business ecosystem that is still developing. The support networks that exist in Austin or Miami are thinner here.

What Puerto Rico offers is a combination that does not exist elsewhere: US legal and financial infrastructure, Caribbean cost of living, and a cultural context that is genuinely distinct. For building an online business targeting LATAM and the US simultaneously, the positioning is almost exact.

#puertorico #remotework #solopreneurship

## POST 9: Learning to Ship Without Perfectionism

The sunk cost fallacy is usually described as staying too long on a failing project. There is a reverse version: staying too long on a good one before shipping it.

Every hour spent polishing something that is already good enough is an hour not spent on the next thing. The polishing feels productive. It is not. It is a delay dressed as diligence.

The habit I built was setting a ship date at the start of a project, not at the end. The date does not move. The scope moves. When the date arrives, whatever exists ships. If something is genuinely broken, that is a real reason to delay. "Could be better" is not.

Most things that ship imperfect get better from real user feedback faster than they would have from more polish time.

## POST 10: The 3 AM Clarity Principle

The best ideas I have had arrived at unusual hours. Not because there is anything special about 3 AM. Because that is when the noise stops.

During normal hours, the brain is processing inputs constantly. Notifications, conversations, tasks, social feedback. There is very little space for a new pattern to surface. Late at night, the inputs stop. The brain keeps processing. Something clicks.

The practical implication is not to stay up until 3 AM chasing ideas. It is to create the same condition intentionally. Long walks. Showers. The 20 minutes before sleep. Anything that removes input and lets the background processing surface.

The ideas that come in those windows are usually not new. They are connections between things the brain was already holding. The quiet just makes them visible.
