## Short 1035: How to Get Clear on What You Want

**Hook (0-3s):** "Most people spend their whole life chasing what they think they should want."

**Duration:** 55 seconds

[SCRIPT]
Most people spend their whole life chasing what they think they should want.

The job they were told was stable. The approval they were conditioned to seek. The success metrics handed to them by someone else.

Getting clear on what you actually want requires a different kind of question. Not "what do I want to achieve?" but "what kind of life do I want to wake up inside of?"

Example: A consultant making $200K a year who hates every Monday has chosen outcomes over life design. A creator making $60K doing work that energizes them every day made a different choice. Neither is objectively correct. But one chose deliberately.

The takeaway: clarity about what you want has to precede strategy. Strategy without a real destination is just busy motion.

Write down what your ideal week looks like in two years. Be specific. Work backward from there.

## Short 1036: The Anti-Burnout Framework
**Title:** How to Build Without Burning Out (The Framework)
**Hook (0-3s):** "Burnout isn't from working too hard. It's from working without clarity or recovery."

**Duration:** 60 seconds

[SCRIPT]
Burnout isn't from working too hard. It's from working without clarity or recovery.

There's a difference between the exhaustion of hard, purposeful work and the depletion of grinding without direction.

The framework that prevents burnout has three parts.

One: define your done for the day. Not a to-do list that expands endlessly, but a specific list of three things that constitute a complete day. When those are done, stop.

Two: protect recovery. Not passive recovery like scrolling, but actual recovery. Sleep, physical movement, time without screens.

Three: reconnect to the reason regularly. When work loses its connection to meaning, effort starts to feel pointless. Revisit why you're building what you're building.

Example: A creator who works six focused hours with clear daily outcomes and real recovery outlasts one who works twelve unfocused hours with no recovery by a factor of years.

Build like you're playing a long game. Because you are.

The creator who blew up in a month had been posting consistently for two years. The business that went viral was the founder's third attempt. The product that sold out overnight had been iterated fifteen times.

The public always sees the result. The decade of foundation never gets the spotlight.

Example: James Clear wrote for years with modest readership before Atomic Habits became a cultural phenomenon. The "overnight success" was the visible tip of years of compounding work below the surface.

The takeaway: stop comparing your chapter one to someone else's chapter ten. What looks like sudden results is almost always long-term consistency finally breaking the surface.

You're not behind. You're early.

Stay consistent. The visible results come after the invisible work, not instead of it.

## Short 1038: Why Your First Product Should Be Cheap
**Title:** Why Your First Digital Product Should Be Under $30
**Hook (0-3s):** "Pricing your first digital product low isn't selling yourself short. It's smart strategy."

**Duration:** 55 seconds

[SCRIPT]
Pricing your first digital product low isn't selling yourself short. It's smart strategy.

A low-price product does three things that high-ticket products can't do at launch.

One: it removes friction. At $9 to $27, the purchase decision is easy. People buy without much deliberation. You get volume. Volume gives you feedback.

Two: it builds social proof. Fifty buyers of a $19 product tell you more about what people actually want than zero buyers of a $197 product.

Three: it starts the relationship. Every buyer of your $19 product is a warm prospect for your $97 product. The trust is already there.

Example: A creator launched a $17 notion template. 200 people bought it. The feedback led to a $97 productivity course. The same 200 people were the first buyers.

The takeaway: your low-ticket product isn't the business. It's the entry point to the business.

Price for access first. Price for value second.

## Short 1039: The Most Dangerous Lie You Tell Yourself
**Title:** The Most Dangerous Lie High Achievers Tell Themselves
**Hook (0-3s):** "The most dangerous lie isn't that you can't. It's that you're almost ready."

**Duration:** 50 seconds

[SCRIPT]
The most dangerous lie isn't that you can't. It's that you're almost ready.

"Almost ready" is the most productive-sounding form of avoidance. It feels like progress. You're studying, preparing, refining. But nothing ships.

The people who built real things didn't wait until they were ready. They started and got ready in the process.

Example: Every creator who published their first post, launched their first product, or sent their first newsletter will tell you the same thing. It wasn't ready. They launched anyway. The feedback made it better.

The takeaway: almost ready is code for not started. The standard of "ready" always moves forward when it has no deadline.

Set a deadline. Ship on that date regardless of how it feels.

Ready is earned through action, not through preparation.
