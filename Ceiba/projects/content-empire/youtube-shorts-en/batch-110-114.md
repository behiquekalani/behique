## Short 110 — Substack vs Self-Hosted Newsletter

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
[Text on screen: "Substack takes 10% of your revenue. Forever."]

**Script (4-55s)**
Here is the real comparison between Substack and a self-hosted newsletter.

Substack gives you built-in discovery. There is a network of readers inside the platform that can find your work without you doing anything extra. Setup is fast. No technical knowledge needed.

The cost: 10% of every dollar you earn, for life. And your subscriber data lives on their platform, not yours.

A self-hosted newsletter with something like ConvertKit or Beehiiv gives you full ownership. Every subscriber, every email, every piece of data is yours. You can move providers without losing anything.

My recommendation: start on Substack to validate your audience. Once you hit 1,000 subscribers and see real traction, migrate to your own infrastructure.

The mistake is not picking the wrong platform. The mistake is spending six months choosing instead of six months publishing.

## Short 111 — LinkedIn for Creators in 2025

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
[Text on screen: "LinkedIn has the highest purchasing power per user of any social platform."]

**Script (4-55s)**
LinkedIn in 2025 is not the HR network it was five years ago.

It still has the most generous organic reach of any major platform. And it has the highest purchasing power per user of any social network. People here have money and problems they need solved.

Most professionals use it wrong. They post achievements, certifications, job announcements. Content nobody reads.

What actually gets traction on LinkedIn is specific analysis of a real topic, honest experience with clear lessons, or a perspective that goes against the dominant narrative in your field.

The platform rewards people who teach. Not people who present themselves.

If you have knowledge in any area, business, tech, health, finance, education, LinkedIn is where that knowledge has the most economic value per post.

The window is open now. In two or three years it will be as saturated as Instagram.

## Short 112 — Micro-Course vs Full Course

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
[Text on screen: "A $37 micro-course can outsell a $500 course. Here's why."]

**Script (4-55s)**
The belief that you need a comprehensive, exhaustive course to sell well is costing creators years of work.

A $37 micro-course that solves one specific problem in three hours can outsell a $500 course that tries to cover everything in 40 hours.

Not because it is cheaper. Because the promise is clearer.

The buyer of a micro-course knows exactly what problem they are solving. The buyer of a full course is buying hope for general transformation, and that creates more friction in the buying decision.

Three scenarios where the micro-course wins: when you are validating whether anyone pays for that topic, when your audience is not ready for depth yet, and when you want a low-price entry into a larger product ecosystem.

The full course makes sense when you have proof the audience wants to go deep with you.

Most people should start with the micro-course. Finishing a small project teaches you more than planning a large one for two years.

## Short 113 — Ghostwriting as a Real Business

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
[Text on screen: "People are paying $3,000-8,000/month for someone to write for them."]

**Script (4-55s)**
Ghostwriting is one of the businesses with the least visible competition and the most silent demand that exists today.

There are executives, founders, consultants, and experts who know what they think but do not know how to express it. They have valuable ideas and no time or skill to turn them into content that builds their brand.

That is where the ghostwriter comes in.

You do not write about what you think. You write in the client's voice, with their values and their experiences. Your job is to become completely invisible.

The business model is straightforward: a monthly retainer for a defined volume of content. LinkedIn posts, newsletters, video scripts, whatever the client needs. Prices range from $1,500 to $8,000 per month depending on volume and client profile.

To enter the market you need three things: writing samples in the style of your target client, the ability to run good interviews to extract ideas, and the discipline to deliver consistently.

The first client is always the hardest. The second comes by referral.

## Short 114 — The Annual Business Review

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
[Text on screen: "Most annual reviews are just organized self-deception."]

**Script (4-55s)**
Most annual business reviews are exercises in self-deception.

You celebrate what went well, justify what went badly, and set goals that are 20% bigger versions of what you were already doing. In January next year you do the same thing.

A real annual review requires uncomfortable honesty.

The questions that matter are not how much you billed or how many followers you gained. They are these: What did you keep doing out of habit even though the data said it was not working? What did you avoid doing even though you knew it was right? What clients or projects did you accept that you should have turned down?

After those questions, the financial review actually makes sense. Where did the money really come from? Not what you expected, what actually arrived. Which channel, which product, which client represented 80% of real results?

Most people discover that 80% of their results came from 20% of their actions, and that the other 80% of their actions was well-organized noise.

The annual review is not for celebrating. It is for cutting what does not work and doubling down on what does.
