## Short 72 — Digital Income

**Title:** The Honest Math Behind Digital Product Income

**Hook (0-3 sec):**
Here is what $3,000 a month from digital products actually looks like in real numbers.

**Main Point (3-45 sec):**
Option one: Sell a $30 product to 100 people. That's 100 individual buyers. Requires volume, paid ads, or a large audience.

Option two: Sell a $100 product to 30 people. More achievable with a smaller but engaged audience.

Option three: Sell a $300 product to 10 people. This is where positioning and specificity matter most. Ten buyers is not a lot of people.

Most digital product beginners aim for low price and high volume. That's the hardest path. High volume requires large distribution.

The more specific the problem your product solves, the higher you can price it. Someone paying $300 for a solution to a painful, specific problem is easier to find than 100 people willing to pay $30.

Start with a specific problem. Price for the pain it solves.

## Short 73 — Positioning

**Title:** Stop Trying to Reach Everyone (Do This Instead)

**Hook (0-3 sec):**
The more specific your message, the more money you make. Here is why that is true.

**Main Point (3-45 sec):**
When your content speaks to everyone, it resonates with no one. Generic messages feel safe but they scroll past.

Specific messages feel like they were written for one person. That person stops. That person shares. That person buys.

The counterintuitive truth of positioning is that narrowing your focus increases your reach. Not immediately, but compoundingly.

A person who says "I help small business owners" is invisible online. A person who says "I help solo consultants get their first five recurring clients without paid ads" has a message that cuts through.

The second person's audience is smaller in theory but far more concentrated, more loyal, and more willing to pay.

Positioning is the decision to be for someone specific. That decision is worth more than any content calendar or distribution strategy.

## Short 74 — Building

**Title:** Build in Public or Build in Secret (The Real Answer)

**Hook (0-3 sec):**
Everyone says build in public. Here is when it actually works and when it backfires.

**Main Point (3-45 sec):**
Building in public works when you have a genuine process worth following. When the journey itself is the content. When your audience learns something from watching you figure it out.

It backfires when it becomes performance. When you're documenting to look like you're building instead of actually building.

The test is simple: if you removed the audience tomorrow, would you still be doing the work? If yes, building in public is a distribution layer on top of real work. That's powerful.

If no, the audience is the work. That's fragile. It means every slow day, every bad metric, every missed launch becomes a content crisis.

Build the thing first. Document the real process. Share the honest version.

The audience will come for the authenticity, not the highlight reel.
