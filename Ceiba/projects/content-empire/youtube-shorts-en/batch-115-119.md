## Short 115 — LinkedIn for Creators in 2025

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
Text on screen: "LinkedIn isn't a job board if you already built your own job."

**Script (4-55s)**
LinkedIn in 2025 is the highest-leverage platform for creators who sell knowledge or services. Here's why most creators waste it.

They post motivational content. Their audience already gets that everywhere. It doesn't convert to clients or buyers because it doesn't prove anything about what you know.

What actually works: documented experience. Not opinions. Not quotes. Specific situations, specific decisions, specific outcomes. The format is: here is the problem I faced, here is what I did, here is what I learned. That structure signals real expertise and creates real trust with people who have real money to spend.

The audience on LinkedIn is older, higher income, and actively looking for solutions to business problems. If what you sell solves a business problem, there is no better organic platform.

Three posts per week for six months is enough to build meaningful inbound. Most creators quit in week three and conclude it doesn't work.

It works. You just have to post like someone who actually does the work.

## Short 116 — Substack vs Self-Hosted

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
Text on screen: "Substack vs your own platform. The real trade-off nobody explains."

**Script (4-55s)**
Substack has two genuine advantages: their recommendation network and zero setup friction. If you're starting from nothing, those matter.

The cost is that your subscriber data belongs to their ecosystem more than yours. And your business model is constrained by their feature set. If you want to build a complex funnel, sell products at different price points, or segment your list in sophisticated ways, Substack's limitations become real.

Self-hosted newsletter platforms, Beehiiv, ConvertKit, Kit, give you full ownership and full flexibility. The trade-off is that the discovery advantage disappears. You have to bring your own traffic.

The decision framework is this: if you're building a business where the newsletter is the product, move to your own platform eventually. If the newsletter is a marketing channel for a larger business, full ownership matters even more.

Substack is a valid starting point. It is not a valid destination for a creator with serious product ambitions.

The migration is real work but it's not catastrophic. Most lists survive it with 70-80% retention. Plan it, do it once, and don't look back.

## Short 117 — Ghostwriting as a Real Business

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
Text on screen: "People are paying $5,000/month for someone to write their LinkedIn posts."

**Script (4-55s)**
Ghostwriting is the most underrated service business in the creator economy right now.

The demand comes from a structural gap. There are thousands of founders, executives, and experts who have deep knowledge and real audiences but don't have time or skill to turn that knowledge into consistent, publishable content.

They need someone who can interview them, synthesize what they say, and write it in their voice. Not your voice. Theirs.

That last part is what separates a freelance writer from a ghostwriter. It requires ego subordination. The goal is that a reader who knows your client should read the piece and say "that sounds like them." If they can tell you wrote it, you haven't done the job.

The business model is retainer-based. Monthly. Fixed deliverables. Predictable income. Good clients stay for years.

The rate range for intermediate ghostwriters doing LinkedIn or newsletter work is $2,000 to $8,000 per month depending on output, niche, and client profile.

First client is the hardest. Every client after that comes from referral.

## Short 118 — The Annual Business Review

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
Text on screen: "Once a year, look at your business like a stranger would."

**Script (4-55s)**
The annual review is not about setting goals. You can set goals any day. It's about seeing clearly what actually happened versus what you told yourself happened.

Four questions that do the real work.

Where did 80% of the revenue actually come from? Not where you think. Pull the numbers. The answer usually reveals that one or two things drove almost everything and most of what felt important was noise.

What produced the most traction per unit of effort? Do more of that next year. Not what looks impressive. What actually moved the needle.

What did you plan to build or launch that you didn't? Make a conscious decision about each one. Commit to a date, kill it permanently, or postpone it with a specific review date. Do not leave things in ambiguous limbo.

What did this year's clients and customers teach you about who you should and shouldn't serve? That knowledge is the most valuable output of any business year, and most people never extract it.

The review doesn't produce a perfect plan. It produces clarity. And a clear-eyed operator outperforms an enthusiastic one every time.

## Short 119 — Paid Workshops vs Courses

**Visual Hook (0-3s)**
Text on screen: "Courses have a completion problem. Workshops don't."

**Script (4-55s)**
The average online course completion rate is somewhere between 5 and 15 percent. That is not a motivation problem. It is a product design problem.

Courses are built for async self-paced consumption. They work when the student has enough intrinsic drive to move through material without accountability. That is a small percentage of buyers.

Workshops are live, time-limited, and synchronous. The student knows there is a session tomorrow. That accountability structure drives completion. It drives application. It drives results that are visible and attributable, which means better testimonials and higher retention.

The trade-off is scale. A course can sell to ten thousand people with no additional time from you. A workshop caps at your calendar availability unless you bring in support.

The right approach depends on where you are. If you have an existing audience and want to build trust quickly, workshops first. They produce real outcomes, real testimonials, and proof of concept before you invest the production time a full course requires.

If you already have traction and social proof, a course makes sense as an asset that generates income without your direct involvement every time.

Neither is superior. They serve different functions at different stages.
