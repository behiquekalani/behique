## Short 80
**Title:** Why Your Content Gets Views But Not Customers

**Hook (0-3 sec):**
You are getting views but not making money.
Here is the structural reason why.

**Main Point (3-45 sec):**
Views and customers require different types of content.

Views come from entertainment, curiosity, and relatability.
Customers come from trust, specificity, and a clear next step.

Most creators optimize for views.
They make broad content that resonates with everyone but converts no one.

The fix is not less broad content. It is adding a second layer.

For every 5 entertainment posts, publish 1 or 2 that go deep on a specific problem your audience has.
Show the solution. Show the result. Then point to something they can buy or download.

The audience that watches your entertainment content will not all convert.
But the subset that sees you solve their exact problem will.

That subset is your customer base.
You do not need it to be large. You need it to exist.

## Short 82
**Title:** How to Price Your First Digital Product

**Hook (0-3 sec):**
You are underpricing your first product.
Here is how to fix it.

**Main Point (3-45 sec):**
The default pricing instinct for a first-time creator is to price low so people buy.
$7, $9, $12.

The problem is that low prices attract low-commitment buyers.
They download and never use it. They ask for refunds at higher rates. They do not leave reviews.

And mathematically, you need 10 times more sales to hit the same revenue.

A better framework: price based on the outcome, not the format.

If your guide saves someone 20 hours of research, and their time is worth $25 an hour, you saved them $500.
Charging $29 to $49 is a good deal for them.

Start at $29 minimum for anything that solves a real problem.
If your audience is professional, $49 to $99 is defensible for a solid resource.

Price communicates quality.
A $9 product signals "quick and cheap."
A $49 product signals "this was built seriously."

## Short 83
**Title:** The Positioning Move That Makes You Obvious to Your Audience

**Hook (0-3 sec):**
In a saturated niche, being good is not enough.
You need to be obvious.

**Main Point (3-45 sec):**
Obvious means that when your specific type of person has your specific type of problem, your name comes to mind first.

That does not happen by accident. It requires a positioning decision.

Most creators position around a broad topic.
"I talk about personal finance." "I talk about fitness." "I talk about business."

Those categories are full. There is no room at the top for another generalist.

The move is to narrow until you own the corner.

"I help first-generation college graduates in Latin America build a financial plan before their first real job."
That is a corner. Very few people own that corner. If you do, you are obvious to that specific person.

The fear is that narrowing means losing reach.
The reality is that narrowing means being found by people who are ready to buy.

Generic content reaches many people.
Specific content reaches the right people.

## Short 84
**Title:** What "Building in Public" Actually Does for Your Business

**Hook (0-3 sec):**
Building in public is not a content strategy.
It is a trust strategy. There is a difference.

**Main Point (3-45 sec):**
Most people who try building in public treat it as a metrics diary.
"I got 47 subscribers this week. Revenue is $230."

That is transparency, but it is not trust.

Trust comes from showing the thinking, not just the numbers.

Share the decision you almost got wrong and how you caught it.
Share the product idea you killed and why.
Share the moment you had to choose between two strategies.

That kind of content does something numbers cannot.
It makes the audience feel like they are inside the process with you.
And people buy from people they feel close to.

The business benefit is tangible.
Your audience grows more slowly than viral accounts, but it converts at a far higher rate.
They have been following the story. They are already invested in the outcome.

Building in public done right is the longest-form trust signal available to a solopreneur.
