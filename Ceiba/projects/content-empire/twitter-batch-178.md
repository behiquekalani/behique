## Threads

**6. The minimum viable solopreneur stack**

1/ You don't need 12 tools to run a one-person business. Here's the minimum viable stack:

2/ A place to write (Notion, Obsidian, or a doc). A place to sell (Gumroad, Lemon Squeezy). A place to build an audience (one social platform, one newsletter). That's it.

3/ Every tool you add past that is overhead. It requires maintenance, syncing, and mental load.

4/ Start minimal. Add tools only when the absence of that tool is costing you real hours or real money. Not because someone in a YouTube thumbnail said to.

---

**7. Content that converts vs content that performs**

1/ High-performing content and high-converting content are not the same thing.

2/ Content that performs: gets views, shares, likes. Usually broad, relatable, taps into a widely shared emotion.

3/ Content that converts: gets people to buy or subscribe. Usually specific, addresses a known pain, has a clear implied solution.

4/ You need both. Use broad content to grow the audience. Use specific content to build the pipeline. Don't confuse the metrics.

---

**8. On digital product pricing**

1/ Most people underprice their first digital product, overprice their second, and are still confused about pricing by the third. Here's a simpler frame:

2/ Price reflects the transformation, not the effort. A 5-page PDF that saves someone 10 hours of research is worth $49. A 200-page book that doesn't solve a specific problem is worth $9.

3/ Test your pricing by raising it until sales slow meaningfully. The resistance point tells you more than any pricing guide.

4/ And remember: price is a positioning signal. Low price says "broad audience." High price says "specific professional problem." Choose based on who you want to attract.

---

**9. Why most solopreneurs burn out**

1/ Most solopreneur burnout isn't from overwork. It's from working on things that don't compound.

2/ If you're doing work today that has to be redone tomorrow, that's energy that evaporates. Writing a new pitch email every day. Explaining your offer from scratch every time.

3/ Compounding work: content that keeps attracting. Systems that keep running. Products that keep selling without your constant presence.

4/ Audit your week. For every hour, ask: does this compound or does this disappear? Shift the ratio. That's the actual cure for burnout.

---

**10. The one-person business thesis**

1/ The one-person business model works because the internet made distribution free and AI made production cheap.

2/ 10 years ago, building a real business alone required outsourcing design, copywriting, tech, and admin. That was expensive. It required capital.

3/ Today you can build all of that with one laptop, free tools, and the willingness to learn. The barrier isn't resources. It's the mental model.

4/ The one-person business isn't a backup plan. For the right person, it's the most rational structure available. Low overhead. High margins. Full ownership.
