**Post 1**
**Hook:** Your first product is not a business. It is a proof of concept.
**Caption:** The goal of your first product is not to maximize revenue. It is to learn whether you can take an idea from zero to something a stranger will pay for. That lesson is worth more than the money you make from it. Pricing it cheap, between $5 and $15, removes the barrier for that learning to happen. When the price is low, the volume of buyers is higher, the feedback loop is faster, and you find out sooner whether the product works.

**Post 2**
**Hook:** A $7 product that sells 50 times teaches you more than a $97 product that sells twice.
**Caption:** Fifty buyers means fifty real people chose to spend money on what you made. That is fifty data points on the title, the problem, the promise, the format. Two buyers is almost no data. Pricing high before you have an audience or social proof is not ambition. It is guessing. The cheap first product is a deliberate data collection strategy. Use it to learn, then raise the price with real evidence behind you.

**Post 3**
**Hook:** Low price lowers the psychological barrier to "yes" for someone who doesn't know you yet.
**Caption:** A stranger on the internet buying from you for the first time has one primary concern: is this worth the risk? At $7, the risk is almost nothing. At $97, the risk requires research, social proof, and trust that takes time to build. The cheap product converts cold traffic into warm buyers. Once someone buys from you and the product delivers, the price of the next thing becomes much less of an obstacle. Cheap first is a trust-building strategy, not a devaluation.

**Post 4**
**Hook:** The creator who priced too high and made zero sales learned the wrong lesson.
**Caption:** Pricing high and getting no sales usually produces the wrong conclusion: "nobody wants this." The real conclusion is: "nobody who didn't know me was willing to pay that price without more trust or social proof." Those are very different problems with very different solutions. One sends you back to the drawing board. The other sends you to a different price point. Before concluding your product doesn't work, test whether the price is what's blocking.

**Post 5**
**Hook:** You can raise prices once you have proof. You cannot build proof at a price nobody will pay.
**Caption:** Proof in a digital product business looks like: reviews, testimonials, screenshots of results, volume of buyers. You build that proof through volume, and volume requires a low enough price that people take the chance on an unknown creator. The sequence matters: cheap to build proof, then raise the price armed with that proof. Skipping the first step and going straight to premium pricing without social proof is just wishful thinking.

**Post 6**
**Hook:** A $9 product that people talk about is worth more than a $49 product they regret.
**Caption:** Word of mouth is your most valuable distribution channel at the beginning. It costs nothing and it comes with built-in trust. For people to talk about your product, it has to have over-delivered at its price point. A $9 guide that is genuinely worth $30 of value gets shared. A $49 guide that barely meets expectations gets refunded and forgotten. Under-promise and over-deliver at a low price. Let the buyer become your marketing.

**Post 7**
**Hook:** There is no such thing as "too cheap" if the product is actually good.
**Caption:** The anxiety around cheap pricing usually comes from confusing price with quality. Your product's quality is determined by what's inside it, not what you charge for it. Pricing it low at launch does not make it a lesser product. It makes it an accessible one. The creator who is embarrassed to sell something for $7 has a self-worth problem masquerading as a business strategy problem. Solve the business problem first. The price can go up once you have the results to justify it.

**Post 8**
**Hook:** The "pay what you want" strategy tells you exactly what your audience thinks your product is worth.
**Caption:** Gumroad has a "pay what you want" feature where you set a minimum and let the buyer choose. For a first product, this is an extremely useful test. If most people pay the minimum, you have a pricing or perceived-value problem. If many people pay above the minimum, you have proof that people see more value than you priced in, and you can raise the floor. It is market research built into the checkout process.

**Post 9**
**Hook:** Your first $100 online is not about the money. It is about knowing you can do it.
**Caption:** There is a psychological shift that happens when someone pays you for something you made. It changes how you see yourself and what you think is possible. That shift does not require selling something expensive. It requires selling something to a real person who chose to spend real money on it. A $7 product that gets you to your first $100 gives you that shift faster than waiting until you have something worth $100 per sale.

**Post 10**
**Hook:** Volume at low prices builds the audience that makes high-ticket sales possible later.
**Caption:** Every person who buys a $7 product from you is now on your email list. They have experienced your work. They know you deliver. When you launch something at $47 or $97 six months later, you are not selling to strangers. You are selling to people who already bought from you and liked what they got. The cheap product is not the end goal. It is the beginning of a relationship that eventually justifies premium pricing.
