## Post 3: What AI Can Actually Do Inside Your DAW

AI can do three things inside a DAW that are genuinely useful: suggest chord progressions, flag mix imbalances before mastering, and speed up repetitive MIDI editing tasks. What it cannot do is replace your ear, your taste, or your arrangement decisions. Most of the AI music tools being marketed right now are doing the first category and calling it the second. Know the difference before you buy or build.

## Post 4: The FL Studio Python API Nobody Talks About

FL Studio has a full Python scripting API. You can write scripts that control the mixer, trigger patterns, and build custom MIDI controllers with it. Most producers who have been on FL for years do not know this exists. Image-Line added it quietly and barely marketed it. If you know Python and you use FL Studio, you already have most of what you need to build your first plugin.

## Post 5: Why FL Studio Users Buy Tools

FL Studio's user base skews younger and more technically curious than most other DAW communities. The average FL user has watched more YouTube tutorials, tried more third-party tools, and spent more on courses and plugins per year than users on Ableton or Logic. This is documented behavior, not a guess. It means the audience is already primed to buy things that help them get better faster. That matters if you are deciding where to launch a plugin or tool.

## Post 6: Building Your First MIDI Plugin Without a Music Degree

You do not need a music degree to build a MIDI plugin. You need to understand three things: note numbers, velocity values, and timing. MIDI is a 40-year-old protocol with very little complexity at its core. The Python scripting layer in FL Studio or a library like Mido in Python gets you reading and writing MIDI data in an afternoon. The music theory you need comes after, and it is easier to learn when you are building something real with it.

## Post 7: AI Chord Progressions, the Technical Side

When people say "AI generated chords," they usually mean one of two things: a language model prompted with music theory context, or a trained model on MIDI datasets like the Lakh MIDI Collection. Both approaches work. The LLM approach is faster to prototype because you can constrain the output with theory rules in the prompt. The dataset-trained approach sounds more natural but takes longer to build. Neither is magic. Both are learnable with a weekend of focused time.
