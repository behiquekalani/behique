## ISSUE 41

**Subject line:** Why most productivity systems fail ADHD builders

**Preheader:** They were not built for your brain. Here is what to build instead.

---

Hey,

Most productivity systems were designed by neurotypical people, for neurotypical people. They assume a baseline that ADHD brains do not have.

That is not a flaw in you. It is a design mismatch. And building on the wrong foundation wastes years.

---

### The Neurotypical Assumption

The classic productivity system assumes a few things that feel obvious until you examine them.

It assumes you can start a task just because it is on a list. It assumes a schedule is motivating. It assumes that once you know what to do, the doing follows naturally.

For a large portion of people, this is approximately true. For ADHD brains, the gap between knowing and doing is the entire problem. The system does not account for the gap. It skips it entirely and calls you lazy when you fall in.

Task lists are not action triggers for ADHD brains. They are inventories. Knowing what needs to happen and initiating the action are two completely separate cognitive events. Standard systems treat them as one.

---

### What Actually Fails

Time blocking fails because ADHD brains do not respond to scheduled start times the way neurotypical brains do. You can block 9-11 AM for deep work and still spend that time doing five other things, not because you forgot, but because the block did not create sufficient initiation pressure.

Prioritization frameworks fail because ADHD brains already know what is most important. Reprioritizing is not the problem. Starting the important thing is.

Long task lists fail because cognitive overload kills initiation. A list of 20 items triggers avoidance, not action.

Habit trackers fail when they become another thing to fail at. The streak breaking becomes its own demotivating event.

---

### What to Build Instead

The ADHD-compatible system has a different architecture.

Start with constraint, not schedule. One task at a time. Not "work on the project from 9-11." Work on this one specific thing until the timer hits 25 minutes. The constraint is the trigger.

Replace lists with decisions. Every morning, you make one decision: what is the first thing. Not the full day. The first thing. Write it somewhere visible and close everything else.

Build in friction reduction. ADHD initiation problems are often friction problems. If starting requires opening five tabs, finding a password, and loading a file, the friction kills momentum before it starts. The system should reduce friction to near zero for the first action.

Use external accountability as structure. Coworking calls, body doubling, public commitments. These are not cheats. They are structural supports that compensate for internally-generated motivation, which is inconsistent in ADHD brains.

Build recovery into the system. Most ADHD productivity systems assume success means zero deviations. The better design assumes deviations and has a reset protocol. When you fall off: one step, not a restart. The next action is always smaller than you think it needs to be.

---

### The Point

You are not bad at productivity. You are using tools built for a different brain.

The system that works for you will look different than the systems you have been taught. That is not a compromise. It is an accurate design.

## ISSUE 42

**Subject line:** How I actually price a digital product

**Preheader:** Start with value. Not cost. Not what feels fair.

---

Hey,

Most people price digital products by asking: how long did this take me to make?

That is the wrong question. Nobody pays for your hours. They pay for their outcome.

---

### The Framework

Here is the actual framework I use every time I price something.

Step one: what is the buyer trying to do? Not what does my product help with in general. What specific outcome does someone buy this to achieve?

Step two: what is that outcome worth to them? Not in abstract terms. Concretely. If someone buys my ADHD Finance template and uses it to stop living paycheck to paycheck, what is that worth? If someone buys the AI Employee Guide and uses it to automate four hours of weekly work, what is four hours a week worth to them over a year?

Step three: what are the alternatives? Not other products like mine. The full alternative set. Hiring someone. Learning it themselves. Not solving the problem at all. What does each of those cost, in time or money?

My price anchors to the alternative. It does not need to be cheaper than the alternative. It needs to be clearly better value than the alternative when the buyer considers the comparison.

---

### Anchoring in Practice

Take a product priced at $14.99. The buyer's alternative is spending four hours researching the same thing online. Their time is worth something. If they value their time at $15/hour, four hours of research is worth $60 to them. My product is not a $15 thing competing against free. It is a $15 thing competing against $60 worth of their time.

That is the frame you present. Not explicitly. Through the way you describe the outcome and the comparison.

The other anchoring mechanism is tiers. If I offer a product at $14.99, and next to it is a bundle at $29.99 with three additional items, the $14.99 suddenly looks cheap. Not because the price changed. Because the context around it changed.

Price psychology works on relative perception. The absolute number matters less than what sits next to it.

---

### What I Do Not Do

I do not price based on what feels humble. Underpricing a product does not make you more accessible. It signals that the product is not worth much. People calibrate quality to price faster than they calibrate price to quality.

I do not price based on what competitors charge unless the competitor is the direct alternative the buyer considers. Most competitors are not. Most digital product creators are not in competition with each other the way they think they are.

I do not leave prices fixed forever. The first price is a test. It will change based on what the data shows about conversion and buyer satisfaction.

---

### The Short Version

Value first. Alternative second. Cost of making it last, and only as a floor.

If your price is below your floor, raise it. If it is above the perceived value, fix the positioning before you lower the price.

## ISSUE 43

**Subject line:** Why I build in Spanish

**Preheader:** The market gap is real. The decision was not hard.

---

Hey,

I sell products in English and Spanish. The Spanish side gets less traffic and more conversion.

That is the whole business case. But there is more to say about why.

---

### The Market Gap

The English-language digital product market is mature. Crowded is not the right word. Saturated is not the right word either. The right word is competitive. There are thousands of people selling productivity tools, AI guides, and solopreneur templates in English. The cost of acquiring attention in that market is high.

The Spanish-language digital product market is five years behind. There are far fewer creators. There are far fewer products. There are tens of millions of Spanish-speaking people who want exactly what the English market offers, and the supply is thin.

I am Puerto Rican. Spanish is my first language. I understand the culture, the references, the trust dynamics. I do not have to simulate authenticity in that market. I am native to it.

That combination, supply gap plus authentic presence, is the actual competitive advantage. It does not require more skill. It requires showing up where other people are not.

---

### What Bilingual Actually Means Online

Being bilingual online is not translating content. Translation is table stakes. Any tool can translate.

Being bilingual online means you can read the emotional register of a Spanish-speaking audience without running it through a cultural adapter. You know which jokes land. You know which formality level signals respect versus distance. You know what the words imply beyond their dictionary definition.

You also understand code-switching. Many of my audience members think in Spanish but consume professional content in English. They have been trained by the internet to expect expertise in English and warmth in Spanish. When I combine both, something registers differently than when a purely English-language creator tries to reach them with a translated version of the same product.

---

### The Practical Side

The practical case is straightforward.

One product becomes two listings. One piece of content becomes two posts. One email becomes two emails. The incremental cost of the Spanish version is low. The incremental reach is significant.

The communities I care about most, Puerto Rican, Latin American, Spanish-speaking diaspora in the US, are my people in a way that goes beyond market strategy. Building in Spanish is not just a channel decision. It is a decision about who I am building for.

I am building for someone who looks like me, thinks like me, was told the same things I was told about what is possible, and needs to see a counterexample.

That is not a niche. That is a purpose.

---

### The Case You Can Make

If you are bilingual and you are building in one language only, you are leaving a specific kind of leverage on the table. Not just market leverage. Identity leverage. The kind of authority that comes from being unambiguously native to a context.

Nobody can out-Puerto-Rico me. That is a defensible position.

## ISSUE 44

**Subject line:** What compound interest looks like for content

**Preheader:** The payoff is delayed. The math is not complicated. Most people quit before month 3.

---

Hey,

Content does not work the way effort works. With effort, you put in work and you get output roughly proportional to the input.

Content works the way interest works. The return is delayed, small at first, then non-linear.

Most people quit during the delay.

---

### The Numbers from Months 1 Through 3

Month 1: you post consistently. Engagement is low. You are building infrastructure that does not feel like infrastructure because the feedback loop is absent. Follower counts move slowly if at all. The posts feel like they are going nowhere.

What is actually happening: the algorithm is learning. Your audience profile is being assembled. The content is sitting in a searchable archive that will be indexed for months. Every post is not a one-day event. It is a permanent piece of content that can surface at any time.

Month 2: some posts from month 1 start bringing in small consistent traffic. You have enough content that people who land on your profile find something to explore. The conversion from visitor to follower improves because there is substance. You are no longer starting from zero every time someone finds you.

Month 3: if you have been consistent, you start seeing posts from two months ago still driving engagement. You have built enough content inventory that any piece of content you publish now links to something else. The audience is small but the signal is there. The algorithm begins to show your content to people who do not already follow you.

---

### Why Consistency Is the Variable

The compounding only works if you do not break the chain long enough to reset the algorithm's model of you.

A week off in month 1 is recoverable. A week off in month 3 costs you some of the momentum you built. The longer you have been consistent, the more costly the break.

This is the opposite of how ADHD brains want to engage with systems. The tendency is to burst, rest, burst. That pattern produces good individual pieces but not the compound growth the platform rewards.

The system I built to solve this is a content queue. I produce content in batches during high-energy creative windows. I schedule it forward so that consistency is pre-built and does not depend on daily motivation.

The batch I produce on a Saturday afternoon covers two or three weeks of posting. The consistency is not me being consistent every day. It is me being consistent once every two weeks in a big session.

---

### When It Clicks

There is a specific feeling when the compounding becomes visible. A post you wrote six weeks ago starts getting shared. A piece you forgot about brings five new followers in a day. The older content starts doing work you did not ask it to do.

It does not happen in month 1. It rarely happens in month 2.

Month 3 is when people who stayed start to understand what they built. It is also the month most people never reach because they made a judgment about content not working based on month 1 data.

---

### The Simple Version

Post consistently. Do not evaluate the system in month 1. Build a queue so consistency does not depend on daily willpower. Let the math work.

## ISSUE 45

**Subject line:** The three machines

**Preheader:** Ceiba thinks. Cobo processes. Hutia runs. Here is why the split matters.

---

Hey,

I have three computers. Each one has a job. The separation is not about hardware specs. It is about cognitive clarity.

---

### The Setup

Ceiba is an Apple M4, 16GB unified memory. It is where I think. It runs my primary Claude session, my Obsidian vault, my writing environment. Everything that requires focused attention and fast feedback happens here. When I am solving a problem or writing something, I am on Ceiba.

Cobo is an older PC with a GTX 1080 Ti, 11GB VRAM. It is where I process. It runs background agents, local AI models, heavy computation. It handles things that take time and do not need my attention. When I launch an autonomous task or run a local LLM, it runs on Cobo.

Hutia is the dedicated server machine. It runs. It hosts the store, the bots, the automated pipelines. Hutia is always on and I rarely interact with it directly. It executes.

---

### Why the Split Matters

The cognitive reason for the split is context contamination.

When everything lives on one machine, every window is a potential distraction. The background process competing with your active session. The notification from the agent you launched an hour ago interrupting the essay you are writing.

When the jobs are separated, the context is separated. Ceiba only has the things that require my presence. Cobo handles things that should not require my presence. Hutia runs things that should never require my presence unless something breaks.

This is not a productivity trick. It is a way of forcing clarity about what actually needs my attention and what does not. Most things do not need my attention. Most things need to run.

---

### The Mental Model Behind It

Each machine maps to a different cognitive mode.

Ceiba is thinking. Active, present, engaged. The work that requires me to actually be there.

Cobo is processing. Background work. The kind of thing that takes 20 minutes to run and produces an output I review later. It does not need my eyes on it.

Hutia is running. Infrastructure. The thing that is always happening whether I am working or sleeping. My products are served from Hutia. My bots respond from Hutia. Hutia does not care what time it is.

The reason this matters is that most solo builders conflate all three modes and run them on the same machine at the same time. The result is that nothing gets the full attention it needs. Thinking gets interrupted by processing. Running competes with both.

---

### The Single-Machine Version

You do not need three computers to apply this principle.

You need three modes. Think, process, run.

When you are thinking, close everything that belongs to the other two modes. When you are processing, run the batch job and walk away. When you are running, automate it and do not look at it until it needs maintenance.

The hardware is incidental. The separation is the point.
