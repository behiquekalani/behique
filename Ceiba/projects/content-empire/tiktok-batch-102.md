## TikTok 1: The Hook That Stopped the Scroll
**Hook (0-2s):**
"You lost them in the first second. Here's how I know."

**Script:**
Your content isn't bad. Your hook is.

Most people write their hook last, as an afterthought. That's why nobody watches.

The hook isn't the introduction to your video. It's the only reason someone stays.

Think of it this way: your viewer has their thumb ready to swipe. The hook is the one thing that makes them stop. It has two jobs.

First: interrupt the pattern. Say something unexpected. Make a claim. Ask a question that creates tension.

Second: promise a payoff. Your viewer needs to believe the next 60 seconds will be worth their time.

The best hooks I've seen do both in under three seconds.

Stop writing your hook as a greeting. Write it as a reason to stay.

## TikTok 2: The 3 Hook Structures That Work Every Time
**Hook (0-2s):**
"There are only 3 hooks that actually stop the scroll. I'll give you all three."

**Script:**
After studying hundreds of high-performing videos, the same patterns show up.

Hook one: The Counterintuitive Statement.
"Consistency is actually killing your growth." Say the opposite of what people expect. They stop to find out why.

Hook two: The Specific Result.
"I went from zero to 10,000 followers in 47 days. Here's exactly what I did." Specific numbers create credibility. Vague claims get scrolled past.

Hook three: The Direct Address.
"If you're a freelancer working more than 40 hours a week, stop and watch this." Naming your exact audience makes them feel like you're speaking only to them.

Every hook you write should be one of these three. Master these before you try anything else.

## TikTok 3: Why Your Hook Is Too Long
**Hook (0-2s):**
"Your hook is killing your video before it starts."

**Script:**
The best hooks are short. Like, uncomfortably short.

Seven words or fewer. That's the target.

When your hook runs long, the viewer's brain reads it as a paragraph before the story. They've already decided to scroll before you get to your point.

The trick: say the most interesting part first, then explain.

Bad hook: "In this video I'm going to share some tips about how I grew my business using content marketing."

Good hook: "Content marketing built my business. Here's the part nobody talks about."

The difference isn't the information. It's the order.

Lead with the tension. Follow with the context.

## TikTok 4: The Hook Mistake That Kills Retention
**Hook (0-2s):**
"Starting your video with 'Hey guys' is costing you views."

**Script:**
Let me tell you what your viewer does when you open with "Hey guys, welcome back."

They scroll.

Not because they don't like you. Because that phrase is a signal. It says: the interesting part hasn't started yet. Come back in 30 seconds.

Most people never come back.

The viewer doesn't owe you a warm-up. They're giving you one second, maybe two. Use it.

Open with the most important sentence in your video. The one you'd normally save for the middle.

Cut every word that comes before the actual value. The greetings. The intros. The context-setting.

Start in the middle of the story. Your retention will tell you this was the right call.

## TikTok 5: Write Hooks for the Worst-Case Scenario
**Hook (0-2s):**
"Write your hook assuming nobody knows who you are."

**Script:**
This is the mindset shift that changed how I write hooks.

Most creators write hooks for their existing audience. People who already trust them, already follow them, already know the context.

But most of your viewers don't know you at all.

They landed on your content through an algorithm. They have zero loyalty. They owe you nothing.

Your hook needs to earn attention from someone who has never seen your face and has no reason to care.

That means no callbacks to previous videos. No inside jokes. No assumed context.

Just: a reason to stay, in two seconds or less.

Write the hook for the stranger, not the fan. The fans will follow anyway.
