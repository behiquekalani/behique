# TikTok EN Batch 144
## Topic: How to write a sales page in 2 hours

## TikTok 1: The Sales Page Is a Conversation, Not a Document
**Hook (0-2s):**
"A sales page is not a brochure. It is a conversation with someone who is almost convinced."

**Script:**
Most bad sales pages fail because they are written for everyone.
They try to cover every possible objection. They add ten testimonials. They list every feature. They write paragraphs nobody reads.
A good sales page is written for one specific person at one specific moment in their decision process.
That person already has a problem. They are already looking for a solution. Your job is to confirm that you understand their problem better than they do, that your product solves it, and that buying is a lower risk than not buying.
Everything else is noise.
When you write from that frame, two hours is enough. You do not need more time. You need more clarity about who you are writing to.

## TikTok 2: The Five Parts Every Sales Page Needs
**Hook (0-2s):**
"Every sales page that converts has five parts. Most people only write two of them."

**Script:**
Part one: the problem. Name it specifically. Not "you feel stuck." Name the exact feeling, the exact situation, the exact failure they are trying to avoid.
Part two: the reason the problem persists. This is usually missing information, missing structure, or wrong approach. This is where you position your product as the missing piece.
Part three: the solution. What your product is and how it works. Be specific and brief.
Part four: proof. Who has used it, what happened, what changed.
Part five: the close. Who this is for, what it costs, what they get, and a clear call to action.
That is it. Five parts. Two hours if you know your customer. Less if you have talked to them directly.

"Save this structure. You will use it."

## TikTok 3: Start With the Customer's Voice, Not Yours
**Hook (0-2s):**
"The best sales page you will ever write starts with your customer's exact words."

**Script:**
Before you write a single word of copy, go find the voice of your customer.
Reddit threads. DMs from buyers. Reviews of competing products. Comments on your own posts.
Look for the specific language people use to describe their problem. The phrases. The metaphors. The frustrations.
Copy that language directly into your sales page.
When someone reads your page and thinks "how did they know exactly what I was thinking," they buy. Because it feels like the page was written for them specifically, not for a theoretical buyer you invented.
The two hours are not for writing. One hour is for research. One hour is for assembly. The customer has already written most of the page. Your job is to find it.

## TikTok 4: The One Sentence That Decides Everything
**Hook (0-2s):**
"There is one sentence on every sales page that either closes the sale or loses it."

**Script:**
It is not the headline. It is not the testimonials. It is the sentence where you name the core transformation.
Before this product: what does their life or business look like?
After this product: what does it look like instead?
That before-after gap is the core of every sales page. If you can state it in one clear sentence, you have the backbone of your entire page.
Everything else supports that sentence. The problem section builds the "before." The proof section shows the "after" is real. The features explain how to get there.
Most people spend two hours writing around that sentence without ever writing it clearly.
Write that sentence first. The rest of the page is commentary.

**CTA:**
"What is the transformation your product creates? Write it in the comments."

## TikTok 5: The Two-Hour Timeline
**Hook (0-2s):**
"Here is exactly how to write a sales page in two hours without overthinking it."

**Script:**
First thirty minutes: research. Pull customer language, identify the core problem, list the three most common objections.
Minutes thirty to sixty: write the problem section and the reason it persists. This is the longest section. It should feel like you are inside their head.
Minutes sixty to ninety: write the solution section, the proof, and the close. Be direct and brief here.
Final thirty minutes: read it out loud. Cut everything that does not move the reader toward a decision. Add specificity wherever something feels generic.
That is two hours. Done.
The reason most people never finish a sales page is not time. It is that they keep rewriting the same section instead of moving forward.
Move forward. Fix it in the edit. A finished page that converts at 2% beats a perfect page that does not exist.
