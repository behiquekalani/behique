## Post 3 — The Most Common Mistake Beginners Make When Building Automations

**Hook:** The most common mistake beginners make when automating is trying to build everything at once.

**Slide 1:** The most common mistake beginners make: trying to automate everything at once.

**Slide 2:** They identify ten tasks to automate. They try to build one giant workflow that handles all of them. It breaks. They do not know where. They give up.

**Slide 3:** The right approach: one workflow per problem. Separate, modular, small. Lead capture is its own workflow. Invoice reminders is its own workflow. Each runs independently.

**Slide 4:** Small workflows are easy to debug. When something breaks, you know exactly where. Small workflows are easy to update. You change one without touching the others.

**Slide 5:** Start with the single most time-consuming task you do every week. Build that one first. Ship it. Then pick the next.

**Slide 6:** The guide is structured around this approach. One workflow at a time. Link in bio.

**Caption:**
Scope creep is what kills first automation projects.

You sit down to build a lead capture workflow and realize you could also add a CRM integration and a Slack notification and a Notion database entry and a follow-up sequence. The workflow grows. The complexity compounds. Something breaks and you have no idea which node caused it.

Experienced automation builders do not build monoliths. They build small, discrete workflows that do one thing well.

This approach is faster to build, easier to test, easier to maintain, and much easier to improve over time. When you want to change how the follow-up email is worded, you open that one workflow and change one node. Nothing else is affected.

The n8n for Solopreneurs guide is structured around this principle. You build one workflow per chapter. By the end you have five independent, working automations that cover the core operations of a one-person business. Link in bio.

## Post 4 — How to Choose Your First Automation (A Simple Framework)

**Hook:** Not sure what to automate first? Use this framework and you will have the answer in five minutes.

**Slide 1:** Not sure what to automate first? This framework gives you the answer in five minutes.

**Slide 2:** Step 1: List every task you do more than twice a week that does not require your judgment. Just your hands moving data.

**Slide 3:** Step 2: Score each task on two dimensions: how often it happens, and how much you hate doing it. Multiply the scores.

**Slide 4:** Step 3: The highest score is your first workflow. That is the one that will deliver the most relief the fastest.

**Slide 5:** For most solopreneurs this turns out to be lead follow-up, invoice reminders, or data entry. These are the ones that eat time without adding value.

**Slide 6:** Build the highest-scoring workflow first. Then move down the list. Guide linked in bio.

**Caption:**
Prioritization is the real skill in automation.

You can automate almost anything. But the question that matters is: what should you automate first?

The framework I use is simple. Frequency times friction equals priority. If a task happens ten times a week and you hate doing it, that is a 10x10 workflow waiting to be built. If it happens once a month and you do not mind it, put it at the bottom of the list.

This prevents a common mistake: building impressive automations that do not actually improve your week. Automating your morning Twitter post is satisfying. Automating your lead follow-up is transformational.

Focus on the high-frequency, high-friction tasks first. Build the workflow that will make you feel the difference on Monday. Then build the next.

The n8n for Solopreneurs guide uses this prioritization logic to sequence the five core workflow builds. You start with the highest-impact one. Link in bio.

**Slide 5:** The compound effect of automation is real. Each workflow you build makes you faster at building the next. Within a month you will have a system, not just a workflow.
