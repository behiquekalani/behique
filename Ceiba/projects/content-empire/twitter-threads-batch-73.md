## Thread 73-1: Your Expertise Is Already a Product

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
You have knowledge someone would pay for right now.

The problem is you think it's too obvious to sell.

It's not obvious to the person who doesn't have it yet.

**Tweet 2:**
The expertise paradox:

The more time you spend in a domain, the less valuable your knowledge feels to you.

Because you forget what it was like not to know it.

That gap between where you are and where a beginner is — that's your product.

**Tweet 3:**
Three types of expertise worth monetizing:

1. Hard skills (you can do something most people can't)
2. Soft skills (you can navigate something most people struggle with)
3. Lived experience (you've been through something and have the scar tissue to prove it)

All three are worth money.

**Tweet 4:**
The fastest path from expertise to income:

1. Identify what you know that others don't
2. Find people actively struggling with that thing
3. Package your knowledge in a form they can consume
4. Sell it before you perfect it

**Tweet 5:**
The minimum viable product for expertise monetization:

A PDF, a template, or a guide that answers one specific question.

Not a course. Not a membership. Not a software product.

One question. One answer. One price.

They price based on what they think the information is worth, not what the outcome is worth.

A $27 guide that helps someone make their first $1,000 online is worth $27.

A guide that helps someone avoid a $10,000 mistake is worth $297.

**Tweet 7:**
You don't need to be the best in the world at something.

You need to be further along than the person you're selling to.

That's the only expertise requirement.

One area of expertise can generate 5 different income streams.

Here's how to build all 5 without starting from scratch each time:

**Tweet 2:**

Effort to create: high (once)

Price: $500-$5,000+ per engagement
Effort: high per client

Stream 3: Consulting or coaching

Sell your thinking, not your doing.

Price: $150-$500/hour or retainer
Effort: medium (thinking, not executing)
Scales moderately with group formats

Stream 4: Content and audience

Write about your expertise publicly. Build an audience around it.

This is the flywheel that makes everything else easier.

Stream 5: Partnerships and affiliates

Recommend tools and resources in your niche. Get paid when people buy through your link.

Requires trust and audience first. But it's pure margin once it's running.

**Tweet 7:**
The sequencing that works:

Start with services (fastest cash).
Use the cash to create products (passive income).
Use the products to build audience (distribution).
Use the audience to scale everything else.

## Thread 73-3: How to Package What You Know

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Knowing something valuable is not enough.

You have to package it in a way someone will actually pay for.

Here's the packaging framework:

**Tweet 2:**
The four packaging formats, from easiest to most complex:

1. Template (a pre-built structure someone fills in)
2. Guide or PDF (your knowledge organized into steps)
3. Course (your knowledge taught in sequence)
4. Community (ongoing access to you and others like them)

Start at 1. Work up as you validate.

**Tweet 3:**
The most important packaging decision:

Outcome specificity.

"How to make money online" doesn't sell.
"How to write one cold email that books 3 discovery calls per week" does.

The more specific the outcome, the more the package sells.

**Tweet 4:**
The packaging mistake that kills sales:

Selling the journey instead of the destination.

People don't buy your process. They buy the result your process produces.

Lead with the result. Explain the process later.

**Tweet 5:**
The fastest packaging workflow:

1. Write down every step you take to achieve the outcome
2. Remove the steps that are obvious to your target buyer
3. Double down on the steps that are surprising or non-obvious
4. Format it so a first-timer can follow it without you present

That's your product.

**Tweet 6:**
Naming your package:

The name should communicate the outcome, not the method.

Not: "The Content Creation System"
Yes: "Write 30 Days of Content in One Afternoon"

Names that sell describe what the buyer gets, not what you built.

**Tweet 7:**
The first draft is never the product.

Put it in front of 3-5 real humans and watch them use it.

Wherever they get confused or stuck, that's where you improve it.

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
Save this thread. Come back to it when you're ready to package what you know.

Follow for more on building products from expertise.

## Thread 73-4: The Expert's Pricing Playbook

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Most experts undercharge by a factor of 10.

Not because they're unconfident. Because they're pricing wrong.

Here's how to price expertise correctly:

**Tweet 2:**
The only pricing framework that matters:

Price = (Value of the outcome) x (Probability the buyer believes you can deliver) x (Urgency they feel)

Get all three high and your price can be high.

Most experts only optimize for value, and ignore believability and urgency.

**Tweet 3:**
The price anchors that raise what people will pay:

- Social proof (results you've generated)
- Specificity (exact numbers, timeframes, outcomes)
- Comparison (what the problem costs them if unsolved)
- Scarcity (limited spots, closing date)

None of these require lowering your price. They raise the perceived value.

**Tweet 4:**
Why raising your price often increases sales:

Low prices signal low value.

If you charge $7 for something that solves a $10,000 problem, the buyer questions whether it actually works.

Price signals quality. Use it deliberately.

**Tweet 5:**
The three price points to test:

Entry: $27-$97 (info products, templates)
Mid: $197-$497 (courses, detailed systems)
High: $1,000+ (done-with-you, consulting, coaching)

Start at the entry point. Once you have testimonials, test mid. Once you have case studies, test high.

**Tweet 6:**
The confidence problem:

Most experts don't raise prices because they're afraid no one will pay.

The solution is not to convince yourself you're worth it.

The solution is to collect testimonials until the market tells you what you're worth.

**Tweet 7:**
The rule: charge what makes you slightly uncomfortable.

If the price feels easy to say out loud, it's probably too low.

Price discomfort is a signal, not a stop sign.

## Thread 73-5: From First Sale to Recurring Revenue

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Your first sale proves someone wants what you're selling.

Your first 10 sales prove it wasn't an accident.

Here's how you get from one to recurring:

**Tweet 2:**
The first sale is about validation, not optimization.

Don't tweak the landing page, the pricing, or the offer before you've sold to 10 people.

Sell it. Learn from real buyers. Then optimize.

**Tweet 3:**
The path to recurring revenue from a one-time product:

1. Sell the product
2. Deliver more value than expected
3. Offer an upgrade or next step
4. Build a reason for them to come back

The second sale is easier than the first. Most people never ask for it.

**Tweet 4:**
The recurring revenue models that work for solopreneurs:

- Newsletter with a paid tier
- Membership community (monthly recurring)
- Retainer consulting (monthly)
- Annual product licenses

Recurring revenue changes how you think about your business. Build toward it.

**Tweet 5:**
The mistake that kills recurring revenue:

Treating every customer as a one-time transaction.

Your best customers are the ones you've already sold to. Build something to sell them next.

**Tweet 6:**
The simple recurring revenue math:

100 customers at $10/month = $1,000/month recurring
100 customers at $50/month = $5,000/month recurring
100 customers at $100/month = $10,000/month recurring

You don't need 10,000 customers. You need 100 good ones who stay.

**Tweet 7:**
The fastest way to build recurring revenue:

Find 10 people who already bought from you. Ask them what they wish still existed after they bought. Build that.

Your best product ideas come from customers you've already won.
