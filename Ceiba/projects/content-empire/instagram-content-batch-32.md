## POST 1 — CAROUSEL
**Label:** Carousel 01
**Post Time:** Tuesday 9:00 AM
**Topic:** What prediction markets are

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
Polls are opinions.
Prediction markets are money on the line.

Here is why that difference matters for how you understand the world.

---

**Slide 2:**
A poll asks: "What do you think will happen?"
A prediction market asks: "How confident are you that you will put value behind that belief?"

When there is something at stake, people research more carefully.
They update their views more honestly.

---

**Slide 3:**
The result is that prediction markets tend to be more accurate than polls, expert panels, or pundits.

Not because participants are smarter.
Because bad predictions cost them.

Incentive alignment forces better thinking.

---

**Slide 4:**
The price of a contract reflects collective belief about probability.
If a contract is trading at 65%, that means participants collectively put 65% probability on that outcome occurring.

It is a live snapshot of distributed thinking from people with skin in the game.

---

**Slide 5:**
This matters even if you never participate in a prediction market.

Reading market prices on current events gives you a fast, incentive-aligned signal.
Better than most news headlines. Often faster than official analysis.

---

**Slide 6:**
The skill prediction markets teach is probability calibration.
The ability to say: "I think this is 70% likely" and be right about that kind of thing over time.

That skill transfers everywhere. Business decisions. Career bets. Project planning.

---

**Slide 7:**
Most people think in terms of "yes" or "no."
Prediction markets train you to think in probabilities.

That is a different, more accurate way to process uncertainty.

## POST 2 — CAROUSEL
**Label:** Carousel 02
**Post Time:** Thursday 7:00 AM
**Topic:** Research framework for evaluating uncertain events

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
Most people research by Googling and reading the first 3 results.

Here is the framework that actually produces accurate conclusions.

---

**Slide 2:**
Step 1: Establish the base rate.
Before forming any opinion, ask: "Historically, how often does this type of event occur?"

Most errors in prediction come from ignoring base rates.
Ground yourself in what usually happens before analyzing what might be different this time.

---

**Slide 3:**
Step 2: Identify the key variable.
Most uncertain situations have one or two factors that actually determine the outcome.

Find those. Do not diffuse your research energy across a dozen factors equally.
80% of predictive power often comes from 20% of variables.

---

**Slide 4:**
Step 3: Find primary sources.
News articles are secondary at best. Often tertiary.

Go to original reports, official statements, data releases.
Verify claims before building a conclusion on top of them.

---

**Slide 5:**
Step 4: Identify incentives.
Every source has an angle. Institutions, journalists, analysts.

Ask: what does this person or organization benefit from saying this?
That does not make them wrong. It just tells you where to apply extra scrutiny.

---

**Slide 6:**
Step 5: Assign a probability, not a verdict.
Do not conclude "X will happen." Conclude "I think X is 60% likely based on what I found."

Then track if that calibration holds over time.
That is how you improve.

---

**Slide 7:**
Step 6: Set a revision trigger.
Identify in advance what new information would change your view and by how much.

This prevents you from either anchoring too hard or updating too rashly.

---

**Slide 8:**
Most people skip steps 1, 5, 6, and 7.
That is why their analysis does not improve over time.

The framework is not magic. It is just structured.

## POST 3 — CAROUSEL
**Label:** Carousel 03
**Post Time:** Saturday 10:00 AM
**Topic:** The information edge concept

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
Everyone has access to the same news.

The information edge is not about having more information.
It is about processing it better than others.

---

**Slide 2:**
Most people consume information passively.
They read a headline. Form an opinion. Move on.

An information edge comes from reading the same headline and asking a different set of questions.

---

**Slide 3:**
The questions most people skip:
- What is the base rate for this?
- Who benefits from this framing?
- What would have to be true for this to be wrong?
- What does the market already know vs. what is actually new here?

---

**Slide 4:**
Timing is part of the edge.
Information has more value before it is priced in.

Once something is widely known and discussed, the signal it contains has already been absorbed.
Speed of interpretation matters as much as accuracy.

---

**Slide 5:**
Synthesis is part of the edge.
Connecting information from different domains is harder than reading within one domain.

Most people specialize their reading too narrowly.
The unusual connections are often where the insight lives.

---

**Slide 6:**
Track record is part of the edge.
If you do not score your predictions, you do not know if you are good at this.

Keep a log. Note your confidence level. Check back.
Calibration is a skill you build, not a talent you have.

---

**Slide 7:**
The information edge is a learnable skill.
Better questions. Faster source evaluation. Probability calibration over time.

None of this requires special access.
It requires consistent practice and honest self-assessment.

**Post Time:** Monday 6:00 PM
**Topic:** Base rate thinking introduction

Most people analyze events like each one is completely unique.

They are not.

Base rate thinking says: before you form an opinion about what will happen, ask how often this type of situation has resolved in a particular way historically.

Election outcomes. Startup survival rates. Economic forecasts. All of these have historical base rates.

If you ignore those rates, you are essentially starting your analysis from zero every time.

The researchers who are consistently accurate are not smarter than everyone else.
They check the base rate first. Then they ask what makes this case different.

Most of the time, it is not as different as it seems.

That single habit will improve your judgment more than almost anything else you could add to your thinking toolkit.

What decisions have you made recently without checking the base rate?

## POST 5 — REEL CAPTION
**Label:** Reel 02
**Post Time:** Wednesday 12:00 PM
**Topic:** How to think about probabilities

We are trained to think in binaries.

It will happen or it will not.
I am right or I am wrong.

But most of reality exists in probabilities.

"I think this is 70% likely" is a more accurate description of the world than "I think this will happen."

Because it forces you to acknowledge uncertainty. It keeps you open to being wrong without abandoning your position entirely. And it gives you a way to track your accuracy over time.

When I started assigning probabilities to my beliefs instead of treating them as certainties or doubts, my decisions got better.

Not because I was suddenly smarter.
Because I stopped treating uncertain things as certain.

Start small. Pick one thing you have an opinion about this week. Assign a probability. Write it down. Check back when the outcome is known.

That is the whole practice. It compounds over months.

What is something you currently believe will happen? What probability would you assign it, honestly?

## POST 6 — REEL CAPTION
**Label:** Reel 03
**Post Time:** Friday 8:00 AM
**Topic:** Why tracking prediction accuracy changes how you think

I started keeping a log of my predictions about a year ago.

Not for financial reasons. Just to see if my thinking was actually as good as I assumed it was.

The result was uncomfortable.

I was confidently wrong about things I thought were nearly certain.
And I was hesitant about things that turned out to be close to inevitable.

My calibration was off in predictable ways.
I underestimated how often established trends continue.
I overestimated how much a single event would change things.

Those are patterns. And because I tracked them, I could correct for them.

If you do not track your predictions, you cannot improve your judgment.
You just remember the times you were right and forget the times you were not.

That is not learning. That is confirmation bias with a better story.

Pick 5 things you have opinions about right now.
Write them down with a confidence percentage.
Revisit in 90 days.

That is how you start building actual judgment.

## POST 7 — TEXT POST
**Label:** Text 01
**Post Time:** Tuesday 12:00 PM
**Topic:** Information evaluation as a skill

We are in an era of information abundance.

The bottleneck is no longer finding information.
It is evaluating it accurately.

This is a skill. It can be practiced. Most people never deliberately practice it.

They read what confirms their existing views.
They trust sources that feel authoritative without checking why.
They form strong opinions fast and defend them instead of refining them.

Information evaluation is the opposite of all of that.

It means reading sources you disagree with and finding what they get right.
It means tracing a claim back to its origin before repeating it.
It means assigning confidence levels to your beliefs and updating them when evidence changes.

The people who consistently make better decisions are not smarter.
They are more disciplined about how they intake and process information.

They know that the map is not the territory.
That a compelling narrative is not the same as accurate analysis.
That the first explanation you find is rarely the most complete one.

This is learnable. It just requires choosing to be a more careful thinker.

Most people do not make that choice deliberately.
The ones who do have a significant advantage in almost every domain.

## POST 8 — TEXT POST
**Label:** Text 02
**Post Time:** Thursday 3:00 PM
**Topic:** Why uncertainty is a feature, not a bug

People avoid saying "I don't know" because it sounds weak.

But "I don't know" is often the most accurate thing you can say.
And it is always more honest than pretending certainty you do not have.

The alternative is false confidence.
Stating opinions as facts. Treating unlikely outcomes as impossible. Acting on noise as if it were signal.

Comfortable with uncertainty means:
- You can hold a view without requiring it to be proven immediately
- You can update when new information arrives without feeling like you "lost"
- You can distinguish between low-probability and zero-probability
- You can act despite imperfect information because you have correctly priced the uncertainty

This is not wishy-washy thinking.
It is more rigorous than the alternative.

The loudest voices on any given topic are often the least calibrated.
Confidence in delivery is not the same as accuracy in content.

Learning to sit with uncertainty without collapsing into false certainty is one of the most useful cognitive habits you can build.

It applies to business, to relationships, to reading markets, to navigating any complex system.
