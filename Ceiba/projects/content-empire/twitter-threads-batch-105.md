# Twitter Threads — Batch 105
## Topic: The 5 Business Models Available to a Creator (And How to Stack Them)

---

### Thread 1: There are only 5 business models available to a creator. Most people use 1 badly.

Most creators are leaving 4 revenue streams untouched.

Here they are, from lowest to highest leverage:

1/ Services. You sell your time and skills directly. Highest revenue per sale, lowest leverage. Trades time for money. The correct entry point for most creators because it requires no upfront product creation and validates what people will actually pay for.

2/ Digital products. You sell a PDF, a course, a template, a guide. Low marginal cost, unlimited scalability. The revenue ceiling is determined by audience size and offer quality, not your available hours.

3/ Affiliate revenue. You recommend products you use and take a cut. Low effort once the content is created. Compounds over time as content gets discovered. Works best when you only recommend what you actually use.

4/ Community or membership. Recurring revenue. The best recurring revenue model available to creators who have an engaged, niche audience. Requires active curation but generates predictable monthly income.

5/ Licensing and IP. You sell the right to use your systems, frameworks, or content. The highest leverage model. Requires a body of work substantial enough that others want access to the framework.

Most creators are stuck at Level 1. The jump to $10K/month usually involves stacking 3 or 4 of these.

---

### Thread 2: The creator service business. How to start, who it is for, when to exit it.

Every creator should start with services.

Here is the logic:

1/ Services validate everything. Before you spend 3 months writing a digital product, sell the service version of it. If people pay for the service, the product has a market. If they do not, you need to change the offer.

2/ Services generate content. Every client engagement is a case study, a process post, a before and after, a specific lesson. The creator who is doing client work has more real material to write about than the one who is only theorizing.

3/ Services build social proof fast. Your first 3 clients are worth more as testimonials and case studies than any follower count. Social proof accelerates every other revenue model.

4/ When to exit services: when you are consistently turning down work because you are at capacity. That is the signal that demand is high enough to productize. Not before.

5/ The transition: do not drop services cold. Raise prices. Fewer clients, higher revenue per client. Use the freed time to build the product. Services fund the product creation. Products eventually replace the service capacity.

Services first. Everything else builds on the foundation.

### Thread 3: Digital products for creators. The 3 price points that work together.

One product is not a business.

Three price points is a business.

1/ The entry product ($9-$49). This is not about revenue. It is about trust calibration. A buyer who spends $19 on your PDF has converted from reader to customer. That conversion changes the relationship. They are now more likely to buy the next product.

2/ The core product ($49-$199). This is where most of your revenue comes from. It is substantive enough to justify the price and specific enough to solve a real problem your audience has. This product should be the thing you are most qualified to teach.

3/ The premium product ($299-$999). Not everyone will buy this. That is fine. It exists for the top 5-10% of your buyers who want the most comprehensive version of what you offer. High margin, lower volume, high value delivered.

The funnel: entry product builds buyers, core product generates revenue, premium product serves your best customers and funds the premium portion of your business.

Most creators launch one product at one price point and wonder why growth is slow. Three price points is the answer.

### Thread 4: How to stack revenue models without losing focus. The sequencing matters.

Most creators try to build everything at once.

That is how you build nothing well.

Here is the correct sequence:

Stage 1: Services only. Build the client base, generate case studies, validate your expertise, fund the business. No products yet. No community yet. Just services and content that supports the services.

Stage 2: Add one digital product. Take your most common client deliverable and productize it. The thing you explain to every client, the framework you use on every project, the template you rebuilt from scratch 10 times. Make it once, sell it repeatedly.

Stage 3: Add affiliate revenue. You are already recommending tools and resources in your content. Make the recommendations systematic. Add affiliate links to the tools you actually use. This is passive revenue that compounds as your content catalog grows.

Stage 4: Build the community or membership once you have an engaged audience. Community only works when people trust each other and trust you. You cannot manufacture that in month 2. It has to be earned.

Stage 5: License your frameworks. This happens naturally for creators who produce enough public intellectual property. Other people want to use what you built. That is a business.

One stage at a time. The creator trying to do all five at once usually masters none.

### Thread 5: The creator who stacks all 5 revenue models. What that business actually looks like.

This is what $10K/month looks like for a solopreneur creator.

Not theoretical. This is the actual income composition:

Services: $2,000-$4,000/month. 2-3 clients paying for direct access to your expertise. High hourly value, limited capacity. This is the floor.

Digital products: $2,000-$4,000/month. A catalog of 3-5 products at different price points. Generates revenue every day without additional time investment once the products exist and the content is driving traffic.

Affiliate revenue: $500-$1,500/month. Compounds over time as old content continues to rank and get discovered. Works best for tools with recurring commissions.

Community or membership: $1,000-$2,000/month. 50-100 members at $20-$40/month. Small, focused, sustainable. Not trying to build the biggest community. Building the most useful one.

The math: $2,500 + $3,000 + $1,000 + $1,500 = $8,000/month. Add one licensing deal or a premium product launch and you are past $10K.

The key insight: no single stream has to be huge. The stacking is what makes it work. Each stream has a ceiling. The combination of streams does not.

Build the first one well. Add the second when the first is stable. This is the path.
