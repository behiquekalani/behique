# Twitter/X Batch 167 — How to Write Content That Attracts Buyers, Not Just Readers

---

**1. Punchy single**

Readers follow you.
Buyers have a problem they need solved.

If your content never names the problem, you will accumulate followers who never spend a dollar.

---

**2. Punchy single**

The difference between content that gets likes and content that gets sales:

Likes: "this is interesting."
Sales: "this is exactly my situation."

Specificity is the conversion variable.

---

**3. Punchy single**

Most content teaches.
Buyer-attracting content shows the gap between where someone is and where they want to be.

Then you become the bridge.

---

**4. 3-tweet thread**

Why your audience doesn't buy even though they love your content:

/1

Educational content creates authority. But authority alone doesn't create urgency. People need a reason to act now. If your content only teaches, you're building a library. Libraries don't have checkout lines.

/2

Buyer-attracting content does something different. It names the cost of inaction. Not in a manipulative way. In a real way. "If you keep doing X, Y keeps happening." That's not fear. That's clarity.

/3

The fix: after every piece of educational content, write one piece that frames the problem your product solves. Not a pitch. Just the problem, stated cleanly. Buyers will recognize themselves. That recognition is what moves money.

---

**5. Punchy single**

The best sales copy doesn't feel like sales copy.

It reads like someone finally said the thing your audience had been thinking but couldn't articulate.

---

**6. 6-tweet thread**

The framework I use to turn content from "interesting" to "I need this":

/1

Every piece of content should do one of three things: show the destination, name the obstacle, or describe the transformation. Most people only do the first one.

/2

Showing the destination is easy. "Here's what's possible." Everyone does this. It creates inspiration but no tension. Without tension, there's no reason to buy.

/3

Naming the obstacle is where buyers live. "Here's the specific thing that's keeping you stuck." When you describe the obstacle better than your reader can describe it themselves, they assume you have the solution.

/4

The transformation is the bridge. "Here's what shifts when the obstacle is gone." This is not a feature list. It's a before-and-after that the reader can see themselves in.

/5

Most content only does destination. Add obstacle and transformation to the same piece and you've written something that moves people from interested to "I need to read everything this person makes."

/6

Then, when you offer something, there's no friction. They already believe you understand their situation. Belief precedes buying. Build belief in the content before you ever mention the product.

---

**7. Punchy single**

Nobody buys information.
They buy the belief that this specific information will solve their specific problem.

Your content has to build that belief first.

---

**8. 3-tweet thread**

The content type that converts best, and why:

/1

Story-based content outperforms instructional content for one reason: it puts the reader inside a situation. When they see themselves in the story, they feel the problem. Felt problems get solved. Understood problems get bookmarked.

/2

The format: start with the before-state (relatable problem). Show the inflection point (what changed). End with the after-state (the result). The product lives at the inflection point. The story does the selling.

/3

This is not manipulation. It's empathy structured as content. The reader doesn't feel sold to. They feel understood. Understanding is the most underrated conversion mechanism in content marketing.

---

**9. Punchy single**

Before you write anything, answer this: what does my ideal buyer search for at 11pm when nobody's watching?

Write content that answers that exact search. That's where the money is.

---

**10. Punchy single**

Your content is competing with everyone who writes about the same topic.

The one that wins says the true thing everyone was afraid to say out loud.
Say it clearly. Say it first.
