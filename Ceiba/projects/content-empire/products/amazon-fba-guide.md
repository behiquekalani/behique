## Chapter 1: Is Amazon FBA Right For You?

Most people start Amazon FBA because they saw someone on YouTube holding a check. That is a terrible reason to start a business.

Here is what FBA actually is: you find a product, buy it in bulk (usually from a manufacturer overseas), ship it to Amazon's warehouse, and Amazon handles storage, shipping, and customer service. You handle everything else. Product selection. Supplier relationships. Listing creation. Advertising. Pricing. Returns. Brand building.

That "everything else" is where people quit.

### The real numbers

Your first product will cost between $2,000 and $5,000 to launch properly. That includes inventory, shipping, product photography, and initial advertising budget. Some people launch for less. Most of those people run out of stock in two weeks or can't afford to advertise long enough to gain traction.

Monthly costs once you're running: Amazon's professional seller fee ($39.99/month), FBA fees per unit (varies by size and weight, but figure $3-6 for most standard items), advertising spend (plan for $15-30/day minimum during launch), and software tools ($50-100/month for keyword research and tracking).

Your realistic timeline: 2-4 months from first research to first sale. 6-12 months to consistent profitability. Anyone telling you faster is selling you something.

### Who this works for

FBA rewards patience and data-driven decisions. If you need money next week, get a job. If you can invest $3,000-5,000 and not touch it for 6 months, keep reading.

You need: basic spreadsheet skills, the ability to read data without emotional attachment, 5-10 hours per week (more during launch), and the discipline to follow a research process instead of going with your gut on product selection.

### Who should stop here

If you cannot afford to lose your starting capital, this is not the right move right now. FBA is a real business with real risk. Products fail. Competitors appear. Amazon changes rules. That is normal. But you need enough financial cushion that a failed first product does not wreck you.

Also, if you want passive income with zero effort after setup, FBA is not that. It requires ongoing optimization, inventory management, and advertising adjustments. The "passive" part is that Amazon ships the boxes. Everything else is active.

> **Common Mistakes in This Phase:**
> - Starting because of hype instead of genuine interest in building a product business
> - Underestimating startup costs by 50% or more
> - Expecting profit in month one (plan for month 6)
> - Skipping the research phase to "just get started"
> - Choosing FBA when a simpler model (retail arbitrage, online arbitrage) would build skills first

**Chapter 1 Checklist:**
- [ ] Calculate your available startup capital (be honest)
- [ ] Block 5-10 hours/week in your calendar for the next 6 months
- [ ] Set a realistic timeline: first sale in 3 months, break-even in 6
- [ ] Decide if you can absorb a total loss on your first product
- [ ] Read chapters 2-4 before spending a single dollar

## Chapter 2: Product Research That Actually Works

Bad product research kills more FBA businesses than bad suppliers, bad listings, or bad ads combined. Most new sellers pick a product they personally like, find it on Alibaba, and order 500 units. That is not research. That is gambling.

Good research is boring. It is spreadsheets and data and saying "no" to 50 products before saying "yes" to one.

### The criteria that matter

**Monthly revenue range:** Look for products generating $5,000-$25,000/month in total market revenue across the top 10 listings. Below $5,000, the market is too small. Above $25,000 with fewer than 10 competitors, it might work, but above that with established brands, you are bringing a water gun to a gunfight.

**Review count on page one:** If the top 10 results all have 1,000+ reviews, move on. You want to see at least 3-4 listings on page one with fewer than 200 reviews. That means new sellers can still compete.

**Price range:** Target $18-$50 for your first product. Below $18, your margins get eaten by FBA fees. Above $50, customers expect perfection and your ad costs climb because fewer people impulse-buy at that price.

**Size and weight:** Small and light wins for beginners. Every extra pound adds shipping cost from your supplier and FBA fees from Amazon. Stay under 2 pounds and smaller than a shoebox for your first product.

**Seasonality:** Check if your product sells year-round or just during holidays. A product that does 80% of its revenue in Q4 will destroy your cash flow the other 9 months. Use Google Trends to verify demand consistency.

### Research tools worth paying for

Helium 10 and Jungle Scout are the two main platforms. Both do roughly the same thing: show you estimated monthly sales, revenue, review counts, and keyword data for any Amazon listing. Pick one. The free tiers are limited but enough to validate your first 5-10 product ideas.

Amazon's own search bar is free and underrated. Type a keyword and look at the autocomplete suggestions. Those suggestions are what real people actually search for. That is market demand data, free.

### The validation process

Once you find a product that passes the criteria above, validate it with three more checks:

**Can you differentiate?** Read every 1-star and 2-star review on the top 5 competing listings. Customers tell you exactly what is wrong with existing products. If you can fix 2-3 of those complaints, you have a real opportunity. If every competitor already has great reviews and no obvious improvement areas, find a different product.

**Can you source it profitably?** Quick math: your target selling price minus Amazon's fees (roughly 35-40% of selling price for FBA) minus your product cost minus shipping should leave you at least 25-30% profit margin. If the math does not work before you even start, no amount of great marketing will fix it.

**Is the category restricted?** Some categories require Amazon approval to sell in (grocery, beauty, health supplements, etc.). Check this before you fall in love with a product. Getting ungated in restricted categories is possible but adds weeks or months to your timeline.

### Using competitor data

Look at your competitors' listing quality. Bad photos, weak bullet points, no A+ content, poor keyword optimization. These are gaps you can exploit. If every competitor has professional listings with A+ content and video, you are entering a mature market that will be harder to crack.

Check the Best Seller Rank (BSR) history of top products. A product that maintains a steady BSR under 5,000 in its main category has consistent demand. Wild BSR swings suggest inconsistent sales or seasonal products.

> **Common Mistakes in This Phase:**
> - Choosing a product because you personally use it (your preference is not market data)
> - Ignoring the review barrier on page one
> - Skipping the margin calculation until after ordering inventory
> - Only looking at one product research tool instead of cross-referencing
> - Falling for "low competition" keywords that also have low demand

**Chapter 2 Checklist:**
- [ ] Evaluate at least 20 product ideas against the criteria before selecting one
- [ ] Verify monthly revenue range ($5K-$25K) using a research tool
- [ ] Read 50+ negative reviews on competitor products to find differentiation angles
- [ ] Run the profit margin calculation (target 25-30%+ net)
- [ ] Check Google Trends for year-round demand stability

## Chapter 3: Supplier Sourcing

Finding a supplier is not hard. Finding a good supplier who delivers consistent quality, communicates clearly, and does not disappear after your first order. That is the actual challenge.

### Where to source

**Alibaba** is the starting point for most FBA sellers. It connects you with manufacturers primarily in China, though you will find suppliers from India, Vietnam, Pakistan, and other countries too. Filter for "Trade Assurance" suppliers and those with Gold Supplier status. These are not guarantees of quality, but they indicate the supplier has invested in their business profile and has dispute resolution coverage.

**Domestic suppliers** (US, depending on your country) cost more per unit but offer faster shipping, easier communication, and simpler quality control. For your first product, this can be worth the extra cost. Search industry-specific directories and trade shows rather than general platforms. Google "[your product category] manufacturer USA" and go past page one.

**Sourcing agents** sit between you and factories. They handle factory visits, quality inspections, and shipping logistics. Worth considering after your first product when you are scaling and need someone on the ground. For product one, direct communication with the factory teaches you more.

### The outreach process

Contact at least 5-10 suppliers for the same product. Your initial message should include: the product you want (with reference images or Amazon listing links of similar products), your estimated order quantity, and any customization requirements. Keep it short and professional.

Ask every supplier the same questions so you can compare apples to apples: unit price at different quantities (100, 500, 1000 units), sample cost and shipping, production lead time, minimum order quantity (MOQ), and whether they can customize packaging with your branding.

### Negotiation that works

Do not negotiate on price alone. The cheapest supplier is rarely the best supplier. Instead, negotiate the full package: price, MOQ, payment terms, shipping method, and quality control processes.

Your best negotiation tool is competition. When a supplier knows you are talking to 5 others, their pricing and terms get more flexible. You do not need to bluff. Just mention that you are evaluating multiple options and will make a decision by a specific date.

Payment terms matter more than most new sellers realize. Standard is 30% deposit, 70% before shipping. Try to negotiate 30/70 with the 70% due after inspection. Use Trade Assurance on Alibaba or PayPal for buyer protection. Never send full payment upfront. Never wire money directly to a personal bank account.

### Samples are mandatory

Order samples from your top 3 suppliers before placing a bulk order. Yes, it costs $50-150 per sample including shipping. That is cheap insurance against receiving 500 units of garbage.

When evaluating samples, check: material quality, dimensions vs. what was promised, packaging quality, print/branding accuracy, and overall build. Compare all 3 side by side. The differences will be obvious.

If the sample is not what you want, do not assume the bulk order will be better. It will not. Either get the supplier to fix the issues in a second sample or move to a different supplier.

### Ordering your first batch

For your first order, start with the minimum viable quantity. Usually 200-500 units depending on the product and MOQ. You want enough inventory to last 30-60 days based on your projected daily sales. Running out of stock kills your ranking momentum. Ordering too much ties up capital in a product that might not sell.

Get a third-party inspection before the shipment leaves the factory. Companies like QIMA or V-Trust charge $200-350 per inspection. They check a random sample of your units against your specifications. This catches problems before they become your problem.

> **Common Mistakes in This Phase:**
> - Contacting only one supplier and accepting their first price
> - Skipping samples to "save time"
> - Paying full amount upfront with no buyer protection
> - Ordering 1,000+ units on a first product
> - Not getting a pre-shipment inspection

**Chapter 3 Checklist:**
- [ ] Contact 5-10 suppliers with identical product requirements
- [ ] Order samples from your top 3 suppliers
- [ ] Negotiate payment terms with buyer protection (Trade Assurance or PayPal)
- [ ] Calculate total landed cost (product + shipping + duties + inspection)
- [ ] Arrange a third-party inspection before bulk shipment

## Chapter 4: Setting Up Your Amazon Business

The legal and administrative setup is not exciting. But skipping it creates problems that are expensive and stressful to fix later.

### Business structure

Register a business entity before opening your Amazon seller account. An LLC is the standard choice for most FBA sellers in the US. It separates your personal assets from your business liabilities. Cost varies by state, typically $50-500 for the filing fee. You can do this yourself through your state's Secretary of State website. You do not need a lawyer for a basic LLC.

Get an EIN (Employer Identification Number) from the IRS. Free. Takes 5 minutes online. You need this for your Amazon account, business bank account, and tax filing.

If you are selling from outside the US, you can still open a US Amazon account. You will need an EIN (which international sellers can get by mail or through an agent), a valid credit card, and identity verification documents. The process takes longer but it works.

### Amazon seller account

Choose the Professional plan ($39.99/month). The Individual plan charges $0.99 per sale and lacks access to advertising, which you will absolutely need. Professional plan pays for itself once you sell more than 40 units per month.

During setup, Amazon will ask for: business name, address, bank account details, credit card, tax information (W-9 for US sellers or W-8BEN for international), and phone verification. Have all of this ready before you start. The verification process can take 24-72 hours.

### Brand Registry

Amazon Brand Registry gives you access to A+ Content, Sponsored Brand ads, brand analytics, and protection against counterfeit listings. You need a registered trademark to enroll. Filing a trademark costs $250-350 through the USPTO if you do it yourself.

The trademark process takes 8-12 months for full approval, but Amazon now accepts pending trademarks from the IP Accelerator program, which can get you into Brand Registry in weeks instead of months. The tradeoff: IP Accelerator uses Amazon's vetted law firms, which charge $600-2,000 for the trademark filing.

If your budget is tight, launch without Brand Registry and file the trademark yourself through the USPTO. You can add A+ Content and brand protections later once approved.

### Business bank account and accounting

Open a separate business bank account. Never mix personal and business finances. This makes tax filing straightforward and gives you a clear picture of actual business profitability.

Track every expense from day one. Product costs, shipping, Amazon fees, software subscriptions, advertising spend, samples, photography. Use a spreadsheet or QuickBooks. Your accountant will thank you at tax time.

Sales tax varies by state. Amazon collects and remits sales tax in most states through their Marketplace Facilitator program, but you may still need to register for sales tax permits in certain states. Talk to an accountant who understands ecommerce. This is one area where professional advice pays for itself.

> **Common Mistakes in This Phase:**
> - Selling under a personal account instead of a business entity
> - Mixing personal and business bank accounts
> - Ignoring sales tax obligations until they become a problem
> - Waiting too long to file for a trademark
> - Choosing the Individual seller plan to save $39.99/month

**Chapter 4 Checklist:**
- [ ] Register an LLC (or equivalent business entity)
- [ ] Get an EIN from the IRS
- [ ] Open a Professional Amazon seller account
- [ ] Open a dedicated business bank account
- [ ] File for a trademark (USPTO direct or IP Accelerator)

## Chapter 5: Creating Listings That Convert

Your listing is your salesperson. It works 24/7. If it is bad, you are paying for advertising that sends people to a page that does not convince them to buy. That is burning money.

### Title optimization

Your title has two jobs: tell Amazon's algorithm what your product is (keywords), and tell the customer why they should click (benefit).

Structure: Brand Name + Primary Keyword + Key Feature + Secondary Keyword + Size/Quantity. Keep it under 200 characters. Front-load your most important keyword because Amazon gives more weight to words at the beginning of the title.

Example of a bad title: "Amazing Kitchen Gadget Tool." Example of a better title: "Stainless Steel Garlic Press, Heavy Duty, Easy Squeeze, Dishwasher Safe, Professional Grade Mincer."

The better title includes the material, the product name, the key benefit, and additional keywords that customers actually search for.

### Bullet points that sell

You get 5 bullet points. Each one should open with a capitalized benefit statement, then explain the feature that delivers that benefit.

Pattern: BENEFIT IN CAPS, then 1-2 sentences explaining it. Focus on what the customer gets, not what the product has. "NEVER STRUGGLE WITH GARLIC AGAIN" beats "Made with zinc alloy handles."

Read the negative reviews on your competitors' listings. Turn their weaknesses into your bullet points. If customers complain that competing products break after a month, your bullet point is about durability and your warranty.

### Product images

This is where most sellers underinvest. Your main image must have a pure white background (Amazon requirement) and show the product clearly at high resolution. Blurry phone photos kill conversions instantly.

Plan for 7-9 images: Main product image on white background, lifestyle image showing the product in use, infographic highlighting key features and dimensions, comparison image (your product vs. "other" generic products), packaging shot, close-up of quality details, and a lifestyle image showing the result of using your product.

Professional product photography costs $150-500 for a full set. This is one of the highest-ROI investments you will make. Good images increase click-through rate and conversion rate, which directly increases sales and lowers your advertising costs.

If budget is extremely tight, you can shoot decent photos yourself with a smartphone, a $20 lightbox from Amazon, and free photo editing tools. Watch tutorials on product photography basics. Editing matters as much as the shoot itself.

### A+ Content (Brand Registry required)

A+ Content replaces your basic product description with rich media: images, comparison charts, brand story sections, and formatted text. Amazon reports that A+ Content increases conversions by 3-10%.

Focus your A+ Content on: telling your brand story (briefly), showing product benefits through images rather than text, including a comparison chart against your other products (not competitors, Amazon does not allow that), and addressing common questions or concerns.

Keep it visual. Most customers scroll through A+ Content quickly. Large images with minimal text overlay perform better than walls of copy.

### Backend keywords

Seller Central has a field for backend keywords that customers never see but Amazon's algorithm indexes. Use this for: misspellings of your product name, Spanish translations of your keywords, synonyms you did not fit in the title, and related terms.

Do not repeat keywords already in your title or bullets. Amazon's algorithm is smart enough to index them once. Use the backend for additional terms only. 250 bytes maximum.

> **Common Mistakes in This Phase:**
> - Writing titles for humans only (no keywords) or keywords only (unreadable)
> - Using low-quality images to "save money"
> - Writing bullet points about features instead of benefits
> - Leaving A+ Content blank after getting Brand Registry
> - Stuffing backend keywords with repeated terms

**Chapter 5 Checklist:**
- [ ] Research top 10 keywords for your product using Helium 10 or similar tool
- [ ] Write a title under 200 characters with primary keyword front-loaded
- [ ] Write 5 bullet points using the BENEFIT + feature explanation format
- [ ] Get 7-9 professional product images (or quality DIY alternatives)
- [ ] Fill backend keywords with terms not already in your listing

## Chapter 6: Amazon PPC Advertising

Amazon PPC (Pay Per Click) is how you get your new product in front of buyers when you have zero organic ranking. It is not optional. Without PPC, your listing sits on page 47 where nobody will ever find it.

### How Amazon PPC works

You bid on keywords. When a customer searches that keyword and clicks your ad, you pay your bid amount (or less). If they buy, great. If they do not buy, you still pay for the click. Your goal is to get enough sales from advertising to boost your organic ranking, at which point you reduce ad spend and profit from free organic traffic.

The key metric is ACoS (Advertising Cost of Sale). Formula: ad spend divided by ad revenue, times 100. If you spend $10 on ads and make $50 in ad-attributed sales, your ACoS is 20%. Whether 20% is good depends on your profit margin. If your margin before ad spend is 35%, a 20% ACoS means you are still profitable on every ad-driven sale.

### Campaign types you need

**Automatic campaigns:** Amazon chooses which keywords to show your ad for, based on your listing content. Start here. It is the fastest way to discover which search terms actually convert for your product. Budget: $15-25/day during launch.

**Exact match campaigns:** You choose the specific keyword. Your ad only shows when someone searches that exact phrase. This gives you the most control and typically the best ACoS. Take your top converting keywords from your auto campaign (after 2-3 weeks of data) and move them here.

**Phrase match campaigns:** Your ad shows when the customer's search contains your keyword phrase, with words before or after it. Broader than exact match, but more targeted than auto. Good for catching related search terms you might miss.

**Broad match campaigns:** Your ad shows for searches related to your keyword, including synonyms and related terms. Wider net, more data, typically higher ACoS. Use for keyword discovery alongside auto campaigns.

**Product targeting (ASIN) campaigns:** Instead of keywords, you target specific competitor listings. Your ad shows on their product page. Effective when you have a better price, better reviews, or a clear visual advantage over that specific competitor.

### Bidding strategy

Start with Amazon's suggested bid for your first campaigns. After 7-10 days of data, adjust based on performance. Keywords with low ACoS and good conversion rates: increase bid to win more impressions. Keywords with high ACoS and low conversions: decrease bid or pause.

During launch (first 30-60 days), accept higher ACoS. You are paying for ranking, not just direct profit. Target 40-50% ACoS during launch. After you establish organic ranking, optimize toward 20-30% ACoS for profitability.

### ACoS targets by phase

**Launch phase (weeks 1-8):** 40-60% ACoS is normal. You are buying visibility and data. Do not panic about profitability yet.

**Growth phase (months 3-6):** Target 25-35% ACoS. You should have organic sales supplementing your ad-driven sales by now.

**Profitability phase (months 6+):** Target 15-25% ACoS. Organic sales should be 50-70% of your total. Ads support and maintain ranking rather than build it.

### Weekly optimization

Every week, pull your search term report. Look for: search terms with good sales and low ACoS (move these to exact match campaigns), search terms with high spend and zero sales (add these as negative keywords), and new search terms that show promise (test with dedicated campaigns).

This weekly habit separates profitable sellers from those who bleed money on autopilot campaigns.

> **Common Mistakes in This Phase:**
> - Running only auto campaigns and never graduating keywords to exact match
> - Panicking about ACoS during launch phase
> - Setting campaigns and never optimizing them
> - Not using negative keywords to block wasteful spend
> - Spending too little ($5/day) and never gathering enough data to optimize

**Chapter 6 Checklist:**
- [ ] Set up an auto campaign on day one of launch ($15-25/day budget)
- [ ] After 2-3 weeks, create exact match campaigns from top converting search terms
- [ ] Pull search term reports weekly and add negative keywords
- [ ] Accept launch ACoS of 40-60%, then optimize toward 20-30% over time
- [ ] Test product targeting (ASIN) campaigns against your weakest competitors

## Chapter 7: Launch Strategy

A launch is not just listing your product and waiting. It is a coordinated push to generate enough sales velocity that Amazon's algorithm takes notice and starts showing your listing to organic shoppers.

### The ranking mechanism

Amazon ranks products based primarily on sales velocity for a given keyword. If your product gets 10 sales per day for "stainless steel garlic press" while a competitor gets 5, Amazon pushes your listing higher in search results for that term. Higher ranking means more visibility, which means more organic sales, which reinforces your ranking.

Your launch strategy is about generating that initial sales velocity through advertising and external traffic, long enough for organic ranking to take over.

### The first 30 days

**Week 1:** Launch all PPC campaigns. Auto campaign, exact match on your top 5-8 keywords, and 1-2 product targeting campaigns against competitors with weaker listings. Set budgets higher than your long-term plan. You are investing in ranking.

**Weeks 2-3:** Monitor your PPC data daily. Which keywords are converting? Which are wasting money? Start optimizing. Simultaneously, work on getting your first reviews (covered below).

**Week 4:** First major optimization pass. Kill underperforming keywords. Increase budgets on winners. Check your keyword ranking, are you moving up? If certain keywords show no ranking improvement despite sales, evaluate whether those keywords are too competitive for your current review count.

### Getting reviews (the right way)

Amazon strictly prohibits incentivized reviews, paying for reviews, or asking for positive reviews. Violating this gets your account suspended. Full stop.

What works: Amazon's "Request a Review" button in Seller Central. Available for every order 5-30 days after delivery. Click it for every single order. Amazon sends the customer an automated email. Typical conversion: 1-3% of customers leave a review.

Product inserts (the small card you include in your packaging) can ask customers to leave honest feedback. Do not offer incentives. Do not direct them to leave a 5-star review. Do say: "We'd love to hear your honest feedback. Scan this QR code to leave a review."

Amazon Vine is a program where Amazon sends your product to trusted reviewers for free in exchange for an honest review. Costs $200 per parent ASIN. You provide 1-30 units. Worth it for your first product, especially because Vine reviews carry a "Vine Voice" badge that builds credibility.

### External traffic

Sending traffic from outside Amazon (Google, social media, email lists) to your listing tells Amazon that your product has demand beyond their platform. Amazon rewards this with ranking boosts.

Google Ads targeting your product keywords can drive relevant traffic directly to your Amazon listing. Start small ($10-20/day), track which keywords convert, and scale what works.

Social media works if you already have an audience. If you do not, building one specifically for launch is a long game that will not pay off in time for your first product. Focus on PPC and Google Ads instead.

### Monitoring launch progress

Track three numbers daily during launch: daily unit sales, keyword ranking position for your top 5 keywords, and conversion rate (sessions divided by orders). If sales are happening but ranking is not improving, your listing might have a conversion problem. If clicks are happening but sales are not, look at your price, images, and reviews vs. competitors.

> **Common Mistakes in This Phase:**
> - Launching with zero advertising budget and expecting organic sales
> - Trying to game the review system (account suspension risk)
> - Not tracking keyword ranking during launch
> - Cutting ad spend too early when organic ranking has not stabilized
> - Ignoring conversion rate and blaming everything on "not enough traffic"

**Chapter 7 Checklist:**
- [ ] Launch PPC campaigns on day one (auto + exact match + ASIN targeting)
- [ ] Enroll in Amazon Vine ($200) and provide up to 30 units
- [ ] Click "Request a Review" for every order, every day
- [ ] Track keyword ranking daily for your top 5 keywords
- [ ] Do not reduce ad spend until organic sales are at least 40-50% of total

## Chapter 8: Scaling and Common Mistakes

Your first product is profitable and ranking. Now what?

### When to launch product two

Not immediately. Make sure product one is stable first. Stable means: consistent daily sales without needing to increase ad spend, enough reviews (50+) that your conversion rate is solid, and healthy inventory levels with a reliable reorder process with your supplier.

Most successful FBA sellers recommend launching product two within the same brand and product category. Selling a garlic press? Consider a lemon squeezer, a peeler set, or a kitchen tool bundle. Same audience, same brand, and you can cross-sell through A+ Content and product targeting ads.

### Inventory management

Running out of stock is the most expensive mistake in FBA. When you go out of stock, your organic ranking drops immediately. Rebuilding that ranking costs time and money. Sometimes you never fully recover.

Set reorder points based on your daily sales velocity and your supplier's lead time. If you sell 15 units per day and your supplier takes 30 days to produce and ship, you need to reorder when you have 600+ units left (30 days x 15 units + safety buffer).

Use Amazon's inventory planning tools or third-party software to track this automatically. Do not rely on checking manually.

### Expanding beyond Amazon

Once you have a working product and brand, consider selling the same products on your own Shopify store, eBay, or Walmart Marketplace. FBA Multi-Channel Fulfillment lets Amazon ship orders from other platforms using your existing FBA inventory.

Your own website has lower fees (no Amazon commission), builds your brand directly, and gives you customer data that Amazon does not share. The tradeoff: you have to drive all the traffic yourself.

### Protecting your brand

Register with Amazon Brand Registry if you have not already. Monitor your listings for hijackers (other sellers listing against your ASIN, sometimes with counterfeit products). Set up alerts in Seller Central for any changes to your listings.

File IP complaints through Brand Registry when you find unauthorized sellers. Amazon takes these seriously and usually removes violators within 48 hours.

Consider registering your trademark internationally if you plan to sell on Amazon UK, Germany, or other international marketplaces.

### The mistakes that kill FBA businesses

**1. Chasing trends instead of building a brand.** Fidget spinners made some people rich in 2017 and bankrupted others in 2018. Trending products have a window. Brands have longevity. Build a brand.

**2. Expanding too fast.** Launching 5 products simultaneously splits your attention, your ad budget, and your ability to troubleshoot problems. Go one at a time until you have a repeatable system.

**3. Ignoring your numbers.** Check your profit and loss weekly. Know your true margins after all fees, advertising, returns, and storage costs. Revenue is not profit. Selling $50,000/month at a 2% net margin means you are working a very hard job for $1,000/month.

**4. Emotional product decisions.** The data said no, but you liked the product and ordered 1,000 units anyway. This is the fastest way to lose money. Let the data decide. Every time.

**5. Neglecting existing products.** Your first product still needs attention. Listing optimization, PPC optimization, inventory management, responding to customer questions. A neglected listing declines. Slowly at first, then all at once.

**6. Competing on price alone.** If your only advantage is being $2 cheaper, someone will be $3 cheaper tomorrow. Compete on branding, product quality, listing quality, and customer experience. Price is the last lever you should pull.

> **Common Mistakes in This Phase:**
> - Launching product two before product one is truly stable
> - Letting inventory run out because you did not track reorder timing
> - Expanding to 3+ products simultaneously with thin margins on each
> - Never checking actual profitability after Amazon fees, ads, and returns
> - Ignoring brand protection and losing listings to hijackers

**Chapter 8 Checklist:**
- [ ] Confirm product one has 50+ reviews and stable daily sales before launching product two
- [ ] Set reorder alerts based on daily velocity + supplier lead time
- [ ] Review full P&L (all costs included) monthly
- [ ] Explore at least one additional sales channel (Shopify, eBay, Walmart)
- [ ] Enroll in Brand Registry and monitor for listing hijackers

## Final Note

This guide covers the foundation. Every chapter could be its own book, and the details change as Amazon updates its policies, algorithm, and fee structure (which happens constantly).

The sellers who succeed long-term are the ones who treat this as a real business, not a side hustle hack. They track their numbers. They optimize continuously. They build brands, not just products.

Start with one product. Get the system working. Then scale.
