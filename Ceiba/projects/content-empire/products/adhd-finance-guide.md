## How to Use This Guide

Read it once straight through. Then go back to each chapter when you are ready to implement that piece. You do not need to do everything at once. In fact, doing everything at once is how people with ADHD abandon systems in week two.

This guide has seven chapters and three appendix templates. Each chapter is a complete module. Start with Chapter 1. Come back for the rest when you are ready.

The goal is not a perfect budget. The goal is a system you actually use.

# Chapter 1: Why Every Budget App Has Failed You (and It Is Not Your Fault)

You have probably tried YNAB. Or Mint. Or a spreadsheet someone linked you to on Reddit. Maybe you used it for three days, felt good about yourself, then opened it two weeks later and felt so behind that you closed it immediately.

That is not a discipline problem. That is a design problem.

Every major budget app was built on an assumption: that the person using it will sit down once a week, categorize every transaction, reconcile their accounts, and feel motivated by watching numbers move. That assumption describes maybe 20% of the adult population. It does not describe you.

Here is what ADHD actually does to financial decision-making, based on what researchers and clinicians have documented:

**Pattern 1: Impaired working memory makes financial tracking collapse.**

Working memory is the brain's scratch pad. It holds information long enough to act on it. For ADHD brains, this scratch pad is smaller and less reliable. When you buy coffee and tell yourself you will log it later, "later" never comes. Not because you are lazy. Because your working memory did not hold the intent long enough for it to become an action. By the time you are home, the purchase is gone from your head.

Apps that require you to remember to log things are built for people with reliable working memory. That is not the tool for you.

**Pattern 2: Reward circuitry differences make delayed consequences feel invisible.**

The ADHD brain has a weaker response to delayed rewards and consequences. Future-you running out of money feels abstract. Present-you wanting that jacket feels urgent and real. This is not a values problem. It is a neurological difference in how consequences register.

Most budget systems rely on future-consequence motivation. "You'll be glad you saved this." "Think about your future." That framing simply does not hit the same way for ADHD brains. Systems built around delayed rewards require a neurotype you do not have.

**Pattern 3: Decision fatigue sets in faster and hits harder.**

Every budget category is a decision point. YNAB has dozens of categories. Mint automatically sorts things into categories, but then asks you to review and correct them. Each correction is a small decision. Each decision uses cognitive resources. ADHD brains hit decision fatigue earlier than neurotypical brains. After enough micro-decisions, the brain opts out entirely.

The person who built YNAB's category system can probably manage 40 categories without breaking a sweat. You cannot. That does not mean you are bad at money. It means you need fewer categories.

**Pattern 4: Novelty fades fast, and systems depend on consistency.**

ADHD brains respond strongly to novelty. A new budget app is exciting. You spend two hours setting it up. You feel productive. Then it is no longer new. The dopamine from the setup is gone. Using the system now feels like maintenance, and maintenance is not interesting. The system dies.

This is the week-two wall. Almost every ADHD person who has tried budgeting has hit it. The problem is not you. The problem is that the system required novelty to sustain and novelty does not last.

**What a budget system needs to do differently for ADHD brains:**

It needs to reduce the number of categories to the minimum that still works. It needs to make logging so frictionless that it takes less than ten seconds. It needs to build in protection against impulse decisions rather than assuming you will just resist them. It needs to surface subscriptions because ADHD brains forget recurring charges. And it needs short, frequent check-ins instead of monthly reviews that feel overwhelming.

That is what this system does.

# Chapter 2: The Five Categories

The single most important design decision in this system is this: five categories only.

Not subcategories. Not tags. Not "food, restaurants, groceries, coffee shops, meal delivery" as separate lines. Five categories. Every expense goes into one of them.

The five categories are:

1. **Needs** - rent, utilities, groceries, insurance, medications, transportation to work
2. **Wants** - clothing, eating out, subscriptions you actually use, hobbies
3. **Savings** - money you move to a separate account before spending anything else
4. **Debt** - minimum payments plus any extra you are paying toward debt
5. **Fun** - discretionary spending with no guilt attached

That is the whole system at the category level.

**Why this works when 40 categories do not:**

Cognitive load research is clear on this. The more categories in a tracking system, the less likely a person is to maintain it past the first month. This effect is stronger for people with ADHD because every categorization decision is a small cognitive cost. With five categories, the question "where does this go?" almost always has an obvious answer. You do not need to think.

If you genuinely cannot decide between two categories, default to Wants. Do not spend more than ten seconds on any categorization decision.

**How to assign every expense:**

Ask one question: "Can I not live without this?" If yes, it is a Need. If you could survive without it, it is a Want. If it is a payment toward debt, it is Debt. If you are moving it to savings before spending, it is Savings. If you budgeted a specific amount to spend freely without tracking specifics, that is Fun.

Some examples that trip people up:

- Spotify: Want (unless you need it for work, in which case Need)
- Gym membership you actually use: Want
- Gym membership you stopped using: this is why Chapter 4 exists
- Netflix: Want
- Work lunch you could bring from home: Want
- Bus pass to get to work: Need
- New phone: Want, unless your current phone is broken and you need it for work

The more you practice this, the faster it gets. By week two, categorization takes under three seconds per expense.

**The percentages to aim for:**

These are starting guidelines, not rules. Adjust based on your income and situation.

- Needs: 50% of take-home pay
- Wants: 20%
- Savings: 10%
- Debt: 10%
- Fun: 10%

If you have high debt, reduce Wants and increase Debt temporarily. If you have no debt, move that 10% to Savings or Fun. The percentages are a starting point, not a mandate.

**The key principle:**

Fewer categories mean more compliance. You will actually use a five-category system. You will not use a forty-category system, no matter how logical it looks on setup day.

# Chapter 3: The Impulse Cooldown Protocol

Impulse purchases are not a character flaw. They are a predictable output of how the ADHD brain evaluates reward versus consequence. The brain says: reward now, consequence later. Consequence later loses almost every time in the moment.

The fix is not willpower. Willpower is a finite resource and it runs out faster for ADHD brains. The fix is a structural delay.

**The 24-hour rule:**

Any unplanned purchase over $20 gets a 24-hour pause before you buy it. That is the whole rule. You do not decide not to buy it. You decide to wait one day.

Why does this work? Because the urgency you feel about a purchase in the moment is largely manufactured by the dopamine spike of novelty. A pair of shoes you found while browsing feels essential right now. After 24 hours, you often do not care. Not always. But often enough that implementing this rule saves real money.

The important part: this is not about denying yourself. If you still want the item after 24 hours, buy it. You did not fail. You just filtered out the impulse buys from the genuine wants.

**How to log the impulse without buying it:**

The moment you want something, capture it immediately. If you do not capture it, your working memory will either keep circling back to it (distracting you) or lose it entirely. Either way, you lose.

Three ways to capture an impulse purchase:

1. **Phone note**: Open your default notes app, type the item name and price. Done. This takes eight seconds.
2. **Calendar event**: Set a reminder 25 hours from now titled "Still want [item]? [$price]" This is the best method because the system follows up with you.
3. **Telegram message**: If you are using BehiqueBot, send it as a note. Type "impulse: [item] $[price]" and it logs it for you.

Do not rely on memory to come back to the item. Set the reminder. Your working memory will not hold this.

**What to do when the 24 hours expire:**

When your reminder fires, ask one question: do you still want this?

If yes, check your Wants budget. If you have room, buy it without guilt. You used the system correctly.

If no, delete the note. You just saved yourself money without any willpower. The system did the work.

If you are unsure, give it another 24 hours.

**Items that do not need the cooldown:**

Groceries, household supplies, anything planned in advance, and anything under $20. The rule is for unplanned purchases over $20. Applying it to a $4 coffee would make the system annoying enough to abandon. Do not do that.

**When someone else is putting pressure on you to buy:**

This is where the protocol matters most. "Limited time offer." "Only three left." "My friend group is all getting one." These are external urgency signals that bypass your own judgment. The 24-hour rule gives you a simple answer that does not require you to argue in the moment: "I have a rule about unplanned purchases. I will let you know tomorrow." That is a complete sentence. It buys you time without confrontation.

Scarcity claims ("only three left") are almost always marketing. Real scarcity is rare. If the item is actually gone tomorrow, it will appear again at another time. If it does not, you probably did not need it.

**What to do when the cooldown still does not stop the purchase:**

Sometimes you wait 24 hours and still want it just as badly. That is fine. That is the system working. The point was never to stop you from buying things. The point was to separate the impulse from the decision. If the desire is still there after 24 hours, it is no longer just an impulse. Buy it if your budget allows.

If your budget does not allow it right now, log it as a planned future purchase. Put it in your Wants category for next month. Give it a line item. Now it is a goal instead of an impulse. That shift matters more than it sounds.

**The long-term pattern:**

After a few months of this, you will notice two things. First, the list of items you still want after 24 hours is much shorter than you would have expected. Research on ADHD impulse spending consistently shows that the desire to purchase often drops significantly within hours of the initial trigger. Second, the items you do buy feel better because you chose them deliberately rather than reactively. The impulse cooldown is one of the few budgeting tools that also improves how spending feels, not just how it looks on paper.

# Chapter 4: The Subscription Audit

Subscriptions are the most common source of financial leakage for ADHD adults. The reason is structural. You sign up when the service is new and interesting. You stop using it when the novelty fades. But the charge keeps coming. And because it is automatic, there is no decision moment to notice it.

The subscription audit is a quarterly exercise. Not annual. Quarterly, because three months is about as long as it takes for a forgotten subscription to cost enough to matter. Annual audits catch the problem too late.

**How to find every recurring charge in 15 minutes:**

Step 1: Pull up your bank account and credit card statements for the last 60 days. You want two months because some subscriptions bill every 30 days and you need to see them twice to confirm they are recurring.

Step 2: Scroll through every line item. Write down anything that appeared more than once with a similar amount. These are your subscriptions.

Step 3: Also check your email for receipts from services you may have forgotten about. Search for "receipt", "invoice", "your subscription", and "your plan" in your inbox. This often surfaces one or two services you completely forgot were billing you.

Step 4: Check your Apple ID or Google Play account for in-app subscriptions. These are often the most forgotten because they show up on your card under "Apple" or "Google" rather than the service name.

That is the full discovery process. It should take 10 to 15 minutes for most people.

**The keep, cancel, pause decision framework:**

For each subscription, ask these questions in order:

1. Did I use this in the last 30 days? If no, go to question 2. If yes, keep it.
2. Do I plan to use it in the next 30 days for a specific reason? If yes, keep it for one more month and check again. If no, cancel or pause.
3. Is pausing available? Some services let you pause instead of cancel. If you want to come back eventually, pause it. If you do not care, cancel it.

There is no middle ground of "I might use it someday." That phrase costs you money every month. If the answer is not a clear yes, it is a no.

**The math on forgotten subscriptions:**

A $9.99 subscription you forgot about costs $120 per year. Most people who do their first subscription audit find two to four services they forgot about. That is $240 to $480 per year being charged without a single moment of enjoyment from it.

This audit is the fastest financial return in this entire system. Do it in your first week.

**Common subscriptions people forget they have:**

These are the ones that show up most often in first-time audits:

- Trial subscriptions that converted to paid after a free period
- Annual subscriptions that renewed without a noticeable charge (because it is annual and you forgot)
- Services a family member signed up for on a shared card
- Apps that shifted to a subscription model after you originally bought them as one-time purchases
- "Freemium" services where you upgraded once and never thought about it again

Check specifically for Adobe, LinkedIn Premium, Audible, Calm, Headspace, unused cloud storage upgrades, and any service you signed up for during a pandemic year when you had different habits.

**How to cancel without getting trapped in the process:**

Some services make cancellation difficult on purpose. They bury the cancel button, require a phone call, or show you a retention offer mid-cancellation. Here is how to handle each:

If the cancel button is buried: go to your account settings, then billing or subscription. If you cannot find it in two minutes, Google "[service name] how to cancel." Someone has documented it.

If they require a phone call: check whether you can cancel through your bank by disputing future charges or flagging the merchant. For Apple App Store subscriptions, you can cancel everything directly through your iPhone settings without calling anyone.

If they offer you a discount to stay: only accept it if you were already planning to keep the service. Do not let a discount turn a service you do not use into a service you keep paying for because it now costs less.

**The quarterly cadence:**

Pick one day per quarter to run this audit. Add it to your calendar now. Set it for 20 minutes, which is more time than you need. Running it every three months means a forgotten subscription costs you at most $30 before you catch it, not $120.

If you want to make this easier, keep a running list of your subscriptions in the template in the Appendix. Update it when you add or cancel something. The quarterly audit then becomes a five-minute review instead of a 15-minute discovery session.

**A note on subscription creep:**

Subscription creep is the gradual accumulation of small monthly charges that individually feel insignificant but collectively drain a meaningful amount of your income. $9.99 here, $4.99 there, $14.99 somewhere else. These feel small because they bill monthly and the amounts are under $20. But four forgotten services at an average of $10 each is $480 per year.

The reason subscription creep hits ADHD adults harder than others is that the charges are automatic and invisible. There is no decision moment. No swipe of a card. No moment where you consciously choose to pay. The money leaves without your attention, and ADHD makes noticing small recurring drains much harder. The quarterly audit is the only reliable fix.

# Chapter 5: Zero-Friction Logging

Every budget system lives or dies on this question: how hard is it to log an expense?

If the answer is "open the app, log in, find the right category, type the amount, add a note, save" then you will log maybe 40% of your expenses. That is not enough data to make any decisions. The system becomes useless.

The answer needs to be: open a message thread, type a number, send.

**Why apps fail for ADHD and texts succeed:**

Apps have friction by design. They are software products with user interfaces. Every interface is a series of decisions. Open the app. Navigate to the right screen. Choose the category from a dropdown. Type the amount. Hit save. That is five to seven steps for one transaction.

A text message is one step. You are already on your phone. You open a thread and type. This is not a minor difference. For ADHD brains, friction is the difference between a habit that sticks and one that does not.

There is also a psychological factor. Apps feel like work. A Telegram message to yourself feels like a text. One of these feels like a chore and one does not. That distinction matters more than any feature set.

**Setting up zero-friction logging:**

The simplest version requires no app, no setup, and no monthly fee.

Create a dedicated notes thread or Telegram saved messages channel. Call it "expenses." Every time you spend money, send a message with this format:

```
[amount] [category] [optional note]
```

Examples:
```
12 wants lunch
45 needs groceries
8 wants coffee
```

That is it. Each log takes under ten seconds.

Once a week, you open that thread and tally the numbers by category. This takes about five minutes. You do not need an app to do basic arithmetic for five categories.

**If you are using BehiqueBot:**

Send the message to your bot in the same format. The bot logs it to your expense record automatically. You never need to open a spreadsheet.

**If you want a slightly more structured version:**

Use a shared Google Sheet with five columns: date, amount, category, note, and week number. Open it on your phone when you need to log. One row per expense. Do not use formulas or automation on your first week. Just log. The habit of logging matters more than the analysis layer on top of it.

**What counts as a log-worthy expense:**

Everything you spend money on. That includes card payments, cash, Venmo, Apple Pay, everything. If money left your possession, log it.

The most commonly missed expenses are:

- Cash purchases (buying something from a street vendor, paying a friend back)
- Shared expenses that feel like "the other person's thing" even though you paid
- Automatic payments you forgot were happening that day
- Small purchases under $5 that feel too minor to log

Log all of them. Especially the small ones, because they add up and they are the most invisible. A $3 coffee three times a week is $46 per month. If you are wondering where your money goes and you are not logging small purchases, now you know.

**The recovery rule:**

You will miss a day. That is certain. The question is what you do next.

The rule is this: never miss two days in a row. One missed day is a gap in data. Two missed days is the start of abandonment. If you missed yesterday, log today before you do anything else. Do not try to reconstruct yesterday. Just start fresh.

This is different from how most people think about habits. Most people feel that missing a day means starting over, which feels discouraging, which leads to quitting. The real system is: one miss is normal, two misses is a pattern you want to interrupt. That mental model makes it much easier to recover.

**Building the logging habit in 7 days:**

The goal for the first week is not accuracy. It is consistency. Log every expense, even the tiny ones. Even if you miss a few, log what you remember.

Here is the seven-day protocol:

- Day 1: Log everything manually. Notice where the friction is.
- Day 2: Log everything. If you forgot yesterday's coffee, do not add it. Start clean from today.
- Day 3: Log everything. Notice if any category surprises you.
- Day 4: Same. Before bed, add up your Want spending so far this week.
- Day 5: Same. You are probably already noticing patterns.
- Day 6: Same. Do not skip because it is the weekend.
- Day 7: Do your first weekly review using Chapter 6.

By day 7, logging will feel normal. Not automatic yet, but normal. That is enough. Automatic comes after six to eight weeks.

**What to do with the data after week one:**

Do not try to analyze it deeply. Look at your Want total and your Needs total. Compare them to your targets. Notice if anything surprised you. That is the whole week-one analysis. The insight value of one week of data is limited. The habit value is everything.

# Chapter 6: The Weekly 10-Minute Review

The monthly budget review is a relic of a time before digital transactions. It made sense when you balanced a paper checkbook once a month. It does not make sense now, and it especially does not make sense for ADHD brains, for whom a month is too long a feedback loop.

A week is the right unit. Short enough that the data is still fresh. Short enough that you have not drifted too far from your plan. Long enough that you have real data to look at.

The review takes ten minutes. Not an hour. Not an afternoon. Ten minutes.

**Pick a time and stick to it:**

Sunday evening works well for most people because the week ahead is mentally present. Saturday morning works if you prefer starting the weekend with a clear picture. The day matters less than the consistency. Same time, every week.

Set a recurring calendar event. Call it "10-minute budget check." Set the duration to 10 minutes. This signals to your brain that this is a bounded task, not an open-ended one. Bounded tasks are much easier for ADHD brains to start.

**The three questions:**

The weekly review is just three questions. Do not add to this list. The value is in the simplicity.

1. **Am I under on Needs?**
Did I spend more than my Needs budget this week? If yes, what happened? A one-time event like a car repair is fine. A pattern of overspending on Needs means the budget number is wrong and needs adjusting.

2. **Am I under on Wants?**
This is the most informative number for most people. Wants tend to expand to fill available money. Seeing it weekly keeps it visible.

3. **Did I move money to Savings?**
Not "did I have money left over to save." Did I actively transfer money to a savings account before spending it? Savings as a category only works if you treat it like a bill you pay yourself first.

That is the entire review. Three questions, five minutes of looking at numbers, five minutes of thinking about next week.

**What to do if you went over:**

If you spent more than your weekly target in a category, the only useful question is: was this a one-time thing or a pattern?

A car repair that blew your Needs budget is a one-time thing. Next week returns to normal. A pattern of going over on Wants three weeks in a row means one of two things: your Wants budget is set too low for your actual life, or there is a spending pattern you have not identified yet.

If the budget is too low, raise it. Do not keep setting yourself up to fail against a number that does not reflect reality. A budget you can actually hit is more useful than a perfect budget you blow through every week.

If there is a pattern you have not identified, the weekly logs will show you. Look at what you bought in the Wants category over three weeks. Usually one or two recurring categories stand out. Once you see the pattern, you can decide whether to budget for it explicitly or reduce it consciously.

**Adjusting the budget over time:**

Your budget is not a contract. It is a model of your spending, and models need updating when reality changes. Review your category targets every two to three months. Ask: are these numbers matching how I actually live, or am I fighting them every week?

A budget you fight is one you will eventually stop using. A budget that fits your real life is one you can maintain indefinitely.

**Making this feel like a win:**

The most common failure mode for the weekly review is that it feels like a performance review for your past self. You look at what you spent and feel judged. That feeling is a fast path to avoidance.

Reframe it this way: the review is information collection, not judgment. You spent $180 on Wants this week. That is a data point. It is not a verdict. Maybe you had a reason. Maybe you did not. Either way, now you know, and knowing is the only way to make different decisions next week.

Keep the tone of the review neutral. You are an observer looking at numbers, not a judge reviewing behavior.

After the review, note one thing you did well this week. Not "I stuck to my budget perfectly." Just one specific thing. Paid a subscription on time. Logged every expense. Used the 24-hour rule on something. Write it down. This builds the identity of "I am someone who manages money" rather than "I am someone who is bad at money."

The identity piece matters. Behavior follows identity over time.

# Chapter 7: Dopamine-Driven Progress

Standard budgets fail for ADHD brains partly because they offer no rewards. You do the work, you stay within your limits, and the reward is... the absence of a bad outcome. That is not motivating. The ADHD brain needs to feel something to sustain a behavior.

This chapter is about designing rewards into your system so that staying on track feels good, not just neutral.

**Why normal budgets do not feel rewarding:**

When a neurotypical person sticks to their budget for a month, they can feel satisfaction from the delayed reward: their savings account grew, their debt went down, they are closer to a goal. The delayed consequence system works well enough for them.

For ADHD brains, delayed consequences are much weaker motivators. The savings account growing over three months registers less emotionally than a small immediate reward does. This is not a moral failing. It is how the brain's reward circuitry is wired.

The solution is to add immediate, visible rewards to the system. Not instead of long-term goals. In addition to them.

**Designing visual wins into your tracking:**

The weekly total for each category is a number. Numbers are not inherently motivating. But progress toward a visible milestone is.

Try this: at the start of each month, set a small target for each category. Spend less than $X on Wants. Save at least $Y. Keep Fun under $Z.

At the end of each week, mark whether you hit your target. Keep a simple tally. Four weeks in a row under Wants budget? That is a streak. Streaks create momentum, and momentum feels good.

You can use a paper habit tracker, a simple spreadsheet column, or even just a row of checkmarks in a notebook. The medium does not matter. The visual evidence of progress does.

**The milestone reward structure:**

Set up a tiered reward system tied to financial milestones. The rewards should be things you enjoy that do not cost much, because the goal is to make the behavior feel good without undermining the progress.

Example tiers:

- Week 1 complete (logged every expense): a specific small thing you enjoy. A movie at home, your favorite meal, an hour of guilt-free gaming.
- Month 1 complete (stayed within budget 3 of 4 weeks): something slightly more meaningful. A meal out you planned for, a small purchase you have been putting off.
- First subscription audit complete (found and canceled at least one forgotten service): put the first month's savings directly into a Fun account. Spend it on something that makes you happy. You earned it.
- First $100 saved (in the Savings category): mark it visibly. Some people put a sticker somewhere they will see it. Others take a screenshot of the balance. The action of marking it matters.

These rewards do two things. First, they give your brain the immediate hit it needs to sustain the behavior. Second, they build an association between financial discipline and feeling good. That association is the foundation of a lasting habit.

**Connecting progress to the actual goal:**

The rewards work better when they are connected to something specific you are working toward. Not "I want to save money" but "I am saving for a trip to see my family in December" or "I am building a three-month emergency fund so I stop feeling anxious every time an unexpected expense hits."

Concrete goals make progress visible. Vague goals do not. "I want to save more" has no milestone. "I want $500 in savings by June 1" has a finish line.

When you have a concrete goal, put it somewhere visible. Not buried in an app. Somewhere you actually see it. The wallpaper of your phone. A sticky note on your desk. The first line of your expense log. Your brain will start connecting daily decisions to that goal in a way it cannot when the goal lives only in an app.

**What to do when motivation drops:**

It will. This is not a warning. It is a certainty. The novelty of the system will wear off, probably around week three or four. The dopamine from setting it up is gone. Now it is just maintenance, and maintenance is the hardest thing for ADHD brains to sustain.

When that happens, do not try to reignite excitement about budgeting. That is a losing battle. Instead, reconnect with the concrete goal. Pull up the number you are working toward. A trip. An emergency fund. A specific purchase you are saving for. The goal is what you want, not the system. The system is just the path.

If even the goal feels distant and abstract, reduce it. Instead of "save $3,000 for an emergency fund," make the current target "$200." Get there. Mark it. Then reset to "$400." Small targets with visible progress are more motivating than large targets with invisible progress, especially for ADHD brains.

**Failure is part of the process:**

You will miss a review. You will go over budget for a month. You will forget about a subscription for three months. None of these are reasons to quit. They are normal parts of using any system over a long period.

The metric that matters is not perfection. It is how quickly you recover after a slip. A person who uses this system imperfectly for two years will be in a fundamentally different financial position than someone who tried it perfectly for two months and quit.

**The longer game:**

None of this is about becoming someone who obsesses over money. The goal is to reach a point where your finances run in the background, not the foreground. Where you know your numbers without anxiety, where impulse purchases get filtered without willpower, and where subscriptions do not drain you silently.

That state is achievable. It takes about three months of consistent use of this system. Not perfect use. Consistent use. Missing a week is fine. Coming back is what counts.

## Quick Reference Card

**The 5 Categories:**
1. Needs (50%) - rent, utilities, groceries, insurance, medications
2. Wants (20%) - anything you want but do not need
3. Savings (10%) - pay yourself first, before anything else
4. Debt (10%) - minimums plus extra if possible
5. Fun (10%) - spend freely, no guilt, no detailed tracking

**The 24-Hour Rule:**
Unplanned purchase over $20. Log it. Wait 24 hours. Still want it? Buy it. Gone? You saved money.

**The Quarterly Audit:**
Every 3 months, audit all subscriptions. Keep only what you used in the last 30 days.

**The Weekly Review:**
10 minutes. Same time every week. Three questions only. One win noted.
