## Introduction: Why Most People Use AI for Writing Wrong

Most people who try to use AI for copywriting give up after a week. Not because the tools are bad. Because they are treating a thinking partner like a vending machine.

Here is what that looks like: you open ChatGPT or Claude, type "write me a landing page for my course," read the generic output, feel disappointed, and decide AI is overhyped for real marketing work.

Here is what actually happened: you gave the model no context about your audience, no information about the specific problem you solve, no constraints on tone or format, and no instruction about what makes your offer different. You asked for a landing page and got back the average of every landing page the model has ever seen.

The model did not fail you. You gave it nothing to work with.

This playbook is built on one core principle: AI does not write copy. You write copy with AI. The distinction matters because it shifts your role from waiting for output to directing output. When you get that right, the speed improvement is real. Ten times faster is not marketing language. It is what happens when a skilled prompt writer with a clear brief goes to work alongside a language model that can draft, iterate, and restructure in seconds.

By the end of this playbook you will have a prompt library for landing pages, emails, and social content. You will know how to run each through an editing layer that makes AI copy sound like you. And you will have a system for maintaining voice consistency across every piece of content you produce.

One rule before we start: do not publish AI output without editing it. Every chapter ends with an editing step. Skip it and your copy will be faster but weaker. Follow it and your copy will be faster and better than what you were producing before.

## Chapter 1: The Prompting Fundamentals

### The 4-Part Prompt Structure

Every high-quality AI copy output starts with a prompt that has four components. When any component is missing, the output degrades.

**Component 1: Role**

Tell the model who it is for this task. Not "act as a copywriter" (too vague) but something specific.

Example: "You are a direct response copywriter specializing in digital products for solopreneurs. Your style is clear, confident, and specific. You do not use hype or vague benefit statements."

The role sets the voice, the perspective, and the constraints. Without it, the model will default to a generic helpful tone that sounds like a first draft from someone who has read a lot of marketing content but never tested any of it.

**Component 2: Task**

Be precise about what you need. Include the format, the length, and the specific element you are writing.

Weak task: "Write a headline for my product."

Strong task: "Write 5 headline options for the hero section of my landing page. Each headline should be under 10 words, lead with the primary benefit, and speak directly to freelancers who are losing clients to cheaper competitors."

The difference is not length. It is specificity. Every detail you add narrows the output toward something usable.

**Component 3: Format**

Tell the model exactly how to structure the response. If you need a table, say table. If you need numbered options, say numbered. If you need a complete section with headers, say that.

Format instruction: "Output the 5 headlines as a numbered list. Include a one-sentence note after each headline explaining the angle it is using."

Without format guidance, you will get output organized however the model decides is appropriate. That is often fine. But when you need to iterate quickly across multiple copy elements, clear format instructions save time.

**Component 4: Constraints**

Tell the model what not to do. This is the most commonly skipped component and the one that most dramatically improves output quality.

Common constraints:
- "Do not use exclamation points."
- "Do not use the phrase 'game-changing' or any similar hype language."
- "Avoid passive voice."
- "Do not include a call to action in this output. I will add that separately."
- "Write for an audience that is skeptical of marketing language and responds to specificity over enthusiasm."

Constraints train the model away from its default patterns. The default is usually good. The constrained version is usually better.

### Putting It Together: A Complete Prompt

Here is the full 4-part prompt for a hero headline:

"You are a direct response copywriter specializing in digital products for solopreneurs. Your style is specific, confident, and free of marketing cliches.

Task: Write 5 headline options for the hero section of a landing page for a $19.99 AI copywriting guide. The guide teaches freelancers and solopreneurs how to write landing pages, emails, and social copy faster using AI prompting frameworks.

Format: Numbered list. Each headline under 10 words. One sentence after each explaining the angle.

Constraints: No exclamation points. No passive voice. No phrases like 'unleash,' 'skyrocket,' 'transform,' or 'game-changing.' Write for someone who is mildly skeptical of AI tools but interested in the practical application."

This prompt will produce usable output on the first try. Not perfect. But 70-80% of the way there, which means editing instead of writing from scratch.

## Chapter 2: Landing Page Copy with AI

### The 5 Sections You Need

A landing page for a digital product needs five sections. Each has a distinct job. Each should be prompted and written separately.

**Section 1: Hero (Headline + Subheadline + CTA)**

The hero's job is to communicate the primary benefit and give the right people a reason to keep reading.

Prompt framework for the hero:

"You are a direct response copywriter. Write 3 hero section options for a landing page. Each option includes: a headline (under 10 words, benefit-first), a subheadline (1-2 sentences expanding the headline, adding specificity), and a CTA button text (3-5 words, action-oriented).

Product: [describe your product in one paragraph]
Audience: [describe your reader's specific problem in two sentences]
Constraints: No hype language. No exclamation points. CTA should feel like a low-commitment next step, not a high-pressure buy."

**Section 2: Problem Statement**

The problem section exists to make the reader feel understood before you present any solution. If they do not feel seen here, they will not trust the solution.

Prompt for problem section:

"Write a 100-word problem section for a landing page. The reader is a [specific audience description]. They are struggling with [specific problem]. They have probably already tried [common failed solutions]. Do not mention the product yet. The goal is to articulate the problem with enough precision that the reader thinks you wrote this specifically for them. Use second person. No bullet points. Write in short paragraphs."

**Section 3: Solution Introduction**

Introduce your product as the mechanism that solves the problem you just named. Do not list features yet. Introduce the approach.

Prompt for solution section:

"Write a 100-150 word solution introduction for a landing page. The product is [product name and one-sentence description]. Following the problem section, this section should present the product as the natural response to the problem described. Focus on the mechanism or approach, not the feature list. End with a transition sentence that sets up the benefit bullets or feature details that follow."

**Section 4: Social Proof**

Testimonials work when they are specific. Generic praise does not convert.

Prompt to strengthen weak testimonials:

"I have a testimonial that reads: [paste testimonial]. Rewrite it in a format that leads with the specific outcome, includes the before state, and ends with the result. Keep the original meaning but reorganize the structure for maximum credibility. Suggest 3 versions."

If you do not have testimonials yet, prompt for a social proof framework:

"Write 3 placeholder testimonial structures for a digital product landing page. Each should follow this format: [Role] who struggled with [specific problem] used [product] and achieved [specific outcome]. Indicate where I need to fill in real customer data."

**Section 5: CTA Section**

The closing CTA section restates the primary benefit, addresses the main objection, and makes the action feel simple.

Prompt for CTA section:

"Write a closing CTA section for a landing page. The product is [name and price]. The primary benefit is [benefit]. The main objection a skeptical reader would have at this point is [objection]. The section should: restate the benefit without repeating the hero headline exactly, address the objection in one sentence, and end with a CTA button text and a low-pressure reassurance line (like a guarantee or 'takes 2 minutes to get started')."

## Chapter 3: Email Copy with AI

### The Swipe File Prompt System

Email copy is where AI performs best because the constraints are clear: a subject line, preview text, a body with one idea, and a CTA. When you give the model a tight brief, it produces tight output.

**Subject Line Prompts**

Prompt for subject line batch:

"Write 10 subject line options for an email about [topic]. The email is going to [audience description]. The goal of the email is [one specific goal: drive a click, build trust, introduce a product, share a lesson].

Include 3 subject lines using the specific number format (e.g., '3 reasons your landing page is not converting'), 3 using an open loop format (starts a thought without finishing it), and 4 using a direct benefit format. No exclamation points. No spam trigger words."

**Preview Text Prompts**

Preview text is the sentence that appears after the subject line in most email clients. It is a second chance to earn the open.

Prompt:

"Write 5 preview text options to pair with this subject line: [subject line]. The preview text should either expand the subject line promise with a specific detail, or create a secondary curiosity hook that complements the subject line without repeating it. Each option should be under 90 characters."

**Email Body Prompt**

Prompt for a value email (no pitch):

"Write a 250-300 word email for [audience]. The subject line is [subject line]. The one idea in this email is [one specific insight or lesson]. Structure: open with a hook that continues the subject line promise. Deliver the insight in 2-3 short paragraphs. End with a question or forward-looking statement that creates a soft open loop. No CTA in this email. No exclamation points. Write in first person, conversational but specific."

Prompt for a sales email:

"Write a 300-400 word sales email for [product name, price, one-sentence description]. Audience: [description]. The email structure: open with a story or problem statement that establishes relevance. Present the product as the solution. Include one specific proof point (outcome or testimonial). End with a clear CTA and a deadline or reason to act now (only if a real deadline exists). No exclamation points. No false scarcity."

**Full Sequence Prompt**

To draft a complete 5-email welcome sequence:

"Write a 5-email welcome sequence for [business description and audience]. Each email has one job:

Email 1: Welcome and immediate value delivery. Under 200 words. Deliver [specific lead magnet or content]. Set expectations for what is coming.

Email 2: Founder story. 250-300 words. Personal, specific, connects to why this work matters to me and why it is relevant to the reader.

Email 3: Standalone value email. 250 words. One lesson the reader can apply today. No pitch.

Email 4: Case study or proof. 250 words. Show a specific result. Name the before and after.

Email 5: Soft offer. 300 words. Introduce [product] naturally as the next step for readers who want to go further. Single CTA.

Voice: direct, specific, no hype. No exclamation points. Write each email separately with a header."

## Chapter 4: Social Media Copy with AI

### Platform-Specific Prompts

**Instagram Hook Prompts**

The hook is the first line of a caption or the first slide of a carousel. It has one job: stop the scroll.

Prompt for hook batch:

"Write 15 Instagram hook options for content about [topic]. The audience is [description]. Each hook should be one sentence, under 15 words, and use one of these formats: a declarative statement that challenges a common belief, a specific number or statistic, a question that names a specific pain, or a short story opener that drops into a specific moment.

Label each hook with its format type. No exclamation points. No vague openers like 'Have you ever wondered' or 'In today's world.'"

**Caption Prompts**

Prompt for a full Instagram caption:

"Write a 150-200 word Instagram caption for a carousel post about [topic]. The hook is: [hook]. Structure: thesis-first (state the main idea in the first sentence), supporting content in the body (2-3 short paragraphs), and a CTA that asks a question or directs readers to follow for more. Add 15-20 hashtags relevant to [topic] and [audience]. No exclamation points. Conversational but specific tone."

**Twitter/X Thread Prompts**

Prompt for a thread:

"Write a 10-tweet thread about [topic] for [audience]. Tweet 1 is the hook. Tweets 2-9 develop the idea one point per tweet, each under 280 characters. Tweet 10 is the CTA and summary. Structure each tweet to work as a standalone piece while connecting to the thread. No exclamation points. No filler phrases. Every tweet should carry weight on its own."

**LinkedIn Prompts**

Prompt for a LinkedIn post:

"Write a 200-250 word LinkedIn post about [topic] for [audience]. Format: short, punchy lines with single-sentence paragraphs. Open with a declarative hook. Develop the idea in the body with specific details or numbered points. End with a question to drive comments. No exclamation points. Professional but conversational tone. Avoid corporate jargon."

## Chapter 5: The Editing Layer

### 6 Specific Edits That Make AI Copy Sound Human

AI copy has patterns. When you recognize them, you can edit them out in 10-15 minutes and produce something that reads like it was written by a person with a specific perspective. Here are the six edits that do the most work.

**Edit 1: Cut the Preamble**

AI outputs frequently open with a sentence that restates the topic before saying anything about it. "In today's digital landscape, copywriting has become increasingly important." Delete that sentence and start with the second one. The second sentence is almost always where the actual content begins.

**Edit 2: Replace "Utilize" and Similar Bureaucratic Verbs**

AI loves "utilize" when "use" is available. It loves "leverage" when "use" is available. It loves "implement" when "do" is available. Scan for these and replace with the simpler verb every time.

**Edit 3: Add a Specific Detail**

AI generalizes because it is averaging across all similar content it has seen. When you read a line that feels vague, inject a specific number, date, name, or example. "This framework saves time" becomes "This framework cut my landing page writing time from 4 hours to 45 minutes." The specificity is yours to add. The model cannot invent your actual data.

**Edit 4: Break Up Long Sentences**

AI tends to construct grammatically complete compound sentences. On a sales page or in an email, those need to be broken into shorter units. If a sentence has a comma in it, ask whether it should be two sentences instead. Usually it should.

**Edit 5: Remove Adverbs That Signal Weakness**

"Significantly," "substantially," "incredibly," "truly," and "really" appear frequently in AI copy and weaken the noun or verb they are supposed to strengthen. Delete them. If the claim cannot stand without the adverb, replace the noun or verb with a stronger one.

**Edit 6: Add Your Contractions and Colloquialisms**

AI copy in formal mode avoids contractions. Your voice probably does not. Go through the copy and add contractions wherever they feel natural in your voice. Add a phrase you actually say. This single edit shifts the copy from "something a model wrote" to "something a person wrote with model assistance." It is five minutes of work and the difference is significant.

## Chapter 6: Building a Prompt Library

### Organizing Prompts for Reuse

A prompt library is a folder of text files, a Notion database, or a simple spreadsheet where you store tested prompts categorized by use case. The goal is to stop rebuilding from scratch and start deploying tested prompts with minor adjustments.

**The Minimum Viable Prompt Library**

Start with three categories: landing page, email, and social. Within each category, keep one tested prompt per element type.

File structure:

```
Prompts/
  landing-page/
    hero-headline.txt
    problem-section.txt
    solution-section.txt
    social-proof.txt
    cta-section.txt
  email/
    subject-lines.txt
    preview-text.txt
    value-email.txt
    sales-email.txt
    welcome-sequence.txt
  social/
    instagram-hooks.txt
    instagram-caption.txt
    twitter-thread.txt
    linkedin-post.txt
```

**The Versioning Practice**

When a prompt produces output you are happy with, note what you changed from the base prompt and save that version. Over time your library fills with variants, not just templates. You will find that certain constraint combinations work for your voice and others do not. The variants capture what you have learned.

**The Iteration Log**

Keep a simple document or note where you log: the prompt, the output quality (good/needs work/failed), and the key change you made to improve it. This is not overhead. It is the practice that turns a generic prompt library into a competitive advantage specific to your voice and your audience.

## Chapter 7: Voice Consistency

### Training AI to Match Your Brand Voice

The model does not know your voice unless you tell it. Once you tell it well, it reproduces your voice with reasonable consistency. Here is how to do that.

**Step 1: Build a Voice Reference Document**

Collect 5-10 examples of your best-performing copy. These can be captions that got strong engagement, emails with high click rates, or sales page sections you are proud of. Paste them into a single document.

Add these voice notes to the document:
- Tone in 3 words: (example: direct, grounded, specific)
- What this writing never does: (example: never uses exclamation points, never makes vague promises, never sounds like a brand talking to a customer)
- What this writing always does: (example: leads with the reader's problem, uses short sentences, ends with one clear action)
- Vocabulary notes: (example: uses "build" not "create," uses "solopreneur" not "entrepreneur," says "write" not "craft")

**Step 2: Add the Voice Reference to Your Prompt**

At the beginning of any prompt where voice consistency matters, add:

"Before you write, review these voice examples and the voice notes below. Your output should sound like it was written by the same person who wrote these examples.

Voice examples: [paste 2-3 short examples]

Voice notes: [paste your voice notes]"

This is the fastest way to get consistent output without fine-tuning a model. It takes 30 seconds to add and measurably improves how closely the output matches your actual writing.

**Step 3: The Voice Calibration Prompt**

Run this prompt once to verify the model is calibrated correctly before a batch session:

"Using the voice examples and notes I provided, write one test paragraph in my voice about [any topic relevant to your brand]. After you write it, identify 3 specific phrases or structures in the paragraph that reflect the voice notes."

Read the output critically. If it does not sound like you, identify which voice note was not followed and refine the instruction. This calibration process, done once at the start of a writing session, prevents multiple rounds of editing caused by off-voice output.

## Chapter 8: The Copy Audit

### Reviewing AI Output Before Publishing

Never publish AI copy without a structured review. The review does not have to take long. With a clear checklist, 10-15 minutes per piece is enough to catch the issues that cause AI copy to underperform.

**The Pre-Publish Checklist**

Run every piece of AI copy through these questions before it goes live.

1. Does the hook immediately deliver on its promise? (landing page: does the hero match what brought the reader there, email: does the opening deliver what the subject line promised, social: does the first line earn the next line)

2. Is every claim specific? Find every superlative, every vague benefit, and every unsupported promise. Replace with a specific number, outcome, or example or remove the claim.

3. Is there one CTA? Count the calls to action. If there are more than one, decide which is primary and remove or subordinate the rest.

4. Does this sound like a person wrote it? Read it out loud. Any sentence that feels mechanical or overly constructed needs to be rewritten in your natural voice.

5. Are there passive constructions? Use find-and-replace to search for "was" and "were" constructions. Rewrite at least 50% of them in active voice.

6. Did you add at least one specific personal detail? This is the hardest for AI to fake and the most valuable thing you can add. A real date, a real number from your own experience, a real name, these are the details that make copy feel authentic.

7. Does the piece do one thing well? The best copy has one idea, one audience, one action. If your copy is trying to serve multiple purposes in a single piece, it is doing all of them poorly. Identify the primary purpose and edit everything else toward it.

## Appendix: Before/After Examples

### Landing Page Hero: Before and After

**Before (AI output, unedited):**
"Unlock the power of AI to transform your copywriting process and achieve unprecedented results in your business communications across all digital platforms."

**After (edited):**
"Write landing pages, emails, and social posts in 45 minutes instead of 4 hours. Without hiring a copywriter."

What changed: removed vague benefit language, added a specific time comparison, added the implied objection (not needing to hire someone), cut 18 words to 15 words.

### Email Subject Line: Before and After

**Before:** "Discover How AI Can Revolutionize Your Content Creation Strategy"

**After:** "The 4-part prompt that cut my writing time by 80%"

What changed: removed "discover" (weak opener), removed "revolutionize" (overused), added a specific mechanism (4-part prompt), added a specific result (80%), cut 10 words to 10 words but made every word carry weight.

### Instagram Caption Opening: Before and After

**Before:** "In today's competitive digital landscape, creating compelling content has never been more important for solopreneurs and freelancers looking to stand out from the crowd."

**After:** "You are posting every day and getting 11 likes per post. Here is why that is a prompting problem, not a content problem."

What changed: deleted the preamble entirely, opened with the reader's specific situation, named the real problem, created forward momentum in two sentences instead of wandering through one.

### Problem Section: Before and After

**Before:** "Many entrepreneurs struggle with finding the time to create quality content consistently while managing all the other demands of running a business."

**After:** "You know exactly what you want to say. The problem is you sit down to write it and two hours later you have a half-finished draft and three other tasks you abandoned to get there. The content is not the issue. The process is."

What changed: moved from "many entrepreneurs" to second person, replaced abstract struggle with a specific scene, added emotional specificity (the abandoned tasks), ended with a reframe that transitions to the solution.
