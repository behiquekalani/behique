# The AI Prompt Engineering Cheat Sheet

**25 Copy-Paste Templates to Get Better Results from Any AI Tool**

By Kalani | Behike

*You already use AI. This guide makes it actually work for you.*

Most people type a sentence into ChatGPT, get a mediocre answer, and assume AI is overhyped. The problem is not the tool. The problem is the prompt. This cheat sheet fixes that with practical laws, copy-paste templates, and real before/after examples you can use today.

No theory essays. No filler. Just the templates and techniques that work.

## Section 1: The 5 Laws of Good Prompts

These five principles apply to every AI tool. Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, Llama. Learn these once, use them everywhere.

### Law 1: Be Specific

Vague input = vague output. Every single time.

| Instead of this | Write this |
|----------------|-----------|
| "Write me a blog post" | "Write a 600-word blog post about meal prepping for college students on a $50/week budget" |
| "Help me with my resume" | "Rewrite this bullet point to highlight measurable results for a marketing manager role" |
| "Give me business ideas" | "Give me 5 low-investment online business ideas for someone with video editing skills and $500 to start" |

The more specific your input, the less editing you do on the output.

### Law 2: Give Context

AI does not know who you are, what you have tried before, or why you need this. Tell it.

**The context formula:**

> I am [WHO YOU ARE]. I need [WHAT YOU NEED] because [WHY YOU NEED IT]. My audience is [WHO IT IS FOR].

Example: "I am a freelance copywriter pitching a SaaS startup. I need 3 subject line options for a cold email because my current open rate is 12%. My audience is B2B marketing directors."

### Law 3: Show Format

If you want a table, show a table. If you want bullet points, say bullet points. If you want a specific structure, give an example of it.

> "Format the output as a numbered list with each item containing: the strategy name in bold, a one-sentence explanation, and a real-world example."

AI mirrors what you show it. Show it what you want.

### Law 4: Set Constraints

Without boundaries, AI will give you everything and nothing useful. Set limits.

**Useful constraints:**
- Word count: "Keep it under 150 words"
- Tone: "Write in a conversational, first-person tone"
- Audience: "Explain this to a 10-year-old"
- Format: "Use only bullet points, no paragraphs"
- Exclusions: "Do not use jargon or technical terms"

### Law 5: Iterate

The first output is a draft, not the final answer. Treat AI like a collaborator, not a vending machine.

**Iteration starters:**
- "Make this more concise"
- "This is too generic. Add specific examples from [industry]"
- "Keep the structure but rewrite the tone to be more direct"
- "Good start. Now expand on point 3 and cut points 1 and 2"

One good prompt plus two rounds of iteration beats ten separate prompts every time.

**Template 1: Blog Post Generator**

*When to use:* You need a full blog post draft on a specific topic with clear structure.

```
Write a [WORD COUNT]-word blog post about [TOPIC].

Target audience: [WHO THIS IS FOR]
Tone: [CASUAL / PROFESSIONAL / EDUCATIONAL / CONVERSATIONAL]
Goal: [WHAT THE READER SHOULD DO OR FEEL AFTER READING]

Structure:
- Hook opening (2-3 sentences that grab attention)
- 3-5 subheadings with actionable content under each
- Include at least 2 real-world examples or case studies
- End with a clear call-to-action: [DESIRED ACTION]

Do NOT use generic filler phrases like "" or "it's no secret that."
```

**Pro tip:** Add "Write the first draft, then review it and improve the weakest section" to get self-editing behavior.

*When to use:* You need scroll-stopping captions for Instagram, LinkedIn, or Twitter/X.

```
Write a [PLATFORM] caption about [TOPIC].

Account context: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION OF YOUR BRAND/ACCOUNT]
Goal: [ENGAGEMENT / SALES / AWARENESS / COMMUNITY]
Tone: [BOLD / WITTY / EDUCATIONAL / VULNERABLE]

- A strong hook in the first line (this is what shows before ".more")
- A call-to-action at the end

Do NOT sound corporate or use buzzwords like "teamwork" or "use."
```

**Pro tip:** Ask for 5 variations and pick the best one. Quantity breeds quality with captions.

## Section 3: Advanced Techniques

Once you are comfortable with the templates above, these techniques will push your results further.

### Chain of Thought

Tell the AI to think before it answers.

> "Think through this step by step before giving your final answer."

This works especially well for math, logic, strategy, and debugging. It forces the AI to show its reasoning instead of jumping to a conclusion.

**When to use:** Complex problems, analysis, anything where the "why" matters as much as the "what."

### Role Assignment

Give the AI an identity and expertise level.

> "You are a senior marketing strategist with 15 years of experience in direct-to-consumer eCommerce."

The output changes dramatically based on the role. A "senior strategist" gives different advice than a "helpful assistant." Be specific about the expertise level and domain.

**When to use:** Any time you want expert-level output instead of generic answers.

### Few-Shot Examples

Show the AI 2-3 examples of what you want before asking it to generate.

> "Here are 3 examples of the writing style I want:
> 1. [EXAMPLE 1]
> 2. [EXAMPLE 2]
> 3. [EXAMPLE 3]
> Now write 5 more in this same style about [TOPIC]."

This is the single most powerful technique for controlling output quality and style.

**When to use:** Creative writing, brand voice matching, any task where "tone" matters.

### Negative Prompting

Tell the AI what NOT to do.

> "Do NOT use bullet points. Do NOT include an introduction paragraph. Do NOT use words like 'innovative,' 'new,' or 'use.'"

AI tools have default patterns they fall back on. Negative prompting breaks those patterns and forces original output.

**When to use:** When you keep getting the same generic output, or when you have specific pet peeves about AI writing.

### Temperature Guidance

Most AI tools let you adjust "temperature" (creativity vs. precision). Even when you cannot change the setting directly, you can guide it with words.

| You want | Say this |
|----------|----------|
| Creative, varied output | "Be creative. Surprise me. Try unconventional approaches." |
| Precise, factual output | "Be precise. Stick to facts. Do not speculate." |
| Balanced | "Be clear and accurate, but do not be afraid to suggest something unexpected." |

**When to use:** Always. This is subtle but it shapes every response.

### Example 1: Blog Post

**Bad prompt:**
> "Write a blog post about productivity."

**Good prompt:**
> "Write a 500-word blog post titled '3 Productivity Systems That Actually Work for People with ADHD.' Target audience: college students and young professionals. Tone: conversational, like a friend sharing what worked for them. Include personal anecdotes (you can make them up). End with a one-sentence takeaway. Do NOT mention 'hustle culture' or generic advice like 'wake up at 5am.'"

**Why it is better:** The good prompt defines the angle, audience, tone, length, structure, and exclusions. The AI cannot give a generic answer because every generic path is blocked.

### Example 2: Email Subject Lines

**Bad prompt:**
> "Write email subject lines for my product."

**Good prompt:**
> "Write 10 email subject lines for a $29 digital course about freelance pricing. Audience: freelancers making under $50k who undercharge. Mix of styles: 3 curiosity-based, 3 benefit-driven, 2 social proof, 2 urgency. Max 45 characters each. Do NOT use all caps or exclamation marks."

**Why it is better:** Categories force variety. Character limits force brevity. Constraints on style prevent the AI from defaulting to clickbait.

### Example 3: Code Help

**Bad prompt:**
> "My code doesn't work, fix it."

**Good prompt:**
> "I have a Python FastAPI endpoint that should return a list of users filtered by role. Instead, it returns all users regardless of the role parameter. I have verified the role parameter is being received correctly (I printed it). The issue seems to be in the database query. Here is the endpoint code and the SQLAlchemy model. Explain what is wrong and show the fix."

**Why it is better:** The good prompt includes the expected behavior, actual behavior, what was already debugged, the suspected area of the problem, and asks for explanation plus fix.

### Example 4: Social Media Content

**Bad prompt:**
> "Write an Instagram caption about my new product."

**Good prompt:**
> "Write an Instagram caption announcing a hand-poured soy candle collection (3 scents: ocean breeze, cedar + sage, midnight vanilla). Brand is minimalist, coastal, calm. Target audience: women 25-40 who value self-care rituals. Start with a hook that does NOT use 'introducing' or '.' Include a question to drive comments. 4-6 relevant hashtags. Keep it under 150 words."

**Why it is better:** Specific product details, defined brand identity, clear audience, format constraints, and explicit things to avoid. The AI has enough to write something that sounds like the brand, not like a generic template.

### Example 5: Business Strategy

**Bad prompt:**
> "How do I grow my business?"

**Good prompt:**
> "I run a one-person web design agency making $4k/month from 3 retainer clients. I want to reach $8k/month within 6 months without hiring. My skills: Webflow, basic SEO, brand strategy. I have 5 hours/week available beyond client work. Give me 3 specific growth strategies ranked by effort-to-impact ratio. For each strategy, include: what to do in week 1, expected timeline to results, and the biggest risk. Do NOT suggest 'post more on social media' as a standalone strategy."

**Why it is better:** Real numbers, real constraints, specific skills, clear timeline, and a filter for low-effort suggestions. The AI gives actionable advice instead of motivational posters.
