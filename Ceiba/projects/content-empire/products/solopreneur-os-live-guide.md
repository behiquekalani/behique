## Introduction

Most solopreneur systems are static. You buy a workbook, read it once, fill out a few pages, and set it on a shelf. Six weeks later you are back where you started, with nothing to show for the intention.

Solopreneur OS Live is different because it is a dashboard, not a document. You open it every morning the way you open email. It remembers what you did yesterday. It shows you where you are in each of the 13 modules. It holds your inputs, tracks your streaks, and reflects your current state, not your optimistic past state.

This guide covers each of the 13 modules in detail: what it is, what data it takes, how you interact with it each week, and what a good output looks like.

Use this guide as a reference when you are stuck on a module, not as a tutorial to read front to back. Go to the module you need. Do the work. Come back when the next question appears.

## Module 1: Vision Builder

### What it is

The Vision Builder is the foundation layer of the entire system. Every other module connects back to it. It is where you define what you are building and why, at a level of specificity that is actually useful.

Most solopreneurs operate with a vague direction: "I want to build a business around content." The Vision Builder forces a sharper answer: who you serve, what transformation you help them accomplish, what your business looks like in 12 months, and what you are willing to give up to get there.

### What inputs it requires

- A one-sentence description of who you help and how (your positioning statement)
- A 12-month outcome in concrete terms (revenue, audience size, product count, hours per week)
- Three values that must be true about how you operate (e.g., no paid ads, ship weekly, stay profitable)
- One thing you are committing to stop doing in order to make space for this

### What it tracks

The module holds your positioning statement as a reference anchor. Every other module displays it at the top so you can check whether what you are building still matches what you said you wanted. It also tracks how many times you have edited your vision, because frequent edits are a signal, not a problem. They mean you are getting clearer.

### Weekly interaction

Once set, the Vision Builder is not a module you update daily. It is a module you review. Once per week, spend two minutes reading your positioning statement before you start working. Note whether what you are about to do that week connects to it. If it does not, that is data.

Every 30 days, the module prompts you to answer one question: "Is this still what I want?" A yes means you continue. A no triggers a guided re-entry where you update the statement without losing the history.

### What a good output looks like

A complete Vision Builder module produces a positioning statement that a stranger could read and immediately understand: who you are, who you serve, and what you do for them. It also produces a 12-month target that is specific enough to reverse-engineer, and a constraints list that tells you what you are not doing so you can stay focused.

## Module 2: Domain Stack

### What it is

The Domain Stack is a map of your expertise. Most solopreneurs undersell themselves because they see their knowledge as scattered. The Domain Stack organizes what you know into layers: the core skill that produces money, the supporting skills that make the core valuable, and the context skills that no one else in your niche has in exactly your combination.

The concept comes from the idea that your edge is not your single best skill. Your edge is the intersection of three to five skills that rarely appear together. A developer who understands marketing and has lived experience in a specific industry is not competing with developers. That person is competing in a category of one.

### What inputs it requires

- Your top skill (the thing people would pay you for directly)
- Two to four supporting skills (things that make the top skill better or more accessible)
- One or two context advantages (lived experience, language, location, industry background)
- The niche or audience where this combination is most valuable

### What it tracks

The module generates a visual stack showing how your skills layer. It tracks which skills you are actively building versus which ones are already proven. Each skill has a confidence level (1 to 5) and a last-used date, so you can see if a skill is getting rusty or if you are developing it through current work.

It also generates a one-sentence niche statement from your inputs: "I help [audience] achieve [outcome] using [skill combination]." This feeds directly into Module 1 (Vision Builder) and Module 3 (Content Engine).

### Weekly interaction

Review your domain stack when you are deciding what content to create or what services to offer. The stack should answer the question: "Does this use my actual edge, or am I creating generic content?"

Once per month, update confidence levels based on whether you used each skill in paid work. Skills you are not deploying are not your edge. They are your hobby.

### What a good output looks like

A complete Domain Stack shows a clear core skill, two to three supporting skills with honest confidence ratings, and a niche statement specific enough to exclude people. If your niche statement describes more than 20 percent of solopreneurs, it is too broad.

## Module 3: Content Engine

### What it is

The Content Engine is your weekly content system. It translates what you know (Domain Stack) into content that reaches the people you want to serve (Vision Builder) and builds the audience that buys your products (Monetization Map).

The module is not a content calendar. A content calendar tells you when to post. The Content Engine tells you what to say and why. It operates from a single principle: every piece of content should teach something real from your direct experience or research, and it should make the reader or viewer want the next piece.

### What inputs it requires

- Your primary platform (where your audience already is or where you are building)
- Your secondary platform (repurposing destination)
- Three content pillars (recurring themes drawn from your Domain Stack)
- Your posting frequency (realistic, not aspirational)
- A content bank: a list of questions your audience asks, problems they face, and beliefs they hold that are wrong

### What it tracks

The Content Engine tracks posts created versus posts published for the current week. It also tracks which pillar each post came from, so you can see if you are over-indexing on one theme. Over time it shows your consistency rate: percentage of weeks where you hit your stated posting frequency.

The consistency rate is the only metric that matters in the first 90 days. Not views, not followers.

### Weekly interaction

Each Monday, open the Content Engine. It shows the current week's posting slots. Fill in one topic per slot from your content bank. Topics come from real questions, not from what you think sounds smart.

Each Friday, mark posts as published. If you missed a slot, note why. The module tracks your reasons over time so you can identify whether the bottleneck is ideas, time, or confidence.

### What a good output looks like

After four weeks, your Content Engine should show at least one completed cycle of each pillar, a consistency rate you can honestly sustain, and a content bank with at least 20 unused topics. The bank depth is the buffer that keeps you from missing weeks.

## Module 4: Monetization Map

### What it is

The Monetization Map is a four-layer revenue structure built around your domain and audience. The four layers are: free (lead generation), low-ticket (under $30, trust products), mid-ticket (under $200, transformation products), and high-ticket (service or community, above $500).

Most solopreneurs either have only one product or jump straight to a $2,000 course before building any audience. The Monetization Map solves both errors by showing your current revenue ladder and the gaps in it.

### What inputs it requires

- Each existing product or service (name, price, format, current monthly units sold)
- Each planned product (name, estimated price, status: idea, in progress, or launched)
- Your current free offer (lead magnet or free content that brings people into your world)
- The primary problem each product solves and which layer it belongs to

### What it tracks

The map visualizes your four-layer ladder and marks which layers are filled, which are empty, and which are generating revenue. It calculates your estimated monthly revenue from the units-sold inputs and shows the gap between current revenue and the 12-month target you set in Module 1.

It also shows customer flow logic: if someone buys your low-ticket product, what is the natural next step toward your mid-ticket product? If that path is unclear, the map flags it.

### Weekly interaction

You do not need to update the Monetization Map weekly. Update it when a product launches, when you change a price, or when you add a new offer. The map is a reference, not a tracking sheet.

Review it monthly alongside your Financial Pulse (Module 6). Together they answer the question: "Is my revenue structure working, or is all my money coming from one layer?"

### What a good output looks like

A filled Monetization Map shows at least one product in the free layer and one in the low-ticket layer. Mid-ticket and high-ticket can be planned but not yet launched. The customer flow arrows should be legible without explanation: if a new customer lands in your world, the path to each successive product should be obvious.

## Module 5: Daily Operations

### What it is

Daily Operations is your morning structure. It is the module you open first, every day, before you open anything else. It holds your task structure for the day, your morning routine checklist, and a single question that frames your work session.

The philosophy is simple. Most solopreneurs make decisions about what to work on while already distracted. By the time they choose a task, they have read 40 emails and are in reactive mode. Daily Operations forces a decision before the noise starts.

### What inputs it requires

- Your non-negotiable morning routine items (max 5, with estimated minutes)
- Your three priority slots for the day (one per energy block: high, medium, low)
- Your current 90-day sprint goal (pulled from Module 12: Quarterly Review)
- Yesterday's incomplete tasks (automatically carried forward from the previous session)

### What it tracks

Daily Operations tracks completion rates for your morning routine and your three daily priorities. It also tracks your streak: how many consecutive days you opened the module before starting work. The streak is not a gamification mechanic. It is a measure of whether you are running your business or your business is running you.

Incomplete tasks are never deleted. They are carried forward and counted. After 3 carry-forwards, the module flags the task for review: is this actually important, or is it something you are avoiding?

### Weekly interaction

Every Sunday or Monday, set the week's three themes. Each day's priorities should connect to one of the three themes. This prevents the situation where you are productive every day but working on things that do not compound.

### What a good output looks like

A Daily Operations record after 30 days shows a morning routine completion rate above 70 percent, a task completion rate you can honestly defend, and no single task carrying forward more than 3 times without a decision. If something has been on your list for 2 weeks, either do it in the next session or delete it with intention.

## Module 6: Financial Pulse

### What it is

The Financial Pulse is your monthly revenue tracking layer. It is not accounting software. It does not replace a spreadsheet or a bookkeeper. What it does is give you a single view of your business revenue in the context of your goal, updated monthly, without requiring a finance degree to interpret.

The key insight behind this module is that most solopreneurs either obsess over daily revenue (which creates anxiety) or ignore it entirely (which creates surprises). Monthly tracking is the right cadence for a content-driven business where revenue compounds over 90-day cycles.

### What inputs it requires

- Monthly revenue by source (each product or service line, separate entries)
- Monthly expenses in four categories: tools, platforms, ads, and contractors
- Your monthly baseline (the amount you need to cover personal expenses)
- Your monthly target (from Module 1's 12-month goal, broken into months)

### What it tracks

The module shows your revenue trend over the last 6 months as a simple line, not a complex chart. It shows your gap between current monthly average and your target. It calculates your net margin and flags months where expenses grew faster than revenue.

It also shows your revenue concentration: what percentage of your total revenue comes from your single largest source. If that number is above 70 percent, the module flags it as a single-point-of-failure risk.

### Weekly interaction

Financial Pulse is updated once per month, ideally on the first of the month. The input takes about 10 minutes. After entering your numbers, the module generates a one-paragraph summary: what changed, what the trend shows, and one question to consider for the next month.

### What a good output looks like

After 6 months of entries, your Financial Pulse should show a clear revenue trend, a declining concentration risk (you have built at least two meaningful revenue sources), and monthly expenses that are proportional to revenue. If your expenses are flat and revenue is growing, that is the right shape.

## Module 7: Audience Builder

### What it is

The Audience Builder tracks your growth on each platform where you are active, in a way that separates vanity metrics from useful ones. Most platform analytics show you numbers that feel important but do not predict revenue. The Audience Builder shows you the metrics that do.

The module operates from one premise: the audience metric that matters most is not total followers. It is the percentage of your content that converts a first-time viewer into someone who follows, subscribes, or joins your list. That ratio tells you whether your content is doing its job.

### What inputs it requires

- Your active platforms (up to 4)
- Monthly follower or subscriber count per platform
- Monthly new email subscribers (if applicable)
- Two to three specific content pieces per month that performed above average
- Your best-performing content format per platform (short video, text post, long-form, etc.)

### What it tracks

The module shows a platform-by-platform growth table: current count, last month's count, month-over-month change, and a trend indicator. It also shows your email list as a separate priority metric, because the email list is the one asset you own.

The "top content" inputs feed a pattern log: over time you will see which formats and topics consistently outperform. This log feeds directly into Module 3 (Content Engine) so you can produce more of what works.

### Weekly interaction

Update the Audience Builder once per month. Between monthly updates, note any content piece that dramatically outperforms your average. Flag it for the monthly review so you can analyze what made it land.

### What a good output looks like

After 6 months, your Audience Builder should show clear growth on at least one platform, a growing email list (even if small), and a pattern log with 3 to 5 repeating themes about what works. The pattern log is worth more than the follower count.

## Module 8: Product Lab

### What it is

The Product Lab is your idea-to-launch pipeline. It is where a product concept goes to get tested before you invest significant time in it. The module applies a simple filter to every idea: does this solve a specific problem for a specific person, and is there evidence that person wants it solved?

The Product Lab does not care about originality. It cares about demand. A product that is similar to something that already sells is a safer bet than a product that has never existed, because the second type requires you to educate the market before you can sell to it.

### What inputs it requires

- Product name and one-sentence description
- Target buyer (specific person, not demographic)
- Problem it solves (phrased the way the buyer would phrase it)
- Evidence of demand (searches, forum posts, competitor sales, direct conversations)
- Format (PDF, web app, video course, template, service)
- Estimated price and time to build

### What it tracks

Each product idea moves through five stages: Idea, Validated, In Production, Launched, and Active. The module tracks how long each idea has been in each stage. Ideas that sit in Validated for more than 30 days without moving to In Production get flagged. You either build them or archive them.

The module also tracks your launch-to-revenue ratio: of all the products you have launched, what percentage have sold at least 10 units? This ratio tells you whether your validation process is working.

### Weekly interaction

Review the Product Lab once per week, for about 10 minutes. Move any idea that has new evidence or new work done. Archive ideas that you have not thought about in 30 days. The goal is to have no more than two ideas in the Validated stage at any time. More than two means you are collecting ideas instead of building products.

### What a good output looks like

A working Product Lab has one product per stage at most. One idea stage, one being validated, one in production, and two to three active products generating revenue. The pipeline is narrow by design. Width produces half-finished projects.

## Module 9: Client Tracker

### What it is

The Client Tracker is the service business layer. If any portion of your revenue comes from working directly with clients (consulting, writing, design, automation, coaching), this module holds that work. It tracks active clients, deliverables, billing status, and the pattern of which clients lead to referrals.

The module is designed around the reality that service revenue is high-margin but high-context. You carry a lot of information in your head about each client, and when that information is in your head instead of on a screen, things fall through. Deliverables are late. Follow-ups are missed. Good clients leave because they felt forgotten.

### What inputs it requires

- Client name and project description
- Engagement type (one-time or retainer)
- Deliverable list with due dates
- Monthly or project billing amount and payment status
- A notes field for anything the client mentioned that you want to remember

### What it tracks

The Client Tracker shows a dashboard of active engagements with a status indicator for each deliverable: not started, in progress, complete, or overdue. It tracks your active monthly recurring revenue from retainer clients separately from one-time project revenue.

It also tracks your client acquisition source: how did each client find you? Over time this shows which content, referral sources, or outreach channels actually produce client work.

### Weekly interaction

Every Monday, open the Client Tracker before Daily Operations. Scan for anything overdue. Move any completed deliverables to done. Note any client who has been quiet for more than 2 weeks, because silence often means the engagement is ending and you want to know before it surprises you.

### What a good output looks like

A well-maintained Client Tracker has no overdue deliverables that are more than 3 days old, a clear picture of incoming revenue for the next 30 days, and at least one column showing client acquisition source. That source column will tell you more about where to invest your marketing time than any analytics dashboard.

## Module 10: Skills Accelerator

### What it is

The Skills Accelerator is your deliberate practice log. Solopreneurs often confuse staying busy with getting better. Busy means you are executing the skills you already have. Better means you are extending your range into skills that will be valuable in 6 to 12 months.

The module applies a concept from performance research: skills improve fastest when practice is deliberate (focused on a specific weakness with feedback) rather than habitual (doing what you already know how to do). The Skills Accelerator structures your practice so that a percentage of your work each week is always pointed at growth, not just execution.

### What inputs it requires

- Your current active skill (one per quarter, from your Domain Stack)
- A specific gap within that skill (not "get better at video" but "cut better transitions in CapCut")
- A weekly practice commitment in minutes (realistic, not aspirational)
- A feedback source (a person, a metric, or a quality benchmark you can compare your work against)
- An example of excellent work in this skill that you are studying

### What it tracks

The Skills Accelerator logs your weekly practice sessions: date, duration, what you worked on, and one observation from the session. Over time the observation log becomes your own manual for the skill, written in your voice from your actual attempts.

It also tracks your feedback loop: did you get feedback this week? Feedback can be a mentor comment, an analytics result (did the video with the better transitions get more watch time?), or a self-review against your quality benchmark. Without feedback, practice is repetition, not improvement.

### Weekly interaction

Two sessions per week is the target. Each session is 25 to 45 minutes. The session starts with reviewing last week's observation, then working on the specific gap, then writing one observation at the end. Total overhead: about 5 minutes. The rest is work.

### What a good output looks like

After one quarter of tracking, your Skills Accelerator shows a specific skill with documented progress, a series of observations that show a learning curve, and at least one feedback event per week. The quality benchmark you chose at the start should be measurably closer than when you started.

## Module 11: Energy Manager

### What it is

The Energy Manager is the ADHD-aware capacity planning layer of the system. It exists because standard productivity advice assumes that effort is a fixed, renewable resource. For people with ADHD or variable energy, that assumption breaks. You can do everything right on Monday and have nothing left by Wednesday.

The Energy Manager does not fight this reality. It works with it. The goal is to build a weekly structure that accounts for your actual energy pattern, not the energy pattern you wish you had.

### What inputs it requires

- Your typical high-energy window (time of day when deep work feels possible)
- Your typical low-energy window (time of day when only administrative or passive tasks are feasible)
- Your non-negotiables for the week (appointments, obligations, deadlines)
- Your energy score each day: a 1-to-5 rating entered in the morning before you start work
- Any factors that reliably change your energy: sleep quality, exercise, social events, stress

### What it tracks

The Energy Manager builds a rolling 30-day energy log from your daily scores. Over 30 days a pattern emerges: which days of the week are consistently high-energy, which are consistently low, and which factors correlate with crashes. This pattern is the input for your scheduling decisions.

It also generates a weekly capacity estimate: based on your average energy score for this week, how many high-focus hours are realistic? This number feeds into Daily Operations (Module 5) to calibrate how many priorities you set each day.

### Weekly interaction

Each morning: enter your energy score and note any relevant factors. Takes 30 seconds.

Each Sunday: review the week's log and set next week's schedule around your predicted energy pattern. Schedule your most important work during your historically high-energy windows. Schedule email, admin, and passive tasks during low-energy windows.

### What a good output looks like

After 60 days, the Energy Manager shows a clear weekly energy pattern, at least two identified factors that reliably help or hurt your capacity, and a scheduling practice that matches your priorities to your energy rather than to a calendar convention. The output is a more honest work week, not a more productive one in the conventional sense.

## Module 12: Quarterly Review

### What it is

The Quarterly Review is the 90-day cycle system. It replaces annual planning, which is too abstract and too far away to drive daily behavior. Ninety days is long enough to produce real results, short enough to stay honest, and frequent enough to correct course before a mistake becomes a pattern.

Each quarter has three phases: set (define the 90-day goal and the key milestones), execute (run Daily Operations and update the other modules week by week), and review (a structured retrospective that feeds into the next quarter's set).

### What inputs it requires

- One primary goal for the quarter (specific, with a measurable outcome)
- Three milestones that mark progress toward the goal
- Two to three risks that could prevent you from hitting the goal
- A weekly check-in commitment (which day, which time)
- At the end: what was accomplished, what was missed, what will change next quarter

### What it tracks

The Quarterly Review tracks goal progress against milestones, the weekly check-in streak (did you review your quarter goal each week?), and the post-quarter retrospective over time. After four quarters you have a year of retrospectives, which shows your actual growth rate and the patterns in what slows you down.

The module also calculates a "quarterly batting average": out of all the primary goals you have set across quarters, what percentage did you hit? The honest answer to this question tells you whether your quarterly planning is calibrated or optimistic.

### Weekly interaction

One weekly check-in: are you on track for each milestone? If not, what changed and what is the adjusted plan? This takes about 10 minutes and should be done at the same time each week. Consistency in the check-in matters more than the content of the check-in.

### What a good output looks like

A working Quarterly Review system produces one completed retrospective every 90 days. After two quarters, your goals become more accurate (you learn your actual capacity). After four quarters, you have a clear picture of your growth rate and can plan forward with confidence rather than optimism.

## Module 13: Legacy Builder

### What it is

The Legacy Builder is the long-term vision layer. It operates on a 3-to-10 year horizon, which means most of its inputs are directional, not specific. It exists because most solopreneur planning is entirely tactical: what to build, what to sell, how to grow the numbers. Legacy Builder asks the question that tactics cannot answer: what do you want to have built by the time the business is done?

This is the module that most people skip. That is exactly why it is here.

### What inputs it requires

- A description of what your life looks like at your target horizon (not your business, your life)
- The people you want your work to have served
- The knowledge or capability you want to have passed on, whether through products, writing, or direct relationships
- The constraints you will not compromise on: what you will not do for money, attention, or growth
- A definition of what "done" means to you, which is the prerequisite for knowing when to stop

### What it tracks

The Legacy Builder is not a tracking module in the conventional sense. It holds your long-term inputs as a reference layer that all other modules can be checked against. When you are in Module 4 deciding whether to build a new product, you can open Legacy Builder and ask: does this product fit the kind of business I said I was building?

It also tracks how often you have revisited the module. Revisits are valuable because they show whether your long-term vision is stable or shifting. Frequent shifts early on are normal. Frequent shifts after 12 months of business operation are a signal worth examining.

### Weekly interaction

There is no weekly interaction required for Legacy Builder. Open it once per quarter, during your Quarterly Review. Read your long-term inputs. Note whether anything has changed. If it has, update with intention, not in response to a bad week.

### What a good output looks like

A complete Legacy Builder entry is one you could read 5 years from now and recognize yourself in it. It is specific enough to be meaningful but not so prescriptive that it becomes a cage. The test: if you hit every goal in your other 12 modules but failed the Legacy Builder vision, would that feel like success? If the answer is no, the Legacy Builder is doing its job.

## How the Modules Connect

The 13 modules are not independent. They are a system. Here is how they flow:

**Foundation layer (Modules 1, 2, 13):** Vision Builder, Domain Stack, and Legacy Builder define who you are, what your edge is, and where you are going long-term. Set these first. Return to them quarterly.

**Production layer (Modules 3, 8, 10):** Content Engine, Product Lab, and Skills Accelerator are where you make things. These are updated weekly. They compound over time.

**Revenue layer (Modules 4, 6, 9):** Monetization Map, Financial Pulse, and Client Tracker are where money flows. These require monthly maintenance and honest inputs.

**Audience layer (Module 7):** Audience Builder sits between production and revenue. Content produces audience. Audience produces buyers.

**Operations layer (Modules 5, 11, 12):** Daily Operations, Energy Manager, and Quarterly Review are the cadence system. They determine whether everything else actually happens.

The system works when all 13 modules are maintained. Ignoring one module does not break the others immediately. But after 90 days, the gaps show up in your results.
