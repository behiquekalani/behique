## HOW TO USE THESE TEMPLATES

Each template is a prompt you paste into Claude, ChatGPT, or your Ghost Writer n8n workflow.

Replace every [BRACKETED SECTION] with your actual content. Do not leave brackets in the final prompt.

Before using any template, paste your full Voice Bible (the completed template from this kit) as the first block of your conversation. This is the context that makes these prompts work.

The templates are designed to be used after your Voice Bible is loaded. Without the Voice Bible, they produce generic output.

## TEMPLATE 1: X / Twitter Single Tweet

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write a single tweet on this topic:

Topic: [YOUR TOPIC]

Requirements:
- 280 characters maximum
- Starts with a hook (specific claim or counterintuitive statement)
- No banned words from my Voice Bible
- No exclamation marks
- Ends with either a period, a question, or nothing

Do not add hashtags unless I ask. Write 3 versions. I will pick one.
```

## TEMPLATE 2: X / Twitter Thread (6-8 tweets)

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write a Twitter thread about:

Topic: [YOUR TOPIC]
Main argument: [THE ONE POINT YOU WANT TO MAKE]
Target reader: [WHO THIS IS FOR]

Thread structure:
- Tweet 1: Hook. State the most specific or counterintuitive claim.
- Tweets 2-5: One supporting point each. State the point first, then one sentence of evidence or example.
- Tweet 6: The practical implication. What the reader can do with this.
- Tweet 7: CTA. Link to [YOUR PRODUCT/POST/PROFILE] or ask a question.

Rules:
- Each tweet under 280 characters
- No banned words from my Voice Bible
- No exclamation marks
- Thesis first in every tweet
```

## TEMPLATE 3: Instagram Caption (Single Post)

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write an Instagram caption about:

Topic: [YOUR TOPIC]
Key insight: [THE ONE THING YOU WANT READERS TO TAKE AWAY]
What you want them to do: [FOLLOW / VISIT LINK / SAVE / COMMENT]

Caption structure:
- Line 1: Hook. One sentence. Specific. No filler.
- Paragraph 1: The problem or context (2-3 sentences)
- Paragraph 2: The insight or shift (2-3 sentences)
- Paragraph 3: The practical application (2-3 sentences)
- Final line: CTA or question (1 sentence)
- Hashtags: 5-8, in the style of [YOUR NICHE]

Length: 150-300 words total.
Rules: No banned words from my Voice Bible. No exclamation marks.
```

## TEMPLATE 4: Newsletter Opening (First 150 Words)

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write the opening of a newsletter issue.

Issue topic: [YOUR TOPIC]
The main thing I want readers to feel at the end of this opening: [CURIOUS / SEEN / MOTIVATED / CHALLENGED]
One personal detail to anchor it: [A REAL MOMENT, OBSERVATION, OR EXPERIENCE FROM YOUR LIFE]

Opening structure:
- Paragraph 1: Personal moment or observation (2-3 sentences, first person, specific)
- Paragraph 2: The connection to the main topic (1-2 sentences, thesis first)
- Paragraph 3: The promise for this issue (what the reader will have or know by the end)

Rules:
- Under 150 words total
- No "In today's issue" opener
- No "Hope you're having a great week" opener
- No banned words from my Voice Bible
- Sounds like a letter to one person, not a broadcast
```

## TEMPLATE 5: Newsletter Body Section

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write one body section of a newsletter.

Section topic: [THE SPECIFIC POINT FOR THIS SECTION]
The reader's assumed knowledge level on this topic: [BEGINNER / INTERMEDIATE / ALREADY AWARE OF BASICS]
One concrete example or story to include: [YOUR REAL EXAMPLE]

Section structure:
- Open with the main point (thesis first)
- Explain in 2-3 sentences
- Give the concrete example (2-4 sentences)
- One-sentence practical implication

Length: 150-250 words.
Rules: No banned words. No exclamation marks. Paragraph breaks every 2-3 sentences.
```

## TEMPLATE 6: YouTube Script Intro (First 60 Seconds)

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write the first 60 seconds of a YouTube script.

Video topic: [YOUR VIDEO TOPIC]
The main question or tension this video resolves: [THE CORE TENSION]
Who is watching: [SPECIFIC VIEWER PROFILE]

Script structure:
- Seconds 0-10: Hook. State a specific problem, stat, or counterintuitive claim. Do NOT say "In this video I'm going to show you..."
- Seconds 10-30: Context. Why this matters to the viewer right now. One sentence.
- Seconds 30-50: Credibility. Why you. One specific thing (not a general "I've been doing this for X years").
- Seconds 50-60: The premise. What you discovered, built, or figured out that changes the answer to the core question.

Rules:
- Write for spoken delivery (contractions are fine here if you use them)
- No banned words from my Voice Bible
- No exclamation marks
- Approximately 140-170 words (60 seconds at average speaking pace)
```

## TEMPLATE 7: YouTube Script Body Section (5-7 Minutes)

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write one main section of a YouTube script.

Section title: [TITLE OF THIS SECTION]
Main point: [THE ONE ARGUMENT OF THIS SECTION]
Supporting evidence or story: [YOUR REAL EXAMPLE, STAT, OR STORY]
Transition to next section: [WHAT COMES AFTER THIS SECTION]

Structure:
- Open with section title stated naturally (not "Now let's talk about...")
- State the main point in the first 2-3 sentences
- Develop it with your evidence/story (2-3 minutes of spoken content)
- One-sentence payoff
- Transition: "Next, [setup for following section]."

Length: 400-500 words (approximately 2-3 minutes of spoken content at normal pace).
Rules: No banned words. No exclamation marks. Write for spoken delivery.
```

## TEMPLATE 8: LinkedIn Post

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write a LinkedIn post about:

Topic: [YOUR TOPIC]
The specific insight: [ONE NON-OBVIOUS THING]
My professional context: [WHAT I DO / WHAT MAKES ME CREDIBLE ON THIS TOPIC]

Post structure:
- Line 1: Hook. Specific and bold. No "Excited to announce" openers.
- Lines 2-4: The context or problem (blank line between paragraphs)
- Lines 5-8: The insight, with one specific example
- Lines 9-11: The practical implication for the reader's work
- Final line: CTA or genuine question (not "What do you think?")

Length: 200-350 words.
Tone note: Slightly more professional than Instagram, but still direct. Not formal. Not corporate.
Rules: No banned words. No exclamation marks. No hashtags unless I ask.
```

## TEMPLATE 9: Blog Post Section (400-600 Words)

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write one section of a blog post.

Section heading: [H2 OR H3 HEADING]
Main argument of this section: [THE POINT]
Where this section falls in the article: [INTRO / MIDDLE / CONCLUSION]
One example or case study to include: [YOUR EXAMPLE]

Structure:
- Open with the main argument stated as a declarative sentence
- Develop the argument in 2-3 paragraphs
- Include the example as its own paragraph or integrated naturally
- Close with a one-sentence summary or transition

Rules:
- 400-600 words
- Paragraphs: 2-4 sentences each
- No passive voice where active is possible
- No banned words. No exclamation marks.
- Write for someone who scans before reading fully — make the first sentence of every paragraph the key point
```

## TEMPLATE 10: Cold Outreach / First Message

```
Using the voice rules in my Voice Bible above, write a first outreach message.

Who I am reaching out to: [DESCRIPTION — not the name, the profile/type]
What I want: [COLLABORATION / INTRO CALL / FEEDBACK / PARTNERSHIP]
One specific reason I am reaching out to THIS person specifically: [REAL SPECIFIC DETAIL]
My one-sentence value proposition: [WHAT I BRING]

Message structure:
- Sentence 1: Specific acknowledgment of their work (real detail, not "I love your content")
- Sentence 2: One-sentence connection or shared context
- Sentence 3-4: What I am doing and why it is relevant to them
- Sentence 5: The ask. Direct. One specific request, not "let me know if you want to connect sometime."

Length: Under 100 words.
Rules: No "Hope this message finds you well." No "I am reaching out because." No banned words. Direct and specific.
```

## TIPS FOR BETTER OUTPUTS

**Tip 1:** The more specific your topic input, the more specific the output. "How to be productive" produces generic content. "Why I do my first 90 minutes without Slack open" produces specific content.

**Tip 2:** Always load your Voice Bible first, then use these templates. Without the Voice Bible, the output is generic.

**Tip 3:** If the output is close but not quite right, use this follow-up prompt:
```
The draft is close but [SPECIFIC PROBLEM — e.g., "the opening is too generic" or "the sentences are too long"]. Revise only that element while keeping everything else the same.
```

**Tip 4:** Run every draft through the Voice Audit Checklist before publishing.
