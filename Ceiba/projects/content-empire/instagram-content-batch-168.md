## Post 2 — The Tool I Was Sleeping on That Changed My Output
**Hook:** I avoided Claude for months because I thought I already had ChatGPT. That was a mistake.
**Slide 1:** I avoided Claude for months because I thought I already had ChatGPT. That was a mistake.
**Slide 2:** What changed my mind: I gave Claude a 50-page document to analyze and it read the whole thing, cited specific passages, and gave me a structured summary in under a minute.
**Slide 3:** For a content business, this matters because the research phase is usually the slowest part. Synthesizing sources, finding the angle, structuring the argument. Claude does all of that faster.
**Slide 4:** Where I now use Claude over other tools: long-form analysis, product research, building content outlines, writing product descriptions, and reviewing my own drafts.
**Slide 5:** The key distinction: I am not using it to replace my thinking. I am using it as a first-pass thinking partner so that when I sit down to write, I already know what I want to say.
**Slide 6:** The output quality of your AI tool depends on how you use it. Follow @behikeai for the prompt system.
**Caption:** There is a version of AI tool use that makes you slower because you spend more time prompting than you would have spent just doing the task. And there is a version that makes you significantly faster because you learned to use the tool in the right contexts. The difference is knowing what kinds of thinking AI accelerates and what kinds it actually slows down. AI is excellent at synthesis, structure, first drafts, and research. It is less good at judgment calls, audience intuition, and creative direction. Keep those for yourself. Offload the synthesis. That is the workflow that actually compounds.

## Post 3 — The One Design Tool That Does Everything I Need
**Hook:** I replaced Canva, Adobe, and three other tools with one. Here is why.
**Slide 1:** I replaced Canva, Adobe, and three other tools with one. Here is why.
**Slide 2:** For a solo content creator, design tool sprawl is real. You have a template here, a brand kit there, assets scattered across platforms.
**Slide 3:** The consolidation: everything goes through Canva Pro. Not because Canva is the most powerful design tool. Because it is the most efficient for someone who is not a designer.
**Slide 4:** The actual value: brand kit in one place, resizable templates, direct publish to social platforms, PDF export for products, and team collaboration when I work with a contractor.
**Slide 5:** The honest tradeoff: I lose some design flexibility. I gain speed, consistency, and fewer places to manage files. For a one-person operation, that trade is worth it every time.
**Slide 6:** The goal is output, not tooling. Follow @behikeai for the full content production workflow.
**Caption:** At a certain point in building a solo business, you realize that having the best tool and having the right tool are not the same thing. The best tool for design is probably Figma with custom components and a proper design system. The right tool for a solo creator who needs to produce 15 pieces of content per week is whatever gets from idea to published the fastest with consistent brand output. For me that is Canva. I am not a designer. I am a content operator. I need templates, brand consistency, and speed. Canva delivers all three without requiring me to become someone I am not.
