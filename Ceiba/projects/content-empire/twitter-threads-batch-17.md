## THREAD 1

**Hook:** The catalog model: why I built 25 products instead of one course (thread)

---

1/ I built 25 digital products instead of one $500 course. Most creator advice says the opposite. Here is why the catalog math works better at my stage.

2/ The flagship model assumes you already have an audience big enough to convert a high-ticket offer. Most of us do not have that yet. The flagship model is a bet that requires infrastructure I am still building.

3/ The catalog model gives you more shots. 25 products, each targeting a specific problem, each at a price point that requires minimal trust to buy. Someone who just found me will buy a $9.99 template. They will not buy a $499 course.

4/ The trust builds in stages. First sale is $9.99. They use it. It works. Now they trust me at $29.99. Then $49.99. Then the $149.99 bundle. The average order value grows over time without me doing anything different.

5/ The risk profile is completely different. A failed flagship launch means a failed product after months of work. A catalog product that does not sell is one data point out of 25. The operation continues. You adjust and move on.

6/ 25 products also means 25 data streams. Which price points convert. Which problem framings resonate. Which outcomes people pay for. That data informs every future product before you build it.

7/ The counterargument is operational complexity. 25 products means more pages, more listings, more support scenarios. My answer: templates. Same page structure, same listing format, same FAQ. The complexity scales slower than it looks.

8/ Flagship is better if you already have a big audience and established credibility. Catalog is better if you are still building both. Know which stage you are in and match the model to the stage.

9/ I am building the audience and the reputation. The catalog is the correct model for where I am right now.

10/ If you are at the same stage, the catalog lets you learn faster, fail cheaper, and compound over time. You do not need the perfect product. You need enough products to find out what your audience actually pays for.

## THREAD 2

**Hook:** How to use Claude Code like a senior engineer (not like a chatbot)

---

1/ Most people use Claude Code the way they use ChatGPT: paste a problem, get an answer, copy it in. That is leaving 80% of the value on the table. Here is how to actually use it.

2/ The biggest shift: stop asking Claude Code to write code. Start asking it to think through the architecture with you before writing anything. The output is better when the design is right first.

3/ Give it context before you give it a task. What is the project, what already exists, what constraints matter, what you have already tried. A senior engineer does not start coding from a one-line prompt. Neither should Claude Code.

4/ Use it to review code, not just write it. Paste what you wrote and ask what is wrong, what is fragile, what you missed. The critique mode finds things you will not find yourself.

5/ Ask it to explain decisions, not just make them. "What are the tradeoffs between approach A and approach B for this specific use case?" This is how you build judgment, not just output.

6/ Give it files to read before asking it to write. If Claude Code can see the existing codebase, it will write code that fits the codebase. If it cannot, it writes code that works in isolation but clashes with what you have.

7/ Use it for the parts that are pure mechanics. Boilerplate, repetitive patterns, format conversions, documentation. The mechanical work is where time gets wasted. The architectural decisions need your judgment.

8/ When something goes wrong, do not just paste the error. Describe what you were trying to do, what you expected, and what happened instead. The better the problem description, the better the fix.

9/ Treat it like a contractor, not a vending machine. Brief it properly. Give it the context it needs. Review the output before you ship it.

10/ The engineers who use Claude Code well are not the ones who are best at prompting. They are the ones who are best at knowing what to ask and what to review. The tool amplifies your existing judgment.

## THREAD 3

**Hook:** The fear funnel: 5 stages every buyer goes through before they pay

---

1/ Every buyer goes through 5 stages before they buy. If you understand the stages, you can create content that meets them at each one. Here is the fear funnel.

2/ Stage 1: Unaware. They do not know you exist and they do not know they have the problem you solve. Your job here is awareness content. You are not selling. You are surfacing the problem.

3/ Stage 2: Problem aware. They know they have the problem but they do not know your solution exists. Your job here is education. Show the problem clearly, in their language, better than they can describe it themselves.

4/ Stage 3: Solution aware. They know solutions like yours exist, but they do not know you specifically. Your job here is credibility. Why you. What makes this specific product or offer worth trusting.

5/ Stage 4: Product aware. They know you and your product. They are deciding whether to buy. Your job here is objection removal. Price, risk, "will this work for me," "do I need this right now." Address the hesitation directly.

6/ Stage 5: Most aware. They are ready to buy. They just need a clear path to do it. Your job here is friction removal. Make the purchase obvious and easy.

7/ Most content creators only produce content for stages 1 and 2. They build awareness and educate. Then they wonder why their conversion is low. They never addressed stages 3, 4, and 5.

8/ A product page is stage 5 content. A case study is stage 3 and 4 content. A thread that names a problem your audience has is stage 1 and 2 content. Plan which stage each piece of content is targeting.

9/ The buyers who are ready to buy right now are not reading your stage 1 content. They are looking for a reason to trust you and a clear way to pay. If you do not have stage 4 and 5 content, you are losing people who were already close.

10/ Map your content to the funnel. Look at what you have and what is missing. Most creators are top-heavy. Balance it out.

## THREAD 4

**Hook:** ADHD productivity is different. Here is the system that actually works.

---

1/ Standard productivity systems were built for neurotypical brains. If you have ADHD, they feel broken because they are, for you. Here is what actually works instead.

2/ The core mismatch: neurotypical systems assume knowing what to do leads to doing it. For ADHD brains, knowing and doing are separate events. The system has to bridge that gap. Most systems skip it.

3/ Task lists are inventories, not action triggers. A list of 20 items creates cognitive overload, which triggers avoidance. The fix is not a better app. It is a smaller list. One task, named specifically, visible, before you start anything.

4/ Time blocking fails because ADHD brains do not respond to scheduled start times the way neurotypical brains do. The block does not create sufficient initiation pressure. What creates initiation pressure: a constraint, a timer, and someone else who expects output.

5/ The 25-minute timer is not just about focus. It is about creating an artificial start condition. "Work for 25 minutes on this specific thing" is a cleaner initiation cue than "work on the project until you finish."

6/ Friction reduction is underrated. If the first action requires opening five tabs and finding a password, the friction kills the initiation before it starts. The environment should be configured before you sit down, not after.

7/ External accountability works because it substitutes for internally-generated motivation, which is inconsistent in ADHD brains. Coworking calls, body doubling, posting your intention publicly. These are structural compensations, not crutches.

8/ Build recovery into the system. Most ADHD productivity systems assume success means zero deviations. Better design assumes deviations happen and has a reset protocol. One step, not a restart. The next action is always smaller than you think.

9/ The check-in before starting matters. Not journaling. Three questions: what is my energy level, what is my emotional state, what am I avoiding. Three minutes. It changes what you choose to work on based on the state you are actually in.

10/ You are not bad at productivity. You are using tools built for a different brain. The system that works for you will look different than what you were taught. Build the right one for your actual brain.
