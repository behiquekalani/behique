## ISSUE 5

**Subject: 15 posts live. Here's what actually got attention.**

---

Hey,

First batch of Instagram content went live this week. Fifteen posts across both accounts. It took longer than I expected to get from "content is ready" to "content is posted." Those are two different jobs.

The gap between drafted and published turns out to be the real bottleneck.

---

### This Week's Build

Posted 15 pieces across @behikeai and @kalaniandrez. Split between educational carousels, single-image text posts, and repurposed written content. Early data: the plain text posts outperformed the designed ones. The posts that felt like I was just talking did better than the ones that looked polished.

That's useful information. It means less time in Canva and more time writing sentences that matter.

---

### Tool of the Week

**n8n** - open source workflow automation. Think Zapier, but it runs on your own machine or server and does not charge per operation.

I am using it to automate content scheduling. You build flows visually: trigger, action, condition, output. The learning curve is a few hours. After that, you stop doing things manually that a computer could do. Free to self-host. Available at n8n.io.

---

### The Number

**15** - posts drafted versus posts that existed last week, which was zero. Content sitting in a file does nothing. The only thing that counts is what actually reaches someone.

---

### What I'm Thinking About

Building is only half the job. The second half is showing up where the audience already is.

Most people building online spend 90% of their time on the product and 10% on distribution. The ratio that works is closer to the reverse. A mediocre product with good distribution beats a great product with no distribution every time.

You can iterate the product after people see it. You cannot get time back from building in a room by yourself.

## ISSUE 6

**Subject: 35 scripts written. Zero recorded.**

---

Hey,

YouTube script production hit 35 this week across 8 content batches and channel concepts. None of them are recorded. The pipeline works great up until the part where I have to actually do something visible.

That is a real observation, not a complaint.

---

### This Week's Build

Wrote 35 YouTube scripts across 8 batches covering topics from AI tools to productivity to product reviews. The content is there. The format is production-ready. Hooks written, calls to action placed, timestamps planned.

The recording step is where everything stops. Not because I do not know how. Because hitting record is public-facing in a way that writing scripts is not.

---

### Tool of the Week

**Kokoro TTS** - open source text-to-speech model that runs locally. The voice quality is noticeably better than older open source models. I am using it to generate test voiceovers before deciding which scripts to record with my own voice.

It runs on CPU. No API keys. No cost. For anyone building faceless YouTube channels, this removes the recording bottleneck entirely. Find it on Hugging Face.

---

### The Number

**35** - scripts written. Zero recorded. The writing part is solvable with systems. The recording part requires a different kind of decision.

---

### What I'm Thinking About

Output without distribution is still just output. But there is a step before distribution that is harder than both: being visible.

Writing a script is private. Recording it is not. Most creative bottlenecks are not skill problems. They are exposure problems. The discomfort is not "I do not know how to do this." It is "someone will see this."

That is worth naming. Because once you name it, you can decide if the discomfort is worth letting it stop you.

## ISSUE 7

**Subject: I built a bundles page. Here's what I learned about pricing.**

---

Hey,

This week I built a bundles page. Three tiers: Starter Bundle at $29.99, Builder Bundle at $49.99, Complete Bundle at $99.99. Took about a day to structure and write the pages.

The interesting part was not the bundles. It was what building them revealed about how individual products are perceived.

---

### This Week's Build

Built out three product bundle tiers for the Gumroad store. Each bundle groups products by stage: starter for people just beginning, builder for people actively running something, complete for people who want everything.

The Complete Bundle at $99.99 contains products that total over $130 individually. Building the bundle did not change the products. It changed how I think about the individual prices.

---

### Tool of the Week

**GIMP** - free image editor. The Photoshop alternative that actually works. I used it to create product cover images for the new bundles. Layer system, mask tools, export options. Everything you need for digital product covers.

The interface takes getting used to. An hour of tutorials handles that. After that, you stop paying $20 a month for Adobe.

Free. Mac, Windows, Linux. gimp.org.

---

### The Number

**40%** - estimated average order value increase from offering bundles versus single products. That is based on Hormozi's data on bundle conversion, not my own numbers yet. The logic holds regardless: when you buy a bundle, the individual product feels cheaper by comparison, and the customer gets more value per decision.

---

### What I'm Thinking About

The bundle makes the individual product feel cheap. Not because the price dropped, but because the context changed.

A $14.99 product sitting alone asks: is this worth $14.99? The same product in a $99.99 bundle asks: does this bundle justify $99.99? Those are different questions. The second one is easier to say yes to.

Pricing is not just a number. It is a frame. Change the frame and you change what the buyer is deciding.
