## ISSUE 29

**Subject:** The one system that runs everything

**Preheader:** Not a framework. An actual operating system for your business.

---

Hey,

Most people run their business the way they run their inbox. They react to whatever shows up. They handle what's loudest. They go to sleep not knowing if today moved anything forward.

That's not a productivity problem. That's an architecture problem.

---

### The Thesis

A personal OS is not a productivity system. It's not a morning routine or a task manager or a second brain.

It's the answer to one question: how does work actually flow through my life?

Not how you want it to flow. How it actually does. Right now, with your real constraints, your real energy levels, your real tools.

Most people can't answer that question. So they borrow someone else's framework, use it for two weeks, and go back to reacting.

---

### What a Real Personal OS Looks Like

There are four components. Every effective one I've seen has all four.

**Capture.** One place where everything goes when it enters your head. Not your notes app and your phone notes and your Telegram saved messages. One place. The entire point is zero friction.

**Process.** A weekly ritual where you take what's been captured and decide what it means. Is this a task? A project? An idea to hold? Most things die here because people skip this step.

**Execute.** Time blocks that match your actual energy. Not manufactured urgency. Real prioritization based on what moves the needle this week, not what feels important right now.

**Review.** The loop that closes the whole thing. Without this, you have a capture system that fills up and a calendar that gets ignored.

That's it. Four things. The tools don't matter. Notion, Obsidian, a notebook. What matters is whether all four are actually running.

---

### The Part Nobody Talks About

Your OS only works if it matches your brain.

Not someone else's brain. Yours.

I have ADHD. That's not a caveat. It's relevant information for how I built my system. My capture layer is voice-first because typing slows me down when an idea is live. My review is daily, not weekly, because I lose context fast.

I've seen people copy Tiago Forte's second brain system exactly and get zero results, not because the system is wrong but because their brain works differently.

Before you borrow any framework, spend one week documenting how you actually work. Not how you want to work. What time do ideas come? When do you do your best thinking? What's the last thing you shipped without struggling? Reverse engineer that.

Build around it.

---

### The Practical Version

If you want to start somewhere specific, here is the minimum viable personal OS:

One inbox. I use Telegram with myself as the contact. Every idea, task, and random thought goes there. It's always in my pocket. Zero friction.

One weekly review. Sunday, 30 minutes. I go through everything in the inbox and either do it, schedule it, or delete it.

One daily priority. Before anything else, I ask: what is the one thing that would make today a win? Not three things. One.

That's the whole system. It runs everything.

## ISSUE 30

**Subject:** Why I stopped using Notion

**Preheader:** The friction was invisible. Until I noticed it was everywhere.

---

Hey,

I used Notion for two years.

I built a beautiful second brain in it. Databases for every project. Linked views. Status columns. Templates for templates.

Then I noticed something: I was maintaining the system more than I was using it.

---

### The Thesis

The best tool is the one you actually reach for. Not the one with the most features. Not the one everyone recommends. The one with the least friction between you and the work.

Notion is powerful. It's also slow to open, slow to search, and it requires you to already know which database you're in before you can put something in it.

That's fine if you're organized. It's fatal if you're moving fast.

---

### What Friction Actually Costs You

There's a type of friction that's hard to measure because it doesn't happen in dramatic failures.

It happens in small moments. You have an idea. You open Notion. It loads. You navigate to the right database. You remember you need to fill in the status field. You do that. By the time you're done, the idea has already lost energy.

Multiply that by fifty interactions a day. That's not a productivity problem. That's a leak.

The cognitive overhead of a complex system is a real cost. Most productivity frameworks ignore it because it doesn't show up on a dashboard.

---

### What I Replaced It With

I want to be specific here because "use simpler tools" is useless advice without examples.

For capture: Telegram message to myself. Zero load time. Works offline. I can send voice notes. There's no wrong place to put something because there's only one place.

For project tracking: A plain markdown file. One file per active project. Current status at the top, notes below, links at the bottom. I can open it in two seconds from any terminal.

For knowledge storage: Obsidian. Local files, browser-based navigation, no internet required, no subscription. Every note is just a text file. If the app dies, the files survive.

For tasks: A single text file called today.md. What needs to happen today. Nothing else.

That's the whole stack. No database. No relational linking. No status columns.

---

### The Honest Counterargument

Notion works well for teams. If you're collaborating with other people, sharing project state, assigning tasks across multiple people, Notion earns its complexity.

For a solo builder, you're paying for team infrastructure you don't need.

This is not about Notion being bad. It's about matching your tool to your actual use case. One person, fast iteration, lots of context-switching. The simpler the infrastructure, the faster you move.

---

### The Pattern Under Everything

I see this with software, with frameworks, with advice. People adopt the most sophisticated version of a tool available, then wonder why they're always behind.

Sophistication is a liability if you're the only person using the system.

The goal is to ship. The system is just how you get there.

## ISSUE 31

**Subject:** The bilingual advantage nobody is talking about

**Preheader:** 500 million people. One underserved market. No competition.

---

Hey,

There are roughly 500 million native Spanish speakers in the world.

The overwhelming majority of AI tools, digital products, and online education is built in English. For English speakers. With English cultural references and English-language assumptions baked into every interaction.

That gap is not a problem to solve. It's a market to build in.

---

### The Thesis

The most underbuilt opportunity in digital products right now is high-quality Spanish-language content for people who think in Spanish, earn in Spanish, and search in Spanish.

Not translated content. Not localized English. Content built from the inside of that culture.

The barrier is not technical. Most people who could build it are too busy consuming English-language content about how to build things to notice the gap in front of them.

---

### What the Data Shows

Google Trends tells you what people want before they can buy it. A search for any major AI productivity concept in Spanish returns a fraction of the volume of the same search in English, not because the demand is lower but because the supply is almost zero.

When supply is low and demand exists, prices are high and competition is thin.

This is true in digital products. It's true in YouTube content. It's true in newsletters. It's true in AI automation services sold to Spanish-language small businesses.

---

### The Specific Opportunity

Let me be concrete about where I see the gap.

AI tools education. Most tutorials for Midjourney, Claude, n8n, Zapier, and every major AI platform are in English. A beginner in Mexico City or San Juan or Bogota who wants to learn these tools has to navigate a language barrier on top of a technical barrier. Remove one of those barriers and you've already won.

Digital products. Templates, guides, frameworks, cheat sheets. The same product that sells for $9 on Gumroad to an English-speaking audience has almost no Spanish-language competition at any price.

Local business automation. A solo consultant who speaks Spanish and knows n8n or Zapier can charge the same rates as their English-language counterparts. The client pool is larger and the competition is nearly zero.

Content creation itself. There are almost no Spanish-language equivalents of the major English-language creator-economy newsletters. The audience is real and waiting.

---

### The Mistake to Avoid

The mistake is to build the thing and then translate it.

Translation creates content that sounds like it came from somewhere else. It doesn't land the same way. The idioms are off. The cultural references miss. The reader feels it even if they can't name what's wrong.

The advantage comes from building in Spanish, not building in English and converting.

I'm Puerto Rican. My first language is Spanish. When I write about AI tools for my community, I'm not adapting. I'm writing from inside it.

That's the moat. Not language skill. Cultural fluency.

---

### What This Means Practically

If you're bilingual and you haven't built anything in your first language, you're leaving one of the clearest arbitrage opportunities in digital business on the table.

Start with one thing. A guide. A newsletter. A YouTube video. Build it in Spanish. Don't translate it. See what happens.

The market is not oversaturated. It's barely started.
