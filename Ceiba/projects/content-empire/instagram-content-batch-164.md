## Post 2 — The Four Information Sources That Actually Compound

**Hook:** Four sources. Every high-output creator pulls from all four.

**Slide 1:** Four sources. Every high-output creator pulls from all four.

**Slide 2:** Source 1: Books. Specifically, books that are 5+ years old. Ideas that survived time are more reliable than ideas that went viral last week. Read slowly. Take notes.

**Slide 3:** Source 2: Primary research. Interviews, original data, your own experiments. First-hand knowledge is the most differentiated thing you can have. No one else has exactly your experience.

**Slide 4:** Source 3: Cross-industry reading. If everyone in your field reads the same sources, you will produce the same ideas. The best insights come from applying frameworks from one domain to another.

**Slide 5:** Source 4: Structured reflection. Journaling, weekly reviews, thinking walks. The best ideas often come not from consuming more but from sitting with what you already know.

**Slide 6:** Four sources. Build the habit. @behikeai for the content system.

**Caption:** The default information diet for most creators is social media plus newsletters plus podcasts. That is not a bad stack, but it is a reactive one. You are consuming what the algorithm serves you and what everyone else in your niche is also reading.

That is a recipe for average ideas. Not because the sources are bad, but because everyone else has the same inputs. Your differentiation lives in what you consume that others do not.

The cross-industry reader has an edge. The person who reads marketing books and philosophy books and biology books will make connections that the person reading only marketing books cannot. Insight is almost always the product of collision between domains.

Restructure your inputs around these four sources. One book at a time. One original experiment. One field you know nothing about. One hour of structured reflection weekly. That is the information diet of someone who has things worth saying.

## Post 3 — The Signal-to-Noise Problem

**Hook:** The average creator follows 500 accounts and does not have a single original idea.

**Slide 1:** The average creator follows 500 accounts and does not have a single original idea.

**Slide 2:** This is the signal-to-noise problem. When you consume hundreds of opinions on the same topic from the same platform, the inputs blur together. Nothing stands out. Your thinking becomes average.

**Slide 3:** The fix: drastically reduce follows. Replace volume with depth. 10 accounts that consistently challenge how you think are worth more than 500 that confirm what you already believe.

**Slide 4:** Same principle applies to newsletters, podcasts, and YouTube subscriptions. Cut by 80 percent. Keep the 20 percent that consistently produces insight, not just content.

**Slide 5:** The goal is not to consume less. It is to raise the floor of what you let in. Set a quality threshold. Enforce it ruthlessly.

**Slide 6:** Fewer inputs. Higher quality. Better ideas. @behikeai for the full system.

**Caption:** Information abundance is not a resource. It is a trap. The more you consume without curation, the harder it becomes to think clearly, because your attention is being divided among too many competing signals.

The curation problem is a real one, and most people solve it by adding more filters (more lists, more tags, more folders) instead of removing more sources. The right move is removal.

A person who follows 20 accounts they deeply trust has a better information environment than someone who follows 2,000 accounts they casually scroll. The trust level matters. When you know a source is reliable, you read carefully. When you follow everything, you skim everything.

Audit your follows this week. Remove anyone who has not made you think differently in the last month. You are not being rude. You are being strategic about the most limited resource you have: your attention.

## Post 4 — How to Read a Book Like a Creator

**Hook:** Most people read books. High-output creators mine them.

**Slide 1:** Most people read books. High-output creators mine them.

**Slide 2:** Reading for entertainment is passive. Reading for creation is active. The difference is what you do with the ideas you encounter.

**Slide 3:** The creator's reading method: highlight the ideas that connect to something you are working on. Write one sentence in your own words for each highlight. Save to your second brain.

**Slide 4:** Every chapter, ask: what is the main insight? How does this apply to my business or my content? What does this contradict that I already believe?

**Slide 5:** After finishing: write a 200-word summary in your own words. No copy-paste. Rewriting forces understanding. That summary is the asset that goes into your content pipeline.

**Slide 6:** Books are raw material. Mine them. @behikeai for the note-taking system.

**Caption:** The problem with reading for most people is that the ideas pass through without leaving a trace. You read a great book, feel smart for a week, and then cannot remember what was in it six months later. The insight evaporated because it was never processed into a form you could use.

The creator's reading method changes the relationship between reading and output. When you are reading with the explicit goal of generating content or insights, your brain processes differently. You are not looking for what the author said. You are looking for what you can do with it.

The one-sentence rewrite per highlight is the most underrated practice. It forces you to understand the idea well enough to translate it, which is a different cognitive process than recognition. If you cannot write the idea in your own words, you do not actually understand it yet.

Build the reading notes habit. One book at a time. The notes compound. The content becomes easier and better.

## Post 5 — The Consumption Cap

**Hook:** Set a consumption cap or your creation time disappears.

**Slide 1:** Set a consumption cap or your creation time disappears.

**Slide 2:** The most dangerous thing about content consumption is that it feels like work. You are reading about business while not doing business. The sensation is productive. The output is not.

**Slide 3:** High-output creators set hard limits on consumption time. 60 to 90 minutes maximum per day. The rest goes to creation. Not the other way around.

**Slide 4:** The ratio matters. If you consume 4 hours and create 30 minutes, your output reflects it. Flip the ratio. Create more than you consume.

**Slide 5:** The constraint forces quality. When you only have 60 minutes to consume, you get ruthlessly selective. The curation problem solves itself under a time cap.

**Slide 6:** 90 minutes of input. The rest is output. @behikeai has the full creator schedule framework.

**Caption:** Most creators have it backwards. They spend most of their time consuming content about content creation and very little time actually creating content.

This feels safe. Consuming is easier than creating. It does not risk rejection or judgment. It provides a continuous stream of small dopamine hits from new ideas and information. And it masquerades as preparation.

But at some point, more information stops helping. You already know what to do. What you need is not more input. It is to stop delaying the output.

The consumption cap is a forcing function. When you know you have 90 minutes for input and the rest of the day is for output, two things happen. You become more selective about what you read. And you start creating more often because the time for avoidance runs out.

Set the cap. Enforce it. Watch your output rate change.
