## Script 82.1 — Building a Product Suite

**Hook (0-3s):**
One product is a transaction. A product suite is a business.

**Body (3-50s):**
The difference between creators who plateau and creators who compound is the architecture of their offers.

[ON-SCREEN: One product = fragile / Suite = compounding]

A product suite is not just multiple products. It's a progression. Each product solves the next problem the customer has after solving the previous one.

[ON-SCREEN: Each product solves the problem the last one creates]

Think about it this way: if you sell someone a guide on how to start a newsletter, what problem do they have next. They need to know how to grow it. Then how to monetize it. Then how to scale without burning out. That's four products, each one a natural next step.

[ON-SCREEN: Start -> Grow -> Monetize -> Scale]

The mistake most creators make is treating each product as standalone. They release something, it sells, they release something else completely different. No compounding. No returning customers.

[ON-SCREEN: Standalone products don't compound]

Build a progression. Make product two the obvious next step after product one. That way, every buyer is already a pre-qualified lead for the next thing you sell.

[ON-SCREEN: Every buyer = lead for the next offer]

## Script 82.2 — How to Price Your Second Product

**Hook (0-3s):**
Your second product is the most important pricing decision you'll ever make.

**Body (3-50s):**
Most creators either underprice their second product or overprice it. Both kill momentum.

[ON-SCREEN: Underpricing and overpricing both kill momentum]

The framework is simple. Your second product should be priced at roughly 3-5x your first product. If your first product is $27, your second should be in the $79-$127 range.

[ON-SCREEN: Second product = 3-5x the first]

The reason is psychological. The buyer has already made one decision to trust you. Each subsequent decision is easier. But there has to be a step. Going from $27 to $27 again doesn't signal increased depth. Going from $27 to $97 does.

[ON-SCREEN: Price signals depth and progression]

This also filters buyers. Someone who buys a $97 product after a $27 product is signaling they're serious. They're not looking for the cheapest option. They want the result.

[ON-SCREEN: Higher price = more serious buyer]

One more consideration: the second product should be more specific, not more comprehensive. Comprehensive products feel risky to buy. Specific products feel actionable. "Email marketing A to Z" is comprehensive. "How to write subject lines that get 40% open rates" is specific.

[ON-SCREEN: Specific beats comprehensive at every price point]

## Script 82.3 — The Bundle Strategy

**Hook (0-3s):**
Bundles are not a discount strategy. They're a positioning strategy.

**Body (3-50s):**
Most creators bundle wrong. They take three things that aren't selling and put them together at a lower price. That's not positioning. That's desperation.

[ON-SCREEN: Bundling desperation products = discounting desperation]

The bundle that works groups products around a transformation, not a category. The question is not "what do I have that I can bundle" but "what does someone need to get from point A to point B, and do I have all of that."

[ON-SCREEN: Bundle by transformation, not by category]

If point A is "I have no email list" and point B is "I have 1,000 engaged subscribers and my first sale," then the bundle might include: a lead magnet template, a welcome sequence framework, and a guide on writing emails people actually open.

[ON-SCREEN: Bundle = everything needed for one transformation]

This also allows you to price the bundle above what each individual product would sell for separately, not below. The logic is that the full transformation is more valuable than any single piece.

[ON-SCREEN: Full transformation > sum of individual parts]

Good bundles make individual products feel incomplete. That's the test. If someone looks at your bundle and thinks "I could just get one of these," the bundle isn't positioned around a transformation.

[ON-SCREEN: Test: does the bundle make individual products feel incomplete?]

## Script 82.4 — What to Build After Your First Sale

**Hook (0-3s):**
Your first sale tells you exactly what to build next. Most people ignore the signal.

**Body (3-50s):**
When someone buys your first product, they're making a statement. They're saying: I have this problem, and I believe you can help me solve it.

[ON-SCREEN: First buyer = your research department]

The next step is to listen to what problem they have after using your product. The most valuable information you can gather at this stage is not reviews. It's the question they ask you after they've consumed your product.

[ON-SCREEN: Post-purchase question = your next product idea]

There are three ways to get this information. You can email every buyer with one question: "What's the next challenge you're running into?" You can add a page at the end of your product that asks the same question. Or you can look at what people are searching for after they find your product.

[ON-SCREEN: Email buyers / End page / Search patterns]

The pattern that emerges from this research is almost always the same. Buyers want the next step, deeper on the same topic, or the implementation of what they just learned.

[ON-SCREEN: Next step / Deeper / Implementation]

Build that. Not the thing you think is interesting. The thing they're asking for.

[ON-SCREEN: Build what they ask, not what you assume]

## Script 82.5 — The Service-to-Product Transition

**Hook (0-3s):**
Services trade time for money. Products trade assets for money. The transition is one of the most important moves you'll make.

**Body (3-50s):**
Most people who build digital products did something as a service first.

[ON-SCREEN: Service comes before product for most people]

The transition works because of a specific insight: if you have done something repeatedly for clients, you have accumulated knowledge that most people don't have. That knowledge is a product.

[ON-SCREEN: Repeated service work = packaged knowledge]

The pattern I've seen work consistently is this: you do a service for 5 to 10 clients. You notice the same problems come up every time. You notice the same mistakes. You notice the same questions. You document your process. You turn the document into a product.

[ON-SCREEN: 5-10 clients -> document -> product]

The product version of a service has several advantages. It doesn't require your time after it's built. It can be sold to people who can't afford your service. And it validates your expertise in a way that makes premium service pricing easier to justify.

[ON-SCREEN: No time cost / Lower entry point / Validates expertise]

The transition doesn't mean you stop offering services. It means you build an asset that earns whether you work that day or not. You keep the service for high-ticket clients. The product serves everyone else.

[ON-SCREEN: Service for high-ticket / Product for everyone else]
