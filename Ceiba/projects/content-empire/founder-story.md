## How a 21-Year-Old in Puerto Rico Built an AI Business for $0

I want to start with something that most people selling AI tools won't tell you: I have made zero dollars from this. Not a cent. Not yet.

That matters. Because everything I'm about to tell you is the truth about building something from nothing, not the polished version you hear from someone who already made it and is selling you their "blueprint" from a beach somewhere.

I'm writing this from Puerto Rico. From a desk with an old laptop that sounds like a small aircraft when it thinks too hard. And I'm building something I believe in.

This is that story.

## Growing Up in Puerto Rico

I grew up on an island where things break and you fix them. The power goes out, you figure it out. The internet drops during hurricane season, you wait. Something you need costs too much, you build a version that works well enough.

That mentality shaped everything about how I think.

Puerto Rico teaches you two things early. First, nothing is guaranteed. Infrastructure fails. Systems fail. You learn to build redundancy into your life without calling it that. Second, community matters more than credentials. Nobody here cares about your LinkedIn title. They care if you can actually do the thing.

I didn't grow up with tech money or a startup culture around me. I grew up with people who worked hard, fixed what broke, and took care of their families. That's the context for everything I build.

## The Engineering Brain

I study computer engineering at university. Not computer science, not IT. Engineering. The distinction matters to me because engineering is about systems, not just code. How things connect. Why things fail. Where the bottlenecks live.

C++ was my first real language. Not Python, not JavaScript. C++. The language that makes you think about memory allocation and pointers and all the things that higher-level languages hide from you. It trained me to think about what's actually happening underneath the abstraction.

That matters when you're working with AI, because most people interact with AI at the surface level. They type a prompt, they get a response, they move on. I want to understand what's happening underneath. Not because I'm an academic. Because if you understand the machinery, you can make it do things other people can't.

The engineering program also taught me something that no YouTube tutorial covers: how to think about problems before you touch a keyboard. Diagram first. Understand the inputs and outputs. Map the data flow. Then build. Most people skip straight to building. I used to do that too. I still do sometimes. But the projects that work are the ones where I thought first.

## The Diagnosis

I got diagnosed with ADHD. Working with a psychologist. Real treatment, not a TikTok self-diagnosis.

Here's what changed after the diagnosis: nothing and everything.

Nothing changed about who I am. I've always been this way. The brain that jumps between five ideas in ten minutes. The hyperfocus that lets me build for twelve hours straight and forget to eat. The difficulty finishing things when the excitement wears off. The constant feeling that there's a better system, a better tool, a better approach, and I should be researching it instead of shipping what's in front of me.

What changed was the framing. Before the diagnosis, all of that felt like failure. Like I was lazy or undisciplined or just bad at finishing things. After the diagnosis, it became a design spec. My brain works differently. Not worse. Differently. And once you understand the spec, you can engineer around it.

My psychologist gave me a framework. Small tasks. Concrete next steps. Capture ideas immediately so they don't distract you from the current thing. Validate effort, not just results. Check in on the emotional state, not just the task list.

I built that framework into a Telegram bot. It's called BehiqueBot. It classifies my ideas, tracks my emotional patterns, and holds me accountable in the way my psychologist designed. I turned my treatment plan into software. That's the most ADHD thing I've ever done, and it's also the most useful thing I've ever built.

The ADHD is not a weakness in my story. It's the engine. The hyperfocus is what lets me build 25 products in a week. The pattern recognition is what lets me see connections between tools that other people use separately. The restlessness is what keeps me building instead of sitting in a comfortable job.

I'm not saying ADHD is a superpower. That's a cliche and it dismisses the real struggles. What I'm saying is that once you understand how your brain works, you can build systems that work WITH it instead of fighting it every day. That's what Behike is. A set of systems designed for a brain like mine.

## Faith as Foundation

I'm a person of faith. I'm not going to preach at you or make this a religious thing. But I'm also not going to pretend it's not there, because it's the foundation of everything.

When I sit down to build something at 2am and I'm tired and the code isn't working and I've been staring at the same error for an hour, the thing that keeps me in the chair is not "hustle culture." It's not Gary Vee screaming at me from a YouTube video. It's something quieter than that. It's the belief that the work matters. That I was given these specific abilities for a reason. That building something useful is a form of service.

I don't put Bible verses on my product pages. I don't use faith as a marketing tool. But if you ask me why I keep going when the revenue is zero and the hours are long, the honest answer involves God. And I'd rather be honest about that than pretend my motivation comes from somewhere more "professional."

Faith also keeps me grounded when things go well. When I built 25 products in a week, my first thought wasn't "I'm going to be rich." It was gratitude. For the ability. For the tools. For the clarity that came after months of processing.

That's the foundation. Everything else is built on top of it.

## Family is the Why

My mother. My family. That's the answer to every "why" question about this business.

Para que mi familia no tenga que preocuparse.

So my family doesn't have to worry.

That's it. That's the mission statement. Not "disrupt the AI industry." Not "build a billion-dollar company." Make enough money, doing something I'm good at, so the people I love can stop worrying about money.

Every product I build, every system I design, every 3am debugging session traces back to that. It's not complicated motivation. It's the most basic, human motivation there is. But it's real, and it doesn't go away when things get hard.

The guru version of this story would say "I'm building generational wealth." The real version is simpler: I want my mom to be okay. I want my family to be okay. And I believe I can make that happen by building useful things and selling them to people who need them.

## Discovering AI

I didn't have a dramatic "aha moment" with AI. It was more like a slow realization that the thing I'd been looking for already existed.

I'd been thinking about systems for years. How to automate repetitive work. How to build things that run while you sleep. How to scale yourself without hiring a team. Computer engineering teaches you all of those concepts in the abstract. AI made them concrete.

The first time I set up a local language model on my laptop, using Ollama, and watched it generate text without any internet connection, without any API key, without any monthly fee, something clicked. This wasn't a service I was renting from a corporation. This was a capability that lived on my machine. Mine.

That's when I started thinking about AI differently than most people. Most people see AI as a product they pay for. ChatGPT is $20/month. Claude is $20/month. Midjourney is $10/month. The average person building with AI has $50-100/month in subscriptions before they've made a single dollar.

I saw AI as infrastructure. Something you install, configure, and own. Like electricity, not like Netflix.

That perspective came from growing up in Puerto Rico, where you learn early that depending on someone else's infrastructure is a risk. If you can run it yourself, run it yourself.

## The Names

Everything in my world is named after Taino culture. The indigenous people of the Caribbean, including Puerto Rico. My ancestors.

**Behike** is the brand. In Taino culture, the behique was the healer, the spiritual leader, the person who saw things others couldn't and translated them into something the community could use. That's what I'm trying to do with AI. Take something complex and make it accessible. Heal the gap between what AI can do and what regular people know how to do with it.

**Ceiba** is my AI system. My thinking partner, my memory, my second brain. Named after the ceiba tree, which in Taino cosmology is the axis that connects all three worlds: the world above, the world below, and the world we live in. Ceiba connects everything in my workflow. Notes, projects, memories, tasks. It's the tree everything hangs from.

**Cobo** is my second computer, the GPU machine that runs heavy AI workloads. Named after the Taino conch shell used to communicate across distances. That's what it does. It receives instructions from my main machine and processes them remotely.

**Hutia** is my dedicated server. Named after the small native mammal of the Caribbean. Quiet, resilient, always working in the background. That's what a good server does.

These names aren't branding exercises. They're identity. I come from somewhere. I build from somewhere. And I want that to be visible in everything I create, not erased by a generic startup name that sounds like every other company on Product Hunt.

## The $0 Philosophy

Here's a number that matters: $0.

That's my monthly infrastructure cost. Zero dollars.

I run AI models locally on a MacBook Air M4 and a desktop with a GTX 1080 Ti. I use Ollama for local inference. I use free-tier APIs strategically and sparingly. I self-host everything I can. My development environment costs nothing except electricity.

This isn't a flex. It's a constraint that became a feature.

I can't afford $200/month in cloud API costs. I'm a university student in Puerto Rico. So I figured out how to get the same output for $0. And then I realized that was the product. Because there are millions of people like me. People who want to use AI to build businesses but can't afford the subscription stack that every YouTube creator recommends.

The $0 AI stack is not a marketing angle. It's the actual way I work every single day. When I write about running AI on old laptops, I'm writing from the laptop I'm running it on. When I talk about avoiding cloud costs, I'm talking about costs I literally cannot pay.

That authenticity is worth more than any marketing strategy. Because the audience for "expensive tools for people with money" is saturated. The audience for "here's how to do it when you have no money" is massive and underserved.

## The Marathon Session

In March 2026, I sat down and did something that should have taken a team of five people three months. I built the entire Behike product catalog in one week.

25 digital products. 18 guides, courses, and templates. 3 Shopify themes. Interactive web tools. A meditation app. A budget calculator. An ebook. Over 80 AI agents working across multiple sessions to generate, format, design, and package everything.

How? The same way you eat an elephant. One bite at a time, except the bites were happening in parallel across multiple AI agents while I directed traffic.

I would start a product brief. Assign it to an AI agent. While that agent worked, I'd start the next brief. While both agents worked, I'd review and edit the output from a third product that finished earlier. Context-switching is supposed to be the enemy of productivity. For most people, it is. For my ADHD brain, running five parallel tracks is where I actually perform best.

By the end of the week, I had a complete product catalog that would cost $5,000-10,000 to produce through a traditional agency. My cost: $0 in tools. Maybe $40 in electricity. And a lot of bad sleep.

Was every product perfect? No. Some needed more polish. Some will need revisions as I get customer feedback. But the machine worked. The system of using AI agents as a production team, directed by one person who understands the architecture, actually worked.

That week proved the thesis: one person with AI can outperform a team of 10. Not because AI is magic. Because AI removes the bottleneck of execution. The ideas, the strategy, the quality control, the taste, the vision. Those are still human. But the execution? The writing, the formatting, the design, the packaging? AI handles that at a speed and cost that makes traditional production look absurd.

## Where I Am Right Now

Let me be honest about the current state of things.

Revenue: $0.
Products built: 25+.
Infrastructure: Fully operational.
Customers: Zero.

I have a Gumroad store. I have a Shopify store. I have Instagram accounts. I have the entire content machine designed and documented. I have email sequences drafted. I have a value ladder from $4.99 to $4,999/year. I have offer architecture that would make Alex Hormozi nod.

What I don't have is a single paying customer.

That gap, between "the machine is built" and "the machine makes money," is where I am right now. And I'm not going to pretend that gap is small or easy to cross. It's the hardest part. Building is comfortable. Selling is exposed. Putting your work in front of strangers and asking them to pay for it is a fundamentally different skill than building the work.

I know this about myself. My AI system (Ceiba) tracks my behavioral patterns, and one of the documented patterns is: "infrastructure building as a proxy for not starting the scary thing." The scary thing is selling. The scary thing is a stranger saying "this isn't worth $4.99." The scary thing is finding out that the machine works but nobody wants what it makes.

I'm naming that fear here because pretending it doesn't exist would make this story dishonest. And if this story is going to be the foundation of the brand, it needs to be true.

## The Vision

Here's what I believe will happen, and what I'm building toward.

One person with AI, the right systems, and a clear vision can build a business that competes with companies that have 10 employees. Not by working 100-hour weeks. By building infrastructure that works while you sleep.

The AI Employee is not a metaphor. It's a literal description of what I've built. AI agents that write content, research products, generate designs, manage social media, analyze data, and handle customer interactions. They don't call in sick. They don't need benefits. They don't quit. And they cost $0/month to run on local hardware.

That's not the future. That's right now. On my desk. Running on machines that cost less than most people's monthly AI subscription budget.

The vision for Behike is to make this accessible to everyone. Not just developers. Not just people with money. Everyone. The single mom in Bayamon who wants to start a business but can't afford Shopify plus AI tools plus a designer plus a content writer. She can do all of it herself with the right knowledge and the right tools. I want to give her both.

The ebook teaches the philosophy. The guides teach the specific skills. The themes give her a professional store. The templates give her professional content. The courses show her how to use AI for everything else. And when she's ready for someone to build it for her, the service tier exists.

That's not a product catalog. That's a ladder. And it's built for people like me, people like her, people who start from zero and need every rung.
