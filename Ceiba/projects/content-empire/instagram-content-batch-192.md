## Post 1 — Why Your AI Outputs Sound Like AI

**Hook:** Bad AI content is not an AI problem. It's a prompting problem.

**Slide 1:** Bad AI content is not an AI problem. It's a prompting problem.

**Slide 5:** When you prompt with all five of those elements, the AI stops generating average. It starts generating your voice scaled up. That's the actual use case.

Every creator who says "AI makes everything sound the same" is correct. But they're diagnosing the wrong problem.

The model is not broken. The prompt is empty. When you give an AI no context, no voice, no constraint, and no example, it defaults to the statistical average of every piece of content it has ever seen. That average is bland by definition.

The solution is not to try different AI tools until one sounds good. The solution is to build a prompt architecture that encodes everything that makes your content yours: your specific audience, your honest opinion, the format that works on your platform, and a hard constraint that prevents the model from retreating to generic.

## Post 2 — The 5-Part Prompt Framework

**Hook:** One prompt structure consistently produces better content than every other approach.

**Slide 1:** One prompt structure consistently produces better content than every other approach.

**Slide 2:** Part 1: ROLE. Tell the AI who it is. "You are a direct, research-backed copywriter writing for solopreneurs who are skeptical of hype." That one sentence changes everything.

**Slide 3:** Part 2: CONTEXT. Give the situation. "This is for an Instagram carousel post. The audience has seen 50 posts about productivity. They are tired of advice that doesn't account for ADHD or irregular schedules."

**Slide 4:** Part 3: TASK. One specific deliverable. "Write a 6-slide carousel. Slide 1 is the hook. Slides 2-5 are one insight each. Slide 6 is a CTA." Be exact.

**Slide 5:** Part 4: CONSTRAINTS. Force specificity. "No exclamation marks. No words like 'transform' or 'unlock.' Each slide is under 30 words. The hook must be declarative, not a question."

**Slide 6:** Part 5: EXAMPLE. Paste one piece of content that hit the tone you want. The AI will mirror the structure and style. That's the fastest voice-training tool you have. Follow @behikeai for more.

**Caption:**
ROLE. CONTEXT. TASK. CONSTRAINTS. EXAMPLE.

That's the full framework. Five parts. Used correctly, this structure will produce more usable output in one generation than most people get from 10 tries.

The part most creators skip is EXAMPLE. They give the AI everything except a clear signal of what "good" actually looks like. Then they're surprised when the output misses the mark.

Pasting one great tweet, one carousel that performed, one email you wrote that got 40% open rate tells the AI more about your voice than three paragraphs of description ever could. The model learns by pattern, not by instruction. Show it the pattern.

The second most skipped part is CONSTRAINTS. Constraints feel like limits. They're actually amplifiers. Telling the AI what it cannot do forces it out of the median and into something with an actual point of view.

Build this framework into a template. Save it. Reuse it for every content type. Change the role, context, and example for each new format. The bones stay the same.

## Post 3 — How to Train AI on Your Voice

**Hook:** You do not need to train a model to make AI sound like you. You need to feed it the right examples.

**Slide 1:** You do not need to train a model to make AI sound like you. You need to feed it the right examples.

**Slide 2:** Voice training is not a technical process. It's a curation process. Take the 10 pieces of content you've written that most sound like you at your best. Those are your voice examples.

**Slide 3:** At the start of every new prompt session, paste 2-3 of those examples and say: "Write in this voice. Match the sentence length, the directness, the level of abstraction, and the relationship to the reader."

**Slide 4:** The AI will not copy your content. It will pattern-match the style. That's the difference between plagiarism and voice modeling. You're telling it how you think, not what you said.

**Slide 5:** The more specific the examples, the better the output. A casual tweet and a formal LinkedIn post confuse the model. Pick examples from the same platform and tone register. Keep your voice library organized by format.

**Slide 6:** Build your voice library today. Pick 10 pieces. Save them in a doc. Paste 2-3 into every content prompt. That's it. That's the whole method. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:**
The best AI-assisted content creators I know all have one thing in common: a voice library.

It's not a complex system. It's a Google Doc with their 10 to 20 best pieces of content organized by format. Before every AI content session, they paste the 2-3 most relevant examples into the prompt and tell the model to match the voice.

The result is AI output that sounds like a faster, slightly more structured version of their natural voice. Not generic. Not robotic. Not like everyone else.

This works because language models are pattern-recognition systems. They do not understand "be more direct" the way a human would. But they do understand the pattern of directness when they see it in your actual writing.

Your existing content is the training set. Every good piece you've written contains information about how you see the world, how you structure an argument, how long you let a sentence breathe, how you end a thought. Feed that information back into the model.

The voice library is a two-hour investment that pays off on every single content sprint from that day forward.

## Post 4 — The Constraint Trick That Makes AI Content Original

**Hook:** The single fastest way to make AI content more interesting is to add a constraint the AI hates.

**Slide 1:** The single fastest way to make AI content more interesting is to add a constraint the AI hates.

**Slide 2:** AI defaults to completeness. It wants to cover all angles, include the counterargument, acknowledge the nuance. That instinct produces safe, balanced, unmemorable content.

**Slide 3:** A constraint breaks that pattern. "Write this as if you only have one point to make and you've already decided which one is right." That's a persona shift. The output becomes opinionated.

**Slide 4:** Other constraints that work: "End every slide with a concrete number." "No adjectives." "Write the first draft, then cut 40% of the words." "Every claim must have a because."

**Slide 5:** The constraint forces the AI to work harder within a narrower space. The output is denser, more specific, and harder to skim past. That's the whole goal of a carousel post.

**Slide 6:** Add one hard constraint to your next AI prompt. Just one. See what happens to the output quality. Follow @behikeai for the full prompting framework.

**Caption:**
AI content is boring because AI is a consensus machine. Its training data is the full breadth of the internet, which means its default output reflects the average of all internet writing. And the average of all internet writing is very, very boring.

The way out of consensus is constraint.

When you add a hard rule to your prompt, you force the model out of its comfort zone. "Write this as if the reader has already heard the obvious answer and you're giving them the contrarian take" is a constraint. "Every slide must make the reader feel slightly uncomfortable" is a constraint. "Do not use the word 'just'" is a constraint.

Constraints narrow the solution space. A narrow solution space forces creativity. This is true for human writers too. The sonnet form produced Shakespeare. The 280-character limit produced some of the sharpest thinking on the internet.

Your prompts should always include at least one constraint that makes the AI work harder. The harder it has to work, the less average the output will be.

## Post 5 — The Prompting Mistake That Wastes Your Time

**Hook:** Most people spend 80% of their AI time regenerating output because they got the prompt wrong at the start.

**Slide 1:** Most people spend 80% of their AI time regenerating output because they got the prompt wrong at the start.

**Slide 2:** The mistake: writing the real prompt in the third or fourth follow-up message. "Actually, make it shorter." "Actually, more like a Dan Koe post." "Actually, no bullet points." That information should have been in the first message.

**Slide 3:** Every regeneration is a signal that your initial prompt was missing something. Instead of regenerating, stop. Ask: what information would have prevented this bad output? Add that to the prompt. Start over.

**Slide 4:** The 3-minute rule: spend 3 full minutes writing the initial prompt before sending anything. Most people spend 30 seconds. The 3-minute prompt generates usable output on the first try. The 30-second prompt generates a conversation that lasts 20 minutes.

**Slide 5:** Build a prompt template for each content format you produce. Instagram carousel, email, LinkedIn post, landing page. Each template has the five parts pre-filled with your defaults. You only change what's specific to today's post.

**Slide 6:** The prompt is not the overhead before the real work. The prompt is the work. Get it right once and everything downstream gets faster. Follow @behikeai for the full system.

**Caption:**
Here is what AI-assisted content creation actually looks like for most creators: 30-second prompt, bad output, regenerate, slightly better output, tweak, regenerate, acceptable output, post.

That process takes 20 minutes per post and produces work that's 60% of what it could be.

Here is what it looks like when you treat the prompt seriously: 3-minute structured prompt with role, context, task, constraints, and example. First output is 80% there. One targeted follow-up gets it to 95%. Done in 8 minutes.

The difference is not the AI. The difference is the investment in the prompt.

The time you spend up front writing a careful, complete prompt is not overhead. It is compression. Every minute spent on the prompt saves three minutes of iteration. At scale, across a full content pipeline, that math is the difference between a creator who publishes consistently and one who is always behind.

Treat the prompt like the brief. No creative director sends a designer to work without a proper brief. Do not send the AI to work without one either.
