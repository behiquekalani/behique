**Hook:** You do not need 40 Notion pages. You need 5 connected databases.

**Slide 1:** You do not need 40 Notion pages. You need 5 connected databases.

**Slide 2:** Database 1: Projects
Every active project. Status, deadline, priority, related tasks.
Your command center.

**Slide 3:** Database 2: Content Pipeline
Idea to published. Status at each stage.
Title, platform, format, publish date, link when live.

Database 3: CRM
Every lead, client, and follow-up.
Status, last contact, next action, deal value.

**Slide 4:** Database 4: Revenue Tracker
Income by product, platform, and month.
Goals vs. actuals. No surprises.

Database 5: Knowledge Base
Frameworks, templates, SOPs.
Everything you learn that should be reusable.

**Slide 5:** Link these five.
A project links to its content. A client links to a project. A revenue entry links to a product.
Now you have a system, not a collection of pages.

## Post 3 — Build a Content Pipeline in Notion in One Hour

**Hook:** Stop losing content ideas. Build a pipeline that takes them from spark to published.

**Slide 1:** Stop losing content ideas. Build a pipeline that takes them from spark to published.

**Slide 2:** The 6 stages of a Notion content pipeline:
1. Idea
2. Outline
3. Draft
4. Review
5. Scheduled
6. Published

Every piece of content lives in one of these stages.

**Slide 3:** What each entry contains:
- Title and hook
- Platform (Instagram, email, Twitter)
- Format (carousel, reel, thread)
- Publish date
- Status
- Link when live

**Slide 4:** How to use it daily:
Morning: move one item from Idea to Outline.
Session: move one item from Outline to Draft.
Weekly: batch review and schedule.

You are never asking "what should I post?" You are executing a queue.

**Slide 5:** The pipeline also doubles as your content audit.
Filter by platform. See what you have published.
Filter by status. See what is stuck.

**Slide 6:** Build the pipeline. Ship consistently.

**Caption:** Inconsistent posting is almost never a motivation problem. It is a system problem.

## Post 5 — Your Morning Dashboard: What to See When You Open Notion

**Hook:** The first thing you see in the morning shapes everything you do that day.

**Slide 1:** The first thing you see in the morning shapes everything you do that day.

**Slide 2:** A Notion morning dashboard should show you:
1. Today's tasks (filtered by due date = today)
2. Current project status
3. Content scheduled for today
4. Revenue vs. goal this month

Four blocks. Everything else is noise.

**Slide 3:** How to build it:
Create a new page called "Today."
Add four linked database views, each filtered to show only what is relevant today.
Pin it. Open it first every morning.

**Slide 4:** What a good dashboard does:
Removes the decision of "where do I start?"
Makes your priorities visible before email or social media.
Gives you a scorecard before the day ends.

**Slide 5:** What a good dashboard does not do:
Overwhelm you with every open task.
Show you everything that is not done.
Make you feel behind before you start.

Design for focus, not completeness.

**Slide 6:** Build this once. Start every day with clarity.
Solopreneur OS at behike.gumroad.com
@behikeai

**Caption:** The average knowledge worker spends 20 minutes at the start of their day figuring out what to work on. For a solopreneur, that is 20 minutes of friction every single morning, compounded over 250 working days a year.

A Notion morning dashboard eliminates that friction completely. You open it, you see four things, you start. The overhead of deciding is already handled because the system is already filtered.

This sounds small. But clarity at the start of the day has a compounding effect on output. When you know exactly what to work on first, you get into deep work faster, you avoid the reactive loop of checking notifications first, and you end the day with a clearer sense of what actually happened.

The dashboard is not about tracking everything. It is about surfacing the right things at the right time. Those are completely different goals.
