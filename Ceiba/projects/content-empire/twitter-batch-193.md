## Thread 1: Why most content has a 48-hour shelf life

**Tweet 1:**
Most content is designed to perform this week.

A few pieces are designed to work for years.

The effort difference is small. The ROI difference is enormous.

**Tweet 2:**
Content with a 48-hour shelf life usually:

- References a current event
- Is tied to a trending format
- Optimizes for shares, not depth
- Answers "what's happening" instead of "why does this keep happening"

**Tweet 3:**
Content that ages well usually:

- Is built around a pattern, not an event
- Answers a question people will still have in 5 years
- Contains a personal perspective that can't be replicated
- Is specific enough to be useful, broad enough to apply

**Tweet 4:**
The practical test: would this post still be relevant in 2028?

If the honest answer is no, you're writing disposable content.

That's not necessarily wrong. But it should be a deliberate choice, not a default.

**Tweet 5:**
The ratio that works for most solopreneurs:

80% evergreen, 20% timely.

The timely brings new eyes. The evergreen converts them.

If you invert that ratio, you're a journalist with no business model.

**Tweet 6:**
Every evergreen post is a salesperson working while you sleep.

Every trend post goes quiet by Thursday.

Build accordingly.

## Thread 2: The ingredients of timeless content

**Tweet 1:**
Timeless content is not vague content.

"Be consistent" ages well but helps no one.

"Here's the specific system I used to publish daily for 90 days without burning out" ages just as well and is actually useful.

**Tweet 2:**
Ingredient 1: A stable problem.

Money, relationships, health, identity, meaning, skill.

These problems don't disappear when the algorithm changes.

**Tweet 3:**
Ingredient 2: A specific mechanism.

Not "work smarter" but "here's the exact decision I make every morning before I open my phone."

Mechanisms survive trend cycles. Generic advice doesn't.

**Tweet 4:**
Ingredient 3: A genuine perspective.

When content contains your actual view, it's no longer replaceable.

AI can write "10 tips for productivity."

Only you can write your experience of what productivity actually cost you.

**Tweet 5:**
Ingredient 4: Evidence or story.

Claims without evidence expire faster.

"Trust me" is fragile.

"Here's what happened" is permanent.

**Tweet 6:**
Ingredient 5: The counterintuitive angle.

Conventional wisdom is forgotten the moment better conventional wisdom arrives.

Counterintuitive observations stick because they cause a small shock. And people remember shocks.

## Thread 3: How to audit your existing content for longevity

**Tweet 1:**
Most content libraries have 10% evergreen content buried in 90% disposable content.

The 10% is doing most of the work.

Here's how to find it and make more of it.

**Tweet 2:**
Step 1: Pull your top-performing posts from 12+ months ago.

If they still get engagement without promotion, they're evergreen.

If they're dead, they were trend-dependent.

**Tweet 3:**
Step 2: Look for the pattern.

Evergreen posts usually cover:

- Process (how you actually do something)
- Belief (why you think what you think)
- Observation (a pattern you noticed that others haven't named)

**Tweet 4:**
Step 3: Extract the frame, not just the topic.

A post about productivity isn't evergreen because productivity is evergreen.

It's evergreen because it frames a specific truth in a way people recognize as real.

Find the frame. Replicate it.

**Tweet 5:**
Step 4: Remove the timestamps.

If a post references "this year" or "recently," it's aging poorly already.

Replace with "in the past" or just remove the temporal reference entirely.

**Tweet 6:**
Step 5: Make it more specific, not less.

People think evergreen means general.

The opposite is true. Specific detail makes things feel real and true, which makes them last.

## Thread 4: The difference between a trend post and a timeless post on the same topic

**Tweet 1:**
Here's a real example.

Trend post: "How to use [New AI Tool] to write content in 10 minutes."

Timeless post: "The writing process I use that hasn't changed in 3 years, regardless of which tools exist."

Both are about content creation. One will matter in 2028.

**Tweet 2:**
Trend posts are about the tool.

Timeless posts are about the principle the tool is trying to serve.

Tools change. Principles don't.

**Tweet 3:**
You can write about trends. You should sometimes.

But anchor the trend to a principle.

"Here's why this new tool works, and the underlying principle that will make every future tool useful too."

That version ages.

**Tweet 4:**
The audience who reads trend content wants novelty.

The audience who reads timeless content wants clarity.

Clarity is more valuable and harder to produce.

It's also the foundation of a real business.

**Tweet 5:**
One more way to think about it:

Trend content makes you relevant today.

Timeless content makes you credible forever.

You need some of both. You probably have too much of one.

## Thread 5: Building a library of content that works for years

**Tweet 1:**
A single piece of evergreen content, promoted correctly, can bring in leads for years.

Most creators publish and move on.

The move is to publish, promote, then republish 6 months later.

**Tweet 2:**
Your best posts deserve more than one appearance.

The algorithm has already moved on.

Your new audience hasn't seen it.

Surface your best work repeatedly.

**Tweet 3:**
Build a content library, not a content archive.

An archive is a graveyard.

A library is a system where old pieces are regularly brought back into circulation.

**Tweet 4:**
How to build the library:

1. Tag every post as evergreen or timely at publish.
2. Set a 6-month reminder to republish your evergreen pieces.
3. Update one detail if needed. Republish.
4. Track which ones continue to perform.

**Tweet 5:**
A library of 50 high-quality evergreen pieces, regularly recirculated, outperforms a feed of 500 disposable posts.

Volume is not the goal.

A body of work is.
