# Newsletter Issues 276-280

## Issue 276: The Lie That Keeps Talented People Broke

You've heard it your whole life. Teachers said it. TED talks said it. Your family probably said it.

"Follow your passion."

It sounds right. It feels motivating. And for most people, it is completely useless advice.

Here's the problem: passion is a feeling, not a strategy. Feelings shift depending on how much sleep you got, whether you're stressed about money, and whether the thing you love is currently making you feel incompetent. When you build a business on a feeling, the first rough month will make you question everything.

I've watched people quit businesses that were six months away from real traction because the passion faded. Not because the business was bad. Because nobody told them passion was supposed to be temporary.

The thesis is this: passion follows mastery, not the other way around.

Cal Newport wrote about this in "So Good They Can't Ignore You," but most people remember the title and skip the content. The argument is straightforward. You don't discover your passion and then get good at something. You get good at something, and then the confidence and craft that come from that competence create something that feels like passion.

The solopreneurs who last are not the ones who loved their niche from day one. They're the ones who got curious about a problem, stayed with it long enough to develop skill, and then found meaning in the mastery itself.

Passion as a starting point is also dangerous because it tends to pick the most crowded rooms. Everyone who loves fitness wants to be a fitness coach. Everyone who loves fashion wants to start a clothing brand. The passion-first people cluster in high-competition spaces because those spaces feel exciting. The skill-first people find weird, specific problems and become the only person who solves them at that level of depth.

The practical reframe: stop asking "what am I passionate about?" Start asking "what am I willing to get good at even when it's boring?"

Consistency through boredom is the filter. Most people can start anything. Almost nobody can stay in the awkward middle, the phase where you know enough to see how bad you still are but not enough to produce work that gets traction. That phase is where passion-first people quit. Skill-first people treat it as the cost of entry.

If you're a solopreneur trying to figure out what to build, here's the exercise. Write down five things you have been paid for, praised for, or asked about more than twice. Not what excites you. What have other people already valued from you. That list is your starting point.

Build the skill. Let the passion come later. It will.

## Issue 277: The One Variable That Actually Predicts Success

There are people with better ideas than you.

There are people with bigger audiences, more capital, better connections, and more raw talent. In almost every room you walk into, someone has a structural advantage you don't.

None of that matters as much as one thing: who shows up every day.

Consistency is not motivational advice. It's a compounding mechanism. The math works the same way savings accounts work. A small amount deposited every day, without interruption, beats a large amount deposited irregularly. Content creation is the same system.

The creator who posts three times a week for two years will outperform the creator who posts ten times a week for two months then disappears. Every time. The algorithm is partly why, but the real reason is internal. The person who shows up daily develops judgment, feedback loops, and pattern recognition that the inconsistent creator never accumulates.

Talent is static. It's what you start with. Consistency is dynamic. It's what you build with.

The problem with talent is that it makes the early phases feel easy, which creates a false ceiling. Talented people often stop putting in reps because the work comes naturally. When the difficulty scales up, they haven't built the habit infrastructure that mediocre-but-consistent creators have. The consistent creator has already learned how to show up on the days when it feels pointless.

There's a specific version of this I see with content creators. They have one piece that performs well. Gets shares, comments, some new followers. And then they stop, waiting for inspiration to strike again. They're treating content like it's an event instead of a practice.

The professionals treat it like a practice.

When I started writing consistently, the first 30 pieces were largely invisible. A few readers, minimal engagement, lots of questions about whether this was worth it. But somewhere in that stretch of invisible work, I developed opinions I didn't know I had. I found angles I couldn't have found without repetition. The consistency created the material; the material didn't create the consistency.

Here's what consistent output does that nothing else can: it reveals your actual voice.

You don't find your voice by thinking about writing. You find it by producing bad work until the bad work gets less bad. Every creator you respect had a long phase of output that they'd prefer you never found. The consistency is what burned through that phase.

The action is simple but the execution is hard. Pick a frequency you can hold for 90 days even in a bad month. Not the frequency you'd hold in an ideal month. The sustainable one. Then hold it past the point where you want to quit.

That's where everyone else has already stopped.

## Issue 278: What Nobody Tells You About Your Second Product

Your first product taught you something.

Maybe it taught you what your audience actually wants versus what you assumed they wanted. Maybe it taught you your price ceiling, or the objections people have before buying, or the specific language that gets someone to click.

Now you have a second product to build. And most creators make the same mistake here: they treat it like a separate project instead of the next chapter of the same story.

The second product problem is this. You have a small but real audience who bought from you once. They trusted you enough to spend money. And now you're about to either deepen that relationship or break it by misreading what they need next.

Most people solve this wrong in one of two ways.

The first mistake is going sideways. You made a productivity guide, so now you make a nutrition guide because you're into nutrition. Your existing buyers followed you for productivity. They didn't sign up for a pivot. You just started over with a new product to a half-relevant audience.

The second mistake is going too high too fast. You sold a $27 ebook. Now you're launching a $2,000 course. There's nothing wrong with premium offers, but you skipped the trust ladder. Your buyers haven't seen enough of your thinking, your depth, or your results to justify that jump.

The right move is to go deeper, not sideways or vertically.

Your first product solved a surface problem. The second product should solve what comes right after. If your first product helped someone start freelancing, your second product helps them price and close clients. If your first product helped them build an audience on Instagram, your second product helps them convert that audience.

This is called product sequencing, and it's how you build a customer for life instead of a one-time buyer.

The math changes when you sequence correctly. Instead of needing to find 500 new customers for every product, you have 200 existing customers who already trust you and are ready to spend more. The cost of acquisition is near zero. The conversion rate is much higher because the trust is already built.

Before you build your second product, answer these two questions. What did buyers do right after finishing your first product? And what do they struggle with once the first problem is solved? Those answers point you at the exact product to build next.

The creator who builds a suite of sequenced products has a business. The creator who launches standalone products has a portfolio.

## Issue 279: Your Bio Is a Sales Page. Write It Like One.

Most bios read like a job application.

"Content creator, entrepreneur, and coffee addict. Helping people live their best life. DM me for coaching."

Nobody reads this and wants to buy anything. They read it and scroll past.

Your bio has one job: make a specific person feel like you are exactly who they've been looking for. That's it. Not to summarize your career. Not to list your interests. Not to seem relatable with the coffee joke.

The problem is that most people write bios from their own perspective. They're writing what feels true about themselves. But the reader is asking a selfish question: what does this person do for me?

Here's the structure that actually works.

Line one: who you help and what result you help them get. Be specific about both. Not "I help entrepreneurs" but "I help first-time freelancers go from $0 to $3k/month without a big audience." The specificity is what makes someone feel seen. Generic claims attract nobody.

Line two: your credibility proof. Not your credentials. Your results. There's a difference between "certified coach with 10 years experience" and "helped 300 solopreneurs build their first income stream." One is a resume line. One is social proof. If you have relevant numbers, use them. If you don't have numbers yet, use a specific result: "went from broke freelancer to booked out in 6 months."

Line three: your invitation. What do you want them to do next? This should be one thing. Not "follow, DM, click the link, join the community." One action. Usually the free lead magnet that starts the relationship.

The last thing most bios miss is what I'd call the implicit promise. Every good bio makes the reader feel something before they've clicked anything. It signals taste, point of view, and the shape of the thinking they'll get if they stay.

You develop this by having an actual stance. Not "I believe in hard work and consistency" which everyone believes. A real stance that creates friction. Something someone might disagree with. That friction is what makes you memorable.

Rewrite your bio this week. Show it to someone who doesn't know you and ask them what they think you do and who you help. If they get it wrong, it's not their reading comprehension. It's the bio.

## Issue 280: The Distinction That Changes How You Build Everything

There's a moment in every creator's journey where the work they're doing stops being enough.

They've built an audience. They're producing content consistently. Some of it performs well. They have a product or two. But the revenue is inconsistent, the growth feels fragile, and they can't figure out why the machine doesn't run without them.

The answer is almost always the same: they're still operating as a creator when they need to start operating as a business owner.

This is not a criticism. It's a description of a transition that most people don't realize they need to make.

A creator's relationship with work is personal. The content comes from their perspective, their experiences, their thinking. The audience follows the person. The products are expressions of the creator's knowledge. This is a valid and powerful model for building an audience and making early sales.

But it has a ceiling, and that ceiling is the creator's time and attention.

A business owner's relationship with work is systemic. They ask different questions. Not "what should I make next?" but "what is the machine producing, and what does it need to produce more?" Not "how can I grow my audience?" but "what is the conversion rate from follower to buyer, and where is it breaking down?"

The shift is not about becoming less authentic or more corporate. It's about developing a layer of thinking that sits above the creative work. You still create. But you also audit, analyze, and build systems that make the creating compound instead of just accumulate.

In practice, this looks like a few specific changes.

You start treating content as a funnel, not just expression. Every piece of content serves a purpose in moving someone from awareness to purchase. You know which pieces are doing acquisition work and which are doing conversion work.

You start thinking in products rather than projects. A creator launches things. A business owner builds a suite. The products connect to each other and create a path that a customer can walk from first touch to highest-value offer.

You start separating yourself from the revenue. A creator gets paid when they work. A business owner builds structures that generate revenue when they're not working. This doesn't happen overnight, but it starts with asking whether each piece of work you do is building toward that separation or just filling the calendar.

The creator mode is where you start. The business owner mode is where you build something that lasts.

Most people never make the shift because nobody told them the shift was necessary.

Now you know.
