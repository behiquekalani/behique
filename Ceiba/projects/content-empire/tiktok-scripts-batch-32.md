**Script 2**
**Title:** The difference between a product and a document
**Hook (0-3s):** Writing a PDF and selling a digital product are two different things.
**Body (3-45s):**
A lot of first-time digital product creators make this mistake: they write down everything they know about a topic, save it as a PDF, and wonder why nobody buys.

The problem isn't the format. It's the frame.

A document answers "what do I know?"
A product answers "what problem does the buyer have, and how do I solve it in the least amount of their time?"

Concrete example. "A complete guide to intermittent fasting" is a document. "How to do your first 16-hour fast without feeling terrible" is a product.

One is organized information. The other is a specific promise to a specific person with a specific problem.

When you write your product, write the title first. If the title doesn't describe a solved problem, rewrite it before you write anything else.

**Script 3**
**Title:** Why your first digital product should be cheap
**Hook (0-3s):** Charge less than you think you should. At first.
**Body (3-45s):**
I know every creator says "charge what you're worth." That advice is for later. Not for your first product.

Your first product has one job: get bought. Not get rich. Get bought.

A low price reduces friction. Someone will spend $9 to test a promising idea. They won't spend $97 without social proof, testimonials, and a reason to trust you.

When you charge $9, you get buyers. Buyers become reviewers. Reviews become social proof. Social proof lets you raise the price.

The progression looks like this: $9 PDF gets 50 buyers, you collect 10 testimonials, you repackage the content as a $47 course, you use the testimonials to sell it. Now you have $2,350 from the same content that started at $9.

The cheap version wasn't a failure. It was the engine that funded everything after it.

**Script 4**
**Title:** The fastest way to validate a digital product before you build it
**Hook (0-3s):** Build nothing. Sell the idea first.
**Body (3-45s):**
Most people spend two weeks building a product, then post it and hear silence. This is backward.

Validate before you build. Here's how.

Write the landing page first. Describe the product as if it exists. Post it somewhere, Reddit, Twitter, a Facebook group, wherever your audience is. Ask: "I'm building this, would you buy it?"

If ten people say yes, build the product. If nobody responds, you saved two weeks.

You can go one step further: presell it. Charge the $9 before the product exists. Tell buyers it's delivered in five days. Then build it in five days.

If nobody buys at checkout, you have hard data that the demand isn't there. Adjust before investing time.

This isn't deceptive. It's efficient. You're not lying, you're confirming demand before building.

**Script 5**
**Title:** What to do after your first digital product sale
**Hook (0-3s):** You made your first sale. Here's what most people get wrong next.
**Body (3-45s):**
The first sale hits and most creators do one of two things: they celebrate and coast, or they immediately start building the next product.

Both are wrong moves.

The right move is to email that buyer. Not a template. A real one. "Hey, you just bought my guide. What made you buy? What were you hoping to solve?"

That answer tells you more about your customer than any research you could do. It tells you their exact words, their exact pain, and their exact expectation.

Those words become your next product's sales page. Literally copy their language back to them.

Then you ask the first buyer to share it with one person who has the same problem. Not a big ask. Just one person.

One sale, handled correctly, can become five. Five can become fifty. It starts with the email most people never send.
