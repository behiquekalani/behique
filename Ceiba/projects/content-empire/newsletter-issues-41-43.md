## Issue 41: "The $2,200 Nobody Is Leaving On The Table"

**Subject line:** The market nobody is building for

---

Hey,

Something clicked for me this week while I was auditing content competitors.

I was trying to figure out how saturated the AI education space is. I spent a few hours searching YouTube, Udemy, and Google for Spanish-language AI content. Not news. Not translated summaries. Actual practical content that teaches you how to use AI to build a business or automate your work.

I found almost nothing.

I want to be specific about what "almost nothing" means. There are maybe four or five Spanish-language channels that do this kind of content consistently. For 680 million people. The entire English AI education ecosystem has hundreds of channels doing exactly this. The Spanish market is running at roughly 1% of capacity.

Here is where the $2,200 number comes in.

A small but engaged email list of 1,000 subscribers can conservatively generate $200 to $500 per month in product sales if the products are good and the trust is there. That is not a dramatic projection. That is just one digital product at $20, sold to 1-2% of the list per month.

Now consider this: the average creator building in English is competing for attention in a market with thousands of other creators doing the same thing. The average creator building in Spanish for a practical AI education audience is competing with almost nobody.

The attention cost is lower. The trust builds faster when you are one of five people covering a topic instead of one of five hundred. The products have less competition. The affiliate deals are just starting to exist in that market.

This is not a niche play. This is a timing play.

I did a rough projection last week. If I build a 5,000-subscriber Spanish-language list in 18 months, running the same product math gives me somewhere between $1,000 and $2,200 per month from that list alone. On top of whatever the English list generates.

The $2,200 nobody is leaving on the table is not a fixed number. It is the result of showing up in a market before it fills up. Every month that passes, the gap closes a little more.

I am not going to let that window close without building something in it.

---

### This Week's Build

Set up the bilingual content pipeline. Write once in English, translate with style-guided AI, edit for Caribbean Spanish idioms, schedule staggered by three hours. First batch of twelve Spanish posts is done and ready.

---

### The Number

**680 million.** Spanish speakers globally. The AI education market serving them in a practical, builder-focused way is effectively zero. This is the number I keep coming back to.

---

### What I'm Thinking About

The most valuable time to be in a market is when it is forming. Not when it is crowded. The people who built English AI education audiences in 2020 and 2021 own their categories now. The equivalent moment for Spanish is right now.

## Issue 42: "What My Abuela's Business Taught Me About AI"

**Subject line:** She never used a computer. She taught me everything.

---

Hey,

My grandmother ran a small food business in Puerto Rico for most of her adult life.

She did not have a database. She had a memory. She knew every customer's name, what they ordered last time, whether they preferred more or less salt, what their kids liked. She kept all of it in her head and she used it every single conversation.

What she was doing, technically, is what we now call a customer relationship system. She was storing context, retrieving it on demand, and using it to personalize every interaction. The difference between her and a CRM is just the medium.

I thought about her a lot this week while I was working on a memory architecture for BehiqueBot.

The bot needs to remember things across conversations. Not just what you said in the last message, but patterns over time. What kind of ideas you tend to have. What categories come up most. What you said you wanted to do six weeks ago but have not started yet.

Building that system made me realize something about the gap in AI tools for communities like the one my grandmother served.

Caribbean oral tradition is a specific thing. We pass knowledge down through conversation, through stories, through the kind of relational memory my grandmother had. The information is dense. The format is human. It does not naturally live in spreadsheets or documentation or any of the systems that most productivity tools are built around.

AI memory systems, when built right, can actually mirror that tradition more closely than any previous technology could. They are relational. They hold context. They recognize patterns over time. They get better the more you talk to them.

My grandmother's business worked because she made every customer feel known. That is the goal with BehiqueBot. Not a task manager. Not a to-do list. Something that actually knows the person using it.

Building for LATAM audiences specifically means building with this in mind. The tools need to feel human. They need to remember. They need to reflect the warmth of the oral tradition back to the person using them, not strip it out in favor of efficiency.

This is not a sentimental point. It is a product design point. The AI products that win in Latin American markets will be the ones that feel like people, not software.

My grandmother would have loved a good AI tool. She would have asked it to remember everything.

---

### This Week's Build

Memory architecture for BehiqueBot. Persistent context across sessions, category pattern detection, callbacks to things mentioned weeks ago. Still in progress. The goal is something that actually knows the person, not just logs them.

---

### Tool of the Week

**Whisper (OpenAI's open-source transcription model).** I have been using it to transcribe voice notes. If you come from a culture where ideas flow better in conversation than in text, voice-to-text with good transcription is the input method that matches how you actually think. Runs locally. Cost: zero.

---

### The Number

**12 years.** Roughly how long my grandmother's business stayed alive on memory alone, no software, no CRM, just relational context built over thousands of conversations. That is a data retention record most SaaS tools cannot touch.

---

### One Thing I'm Thinking About

Every culture has a knowledge transmission system. Caribbean oral tradition is one of them. The question worth asking is not "how do I get my community to use AI tools." It is "how do I build AI tools that already work the way my community thinks." The answer changes the whole design.

## Issue 43: "Building Something Before Anyone Else Sees the Market"

**Subject line:** I'm making the same bet I would have made in 2021

---

Hey,

In late 2021, if you had started a serious English-language YouTube channel about practical AI tools for entrepreneurs and creators, you would have had almost no competition. The topic existed. A handful of people were covering it. The audience was not yet there in force.

Two years later, that category is crowded. The people who got in early built audiences of hundreds of thousands. They became the trusted voices. They are selling courses, running communities, landing sponsorships.

The people who tried to enter in 2023 and 2024 are fighting for scraps.

I did not get there in 2021. I was too early in my own building journey. I was still figuring out the fundamentals.

But this week I realized I have a second shot at the same bet.

The Spanish-language practical AI education space is where the English market was in early 2021. The tools are accessible. The audience is growing. The need is real and proven in other markets. The competition is almost zero.

I am not making this bet because I am optimistic by nature. I am making it because the structural conditions are the same. Same market formation pattern. Same content gap. Same early-mover dynamics. Just shifted by about three years and translated into a different language.

Here is what I know about early markets from watching the English space develop.

First: consistency matters more than production quality in the early period. The creators who built English AI audiences in 2021 were not making polished videos. They were making useful videos, consistently. The audience was so hungry for the content that they forgave rough production.

The Spanish market is in that same phase. A well-researched, clearly explained video with decent audio will outperform a perfectly produced video that comes out once a month.

Second: the first people to establish authority in a category carry that authority for a long time. There is a trust transfer that happens early. Once an audience associates a name with reliable information, they are slow to switch. The activation energy required to build that association is highest at the start and drops fast as you accumulate content.

Third: the people who win early markets are almost never the most talented. They are the most consistent. Talent helps. But in a market with no leaders, showing up every week beats being brilliant once a month.

I am running a 90-day sprint on this starting now. Twelve weeks. One YouTube video per week in Spanish. Four Instagram posts per week. One newsletter issue. Consistent. Not perfect. Just there.

If the dynamics hold, that 90-day sprint will be the hardest 90 days. And the most important.

The bet is the same one I would have made in 2021. I am just making it in the right market this time.

---

### This Week's Build

Committed to a 90-day sprint on the Spanish content channel. Batch 23 of YouTube scripts is written and ready. Twelve Instagram posts for the LATAM audience are done. The pipeline is in place.

---

### The Number

**3 years.** Roughly how far ahead the English AI education market is compared to where the Spanish market sits today. That gap is the opportunity.

---

### What I'm Reading

I keep coming back to chapters from "The Almanack of Naval Ravikant." Specifically the parts about leverage. One of the highest-leverage positions you can be in is owning a distribution channel in a market before the market is crowded. Every piece of content I post in Spanish is building that distribution channel.

---

### One Thing I'm Thinking About

The best time to build an audience is before the audience knows it wants to be an audience. That sounds backwards. But the people who built early communities around Bitcoin, around AI tools, around no-code, around specific sub-niches within personal finance, they did not wait for the demand to be obvious. They built for demand they could see forming before it was visible to everyone else. That is the game.
