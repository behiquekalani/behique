## ISSUE 21: "ADHD money for a real ADHD brain"

**Subject Line A:** ADHD money for a real ADHD brain
**Subject Line B:** The impulse timer that saved me $94/month

**Preview text:** Most budgeting advice assumes willpower. This doesn't.

---

Hey,

Quick one this week. I want to tell you about the $94 I found in subscriptions I had completely forgotten about.

Not hidden charges. Things I signed up for, used for a week, and stopped. Thought about canceling. Never did. Paid for months.

That's not a discipline problem. That's an ADHD tax. And it shows up in four specific places.

---

**ADHD money for a real ADHD brain**

Here is what most budgeting advice gets wrong: it assumes the issue is information.

"If you just tracked your spending, you'd make better decisions." Sure. Except knowing you spent $200 on takeout last month does not make you less likely to order food when you're in the middle of a task and hitting a wall.

ADHD money problems are not knowledge problems. They're regulation problems.

Four specific ones:

**1. Impulse purchases.** The pause between "I want this" and "should I buy this" is genuinely shorter in ADHD brains. This is neuroscience. The fix I use: before buying anything over $30, I put it in a note, not a cart. I wait 24 hours. Most of the time the urge is completely gone by morning.

**2. Forgotten subscriptions.** I had a subscription audit I'd been putting off for six months. When I finally ran it, I found $94/month in services I either didn't use or had a free alternative for. One 30-minute session. Canceled eight things.

**3. Dopamine spending.** This is the one nobody talks about. Buying things to feel something. It's not greed. It's a brain reaching for a dopamine hit it's not getting from the current task. The fix is building cheaper dopamine sources into your day before boredom gets expensive.

**4. Decision paralysis.** Too many accounts, too many options, too much financial complexity leads to doing nothing, which leads to money sitting or getting spent impulsively because the intentional option felt too hard.

The ADHD Finance App I built handles all four. It has a built-in impulse timer. A subscription tracker with a quarterly audit flag. A five-category budget that reduces decision points. And it's designed to be used in five minutes, not an hour.

Because if it takes an hour to open, an ADHD brain won't open it.

## ISSUE 22: "Why I track revenue in a browser tab"

**Subject Line A:** Why I track revenue in a browser tab
**Subject Line B:** The 6-month chart that changed how I work

**Preview text:** No dashboard. No analytics app. Three numbers in a spreadsheet.

---

Hey,

I've been running a one-person business for a few months now. Multiple products, a few income streams, nothing fancy.

And I track all of it in a spreadsheet I keep open in a browser tab.

Not Stripe's dashboard. Not a fancy analytics tool. A spreadsheet. Three cells that matter. The rest is noise.

---

**Why I track revenue in a browser tab**

The first time I tried to track revenue "properly," I opened four different tools and learned nothing useful.

Stripe for payment data. Gumroad's analytics panel. A conversion tracking sheet I built but never updated. An n8n dashboard I set up and forgot about.

Four sources. No clarity. Every time I opened them I spent 20 minutes clicking around and closed everything with the same vague sense that things were "fine."

So I simplified.

Three numbers in a Google Sheet:

1. Revenue this month.
2. My target for this month.
3. My best month ever.

That's the whole thing. I update it once a week, Sunday morning, takes two minutes.

Here is what changed when I started doing it this way.

The comparison stopped being me vs. some external benchmark. It became me vs. my own best month. That's a healthier competition. It's concrete. It's in my control. And it's actually motivating in a way that "other creators are at $10K/month" never was.

The second thing that changed: I stopped checking revenue every morning.

Revenue checking was procrastination. Every open tab with a dashboard was a way to feel like I was working without doing anything that moved the number. When I limited it to once a week, I had more time to actually build.

Six months in, the chart shows something else. The line has a direction. Not smooth, not fast, but going somewhere. Looking at that over six months is more useful than any single month's data.

The lesson is not that spreadsheets are superior to real analytics tools. At scale, you need the breakdown. The lesson is that simplicity serves you when you're small. Three numbers and honesty beats twelve screens and confusion.
