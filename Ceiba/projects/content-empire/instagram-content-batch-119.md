## Post 2 — The Reach Comparison

**Hook:** 1,000 Instagram followers will see 50 to 100 of your posts. 1,000 email subscribers will receive all of them.

**Slide 1:** 1,000 Instagram followers will see 50 to 100 of your posts. 1,000 email subscribers will receive all of them.

**Slide 2:** Organic reach on Instagram for most accounts is 3 to 7% of followers per post. On Facebook it is closer to 1 to 3%. On Twitter/X it varies wildly based on algorithmic mood. On email, your reach is limited only by your deliverability and open rate, both of which you control.

**Slide 3:** A well-run email list with 1,000 subscribers and a 40% open rate delivers 400 reads per send. An Instagram account with 1,000 followers and 5% organic reach delivers 50 views per post. Same audience size. Eight times the reach.

**Slide 4:** This compounds over time. Email subscribers accumulate. Your reach does not fluctuate based on whether your last post got engagement. The list grows at a steadier rate than follower counts on most platforms.

**Slide 5:** The math case for email over social is not close. The only reason most creators prioritize social is that it is visible and social media is itself better at showing you social media metrics.

**Slide 6:** Save this comparison. Follow @behikeai for more on building owned reach.

**Caption:** The vanity metrics problem in content creation is that the visible numbers are social. You can see your follower count. Other people can see it. It feels like progress. Your email subscriber count is invisible to everyone except you, so it does not produce the same dopamine hit. But the invisible number is the one that actually predicts your business outcomes. A creator with 500 email subscribers and a solid offer can build a real income. A creator with 50,000 social followers and no list has attention they cannot reliably monetize. Build the invisible asset. It is the one that matters.

## Post 3 — What Actually Goes in an Email

**Hook:** Most creators do not start an email list because they do not know what to send.

**Slide 1:** Most creators do not start an email list because they do not know what to send.

**Slide 2:** The email list barrier is not technical. The tools are simple. The barrier is creative: people do not know what to put in the email that would be different from what they already post on social.

**Slide 3:** The answer is depth. Social media rewards short, punchy, high-frequency output. Email rewards slower, more thoughtful, more personal content. The email is where you send the thing you cannot compress into a caption.

**Slide 4:** What works in email: one main idea explained in full, a personal story or observation that connects to the idea, one practical application, and a brief note about what you are working on. That is it. One email, once a week, around 400 to 600 words.

**Slide 5:** The email should feel like a letter to a specific person, not a broadcast to a list. When it reads like a letter, it converts at a higher rate and produces more replies, which improves your deliverability.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. One email per week is a sustainable, high-value publishing cadence.

**Caption:** The email list intimidates creators because they think it requires a different content skill set. It does not. It requires the same skills as social media content but applied at a different level of depth. The social post is the trailer. The email is the full documentary. Same story, same voice, same ideas. Just more room to go further. If you have been posting on social and getting engagement, you already have the feedback you need to know what your audience wants to read more about. Write the email version of your best performing posts. Start there. The format will develop naturally from there.

## Post 4 — The Lead Magnet That Actually Converts

**Hook:** A lead magnet that solves one specific problem in 10 minutes converts better than any ebook.

**Slide 1:** A lead magnet that solves one specific problem in 10 minutes converts better than any ebook.

**Slide 2:** Most lead magnets fail because they are too broad. A "Complete Guide to Social Media Marketing" sounds valuable but is intimidating to consume and does not feel like an immediate win. Subscribers opt in and never open it.

**Slide 3:** A lead magnet that converts is hyper-specific. It solves one problem for one type of person in a defined timeframe. The more specific and the faster the payoff, the higher the conversion rate.

**Slide 4:** Examples that work: "The 5-minute weekly review template," "The email template that re-engages cold leads," "The one-page content calendar for creators posting 3 times a week." Each is specific, fast, and immediately useful.

**Slide 5:** The test: can someone use your lead magnet and get a result within 15 minutes of downloading it? If yes, it will convert. If no, it is too complex and needs to be broken down further.

**Slide 6:** Save this framework. Follow @behikeai for the full lead magnet system.

**Caption:** The lead magnet is often where email list building breaks down. Creators put together a 40-page guide that took them three weeks to write, and the conversion rate is 2%. A one-page template built in an afternoon converts at 8%. The difference is not quality. The difference is perceived speed of value. People opt in to email lists in exchange for an immediate win. The bigger and broader the resource, the less certain they are that the win will come quickly. Build your lead magnet around the smallest meaningful result you can reliably deliver. That is the thing that will grow your list.
