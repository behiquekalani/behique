**Tweet 7 (6-Tweet Thread)**
Why overnight success stories actively harm most builders:

They create the wrong mental model for how growth actually works.

If you believe success can happen fast, you will:
- Quit too early when it doesn't
- Chase tactics instead of building systems
- Optimize for the wrong metrics

Here's what the real model looks like.

/2

Growth is not linear. It's logarithmic, then exponential.

The first year feels like nothing is working.
The second year feels like slow progress.
The third year, things start to compound.
The fourth year, outsiders think you exploded overnight.

You can't compress this. You can only stay in it.

/3

The viral moment is not the cause. It's the result.

When someone's post goes viral, it looks like: they got lucky.

What actually happened: years of building reputation, refining the message, understanding the audience, until finally the algorithm amplified what was already working.

The algorithm didn't create the audience. The creator did. The algorithm just accelerated it.

/4

Skills take longer than tactics to build, but they last longer.

Most "overnight success" stories involve someone who had spent years developing a transferable skill: copywriting, product intuition, audience psychology, distribution.

When the opportunity came, they were ready. The preparation happened before anyone was watching.

/5

The short game and the long game compound differently.

Someone focused on quick wins will cycle through tactics forever.

Someone building skills, relationships, and distribution for 3 years will have assets that pay dividends for decades.

These are not the same game. Pick one.

/6

The practical implication:

Stop measuring your progress against the highlight reel.
Start measuring against your own baseline from 6 months ago.

The question is not "why haven't I blown up yet?"
The question is "am I meaningfully better and further along than I was?"

If yes, keep going.

**Tweet 8 (3-Tweet Thread)**
The three things overnight success stories never show:

1. The boring middle — months of posting with no feedback loop, no signal that it's working, no external validation.

2. The pivots — the failed version that taught them what actually worked.

3. The infrastructure — the email list, the network, the skill set that made the "overnight moment" possible.

/2

The boring middle is the most important part.

Most people's problem is not strategy. It's tolerance for the boring middle.

The person who can stay consistent for 18 months with minimal results will almost always outperform the person chasing shortcuts.

Not because patience is a virtue. Because the boring middle is where compound interest builds.

/3

The only honest overnight success story:

"I worked in obscurity for years. I built things that didn't take off. I kept learning anyway. Then one day it clicked — not because I did something special, but because everything I'd built finally had enough mass to move."

That's the real story. Every time.
