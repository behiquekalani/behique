## Tweet 1
Pricing psychology, simplified:

The number $97 outsells $100 every time. Not because people are bad at math. Because $97 signals you thought about it.

Round numbers signal laziness. Odd numbers signal precision.

## Tweet 2
Your first price is a test.

If nobody pushes back, you priced too low.

If everybody pushes back, you explained the value wrong.

The right price has a friction-to-conversion ratio that teaches you something.

## Tweet 3 (thread)
1/ Why raising your price increases sales:

This is not a paradox. It's basic signal theory.

2/ Low prices say "I'm not sure this is worth much."

High prices say "I know exactly what this is worth, and I'm comfortable saying it."

Buyers buy confidence as much as they buy product.

3/ The caveat:

The product has to be real. The confidence has to be earned.

But if you've done the work, stop apologizing for it with a $7 price tag.

## Tweet 4
Anchor pricing works because the human brain doesn't evaluate things in absolute terms.

It evaluates things relative to what it saw first.

Put the expensive option first. Always.

## Tweet 5
The free tier is not generosity. It's a filter.

You're filtering for the people who will never pay.

Build a free offer that solves one problem and creates three more your paid product solves.

## Tweet 6 (thread)
1/ The pricing mistake that kills digital product businesses:

Pricing based on how long it took you to make it.

2/ Time is your cost structure. Not your customer's problem.

Your customer is buying the outcome. Price the outcome.

3/ A 20-page PDF that saves someone 40 hours is worth more than a 300-page book they'll never finish.

Outcome. Not output. Not effort.

4/ Ask: "What is the tangible result of buying this?"

Then find someone who would pay well for that result.

Price for them, not for the person who will complain about the price regardless.

## Tweet 7
Scarcity works until it doesn't.

"Only 3 left" on a digital product is a lie people can smell.

Real scarcity: cohort access, limited coaching spots, time-bound bonuses. These work because they're true.

## Tweet 8
The most honest pricing move:

Tell people what the product is not for.

"This is not for beginners." "This is not a quick fix." "This requires 30 minutes a day."

Disqualifying the wrong buyers before they buy is better than refunding them after.

## Tweet 9
$500 product with 10 buyers = $5,000.
$27 product with 185 buyers = $4,995.

Same revenue. Completely different business.

The $500 one requires 175 fewer customer service conversations.

## Tweet 10 (thread)
1/ Three pricing principles that hold across every market:

2/ 1. Price is a signal.

Before anyone knows what your product does, price tells them whether it's worth their attention.

A $7 product says "this is a side project." A $197 product says "this is a real thing."

3/ 2. Anchoring beats discounting.

Show the full price. Then show the sale price. The gap does the selling.

Never lead with the lowest price. The first number sets the expectation.

4/ 3. The right buyer doesn't haggle.

If someone is trying to negotiate the price of your $97 course, they are not your customer.

Build your pricing for the person who sees the value and buys. Not for the person who needs convincing.
