# Twitter/X Batch 157 — How to Build a Waitlist Before Your Product Exists

---

**Tweet 1 (single)**
The biggest mistake new founders make: building the product before building the audience.

Flip the order. Build the crowd first. Then build the thing.

---

**Tweet 2 (single)**
A waitlist isn't hype.

It's proof that your idea is real before you waste 6 months building something nobody asked for.

---

**Tweet 3 (single)**
You don't need a product to start selling.

You need a problem, a promise, and a place to collect emails.

That's it.

---

**Tweet 4 (3-tweet thread)**
Tweet 1:
How to build a waitlist with zero budget, no product, and no existing audience.

This is the exact sequence that works.

Tweet 2:
Step 1: State the problem clearly.
"Freelancers waste 5 hours a week on invoicing."

Step 2: Make a specific promise.
"We're building a tool that cuts that to 10 minutes."

Step 3: Give them a reason to sign up now, not later.
"First 100 get lifetime access at 60% off."

Step 4: Put it on a single landing page and start talking about the problem publicly.

Tweet 3:
Notice what's missing from that list:
- A finished product
- An investor
- A team
- A launch date

The only thing you need is clarity on the problem and enough courage to talk about it before you've solved it.

---

**Tweet 5 (single)**
Talking about the problem you're solving IS the marketing.

Every post you write about the pain your product eliminates is a recruitment ad for your waitlist.

---

**Tweet 6 (single)**
Your waitlist number tells you one thing: whether you've explained the problem well enough.

100 signups means you explained it clearly.
10 signups means you need to reframe.

The product isn't the question yet.

---

**Tweet 7 (6-tweet thread)**
Tweet 1:
I've watched people build for a year and launch to silence.

I've also watched people build a 2,000-person waitlist in 60 days with nothing but a landing page and a content strategy.

Here's the difference.

Tweet 2:
The people who launched to silence built in private.

They didn't talk about the problem. They didn't publish. They didn't ask for feedback.

They trusted that the product would speak for itself.

It didn't.

Tweet 3:
The people who built the waitlist did something counterintuitive.

They talked about the problem constantly before they had a solution.

Every post answered a question their future customer was already asking.

They positioned themselves as the person who understood the problem best.

Tweet 4:
When you do that for 60 days, something happens.

People start asking "when is this launching?"

They're not asking because you advertised it.
They're asking because you've made them feel the problem so clearly that they want the solution to exist.

That's earned demand.

Tweet 5:
The waitlist page itself is almost irrelevant.

It needs to say what the product does, who it's for, and what happens when they sign up.

That's it. One clear sentence for each. No design awards needed.

The distribution is the hard part. The page is just the bucket.

Tweet 6:
Build in public. Talk about the problem. Collect emails.

Build the product after you know people want it.

That's not a hack. That's just the right order of operations.

---

**Tweet 8 (single)**
The waitlist is not the end goal.

It's validation. It's leverage. It's a list of people who raised their hand.

When you launch, you're not shouting into the void. You're talking to people who already said yes.

---

**Tweet 9 (3-tweet thread)**
Tweet 1:
Most people build the product, THEN look for customers.

The smarter move is finding the customers first.

Here's a simple way to do it.

Tweet 2:
Go where your ideal customer already lives.
Find the forums, subreddits, communities, Twitter replies where they're talking about the problem.

Engage. Answer questions. Offer value with no ask.

Do that for 30 days. Then mention the waitlist once.

Tweet 3:
By then, people know who you are.

Your waitlist post doesn't feel like a cold pitch.
It feels like the next logical step from someone they already trust.

That's the whole strategy.

---

**Tweet 10 (single)**
You don't need a product launch.

You need a problem launch.

Start there. The product is just the answer you're building to the question the market is already asking.
