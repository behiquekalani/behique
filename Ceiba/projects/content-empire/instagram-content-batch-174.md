## Post 1 — The Weekly Email System

**Hook:** Most creators do not have a content problem. They have a system problem.

**Slide 1:** Most creators do not have a content problem. They have a system problem.

**Slide 2:** Without a system, every email feels like starting from scratch.
What should I write about?
Is this good enough to send?
Will people unsubscribe if I send this?

The system answers all of these before you sit down to write.

**Slide 3:** The weekly email system has three components:
1. A content framework (so you always know what to write)
2. A writing schedule (so you always know when to write it)
3. A sending cadence (so your subscribers always know when to expect it)

**Slide 4:** The content framework that never runs dry:
- Monday: Identify the topic from your content notes (5 min)
- Wednesday: Draft the email in one sitting (20-30 min)
- Friday: Edit and schedule (10 min)

Total: 45 minutes per email. One email per week.

**Slide 5:** Consistency compounds.
The list that gets one email per week for a year
is worth more than the list that gets sporadic emails
whenever inspiration strikes.

Show up on a schedule. Your subscribers notice.

## Post 2 — The 4 Types of Emails That Build Trust

**Hook:** You do not need infinite ideas. You need four email types on rotation.

**Slide 1:** You do not need infinite ideas. You need four email types on rotation.

**Slide 2:** Email Type 1: The Insight
One thing you learned, observed, or realized this week.
Not a comprehensive guide.
Just one idea, explained well.
This is the most sustainable format.

**Slide 3:** Email Type 2: The Story
A personal experience that illustrates a lesson.
The more specific the story, the more universal the lesson.
People connect with stories before they connect with information.

**Slide 4:** Email Type 3: The Framework
A repeatable system your subscribers can apply immediately.
Three-step process. Four-part model. One simple diagram described in words.
Frameworks are the emails people save and share.

**Slide 5:** Email Type 4: The Resource
A curated list of tools, books, links, or examples.
Low writing effort, high perceived value.
Best used once a month, not every week.

The rotation: Insight, Story, Framework, Insight, Story, Resource.
Repeat indefinitely. Never run out of ideas.

## Post 3 — The One-Idea Rule

**Hook:** The best email you will ever send is about one thing.

**Slide 1:** The best email you will ever send is about one thing.

**Slide 2:** The biggest writing mistake in email marketing:
Trying to cover multiple topics in one email.

You dilute the message.
You confuse the reader.
You bury the CTA.

**Slide 3:** One email. One idea. One CTA.

That is the entire structure.
Anything outside that structure is noise.

**Slide 4:** How to apply the one-idea rule:
Before you write, answer this question:
"If this email could only make the reader do or believe one thing, what is it?"

Write toward that. Cut everything else.

**Slide 5:** What happens when you follow this rule:
- Your emails get shorter (better)
- Your subject lines get clearer (better)
- Your CTAs get clicked more (better)
- Your unsubscribe rate drops (better)

One idea. Every time.

## Post 4 — The Weekly Email in 30 Minutes

**Hook:** A good email does not take hours. Here is the 30-minute process.

**Slide 1:** A good email does not take hours. Here is the 30-minute process.

**Slide 2:** Minutes 0-5: Choose the idea.
Look at your content notes or recent thoughts.
Pick the one thing you want to say.
Write the subject line first. If you can't write it clearly, the idea is not ready.

**Slide 3:** Minutes 5-20: Write the draft.
Do not edit while writing.
Start with the point, then support it.
Aim for 200-400 words.
End with one clear CTA.

**Slide 4:** Minutes 20-30: Edit and finalize.
Read it once out loud.
Cut every sentence that does not move the reader forward.
Fix the subject line if it changed while writing.
Schedule for your regular send time.

**Slide 5:** What slows this process down:
- Starting without a clear topic
- Editing while writing
- Trying to make it perfect before sending
- Adding a second idea halfway through

Remove the obstacles. Hit 30 minutes consistently.

## Post 5 — What Actually Keeps Subscribers Around

**Hook:** People unsubscribe when they forget why they signed up.

**Slide 1:** People unsubscribe when they forget why they signed up.

**Slide 2:** The highest-risk moment for unsubscribes:
When the emails stop delivering on the original promise.

Your lead magnet made a specific promise.
Your weekly emails should keep making that promise, every week.

**Slide 3:** The consistency trap:
Most creators start strong and drift.
Week 1: tight, focused, on-topic.
Week 8: tangents, off-brand, unclear.

Subscribers notice drift before you do.

**Slide 4:** How to stay on-brand in every email:
Before writing, ask: "Does this serve the person who downloaded my lead magnet?"

If yes, write it.
If no, save it for a different channel.

**Slide 5:** The re-engagement strategy when drift happens:
Send a direct email that says:
"I want to make sure I am still serving you well. Reply and tell me what would be most useful right now."

You will get replies, new ideas, and a cleaned list of people who actually still want to hear from you.
