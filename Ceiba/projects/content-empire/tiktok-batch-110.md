## TikTok 2: The Content Mistakes I Kept Making (And Finally Stopped)

**Hook (0-2s):**
I made the same content mistakes for months before I stopped. Here they are.

**Script:**
Mistake one: posting without a strategy. Posting for the sake of posting generates data but not direction. Every video should have a thesis, a target person, and a next step for that person to take.

Mistake two: optimizing for views instead of conversions. I chased virality for months. The videos that went wide brought in followers who never bought anything. The videos with a clear CTA brought in fewer views but more buyers.

Mistake three: ignoring email. I treated social media as the destination for too long. The moment I started treating it as a pipeline to my email list, the revenue picture changed.

Mistake four: not batching. Creating one video at a time is exhausting and inconsistent. Batching five scripts in one sitting, recording three in one session, editing the week before publishing. This is what sustainable looks like.

Mistake five: waiting to be ready. The first twenty videos are always bad. That's not a problem to solve. That's the tuition you pay to build the skill.

## TikTok 4: What I Would Do Differently If I Started Today

**Hook (0-2s):**
If I was starting a content business today, this is what I'd change.

**Script:**
I'd build the email list from day one. Not month six. Day one. Before the first video went up, the lead magnet would exist and the link would be in the bio.

I'd pick a narrower niche. Not "digital marketing." Something like "email marketing for people who sell digital products under fifty dollars." The fear of being too specific is what keeps most people from building any real authority.

I'd publish long-form first. One newsletter issue per week as the anchor. Then strip it apart for short-form. Not the other way around. The long-form thinking forces clarity that makes the short-form better.

I'd launch a product earlier. Most creators wait too long to monetize. A simple digital guide at fifteen dollars, available from month one, tests the market and creates a customer relationship before you've invested years in an audience that might not convert.

I'd document the process instead of performing expertise. "Here's what I'm figuring out" builds more trust faster than "here's what I know." People connect to the struggle more than the polish.

## TikTok 5: The Only Metric That Matters

**Hook (0-2s):**
There is one metric that tells you whether your content business is working. It's not views.

**Script:**
Revenue per subscriber.

This number tells you how much money each person on your email list is worth over time. It tells you whether your content is attracting buyers or browsers. It tells you whether your product is solving a real problem or just a surface one.

You can have a hundred thousand followers and a revenue per subscriber of zero. That's not a business. That's an audience.

You can have eight hundred email subscribers and a revenue per subscriber of twenty dollars per year. That's sixteen thousand dollars. That's a business.

The goal is not to maximize the size of your audience. The goal is to maximize the value you create for the right people.

High revenue per subscriber means you understand your audience deeply, your content is positioned correctly, and your product solves a problem worth paying to fix.

Every decision you make about your content should move this number.
