## Post 1 — I Write 30 Pieces of Content in 3 Hours. Here's the System.

**Hook:** I don't write more than other creators. I just write once and distribute seven times.

**Slide 1:** I don't write more than other creators. I just write once and distribute seven times.

**Slide 2:** The system is called a content waterfall.
One source idea becomes:
- 1 long-form post (LinkedIn or newsletter)
- 3 Twitter threads
- 5 Instagram carousels
- 7 short clips or quotes

**Slide 3:** Step 1: Write the source post.
This is the 600-900 word version of a single idea.
It's the parent. Everything else is a child.
Time: 45 minutes max.

**Slide 4:** Step 2: Break it into three threads.
Each thread = one argument from the source post.
Already written. Just reformatted.
Time: 20 minutes.

**Slide 5:** Step 3: Pull the five strongest lines.
Each line becomes the hook for a carousel.
The rest of the carousel fills in the reasoning.
Time: 40 minutes for all five.

**Caption:** Content block is not a creativity problem. It's a systems problem.

## Post 2 — The 5-Part Formula That Writes Every Post for Me

**Hook:** Every piece of content I publish follows the same 5-part formula. Here it is.

**Slide 1:** Every piece of content I publish follows the same 5-part formula. Here it is.

**Slide 2:** Part 1: The Hook
One declarative sentence that makes the reader feel something is at stake.
Not a question. Not a mystery.
A statement that earns the next sentence.

**Slide 3:** Part 2: The Tension
What's broken about the current way?
What does the reader believe that's making their situation worse?
Name it. Make them feel seen.

**Slide 4:** Part 3: The Reframe
Here's a different way to look at it.
This is where you change the frame, not just give advice.
The reader should think: "I never thought about it that way."

**Slide 5:** Part 4: The Proof
One concrete example. One piece of data. One case study.
Specificity builds trust. Vague theory doesn't.

**Slide 6:** Part 5: The CTA
One next step. Clear, low-friction, relevant.
Never ask for everything at once.

**Caption:** Good writing is not about having better ideas. It's about having a better container for ideas.

The 5-part formula is a container. Hook, tension, reframe, proof, CTA. Once you internalize this structure, you stop staring at a blank page and start filling in a template you already know works.

The reason most people's content doesn't convert is not that the idea is bad. It's that the structure is inconsistent. One post has a great hook but no tension. Another has great proof but no reframe. The reader feels the wobble even if they can't name it.

Consistent structure creates consistent engagement. Your readers know what to expect, and they start trusting your format the same way they trust your ideas.

This formula works on Instagram carousels, Twitter threads, newsletter issues, and LinkedIn posts. One structure, all platforms.

## Post 3 — How I Batch 5 Days of Content in One Sitting

**Hook:** Batching content is not about discipline. It's about designing the session right.

**Slide 1:** Batching content is not about discipline. It's about designing the session right.

**Slide 2:** My batching session setup:
- One 3-hour block, no meetings after
- Phone on airplane mode
- One doc open, nothing else
- A list of 5 ideas I already decided on the day before

**Slide 3:** Why decide ideas the day before?
Because idea generation and content writing are different cognitive modes.
Doing both in the same session is why batching sessions fail.
Decide what to write first. Then write it.

**Slide 4:** The session order:
- First 30 min: write all 5 hooks
- Next 90 min: fill in the bodies
- Last 30 min: write all 5 captions
- Final 30 min: scheduling and hashtags

**Slide 5:** What to do when you're stuck on a slide:
Skip it. Write the next one.
Come back to the stuck slide after everything else is done.
A stuck slide is always easier to write after everything around it is clear.

**Slide 6:** The Ghost Writer Kit has templates for every format.
5 hours of setup. Weeks of content.
behike.gumroad.com | Link in bio.

**Caption:** The biggest myth about content batching is that it requires a special kind of motivation. It doesn't. It requires a designed environment and a clear constraint.

Here's the constraint that changes everything: decide what you're writing before you sit down to write. Not the day of. The night before. Five ideas, five topics, five hooks. Done. Written down somewhere.

The session itself is just execution. You already made the creative decision. Now you're just filling in the structure.

The reason most batching sessions fail is decision fatigue. You sit down to "create content" and then spend 45 minutes deciding what to create. By the time you have an idea, you're tired. The writing is bad. You quit early.

Remove the decision from the session. Make it a production run, not a brainstorm.

This single shift will double your output without adding a minute of extra work.

## Post 4 — The Idea Vault That Ends Content Block Forever

**Hook:** I haven't had content block in months. Not because I'm more creative. Because I have an idea vault.

**Slide 1:** I haven't had content block in months. Not because I'm more creative. Because I have an idea vault.

**Slide 2:** What is an idea vault?
A single place where you store every content idea the moment it appears.
Not a to-do list. Not a notes app you never open.
A living document you feed constantly and pull from deliberately.

**Slide 3:** What goes in the vault:
- Every frustration you hear from your audience
- Every question someone asks you twice
- Every insight from a book, podcast, or conversation
- Every time you think "I wish someone had told me this"

**Slide 4:** The vault rule:
Capture first. Evaluate never.
No idea gets filtered at the point of capture.
Filtering happens during session planning, not during capture.

**Slide 5:** How to use the vault:
Every week, pull 5 ideas. That's your content batch.
Over 30 days: 20 pieces of content from ideas you already had.
No brainstorming required. No blank page.

**Slide 6:** Your ideas already exist.
You just haven't built a system to catch them.
Start today. Link in bio.

**Caption:** The blank page problem is a capture problem, not a creativity problem.

You have more ideas than you think. They appear while you're in the shower, in a conversation, reading something unrelated, lying awake at 2am. The problem is that you don't have a system that captures them the moment they surface.

By the time you sit down to write, those ideas are gone. And you're staring at a blank page wondering why you have nothing to say.

The idea vault solves this at the root. Every idea gets captured immediately, without judgment, without trying to figure out if it's good. The vault grows passively. You pull from it deliberately.

One month of consistent capturing gives you a vault with 60-100 ideas. At 5 posts per week, that's 3 months of content without a single brainstorming session.

Build the vault. Feed it daily. Never face a blank page again.

## Post 5 — The Writing Habit That Changed My Output

**Hook:** Writing 500 words a day is not a productivity hack. It's the foundation of every other content system.

**Slide 1:** Writing 500 words a day is not a productivity hack. It's the foundation of every other content system.

**Slide 2:** 500 words takes 20-25 minutes.
It's one shower idea expanded into full sentences.
It's one thing you learned written down clearly.
It compounds faster than almost any other habit.

**Slide 3:** What 500 words a day produces in 30 days:
- 15,000 words of raw material
- 10-15 finished blog posts
- 30-40 Instagram carousels
- Enough for a short digital product

**Slide 4:** The rule that makes it work:
Write before you consume.
No email, no social media, no news.
First 20 minutes of your day: write.
Then consume all you want.

**Slide 5:** Why "before you consume"?
Because every piece of content you consume primes your brain to think in someone else's frame.
The morning is the only time your thinking is fully your own.
Use it.

**Slide 6:** 500 words. 20 minutes. Do it before anything else.
The Ghost Writer Kit has templates to structure every session.
behike.gumroad.com | Link in bio.

**Caption:** The most consistent writers I've studied don't have better ideas. They have a daily practice that generates ideas on schedule.

500 words a day is not about the word count. It's about showing up at the page before the day has a chance to fill your head with other people's priorities.

The morning writing session is protected time. The only rule is that it happens before any consumption. No feeds, no messages, no email. Just you and what you actually think about the thing you're working on.

Some days, those 500 words are brilliant. Most days, they're mediocre. That doesn't matter. What matters is that you showed up, and the habit got reinforced.

The mediocre days are still producing raw material. They're still building the muscle. And every now and then, something in those 500 words turns into the best thing you've ever published.

You can't predict which session will produce it. So you show up for all of them.
