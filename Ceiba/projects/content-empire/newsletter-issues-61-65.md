## Issue 61: The service-to-product transition

There is a specific order most successful solo businesses follow, and it is almost never the one they planned.

They start with a product idea. They spend weeks or months building it. Then they launch to silence, or worse, a trickle of sales that do not tell them anything useful.

The ones who break out of that loop almost always made the same shift: they went back to services first.

Here is why that works.

A service is a conversation. You are solving a real problem for a real person who is paying you to solve it. In that conversation, you learn things no market research will tell you. What the client thought the problem was before they hired you. What they had already tried. The exact language they use to describe their pain. What they would have paid to fix it six months ago.

That is your product brief. Not a brainstorm. Data from the market, bought with your time and delivered as value.

After three to five service clients, patterns emerge. The same problems show up. The same questions. The same bottlenecks. That repetition is telling you what to package.

The product you build after doing services is not a guess. It is a distillation of what you already know works, made available to people who cannot afford to hire you directly.

The transition question is not if but when. The signal is repetition. When you find yourself explaining the same thing, building the same piece, answering the same question repeatedly, that is the thing to package.

You do not quit services when you launch a product. Services fund your time while the product builds distribution. The flywheel is: services give you income and market signal, products give you leverage and scale. Both run together for a long time before you shift the ratio.

The builders who skip the service phase and go straight to products are usually the ones wondering why nobody is buying.

Go get three clients first. Build after.

## Issue 62: Time blindness

There is a symptom that affects nearly every ADHD builder and almost nobody talks about it directly: time blindness.

Time blindness is not being bad at time management. It is a structural difference in how your brain perceives time passing.

Most brains have a passive background clock. It runs automatically and gives a continuous sense of how much time has elapsed and how much remains. This is why most people feel urgency as a deadline approaches. They can feel time running out.

ADHD brains frequently lack this passive clock. There is now and there is not-now. That is it. A deadline at 5pm feels completely abstract at 10am. It is real information but it does not feel real. It does not generate urgency until it becomes now.

This explains a lot. Why two hours disappear during hyperfocus. Why being late is a chronic pattern regardless of intent. Why the urgency to start work only appears when the deadline is imminent. These are not character failures. They are the predictable output of a brain navigating without a time sense.

The fix is not motivation. You cannot motivate your way into perceiving time differently.

The fix is external structure that does the job your internal clock is not doing.

Three systems that work:

A visible countdown timer on your desk or screen. Not on your phone where you have to open an app. Visible, always. The timer makes time observable instead of invisible.

Time anchors through the day. Fixed external events, meals, calls, alarms, that divide your day into navigable segments. The space between two anchors is much easier to manage than open unstructured hours.

The 10-minute warning before every transition. An alarm that fires 10 minutes before you need to switch contexts. This gives your brain notice instead of requiring an abrupt shift.

None of these require discipline. They require setup. Do it once, use it every day.

The most important reframe here is this: you are not broken for experiencing time this way. You are navigating without an instrument most people have but do not know they have. Build the instrument externally.

## Issue 63: The first client is the hardest

Getting the second client is significantly easier than getting the first. Getting the third is easier than the second. This is not a comforting story. It is actually how it works, and understanding why changes your approach to getting started.

The first client requires you to do something without evidence that you can do it. Not proof of concept. Not testimonials. Just the belief, communicated with enough confidence, that you can solve their problem. That is hard. It requires you to trust yourself before the market does.

The second client gets to hear: "I did this for someone last month." One sentence. That sentence does the work that took you an entire pitch before.

The third client gets to hear about the first two. The objection surface shrinks with every engagement.

The first client teaches you things nothing else can. They tell you what was actually confusing about your offer. They show you where your scope was wrong. They reveal the questions you did not know to ask. They give you a real use case to describe to the next prospect.

Even a difficult first client is more valuable than waiting for a perfect one. The difficulty itself is the data.

The implication is that your goal with the first client is not profitability. It is completion. You need one finished project with a real outcome you can describe. Everything follows from that.

This is why underpricing the first one is a reasonable trade, once. Not as a standard. As an investment in getting the story. A $1,500 project that gives you a real result and a client who will give a referral is worth more than zero projects at your target price.

Get the first one done. Get the testimonial. Get the referral. Then raise the price.

The gap between zero clients and one client is the largest gap in your business. Cross it. Price and positioning fix themselves after that.

## Issue 64: Content as customer service

Most people think about content as a growth strategy. Post content, get followers, convert followers to customers.

That framing is not wrong but it misses the most durable mechanism: content as customer service at scale.

The idea is simple. When someone asks you a question, and you answer it publicly instead of privately, you have done two things. You have helped the person who asked, and you have created a discoverable record of that answer for everyone who asks the same question in the future.

This is not a content hack. It is a structural advantage.

Most people ask questions privately and get answers privately. That exchange helps one person once. A public answer helps an unlimited number of people indefinitely.

Search, YouTube, and social platforms are essentially organized around this. When someone searches for "how to price AI services," they are looking for the public answer to a question they have. The person who put that answer out there, in a form the platform can surface, gets found.

The practical application is this: pay attention to what people ask you. In DMs. In comments. In client conversations. In forums you participate in. The questions you hear repeatedly are the content you should make.

You are not guessing what people want. You are observing it directly.

This is also why the "who am I to talk about this" block is not as relevant as it feels. You do not need to be the world's foremost expert on something to answer the questions of someone who knows less than you. Most people asking search questions need a clear, practical answer from someone who has done the thing. They do not need a PhD in it.

Answer the questions you already know the answer to. Do it in public. The right people will find you.

## Issue 65: The 6-month mark

Six months of consistent building is not where you see the results. It is where you start to see the mechanism.

At month one, you are producing content or products or clients into what feels like silence. Very little feedback. Almost no compounding.

At month two and three, you start seeing the first signals. A post gets slightly more reach. A subscriber replies to an email. A product makes its third sale. None of it feels meaningful yet.

Month four and five is where most people quit. The work is accumulating but the output is not visible yet. The gap between input and outcome feels permanent.

Month six is different.

At month six, your content catalog is large enough that it surfaces in search regularly. The first 100 subscribers start referring you to people they know. Products that have been up for two months make their first sale without any promotion. An old post brings in three new email subscribers in one day with no action on your part.

This is what compounding looks like. It is not exponential. It is gradual, then it is not. You do not feel the curve turning until you are already on the other side of it.

The specific inflection points, based on what actually happens at six months:

Inbound starts replacing outbound. You spend less time finding people and more time responding to people who found you.

The second sale of a product is faster than the first. The third faster than the second. Your catalog starts selling the rest of your catalog.

Content from month one starts doing work you did not plan for. A video from four months ago gets recommended. An old newsletter gets forwarded. The archive becomes an asset.

Trust accumulates. People who have been watching you for six months feel like they know you. That relationship converts differently than a cold audience.

None of this is visible at month one. All of it is building the whole time.

The compounding is real. The timeline is real. The only variable is whether you are still building at month six.
