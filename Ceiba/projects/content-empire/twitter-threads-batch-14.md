## THREAD 1: "I tracked my mood every day for 90 days. Here is what I found."

**Hook tweet:**
I tracked my mood every single morning for 90 days.

Energy level. Emotional state. The one thing I was avoiding.

Here is what the data actually showed. (thread)

**Tweet 2:**
First, why I started.

I was having days where I worked for 6+ hours and produced almost nothing usable.

I assumed it was discipline. It was not. It was working in the wrong state on the wrong tasks with no awareness of either.

**Tweet 3:**
The method was simple.

Three questions every morning:
1. Energy level, 1-10
2. One word for emotional state
3. The one thing I was avoiding today

Took 3 minutes. No app needed. Paper worked.

**Tweet 4:**
What I found after 30 days:

My actual peak cognitive window was not when I thought it was.

I thought I was a morning person. The data said late morning. Specifically 9:30-11:30 AM.

Work done before 9:30 had more errors and weaker logic than work done in that window.

**Tweet 5:**
What I found after 60 days:

I tracked "anxious" as my morning state 31 out of 90 days.

More than a third of my mornings.

On those days, pushing through and trying to work normally did not work. A small quick win first, under 20 minutes, was the only thing that actually helped.

**Tweet 6:**
What I found after 90 days:

The avoidance item predicted my whole day more accurately than anything else.

Days I did the avoided task before noon: higher output, higher satisfaction, less mental fatigue.

Days I skipped it: more context-switching, less completion, a persistent sense of unfinished business.

**Tweet 7:**
The biggest surprise.

Creative and analytical work consistently required different emotional states.

Creative work went better when I was open or lightly excited.
Analytical work went better when I was calm.

I was scheduling these randomly. The mismatch cost me probably 30-40% of my output quality.

**Tweet 8:**
The real lesson from 90 days of data:

Productivity is not about working harder or longer.

It is about task-to-state matching. Right work in the right window for the right emotional condition.

You cannot do that without the data. You are guessing.

**Tweet 9:**
How to start:

Three questions, every morning, before you open your laptop.

Energy 1-10.
One word for emotional state.
The one thing you are avoiding.

Do it for 30 days. The patterns will show up on their own.

## THREAD 2: "The 7 emotional states that matter for productivity (not what you think)"

**Hook tweet:**
Most people have two modes: motivated and not motivated.

That binary is why they produce inconsistently.

There are actually 7 emotional states that matter for productivity. Here is what they are and what work belongs in each. (thread)

**Tweet 2:**
State 1: Focused.

Moderate arousal, clear direction, mild urgency.

Best for: output work. Writing, coding, building, executing tasks with a defined deliverable.

What kills it: interruptions, undefined tasks, and checking notifications mid-session.

**Tweet 3:**
State 2: Calm.

Low arousal, low stress, settled nervous system.

Best for: decision-making, learning complex material, reviewing work, strategic thinking.

What kills it: trying to produce fast output in this state. It is a slow and precise mode, not a fast one.

**Tweet 4:**
State 3: Curious.

Elevated arousal, open attention, no strong direction.

Best for: research, exploration, ideation, finding connections between ideas.

What kills it: forcing a concrete deliverable before the exploration has finished. Curiosity needs room.

**Tweet 5:**
State 4: Anxious.

Elevated arousal, negative valence, scattered attention.

What works here: small, completable tasks only. Quick wins. Nothing open-ended, nothing ambiguous, nothing that requires sustained concentration.

What does not work: trying to power through it with a deadline.

**Tweet 6:**
State 5: Depleted.

Low energy, low motivation, difficulty initiating.

What works: passive inputs. Reading. Watching something educational. Light admin. Physical movement.

What does not work: anything that requires high cognitive output. The tank is empty. Refill before you drive.

**Tweet 7:**
State 6: Excited.

High arousal, positive, forward-moving energy.

What works: starting new things. Creative generation. Pitching, selling, connecting.

What does not work: detailed revision, technical problem-solving, tasks that require patience. Excitement makes you rush.

**Tweet 8:**
State 7: Regulated.

This one is not a state you work in. It is a precondition for working in any of the other states.

Regulated means your nervous system is not in stress response. Without it, even "focused" is inaccessible.

Short walk, breathing, cold water. Get here first.

**Tweet 9:**
The framework in practice:

Before you start your workday, identify your current state.

Then look at your task list and assign: which tasks require focused, which require calm, which require curious.

Do the match. Not the default.

**Tweet 10:**
Most people skip this step and then wonder why they sit at a desk for four hours and do not produce anything real.

The state-to-task mismatch is the problem. It is invisible until you name it.

## THREAD 3: "Calm and focused are not the same state. Here is the difference and why it matters."

**Hook tweet:**
"Getting focused" is not one thing.

Calm and focused are two different states that require different environments, different music, and different types of work.

Conflating them is why you can sit at your desk for 4 hours and produce almost nothing. (thread)

**Tweet 2:**
Calm is a low-arousal positive state.

Your nervous system is settled. You are available but not activated. Processing is gentle and broad.

Best for: reviewing work, making nuanced decisions, learning, absorbing complex material, writing that needs real depth.

**Tweet 3:**
Focused is a moderate-to-high-arousal positive state.

Attention is narrowed. There is intentional tension toward a target. Processing is fast and directed.

Best for: producing output at pace, building, coding, getting through high-volume tasks, executing against a deadline.

**Tweet 4:**
What happens when you mismatch:

Trying focused work in a calm state: you drift. The urgency is not there.

Trying calm work in a focused state: you rush it, skip nuance, and miss things you will regret later.

Neither produces what you need.

**Tweet 5:**
How to reach calm on purpose.

Reduce inputs before you start. No notifications. No unread messages. Five minutes with no stimulus.

Music: ambient, low tempo, no lyrics or lyrics in a language you do not understand.

Environment: natural light, low noise, no urgency signals anywhere in view.

**Tweet 6:**
How to reach focused on purpose.

You need slightly elevated arousal. Use an artificial constraint with stakes.

Set a 25-minute timer. Name the specific deliverable before you start. Make it small enough to finish in the window.

Music: mid-tempo instrumental or lo-fi with consistent rhythm. The rhythm is an external metronome for your attention.

**Tweet 7:**
The ADHD dimension.

For ADHD brains, the transition between states is harder than it is for neurotypical people.

You cannot just decide to be calm. You cannot just decide to be focused.

You need rituals that signal each state to your nervous system. The same playlist, the same environment setup, repeated consistently.

**Tweet 8:**
There is also a third state that has to come before either one.

Regulated.

If you are dysregulated, anxious, depleted, or emotionally activated, neither calm nor focused is accessible. You have to get to baseline first.

Physiology first. Walk, breathing, cold water. Then work.

**Tweet 9:**
The practical system:

Label your tasks before the day starts.
C = requires calm.
F = requires focused.

Schedule C tasks in your natural low-arousal windows.
Schedule F tasks in your activation windows.

Use the corresponding music and environment for each.

## THREAD 4: "Music therapy is real. Here is the science behind what I built."

**Hook tweet:**
Music affects your brain state in measurable, predictable ways.

It is not soft or subjective. It is neuroscience.

Here is what the research shows and how I built a focus system around it. (thread)

**Tweet 2:**
The brain has a response to rhythm called entrainment.

When you listen to a consistent rhythmic pattern, your brainwaves synchronize to it. The frequency of the music influences the frequency of your neural oscillations.

This is not metaphor. This is measurable with an EEG.

**Tweet 3:**
Different brainwave states correspond to different mental modes:

Beta waves (13-30 Hz): active thinking, focus, problem-solving.
Alpha waves (8-12 Hz): calm, relaxed alertness, light creative states.
Theta waves (4-7 Hz): deep relaxation, intuition, creative flow.

Music tempo and frequency influence which mode you are in.

**Tweet 4:**
Binaural beats work by playing slightly different frequencies in each ear.

Your brain processes the difference and generates a beat at that frequency.

40 Hz binaural beats (gamma range) are associated with focused attention and cognitive processing. Multiple studies have shown measurable improvements in working memory and concentration.

**Tweet 5:**
Why tempo matters for focus.

Uptempo music, roughly 120-140 BPM, matches the pace of active cognitive work. It serves as an external rhythm that keeps your attention moving forward.

Music that is too slow creates drift. Music that is too fast creates scattered attention. The middle range is what you want for focused output work.

**Tweet 6:**
Why lyrics matter less than people think.

Lyrics activate the language processing centers of your brain. If you are doing work that involves reading, writing, or language processing, lyrics compete directly with your task.

For focused writing or reading: no lyrics, or lyrics in a language you do not speak.
For repetitive physical or mechanical work: lyrics are fine.

**Tweet 7:**
The emotional state dimension.

Music does not just affect arousal. It affects valence, meaning whether your emotional state is positive or negative.

Major keys and upbeat rhythms shift valence positive. Minor keys and slower tempos can lower it.

For anxious states, positive-valence ambient music can shift the mood without overstimulating. For depleted states, slightly upbeat instrumental can lift energy without demanding attention.

**Tweet 8:**
How I built this into a system.

I have specific playlists for each state:
- Calm and reviewing: Brian Eno ambient, very low tempo
- Focused and building: lo-fi hip hop, 90-100 BPM
- Creative and generating: mid-tempo electronic, varied but rhythmic
- Regulated and starting: a specific short playlist I always use to signal "work mode" to my brain

The consistency matters. Your brain learns the association over time.

**Tweet 9:**
For ADHD specifically.

ADHD involves dysregulation of the dopamine system. Music activates the reward pathways in the brain and can serve as a mild dopamine supplement during tasks that do not provide their own intrinsic reward.

This is one of the reasons ADHD people often report working better with music than without.

## THREAD 5: "ADHD + anxiety + building a business: the combination nobody talks about honestly"

**Hook tweet:**
ADHD and anxiety together is not twice as hard as having one.

It is a specific combination that creates specific problems nobody in the productivity space actually talks about honestly.

Here is what it actually looks like, and what has helped. (thread)

**Tweet 2:**
ADHD and anxiety seem like opposites.

ADHD: underactivated, seeking stimulation, impulsive, scattered.
Anxiety: overactivated, avoiding threat, rigid, hypervigilant.

But they coexist in a lot of people. And when they do, they create a loop that is hard to break.

**Tweet 3:**
The loop looks like this:

ADHD makes it hard to start and sustain tasks.
The incomplete tasks create anxiety.
Anxiety makes it harder to start the next task.
The avoidance feeds the ADHD's stimulation-seeking.
Stimulation-seeking produces more half-finished things.
More half-finished things produce more anxiety.

Round and round.

**Tweet 4:**
The business dimension makes it worse.

When you are building something, you are almost never done with anything. Everything is partial, in progress, uncertain.

For someone with ADHD and anxiety, that environment is not just uncomfortable. It can become genuinely disabling if you do not have systems to interrupt the loop.

**Tweet 5:**
What has actually helped me.

Not motivation. Not discipline. Not "just push through."

Small completable tasks as the entry point to the day. Not the most important task. The smallest task that can be genuinely finished in under 20 minutes.

Completion is a drug. One win lowers the anxiety enough to access real work.

**Tweet 6:**
What has helped with the scattered attention.

Single-focus days. Not multi-project days.

When I give myself permission to work on one thing all day and explicitly delay everything else, the anxiety about the other things decreases. The ADHD has a clear target.

Trying to juggle five projects in one day is compatible with neither ADHD nor anxiety.

**Tweet 7:**
What has helped with the avoidance.

Naming the avoided thing out loud, or writing it down, every morning before starting work.

The avoidance has less power once it is seen. It is strongest when it is ambient and unnamed, sitting somewhere in the background of your awareness draining your cognitive resources all day.

**Tweet 8:**
What has not helped.

Inspirational content about "just starting."
Productivity systems designed for neurotypical people.
Trying to replicate someone else's morning routine exactly.
Telling myself I will feel like doing it later.

None of these address the actual mechanism. The actual mechanism is nervous system regulation, not willpower.

**Tweet 9:**
The realistic picture.

Some days the combination wins. That is true and it is okay.

The goal is not to eliminate the ADHD or the anxiety. It is to build a system that still produces output even when both are present.

Small wins. Single focus. Name the avoidance. Regulate before you produce.

That is the framework. It is not glamorous. It works.

**Tweet 10:**
If you are building something while carrying this combination, I want you to know it is not a character flaw.

It is a hardware and software difference that requires different strategies. You are not broken. You need a different system.
