## Script 66.1 — Building Without an Audience

**Hook (0-3s)**
"Nobody's watching. That's actually your advantage."

**Body (3-50s)**
Most people wait until they have followers to start building. That's backwards.

Point 1: When no one's watching, you can experiment without the cost of reputation. You can post bad content, test pricing, ship ugly products, and learn without anyone screenshotting your failures.

Point 2: The builders who grow fastest started in public before they had an audience. They didn't wait for permission. The audience came because they were already moving.

Point 3: Zero followers means zero pressure. That's the most creative environment you'll ever have. Use it before it disappears.

Point 4: Your first hundred followers aren't from going viral. They're from being consistent enough that the algorithm finally shows you to someone who needed what you were already making.

The audience is the byproduct, not the starting condition.

## Script 66.3 — Pricing Mistakes

**Hook (0-3s)**
"You're not charging too much. You're explaining it wrong."

**Body (3-50s)**
Pricing is a communication problem before it's a math problem.

Point 1: Most first-time creators underprice because they're comparing their product to the cost of making it, not the value it delivers. A $27 PDF that saves someone 40 hours of research is not a $27 product. It's a time machine.

Point 2: The second mistake is copying competitors without understanding positioning. Someone charging $9 built their audience to buy at $9. Someone charging $97 built theirs to expect that. You inherit the context when you copy the number.

Point 3: The third mistake is tiered pricing that's confusing. One product, one price, one clear promise. You can add tiers later. Not at launch.

Point 4: Test upward, not downward. Drop price only after you understand why it's not converting. Most of the time it's the sales page, not the price tag.

Price is the last thing to fix. Fix the message first.

**Caption**
Pricing isn't a number you pick. It's a position you defend. Most people lose before they start because they never learned this.

## Script 66.4 — Content That Converts, Not Just Goes Viral

**Hook (0-3s)**
"Viral content feeds the ego. Converting content feeds the business."

**Body (3-50s)**
These are not the same thing and most creators confuse them.

Point 1: Viral content gets shared because it's surprising, funny, or emotionally triggering. Converting content gets shared because it's useful to a specific person with a specific problem. One brings traffic. The other brings buyers.

Point 2: The format that converts is not the one with the most views. It's the one that makes the right person feel like you read their mind. Specificity beats reach every time.

Point 3: A 10,000-view video that drives 200 buyers outperforms a 1,000,000-view video that drives 20. Optimize for conversion rate, not vanity metrics.

Point 4: The shift is simple. Stop writing for the algorithm. Start writing for one person who has a problem you solved. Describe their situation better than they can. The offer follows naturally.

Virality is a lottery. Conversion is a system you can build.

## Script 66.5 — Your First Digital Product

**Hook (0-3s)**
"Your first digital product doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to exist."

**Body (3-50s)**
Here's the minimum viable path to your first product.

Point 1: Start with a problem you already solved. Not a problem you researched. Something you personally worked through and have a repeatable answer for. That's your product brief.

Point 2: The format is irrelevant at first. PDF, checklist, template, short guide. The medium is not the value. The solution is. Pick the format that requires the least production time and start there.

Point 3: Price it at $7 to $27. You're not optimizing for revenue yet. You're optimizing for proof of concept. One sale from a stranger is more valuable than a hundred pre-orders from friends.

Point 4: Ship it before you're ready. The feedback from real buyers is more valuable than six more weeks of refinement in private. Done and imperfect beats polished and invisible.

The first product teaches you everything the second one profits from.
