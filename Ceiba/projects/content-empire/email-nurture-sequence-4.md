## Email 16

Send day: Day 42
Subject line: What I learned from 25 products (3 sold well, most did not)
A/B variant: The 3 digital product types that actually sell
Preview text: Most of what I built flopped. Here is what the winners had in common.

---

I have shipped around 25 digital products across different formats over the past year.

Guides, checklists, templates, system walkthroughs, mini-courses. Some were things I was genuinely proud of. Some were made in a weekend to test an idea. Almost all of them taught me something.

Three product types consistently sold. The rest did not.

The ones that sold: tools that saved time on a specific, recurring task. Guides that answered one question very precisely. Systems that removed decision-making from a process someone already had.

The ones that did not sell: broad guides covering too many topics. Products targeting people who had not yet decided to start something. Anything that required the buyer to change a habit before they got value.

The pattern, once I saw it: the products that sold solved a problem that already existed in someone's workflow. The ones that flopped tried to convince someone they had a problem first.

This sounds obvious. It was not obvious to me at the time. I thought quality and completeness were the main factors. They are not. Specificity is.

The AI Employee Guide sells because people already want to automate parts of their business and do not know how to start. It does not ask them to believe in automation first. It assumes they already do.

The Social Media Pipeline Guide sells because people already hate the posting grind. It does not argue that social media matters. It assumes they already know.

If you are thinking about making a digital product, that is the test I would run before building anything: does my buyer already feel the problem, or am I hoping to create the feeling?

Everything in the Behike catalog was built with that filter.

## Email 17

Send day: Day 46
Subject line: The Spanish AI market is about 3 years behind
A/B variant: If you speak Spanish, there is almost no competition in AI tools content
Preview text: I was born in Puerto Rico. This gap is something I am actively building around.

---

Something I have not talked about much in this list is the Spanish-language angle.

I grew up in Puerto Rico. I am bilingual. And about a year ago I started paying close attention to where the AI tools conversation was happening in Spanish versus English.

The gap is significant.

In English, there are thousands of creators covering AI tools, automation, productivity systems, Claude, ChatGPT, n8n, Zapier. The space is crowded. Getting attention requires either a very specific niche or an already-large audience.

In Spanish, the equivalent content barely exists. Most of what is out there is either poorly translated English content or very surface-level overviews. The actual how-to layer, the practical "here is how I built this system" content, is almost entirely missing.

Latin America has hundreds of millions of people. Many of them are business owners, freelancers, and students who want the same things the English-speaking audience wants. They want to work less, earn more, automate things that drain time. They are not getting that content in a voice that speaks to their experience.

I am not saying this to hype up an opportunity. I am saying it because this is what I am actively building around. Some of what I make goes to the English market. Some of it goes to the Spanish market. And in the Spanish market, I am one of a very small number of people doing this at all.

If you are bilingual and thinking about building something in the AI or automation space, this is worth paying attention to. The Spanish market for this content is not saturated. It is barely started.

I have a guide and a few resources in Spanish that I have been building out. If you want access to those, they are in the store.

## Email 18

Send day: Day 50
Subject line: How I use Claude Code and I am not a senior developer
A/B variant: Claude Code is not just for engineers. Three ways I use it every day.
Preview text: It makes mistakes. Here is how I catch them and use it anyway.

---

I want to be upfront about something before I describe how I use Claude Code.

I am a computer engineering student. I know enough to read code, understand what is happening, and catch obvious errors. I am not a senior developer. I do not have years of production experience. That matters for context.

With that said, Claude Code has changed the speed at which I can build and ship things.

Three ways I use it daily:

One. Automating data workflows. I have a product research pipeline that pulls data from multiple sources, scores items, and outputs a ranked list. I built most of that by describing what I wanted in plain language, reviewing what Claude Code produced, and iterating. I could not have built it from scratch in the same timeframe.

Two. Editing files and fixing broken scripts. When something in my automation stack stops working, I paste the error into Claude Code and describe what was supposed to happen. It usually finds the problem faster than I would searching through logs manually.

Three. Writing code I can read but not write from memory. There are patterns I understand conceptually but would take me an hour to write correctly from scratch. Claude Code produces a working version in a few minutes. I review it, adjust it, and move on.

The limitation I have run into most often: it confidently produces code that has subtle bugs. The logic looks right but something is off in the edge cases. If you do not understand enough to review the output, you will not catch these. This is the actual barrier to entry, not the tool itself.

That is why I put together the Claude Code course. It is not for senior developers. It is for people who can think clearly about what they want to build and need help translating that into working code.

These are the highest-signal subscribers. Anyone who took the time to write more than a number is telling you exactly what to make next. Read each one. Tag by topic. Recurring themes in these replies are product ideas.

Prioritize personal replies to these. Even a two-sentence response builds more loyalty than any automated sequence.

**Segment E: No reply**

Re-engagement sequence after 14 days of silence. Keep it short. One email, direct subject line, low-pressure ask. If no engagement after that, move to a low-frequency track (once per month) rather than removing them. Some people buy after months of silence.
