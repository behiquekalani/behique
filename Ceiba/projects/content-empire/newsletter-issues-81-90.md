## Issue 81: The Launch Email That Got Me My First 10 Sales
**Subject:** The exact email that got me 10 sales
**Preview text:** It was 3 sentences. Here's what I wrote and why it worked.

I want to tell you about the first email that actually moved people to buy.

Not a sequence. One email. Three sentences in the body. I sent it on a Tuesday at 11am to a list of 34 people, most of whom I knew personally or had met through Instagram DMs.

Here is what it said, roughly: "I built something that helped me think about building a solo business. It's $9. Here's the link."

No countdown timer. No "only 3 left." No before-and-after story. Just an honest description of what I made and where to get it.

Six people bought within 24 hours. Four more in the following week. That was my first 10 sales.

I want to talk about why that worked, because it goes against almost everything you read about launch emails.

Most launch email advice tells you to build tension. Scarcity, social proof, a big promise, a story arc. And for a cold audience that doesn't know you, that structure makes sense. But my list of 34 people knew me. They knew I build things. They knew I don't hype. So the short, honest email landed because it matched what they already believed about me.

The signal was: this is real, this person made something, they're not performing.

What didn't work: I sent a follow-up the next day that was longer, more polished, tried to explain the value proposition in detail. Two people unsubscribed. Nobody bought from that one.

So the lesson I took is this: your first sales come from trust, not from copy. And trust is built by every interaction that came before the email, not by the email itself.

If you have a small list and you're about to launch something, here's what I'd suggest. Write the shortest honest email you can. Tell them what you made, what problem it solves, and where to get it. Do not explain everything. Let the page do that.

The other thing: send it to everyone who has talked to you in the last 60 days, not just your newsletter list. DMs count. Discord counts. If someone asked about what you're building, they're on the list.

Your launch email is not a marketing document. It's a message to people who already have a reason to care. Treat it that way.

## Issue 82: How to Price a Bundle Offer (The Math I Use)
**Subject:** How I price bundles (the actual math)
**Preview text:** There's a formula I use. It's not just "cheaper than buying separate."

Pricing a bundle is one of those things that looks simple and is actually full of traps.

The obvious approach is to add up the individual prices and discount the total. If product A is $27 and product B is $37, you sell the bundle for $49 and call it a deal. That works as a starting point but it misses the real logic.

Here's how I think about it.

First: what is the bundle for? There are two types. The first is a convenience bundle. You're grouping products that someone would reasonably buy together anyway, and you're removing the friction of buying them separately. The price should reflect that the customer is saving time and getting a coherent package. For this type, I price at 30-40% off the sum of individual products.

The second type is a thematic bundle. You're creating something that didn't exist before, a new offer that has its own logic. "Everything you need to go from idea to first dollar in 30 days." That's not three products in a zip file. That's a curriculum. For this type, I price based on the outcome, not the components. I ask: what is a customer willing to pay for that specific result? Then I work backward to see if the value makes sense.

The math I actually use looks like this.

I take the highest-priced product in the bundle and multiply by 1.5. That becomes my floor. So if the most valuable product is $37, I won't price the bundle below $55. That way the bundle always represents a clear upgrade from buying the top product alone.

Then I look at what the full stack would cost at individual prices. I want the bundle to be at least 35% off that total, because anything less doesn't register as a real deal to most people.

The ceiling is about perceived value. I ask someone I trust: "If you didn't know what was in this, what would you pay based on the promise?" That gut check usually tells me more than the math alone.

One thing I learned the hard way: don't price bundles too low. A bundle at $19 for three products tells people the products aren't worth much. I had a bundle that sold badly at $19, repriced it to $39 with better framing, and it outsold the original by 4x. Price is a signal.

The other thing: update your bundle page every 60-90 days. Add a new product to it. Keeping it fresh gives you a reason to email your list about it again without it feeling like a re-promotion.

If you're sitting on 2 or more products right now, the bundle is the easiest next offer you can launch. It takes one afternoon and costs you nothing.

## Issue 83: What I Learned From My First AI Service Client
**Subject:** My first AI service client: what I got wrong
**Preview text:** I thought the hard part was the deliverable. It wasn't.

I landed my first AI service client through a DM conversation that took about a week. They were a small e-commerce business. They wanted help setting up automated content for their social channels.

I thought the hard part would be the technical work. Building the n8n workflow, writing the prompts, connecting everything to their accounts. That stuff took maybe 6 hours total.

The hard part was everything else.

Here's what I didn't prepare for.

The first thing: scope creep starts before the contract. During the sales conversation, I was saying yes to everything because I wanted the deal. "Can it also do email?" Sure. "Can we add a weekly report?" Absolutely. By the time we agreed on price, I had committed to about twice the work I originally quoted for. I was excited. I didn't stop to recalculate.

Lesson: write down every single thing the client mentions wanting. Before you quote, go through that list and decide explicitly what's in scope and what's not. Send it to them in writing. "Here's what I'm building. Here's what's not included." This conversation, done before payment, prevents 80% of the problems.

The second thing: clients don't know how to evaluate AI output. My workflows were producing solid content. But my client kept asking if it was "good" in a way that meant they didn't have a framework for judging it. I spent hours on feedback rounds that were really just them feeling uncertain, not them identifying specific problems.

Lesson: build in an approval system from day one. Instead of sending raw outputs for open-ended feedback, send three variations and ask them to pick one. Constrain the decision. People make better decisions when the options are bounded.

The third thing: documentation is your leverage. By week three, my client had forgotten what I had originally promised and started asking for things I had never agreed to. Because I hadn't documented our original scope clearly, I had no easy way to point to it.

Lesson: after every call, send a 5-bullet "here's what we aligned on" summary. Copy it to a shared doc. This takes 10 minutes and it will save you hours of confusion later.

The client ended up being a good one. We figured it out. But I went in thinking I was selling a technical product and I was actually selling a working relationship. The technical product was the easy half.

## Issue 84: The Bilingual Content Strategy in Practice
**Subject:** What my bilingual content numbers actually show
**Preview text:** I've been running English and Spanish content for 90 days. Here's the data.

About three months ago I started posting in both English and Spanish consistently. Not translating posts. Writing separate content for separate audiences. I want to tell you what I've actually seen.

The Spanish content gets less reach but higher engagement rate. When I post in Spanish about AI tools or building a business, the saves and shares per view are roughly 2x what I see on English content with similar topics. My theory is that the Spanish-speaking audience for this type of content is smaller and more hungry. Less noise in their feed. When something relevant appears, they engage harder.

The English content grows faster in raw numbers. More impressions, more follows per post. But the conversion rate to email subscribers from English content is lower. Someone following because they saw a viral reel is a different kind of follower than someone who found you through a specific, relevant post about their actual situation.

Here's what surprised me: the posts that do best in Spanish are not translations of my English hits. They are posts that speak to the specific context of building in Latin America. The friction of international payments. Building without a startup ecosystem behind you. Growing an audience in a market that big creators haven't fully discovered yet. That specificity is what connects.

The content I thought would work in Spanish was "AI tools" and "how to make money online." The content that actually worked was closer to "building without the safety net that US creators take for granted."

What I'm going to do differently: I'm going to write the Spanish content first more often. Not as translation but as its own voice. Then decide whether to adapt it for English, rather than the other way around.

One more thing the numbers show: posting consistency matters more than post quality at this stage. The accounts I've studied that are growing in both languages are posting 5-7 times a week per platform, not posting 3 polished pieces a week. Volume creates surface area. Surface area creates discovery.

My current split is about 60% English, 40% Spanish. I think the right ratio for me is 50/50, and I'm moving toward that.

## Issue 85: Why I Built a Quiz Instead of a Sales Page
**Subject:** I built a quiz instead of a sales page. Here's why.
**Preview text:** Sales pages answer questions. Quizzes ask them. That difference matters.

Most people building a product funnel go straight to the sales page. Here's the offer, here's the price, here are the testimonials, here's the button. That's the standard move.

I tried something different for one of my recent products. I built a quiz first.

The quiz is six questions. It takes about 90 seconds to complete. At the end, it tells you which of my products matches your current situation. If you're just starting out, it sends you to the free lead magnet. If you've been building for a year or more, it sends you to the paid product.

Here's why I did this.

The main reason is qualification. A sales page makes the same pitch to everyone who lands on it, regardless of where they are in their journey. A beginner and an advanced builder see the same page. The beginner feels overwhelmed. The advanced builder thinks it's too basic. Both leave.

The quiz segments people before they ever see a pitch. By the time they land on the product recommendation, it's positioned as a fit for their specific situation, not a generic offer. That's a fundamentally different conversation.

The second reason is data. Every quiz response tells me something. I can see where people cluster. If 60% of my quiz takers identify as "early stage, no sales yet," that tells me what content to write, what products to build next, and what problem my audience most needs help with. A sales page gives me conversion data. A quiz gives me audience data.

The third reason is trust. The quiz asks questions before making a pitch. That ordering matters psychologically. It signals that I care about fit, not just about selling. Even if someone ends up on a page they would have found anyway, they arrived there through a different path. They feel understood.

Does it convert better than a direct sales page? Honestly, I don't have enough data to say definitively. The conversion rate on quiz completions to product page visits is around 68%. From there, the sales page converts at roughly the same rate as my other pages.

But the quality of buyers who came through the quiz is noticeably higher. Lower refund rate. More replies to post-purchase emails. More people who actually use the product.

That's what I optimized for.

## Issue 86: La Ceiba Guild: What I'm Building and Why
**Subject:** La Ceiba Guild: what it is and why I'm building it
**Preview text:** It's not a course. It's not a Discord server. Let me explain.

I've been thinking about community for a long time. Not because I think it's the obvious next move for a content creator, but because I keep running into the same problem: building alone is slow, and most online communities don't actually help you build faster.

So I'm trying to design something different. Here's what La Ceiba Guild is supposed to be.

The name matters. In Taino cosmology, the ceiba tree is the axis that connects all three worlds. That's the metaphor I'm working with: a structure that connects people across different levels of the journey, not just people at the same stage talking to each other.

The model I've seen fail most often is the forum model. Someone posts a question. Someone else answers. There's no real relationship, no accountability, no forward movement. It's information exchange, not community.

What I'm building instead is a cohort model with accountability built in. Small groups, 6-8 people, each group working on something specific for 30-60 days. Revenue goals. Content output goals. Product launch milestones. Each group has a lead, someone who has already done the thing the group is working toward.

The idea is that you don't just get answers. You get people who are watching you, who will notice if you go quiet, who have a stake in your progress because you have a stake in theirs.

Why am I building this instead of just scaling content?

Because I think the people who would benefit most from what I know are not people who need more information. They're people who need a structure that makes action harder to avoid. The information is everywhere. The accountability is rare.

I don't know exactly what La Ceiba Guild will look like in six months. Right now it's more of a design experiment than a finished product. I'm running a small beta with people who have been asking about it.

If you want to be part of the beta, reply to this email. Tell me one specific thing you're trying to accomplish in the next 60 days.

## Issue 87: The Focus DJ App: How I Built a Tool I Use Every Day
**Subject:** I built a focus app for my own brain
**Preview text:** Not for a market. For me. Here's how that changes the building process.

Most of what I build, I'm building with an audience in mind. What does this person need? What problem are they paying to solve? That's the right way to build a product.

The Focus DJ app was different. I built it entirely for myself.

The problem I was trying to solve: I can't focus with silence, but I also can't focus with music that has lyrics. I've tried every Spotify focus playlist. They either have lyrics that hijack my attention or they're so ambient that they feel like background wallpaper and I drift. What I needed was something that generated music in real time, tuned to what I was trying to do.

So I built it.

The app takes three inputs: the type of task you're doing (creative work, analytical work, writing, learning), your current energy level (low, medium, high), and your preferred sonic texture (minimal, layered, or chaotic). Based on those three things, it generates a sound environment using a local audio synthesis layer. No streaming. No lyrics. No fixed playlist.

Building it took about three weeks of scattered evenings. I used Python for the backend logic, a small React frontend that I didn't overthink, and a synthesis library that I spent most of my time learning.

What I learned from building something purely for my own use:

The feedback loop is immediate. I used it every day from the first working prototype. Every irritation, every thing that almost worked but didn't, I fixed it that week. No user research needed. I was the user.

The scope stayed small. Because I wasn't trying to market it, I didn't add features I didn't need. There's no analytics dashboard. There's no settings panel with 40 options. There's the three inputs and the output. That's it.

The product is better because I care about it in a way that's not commercially motivated. When I fix something in Focus DJ, I fix it because it was annoying me personally. That's a different energy than fixing something because a user complained.

I'm now thinking about whether to release it publicly. The honest answer is I haven't decided. It works for my brain. I don't know if it works for yours.

## Issue 88: Consistent vs. Viral: What Actually Builds an Audience
**Subject:** Consistency vs. viral: what the data actually says
**Preview text:** I tested both. The answer isn't what most people want to hear.

I want to settle this question with my own data, because the discourse around it is mostly noise.

For about 45 days, I focused on making content that had the best chance of going viral. High-concept hooks. Trending audio. Format-chasing. I was studying what was popping in my niche and trying to replicate the structure while putting my own ideas in.

For the next 45 days, I ignored virality entirely. I posted on a fixed schedule, three times a week, the same format every time, about topics I actually wanted to talk about whether or not they were trending.

Here's what happened.

The viral-chasing period produced two posts that hit above 50k views. Both drove a spike in followers. The following week, both of those follower spikes were largely inactive. They followed because the content was stimulating in the moment. They didn't follow because they care about me or what I'm building. The email list conversion from those new followers was under 1%.

The consistency period produced no viral posts. My top post in that period did about 8,000 views. But the email list conversion from new followers during that period was around 6%. The people who followed were people who saw multiple posts from me, decided they liked the perspective, and wanted more.

The compound math here matters a lot. One viral post that adds 500 passive followers is not the same as 45 days of consistent posting that adds 200 active ones. Active followers read your emails, buy your products, and tell other people. Passive followers inflate your count.

I'm not saying virality is worthless. If a piece of content is genuinely good and it happens to go viral, that's great. But optimizing for virality means optimizing for the stimulus of the post, not for the relationship it builds. Those are different targets.

What I'm doing now: fixed schedule, consistent format, topics I would write about even if nobody was watching. The audience is smaller than I'd like. But every person on my list is there because they chose to be there.

That's the one I'm building on.
