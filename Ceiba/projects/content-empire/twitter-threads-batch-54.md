### Thread 1 — The AI Stack I Actually Use for Content Creation

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
I have tested 30+ AI tools for content creation. Most are noise. Here are the 5 I actually use every week and what each one does that the others do not.

**Tweet 2:**
Tool 1: Claude. Primary writing assistant. Better than GPT-4o for long-form reasoning and for maintaining a consistent voice when you give it proper context. I use it for YouTube scripts, newsletter drafts, and copy iteration.

**Tweet 3:**
Tool 2: Perplexity. Research and fact-checking. Gives sources, is current, and does not hallucinate as aggressively as Claude or ChatGPT on factual questions. Any time I need external data, this is the first stop.

**Tweet 4:**
Tool 3: Whisper (via Python script). Voice transcription. I record voice notes throughout the day and they get auto-transcribed into my Notion inbox via a Telegram bot. This is the highest-leverage tool in my stack because it captures ideas without interrupting flow.

**Tweet 5:**
Tool 4: Runway or CapCut AI. Short-form video editing. CapCut for quick edits and auto-captions. Runway for any AI video generation or style transfer I am testing. The short-form video creation time dropped by 60% with these two.

**Tweet 6:**
Tool 5: ElevenLabs. AI voice for any content where I want audio without recording. Product demos, course content previews, some social content. The voice quality is good enough that most listeners cannot tell.

**Tweet 7:**
What I do not use: Jasper, Copy.ai, Writesonic. They are wrappers around the same models with worse interfaces and higher prices. If you have API access to Claude or OpenAI, those tools add no value.

**Tweet 8:**
The integration: voice note gets transcribed into Notion (Whisper). I process the idea into a script outline (Claude). I record or generate the video content (CapCut/Runway). I write the copy derivatives (Claude). I research any factual claims (Perplexity).

**Tweet 9:**
Total monthly cost for this stack: Claude Pro ($20), Perplexity Pro ($20), CapCut ($10), ElevenLabs ($11). Runway is usage-based, roughly $20/month for my volume. Total: about $80/month. Whisper I run locally, cost is zero.

**Tweet 10:**
The ROI: this stack saves me roughly 15-20 hours per month compared to doing everything manually. At any reasonable hourly value of my time, the $80 cost is the best investment in the business.

**Tweet 11:**
Start with one tool, not five. If you are building a content business, start with Claude. It covers ideation, drafting, and iteration. Add tools only when you hit a specific limitation that another tool solves. Do not collect tools.

### Thread 2 — How AI Changed Content Creation (And What It Did Not Change)

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
AI did not make content creation effortless. It made the easy parts faster so you can spend more time on the hard parts. Most people have this backwards.

**Tweet 2:**
The easy parts that AI handles well: generating first drafts, repurposing long-form into short-form, formatting, summarizing, translating, and researching. These are the parts that were always bottlenecks of time, not of creativity.

**Tweet 3:**
The hard parts that AI still cannot do well: having a genuine perspective, deciding what actually matters, knowing when a piece is done, building a relationship with an audience, and producing the specific observations that come from lived experience.

**Tweet 4:**
The creators who are losing to AI: the ones whose content was always generic. If you were writing "10 tips for productivity" before AI, the AI can now produce that faster than you and at scale. That content was never your actual competitive advantage.

**Tweet 5:**
The creators who are winning with AI: the ones whose content was always specific. They use AI to produce the scaffold and spend their time adding the specificity that only they can provide. The output is better and faster.

**Tweet 6:**
The quality floor went up. AI means the average piece of content is more coherent, more complete, and better formatted than before. This raises the bar for what humans need to bring. Generic is no longer good enough even as a starting point.

**Tweet 7:**
What the bar is now: your own experience, your own observations, your own perspective on why something works. Not research that AI can also access. The signal of lived experience is what separates a human-written piece from an AI-generated one.

**Tweet 8:**
The content creation process that works in 2026: use AI for structure, speed, and first drafts. Use your judgment for what to include, what to cut, and what the piece is actually saying. These are different skills. Both matter.

**Tweet 9:**
Voice is the remaining moat. If you develop a strong, distinctive way of writing, AI can assist but cannot replicate it. The creators who invest in voice development now will have an advantage that compounds as the generic content pool grows.

**Tweet 10:**
The shift in skill demand: from writing volume to editing judgment. From starting from blank pages to improving first drafts. From research to interpretation. These are the skills that AI makes more valuable, not less.

**Tweet 11:**
AI is the best thing that happened to high-quality human content. It filtered out the generic and raised the demand for original perspective. If you have something real to say, you now have tools that let you say it faster.

### Thread 3 — The Prompt Patterns That Make AI Useful for Content

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
90% of people use AI for content by typing "write me a blog post about X." That is why 90% of AI content sounds the same. Here are the patterns that produce differentiated output.

**Tweet 2:**
Pattern 1: the constraints prompt. "Write a 5-tweet thread. Each tweet must contain exactly one actionable insight. No more than 280 characters per tweet. Do not use generic phrases like 'in today's world' or 'it's important to note.'" Constraints force specificity.

**Tweet 3:**
Pattern 2: the persona prompt. "You are a solopreneur who has been running a one-person business for 3 years. You have made specific mistakes and learned from them. Write from that perspective, not as a generic content writer." This shifts the voice significantly.

**Tweet 4:**
Pattern 3: the example prompt. Paste in two or three pieces of your own writing and say "Match this voice and style for the following task." Claude reads the examples and produces output that resembles your style more closely than any abstract style description.

**Tweet 5:**
Pattern 4: the iteration prompt. "Here is my first draft: [paste]. What is weak about it. Then rewrite it with the weaknesses fixed." Two-pass iteration with AI critique between passes consistently produces better outputs than a single-pass prompt.

**Tweet 6:**
Pattern 5: the anti-pattern prompt. "Write a version of this that does NOT do the following: start with a question, use the word 'leverage,' make claims without specifics, end with 'share this thread.'" Telling AI what to avoid is sometimes more effective than telling it what to do.

**Tweet 7:**
Pattern 6: the perspective prompt. "Here is a common belief about [topic]: [belief]. Write a thread that argues the opposite is true, with specific evidence and examples." Contrarian content performs because it surprises. AI can generate contrarian angles faster than you can brainstorm them.

**Tweet 8:**
Pattern 7: the compression prompt. "Here is a 1,500-word article. Compress the three most important insights into a 10-tweet thread without losing specificity." Compression tests forces the AI to identify what actually matters and discard the filler.

**Tweet 9:**
The prompt that does not work: "write something interesting about [topic]." Interesting is subjective. Give AI objective criteria: specific audience, specific format, specific constraints, specific voice. Vague prompts produce vague outputs.

**Tweet 10:**
Building a prompt library: every time you craft a prompt that produces a result you like, save it. Strip out the specific content and replace it with variables in brackets. In 30 days of doing this you will have a collection of prompts that produce consistent results for your use cases.

**Tweet 11:**
The meta skill: know when the AI output is good enough to use as-is and when it needs significant human revision. Most prompts produce 60-80% good output. Your judgment about where the remaining 20-40% lives is the skill that matters.

### Thread 4 — AI for Short-Form Video: The Current State

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
Short-form video used to require a camera, decent editing skills, and hours of time. The tools changed this in the last 18 months. Here is what is actually possible now.

**Tweet 2:**
The pipeline that works today: write a script with AI, record raw footage (or not, if you use AI avatar), add auto-captions with CapCut or Submagic, adjust pacing with the trim tools, add background music. Total time for a 60-second Reel: 25-40 minutes.

**Tweet 3:**
Auto-captions are the single highest-leverage AI feature in short-form video. 80% of mobile viewers watch without sound. Auto-captions with animated text have been shown to increase watch time by 20-40% in most studies.

**Tweet 4:**
CapCut's AI features that are actually useful: auto-captions (fast, accurate), background removal, smart cut to music beats, script-to-video for basic clips. The interface is fast and the mobile app is good enough for most content.

**Tweet 5:**
Runway and Pika for AI video generation: still developing. The outputs are compelling for abstract or ambient content. For talking head or product demo content, they are not yet reliable enough to replace human footage. Check back in 6-12 months.

**Tweet 6:**
HeyGen and Synthesia for AI avatars: these now work well enough for explainer content and course previews. The uncanny valley problem has mostly been solved at the higher tiers. If you have strong content but struggle with on-camera presence, these are worth testing.

**Tweet 7:**
The content type that performs best with AI-assisted production: screen recordings, text-based content, tutorial overlays, and anything where the value is in the information rather than the personality. Talking head content where personality is the product still requires a human.

**Tweet 8:**
The workflow for repurposing long-form to short-form with AI: paste YouTube transcript into Claude, ask it to identify the 3 most interesting 60-second segments, use those timestamps to clip the video, add captions, post. Three repurposed clips per long video in under an hour.

**Tweet 9:**
The SEO play for short-form: post 3-5 Reels per week consistently for 90 days, all on the same narrow topic. The algorithm pattern-matches your account to that topic over time and distributes your content to people interested in it. Consistency in topic is more important than volume.

**Tweet 10:**
The tool trap: most AI video tools overpromise. The ones worth paying for are the ones that save you real time on real tasks you do regularly. Auto-captions: yes. Fully AI-generated content at scale: not yet reliable enough for most use cases.

**Tweet 11:**
The current competitive advantage in short-form video is speed combined with specificity. AI handles the speed. You handle the specificity. That combination produces more content, faster, at a quality level that generic AI-only production does not reach.

### Thread 5 — What Most People Get Wrong About Using AI for Content

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
Using AI for content does not make you faster if you use it wrong. Most people use it wrong. Here are the 5 mistakes I see most often.

**Tweet 2:**
Mistake 1: treating AI output as finished. AI generates a draft. A draft is the beginning of a piece, not the end. If you are publishing AI output without editing, your content sounds like AI output. People notice this. It erodes trust.

**Tweet 3:**
Mistake 2: vague prompts. "Write a thread about productivity" is a vague prompt. You will get a generic thread. The output quality is directly proportional to the specificity of the input. Garbage in, garbage out still applies.

**Tweet 4:**
Mistake 3: using AI to cover topics you do not have opinions about. AI can generate informed content on almost any subject. That does not mean you should publish it. If you do not have a perspective on the topic, the AI output will not have one either.

**Tweet 5:**
Mistake 4: over-relying on AI for ideation. What to create is a strategic decision based on your audience, your business goals, and your competitive positioning. AI can help you generate angles once you have a topic. It should not be deciding what topics matter.

**Tweet 6:**
Mistake 5: using the same prompt every time. The best prompts are specific to the task, the audience, and the format. Repurposing a YouTube script requires a different prompt than writing a LinkedIn post from scratch. Build prompts for specific tasks.

**Tweet 7:**
The correct mental model: AI is the first draft machine. You are the editor. The editor decides what stays, what gets cut, what gets rewritten, and whether the piece is actually saying something worth saying.

**Tweet 8:**
The editing process for AI content: remove all hedging language, replace vague claims with specific examples, add at least one personal observation that only you could have written, and verify any factual claims before publishing.

**Tweet 9:**
The sign that you are using AI correctly: your content pipeline is faster and your quality is maintained. The sign you are using it incorrectly: your content pipeline is faster but your engagement, replies, and conversion rates are dropping.

**Tweet 10:**
The audience can tell the difference between content that was written with AI assistance by a human with something to say, and content that was fully generated by AI and cleaned up slightly. The former builds an audience. The latter builds a noise machine.

**Tweet 11:**
AI is the best leverage tool available for content creators right now. It rewards the creators who already know what they want to say and use AI to say it faster. It does not create the perspective. It amplifies the one you already have.
