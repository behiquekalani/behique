## Script 1: "How to sell AI automation to a local business in 30 minutes (the exact pitch)"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
Most people who want to sell AI services spend three weeks building decks and writing proposals before they talk to a single client. That is backwards. The pitch takes 30 minutes. Here is exactly how it goes.

**The setup (0:30-2:00)**
You are not selling software. You are not selling a subscription. You are selling time back to a business owner who is doing something manually that a machine can do better.

The typical local business owner spends 5 to 15 hours a week on tasks that qualify for automation: answering the same customer questions, manually following up with leads, copying data between systems, scheduling appointments by hand.

Your job in the first 30 minutes is not to explain how AI works. It is to find one of those tasks and put a number on it.

**The three-question audit (2:00-5:00)**
Walk in with these three questions. Write the answers down.

Question one: What is the most repetitive thing you or your team does every week?

Question two: How many hours does that take?

Question three: What would happen if it just stopped getting done, or got done three times faster?

You are building a before/after picture with their own words. They will sell themselves.

Most business owners have never been asked these questions. They will answer immediately. The pain is right there.

**The reframe (5:00-7:00)**
After they answer, do not pitch yet. Reflect it back.

Say: "So you're spending about [X hours] a week on [task]. At your hourly rate, that's roughly [dollar amount] in time. Every week."

Let that land.

Then: "What I build automates that. You get that time back, and the process gets more consistent than it is now."

That is the entire pitch. No demo. No deck. No jargon.

**Handling objections (7:00-9:00)**
Three objections you will hear:

"We already have software for that." Ask what it is. Half the time it is not connected to anything else and still requires manual steps. That is your opening.

"How do I know it will work?" Offer a paid proof of concept. $500 to build a working prototype against their actual data. If it works, they pay for the full build. If it does not, they keep the prototype and you refund the difference. Almost no one says no to this.

"We don't have budget right now." This one is usually real. Move on. Come back in 60 days.

**The close (9:00-11:00)**
End the meeting with one question: "Which one task, if automated this week, would make the biggest difference for you?"

They will pick one. Write it down. Send a one-page scope document within 24 hours. The scope document includes: what gets built, what it connects to, what they need to provide, timeline, and price.

Do not send a proposal. Send a scope document. Proposals ask for approval. Scope documents describe what is already going to happen.

**Outro (11:00-12:00)**
The 30-minute pitch works because you are not asking them to understand AI. You are asking them to describe their pain. You are a translator between their problem and the technology that solves it.

That is the job. That is the sale.

## Script 2: "The AI installation service: what clients actually pay for"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
There is a gap between what you think clients are buying from you and what they are actually paying for. Understanding that gap is the difference between a $300 project and a $3,000 one.

**The misconception (0:30-2:30)**
Most people getting into AI services assume clients pay for the technical output. The workflow. The automation. The chatbot.

They do not.

Clients pay for three things: certainty, time savings, and not having to think about it.

Let me break each one down.

**Certainty (2:30-4:30)**
A business owner does not know if AI will work in their specific situation. They have heard about hallucinations. They have seen demos that did not match reality. They do not know what happens when the automation breaks at 2am on a Friday.

When you show up and say "I've done this before, here is what we will build, here is what it will do, here is how I will support it" you are selling certainty.

Most of your competitors are selling features. Sell certainty and you win almost every time.

**Time savings (4:30-6:00)**
The automation itself is not the product. The recovered hours are the product.

A client who pays you $2,500 to automate their customer inquiry intake does not care about the webhook or the API call. They care that they got four hours back every week. Four hours times 50 weeks is 200 hours. At a $50 hourly value, that is $10,000 in time, bought for $2,500.

When you price and pitch through this lens, the conversation changes completely.

**Not having to think about it (6:00-8:00)**
This is the most underrated thing you sell.

Business owners are already overwhelmed. The decision to hire you is partly a decision to stop having to think about this problem. Maintain it. Monitor it. Fix it when it breaks. Send them a monthly update so they know it is running.

That is what separates a one-time project from a retainer client.

**What this means for how you package your work (8:00-10:00)**
Package one: the build. You design and deploy the automation. Fixed price. One-time.

Package two: the build plus support. Same as above but includes 3 months of monitoring, updates, and fixes. Higher price.

Package three: the full installation. Build, support, documentation, training for their team, and a 15-minute monthly check-in call for 6 months. This is your highest-priced package.

Most of what clients pay for is in packages two and three. Not the code. The ongoing certainty.

**Outro (10:00-11:30)**
The technical work is a commodity. The certainty is not.

If you are pricing your AI services on hours or features, you are leaving most of the value on the table. Price on outcomes. Package for certainty. Charge accordingly.

## Script 3: "Pricing AI services: why $500 is wrong and $2,500 is right"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
Every person who gets into AI services prices their first project at $300 to $500. I did it. Most people watching this have done it or are about to do it. Here is why that number is wrong and what to charge instead.

**Why $500 kills you (0:30-3:00)**
A $500 project takes the same amount of scoping, communication, and delivery work as a $2,500 project. The difference is not the output. The difference is the scope.

At $500, here is what happens: you attract clients who are price-sensitive first and results-focused second. They will ask for changes. They will disappear for two weeks then come back with new requirements. They will negotiate on the back end after the build is done.

Low prices do not just hurt your income. They attract the wrong clients, which makes your work harder, which makes you worse at your job.

**The real cost of underpricing (3:00-5:00)**
Think about what a $500 project actually costs you.

Time to scope: 2-3 hours.
Time to build: 8-15 hours depending on complexity.
Time to deliver, explain, iterate: 3-5 hours.
Ongoing questions after delivery: 2-3 hours over the next month.

You are at 15 to 26 hours minimum. At $500, that is $19 to $33 per hour. For work that requires actual skill and saves your client thousands of dollars.

This is not a math problem. It is a positioning problem.

**How to think about pricing (5:00-8:00)**
Price based on value delivered, not time spent.

Start with the client's problem. How much time does it waste per week? What is the dollar cost of that time? What does the problem cost them in missed revenue, errors, or unhappy customers?

A conservative multiplier is 3x. If the automation saves them $1,000 a month, you can charge $3,000 for the build. They recover the investment in three months and save money for years after that.

This is the conversation your client is having internally whether you are involved or not. Get in front of it.

**The $2,500 anchor (8:00-10:00)**
$2,500 is the right starting price for a first automation project. Not because of a rule. Because of what it signals.

It signals that you are a professional, not a freelancer doing side work. It signals that the work is real and the outcome is real. It signals that you have done this before and know what it is worth.

Clients who balk at $2,500 for a working automation that saves them 10 hours a week are not your clients. Let them go.

**Handling "that seems high" (10:00-11:30)**
They say: "That seems like a lot."

You say: "It might be. Tell me what you are currently spending in time on this every week. Let's see if the math works."

Then you run the math with them. In front of them.

If the math works, they know it works. You do not need to convince them. The numbers do.

**Outro (11:30-12:30)**
The reason most AI freelancers are underpaid is not competition. It is pricing failure.

Start at $2,500. Hold the price. Do the math with your clients. The ones who are right for you will understand immediately.

## Script 4: "How to find your first AI client without cold outreach"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
Cold outreach works. It also takes weeks and most people quit before they see results. There is a faster path to your first AI client, and it does not require a single cold email.

**Why cold outreach fails for most beginners (0:30-2:30)**
Cold outreach requires three things most beginners do not have: a track record, a clear offer, and enough volume to survive the rejection rate.

When you are starting from zero, cold outreach is the hardest way to get your first client. It is a volume game and you do not have volume yet.

The faster path uses what you already have: your immediate network and your knowledge.

**The warm network audit (2:30-5:00)**
Take 20 minutes and write down every business owner or manager you know personally. Family. Friends. Former employers. People from your neighborhood. Anyone who runs or manages something.

Do not filter by industry. Do not filter by size. Just write the list.

Now go through the list and ask: which of these people do I know well enough to have a real conversation with?

That is your first prospect list. Not strangers. People who already trust you enough to take a call.

**The conversation that works (5:00-7:30)**
Do not lead with AI. Do not lead with automation. Lead with a question.

"Hey, I've been doing some work helping local businesses cut down on repetitive tasks using automation. I know you deal with [specific thing you know about their business]. Would you be open to a 20-minute call just to see if there's anything useful there?"

You are not selling. You are offering a conversation.

Most people will say yes because you framed it as potentially useful to them and low commitment. Twenty minutes. No obligation.

**The referral chain (7:30-9:30)**
Your first client is not the most valuable part of your warm network. The referrals are.

After every project, say this: "Do you know anyone else who runs a business dealing with similar problems? I'm working with a few more businesses this quarter."

One client who gives you two referrals is worth more than twenty cold leads.

The best AI service businesses are built on referral chains. Not cold email. Not ads. Not LinkedIn outreach.

**Content as passive outreach (9:30-11:00)**
Posting content about what you do works in parallel. Not instead of conversations. In parallel.

When someone in your network sees your posts about AI automation for small businesses, they think of you when the problem comes up. That is a warm inbound lead. It costs you 30 minutes to write a post.

One real post per week showing your work, a client result, or something you learned on a project. That is enough.

**Outro (11:00-12:00)**
Your first client is probably one conversation away. Not one cold email campaign away.

Work the warm network first. Collect the referrals. Build the referral chain. Let content run in the background. That is the actual sequence.

## Script 5: "The service-to-product flywheel: how selling AI services funds your product catalog"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
There is a specific order of operations that most solo builders get backwards. They build products first, then wonder why nobody buys. The correct sequence starts with services. Here is why.

**The product-first trap (0:30-2:30)**
A product requires distribution. Distribution requires an audience or paid traffic. Both take time or money you probably do not have yet.

A product built without market signal is a guess. You spend weeks or months building something, then find out nobody wants to pay for it the way you packaged it.

Services give you market signal before you build anything.

**What services teach you (2:30-5:00)**
Every service client is a research session.

They tell you exactly what their problem is in their own words. They tell you how they have tried to solve it before and why it did not work. They tell you what they would pay to have it solved permanently.

After three to five clients, you have clear patterns. Same problems. Same objections. Same language.

That is your product brief. Not a brainstorm. Actual market data from real paying clients.

**The flywheel mechanics (5:00-8:00)**
Here is how the flywheel works in practice.

First three months: services only. One or two clients. $2,500 to $5,000 per project. You are learning, you are getting paid to learn, and you are documenting everything.

Month four: you take the most common piece of what you do for clients and package it. Not the whole service. The repeatable core. A template, a guide, a checklist, a starter kit. Price it at $25 to $100.

Month five onward: service clients fund your time. Products build while you sleep. When a product sells, it tells you there is demand for a related service. When a service project reveals a new problem, you build a new product around the solution.

The two channels feed each other. That is the flywheel.

**Revenue math (8:00-10:00)**
One service client per month at $2,500: $2,500.
Two service clients per month: $5,000.
Five products selling 10 units each at $50: $2,500.

At month six, a reasonable combination looks like: two service clients ($5,000) plus product sales ($1,000 to $2,500). You are approaching $7,500 monthly with no ads and no cold email.

The service clients fund your lifestyle while the products build the asset base.

**When to shift the ratio (10:00-11:30)**
Keep the service-heavy ratio until your products generate at least 30 percent of your income consistently. Then you have proof of concept.

From there, you can reduce service hours, raise service prices to filter for better clients, and allocate more time to products. The leverage shifts gradually. You do not quit services overnight.

**Outro (11:30-12:30)**
The people who build sustainable solo businesses do not skip to products. They use services as the on-ramp.

You get paid to learn what the market wants. Then you package that into products. Then the products run while the services keep the lights on.

That is the sequence. Start with services.
