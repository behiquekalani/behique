### Hook (0:00-0:15)

### [00:15] The Problem

I want to be specific here because "I have trouble focusing" is not useful.

The actual problem was this: I would sit down to study. I would open the material. And within four minutes, I was either on YouTube, reading something unrelated, or just... gone. Staring. Not tired, not bored. Just not there.

The apps I tried assumed two things. First, that you can plan your study time in advance. Second, that finishing a task feels rewarding on its own.

Neither of those is true for an ADHD brain.

Planning feels arbitrary when your sense of time is foggy. And finishing a flashcard deck does not register as a win. There is no dopamine spike. Nothing. You just move to the next deck and eventually close the laptop.

So I stopped trying to fix my focus with other people's tools. I asked a different question. What does my brain actually respond to?

The answer was: short cycles, visible progress, and questions that force me to retrieve information rather than just read it.

That became the spec for Study Buddy.

---

### [02:00] What I Built

Study Buddy is simple. Intentionally.

You load a topic. The app breaks your session into 25-minute blocks. Inside each block, you study normally. At the end of the block, it asks you three comprehension questions. Short answer, not multiple choice.

Why questions? Because re-reading feels like studying but does almost nothing for actual retention. Active recall, where you have to pull information out of memory rather than recognize it, is significantly more effective. The research on this is not close.

After you answer the questions, you get XP. Not based on whether you got them right. Based on completing the block. The XP is the signal to your brain: you did something. Something happened.

Then you get a five-minute break. Timed. With a countdown.

Then the next block starts.

That is the whole system.

---

### [04:00] Three Weeks of Data

Week one was rough. I kept stopping early. Not because the app failed. Because I was not used to committing to a block. Twenty-five minutes felt long when I had always been running in bursts of three to seven minutes.

By day five, I finished my first full two-block session. Fifty minutes of actual studying with a break in the middle. That had not happened in months.

Week two, something shifted. The XP started to feel real. Not in a childish way. More like a scoreboard that I actually cared about. I started tracking how many blocks I could do per day.

Week three, I studied consistently across five subjects for the first time this semester.

What changed was not my focus. I do not think my focus changed. What changed was that I had structure with a feedback mechanism. My brain stopped fighting the session because it knew what was coming and it knew there was a reward attached.

### Hook (0:00-0:15)

The most recommended study method for ADHD students is "just review your notes." It is also one of the least effective things you can do. Here is what the data says, and here is what ADHD students actually end up using when the standard advice fails.

---

### [00:15] Active Recall vs. Re-reading

There is a concept in learning science called the testing effect. When you retrieve information from memory, even incorrectly, you retain it better than if you just read it again.

The data on this is clear. Active recall, where you close your notes and try to answer questions from memory, produces better retention than re-reading by a wide margin. Some studies show two to three times the retention rate.

Re-reading feels productive. Your eyes move across familiar words. You recognize things. That recognition creates a feeling of knowing. But recognition is not retrieval. When you are in an exam, you are retrieving, not recognizing.

For ADHD students, re-reading is especially risky. It is passive. Passive input does not hold your attention. Within minutes you are physically reading but not processing. You have done the motion of studying without any of the encoding.

---

### [02:00] Why Time Blocks Work for ADHD, But Long Sessions Do Not

Time blocks work because they create a defined container. Your brain knows this session ends at a specific point. That removes the open-ended anxiety of not knowing when you will be done.

Long sessions fail because attention degrades. Everyone's attention degrades over time. For ADHD, that degradation is faster and less predictable. Two hours of studying does not produce two hours of retention. It often produces twenty minutes of retention and ninety minutes of mental noise.

The research on optimal work intervals for attention-heavy tasks tends to cluster around 25 to 45 minutes. After that, error rates go up and encoding goes down.

Short blocks are not a compromise. They are the correct approach for how brains actually process information.

---

### [03:30] The 3-Component Formula

Here is the formula that works. Block, question, break.

One: a timed study block. 25 minutes. One topic. No switching.

Two: comprehension questions at the end. Not multiple choice. Short answer, written or spoken. You are forcing retrieval.

Three: a fixed break. Five minutes. You do not extend it. You do not use it to review. You stop.

Then repeat.

This is not a new idea. The components have research behind each of them. What is new is combining them in a way that fits the specific attention pattern of an ADHD brain. Short enough to feel possible. Feedback-rich enough to sustain motivation. Structured enough to remove decision fatigue.

Block one felt slow. I caught my eyes drifting twice. Both times I brought them back without stopping the session.

Comprehension questions at minute 25: two out of three answered correctly. One I had missed entirely.

Something was different about block two. The friction was lower. Not gone, but lower.

### Hook (0:00-0:15)

Motivation does not work the same way for ADHD brains. That is not a personal failing. It is neurological. The system I replaced it with is older than smartphones, used by every video game ever made, and it costs nothing to start today.

---

### [00:15] Why Motivation Fails for ADHD

Motivation in a neurotypical context works something like this: you want a result, you do the work, you feel satisfied, you repeat.

For ADHD, the middle part breaks down. The dopamine response to completing a task is flatter. The delay between effort and reward feels longer. And when the reward is abstract, like "a better grade in three months," the signal is nearly invisible.

This is not about trying harder. The mechanism is different. Expecting standard motivation to work on an ADHD brain is like expecting a car to run without fuel and being surprised when it stalls.

---

### [01:30] How Gamification Creates External Structure

Every video game uses the same architecture. Small actions produce immediate visible rewards. Progress is tracked and displayed. Milestones trigger a response.

This architecture exists because it works on human attention. It is not manipulation. It is alignment. The game gives your brain what it needs to keep going: a signal that something happened.

XP systems do this for real tasks. You complete a study block. You earn XP. The number goes up. That is it. But that moment, where a number changes because of something you did, produces a small dopamine response. Small enough to seem trivial. Large enough to sustain behavior across a session.

---

### [02:30] How to Build Your Own XP System

Start manual. You do not need software.

Write a list of daily tasks. Assign XP values based on effort. Studying one block: 50 XP. Exercise: 75 XP. Completing a work task: 100 XP. Track the total in a notebook or a note on your phone.

Set level thresholds. 0-500 XP is Level 1. 500-1200 XP is Level 2. Set a reward for each level. It does not have to be significant. The point is a marker.

Run this for two weeks. See what happens to your consistency.

## Script 5: "Why Most ADHD Study Advice Is Wrong"

**Format:** Talking head or faceless (voiceover with b-roll), contrarian take
**Length:** 6-7 min

---

### Hook (0:00-0:15)

Three pieces of advice show up in every ADHD study guide. "Just focus harder." "Set a timer." "Eliminate distractions." I followed all of them for years. None of them worked. Here is why, specifically, and what actually does.

---

### [00:15] Advice 1: "Just Focus Harder"

This is not advice. This is a description of what studying would look like if it were already working.

Telling an ADHD student to focus harder is equivalent to telling someone with a broken leg to walk normally. The mechanism is not functioning the same way. More willpower does not fix the mechanism.

What this advice misses: attention regulation in ADHD is not a discipline problem. It is a neurological pattern. The prefrontal cortex, which handles sustained attention and impulse control, works differently. You cannot override that with effort. You route around it with structure.

---

### [02:00] Advice 2: "Set a Timer"

This one is closer. Timers are useful. But "set a timer" as standalone advice misses everything that makes timers actually work.

A timer without a defined task is just a countdown to nothing. What happens when it ends? What do you do with the time? Is there a break? What comes after?

ADHD brains do not deal well with open-ended structure. A timer that says "study for 45 minutes" leaves too many decisions open. What are you studying. How are you studying it. What happens at minute 46.

Timers work when they are part of a complete system. Block plus task plus break plus signal. The timer alone is the least important part of that.

---

### [03:30] Advice 3: "Eliminate Distractions"

This one is partially right and mostly wrong.

Eliminating major distractions, phone in another room, notifications off, helps. That part is true.

But the framing misses something important. ADHD attention does not just drift toward external distractions. It drifts toward internal ones. Random thoughts. Incomplete ideas. Things that have nothing to do with what is in front of you.

You can sit in a silent room with no devices and still not be present. The distraction was internal. Eliminating external noise does not solve that.

What solves internal drift is engagement. Active tasks, questions, retrieval, anything that requires output rather than just input. When your brain has to produce something, it has to stay in the room. Passive input, reading, re-reading, listening, creates the conditions for internal drift even in a perfect environment.

---

### [04:45] What Actually Works

Three things, used together.

Short blocks. Twenty-five minutes is not a compromise. It is the correct interval for sustaining encoding. Longer sessions produce more time in the room and less information in memory.

Active retrieval. At the end of every block, close your notes. Answer questions from memory. Write down what you remember. This forces encoding instead of recognition.

Immediate feedback. Your brain needs a signal that the session happened. XP, a checkmark, a streak counter. Something visible that registers completion.

None of these are new. The difference is applying them specifically to the ADHD attention pattern rather than adapting standard advice that was not designed with that pattern in mind.
