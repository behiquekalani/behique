## Script 1: How to Set Up a Gumroad Store That Actually Converts

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Most Gumroad stores make no sales not because the product is bad. Because the store is set up like the creator doesn't actually expect anyone to buy. I'm going to show you exactly how I set mine up, what I changed after the first version didn't convert, and what the store looks like now.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I'm not going to walk you through clicking buttons. You can figure out buttons. What I'm going to walk you through is the decision layer: what goes on your profile, how you structure your product pages, what your pricing logic should look like, and why most stores repel buyers before they even read the description.

Gumroad is simple. That's the point. The problem is that simplicity puts all the weight on your copy and your positioning. There's nowhere to hide. If your store doesn't convert, the store isn't broken. Your messaging is.

**Section 1 -- Profile setup (1:30-3:30):**
Your Gumroad profile is the first thing buyers see when they find you through organic search or someone sharing a link. Most people fill it out like it's an about-me page on a resume. That's wrong.

Your bio should answer one question in the first sentence: what kind of person should buy from you. Not who you are. Who your buyer is.

Here's the difference. Bad version: "I'm a solopreneur who builds AI tools and digital products." Good version: "I make guides for people building a one-person business with AI. No fluff, no theory, just what works."

The second version pre-qualifies your buyer. Anyone who fits that description feels like they found the right place. Anyone who doesn't self-selects out. That's what you want.

Your profile photo matters more than people admit. It doesn't have to be professional photography. It has to be clear and look like a real person made a deliberate choice about what to show. A dark blurry image in a t-shirt sends a signal. A clear image, good light, even from a phone, sends a different one.

**Section 2 -- Product page structure (3:30-6:00):**
Every Gumroad product page needs four things in order: a clear headline that states the outcome, a two or three sentence summary of what the product is, a breakdown of what's inside, and a clear statement of who it's for.

The headline is not the product title. The product title is the name. The headline is the first line of your description. Most people skip it entirely and start with "This guide covers..." That's a wasted opportunity.

Your headline should name the specific problem or outcome. "How to get your first Gumroad sale in under two weeks" is a headline. "Digital Product Starter Guide" is a title. Both can coexist on the same page. They serve different purposes.

The "what's inside" section should be specific. Not "actionable frameworks" or "proven strategies." Those phrases mean nothing. Write what you actually include. "A 14-step setup checklist," "three real product page examples with annotations," "a pricing decision tree." Specificity builds trust. Vagueness destroys it.

**Section 3 -- Pricing logic (6:00-8:00):**
The most common pricing mistake on Gumroad is pricing too low because you're nervous. Underpricing a product doesn't make it easier to sell. It makes buyers question whether it's worth their time.

I price based on the specificity of the outcome. A broad general guide on productivity: $7-12. A specific guide on one narrow problem: $17-27. A complete system with multiple files and a clear use case: $37-67.

The "pay what you want" feature on Gumroad is worth using if you want data on what buyers think your product is worth, or if you want to build volume early. But set a floor. A zero floor trains people to expect free. A $5 floor filters out the people who weren't going to engage with the content anyway.

**Section 4 -- The one thing most stores miss (8:00-10:00):**
Social proof. Not fake social proof. Actual signals that real people bought and found it useful.

If you're starting from zero, you solve this by giving the product to five people you know in your target audience before you launch. Ask them for honest feedback. If it's good, ask if you can quote them. That's your first five reviews.

You don't need fifty reviews. You need two or three specific ones that name the result the person got. "This helped me figure out my pricing" is not useful. "I used the pricing decision tree and went from $7 to $29 on my first guide. Sold three the first day" is useful. That's the level of specificity you want.

**Section 5 -- The last 10% (10:00-11:30):**
After your store is live, the thing that will move the needle most is traffic. Gumroad does not send you buyers. You send buyers to Gumroad.

The setup I just described will not fix a traffic problem. But it will make sure that when you do send traffic, it converts instead of bouncing. That's the point.

Set it up right once. Then focus on distribution.

## Script 2: The Gumroad Description That Gets Clicks: My Formula

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I rewrote one product description on a Tuesday. By Friday that product had more sales than the previous two months combined. Same product. Same price. Same traffic source. The description was the only variable. Here's exactly what I changed and why it worked.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
A Gumroad description has one job: get the right person to click buy before they talk themselves out of it. That's it. It's not a sales letter. It's not a blog post. It's a short, clear answer to the question every buyer is asking: will this actually help me.

I'm going to give you the formula I use. Five components, in order. I'll explain the logic behind each one and give you examples so you can apply it immediately.

**Section 1 -- The opening line (1:30-3:00):**
Your opening line is doing the same job as a headline in a newspaper. It decides whether anyone reads the rest.

Most Gumroad descriptions start with "In this guide..." or "This product is for..." Both of those are throat-clearing. They're not wrong. They're just slow. You've already lost a percentage of your audience before you said anything real.

The opening line should name either the problem or the outcome. One or two sentences maximum.

Problem version: "You have a Gumroad store. Nobody is buying. Not because the product is wrong, because the description is invisible."

Outcome version: "By the time you finish this guide, you'll have a product description that pre-qualifies your buyer, answers their objections, and ends at the checkout button."

Both work. The problem version works better for frustrated buyers. The outcome version works better for motivated ones. Know which you're writing for.

**Section 2 -- The what (3:00-5:00):**
After the opening, tell them what they get. Not what you made. What they get.

The framing matters. "I created a 12-page PDF covering..." is creator framing. "You get a 12-page guide that walks you through..." is buyer framing. Buyer framing converts better because the reader stays in their own perspective.

Be specific about format and volume. "A detailed guide" tells them nothing. "A 12-page PDF with three annotated real examples and a one-page checklist" tells them exactly what they're holding. Specific beats vague every time.

List your contents as bullet points. One line per item. No fluff attached to each bullet. The bullets should be self-explanatory. If you need to explain what a section of your product is, the section title is too vague.

**Section 3 -- Who it's for (5:00-6:30):**
This section does two things. It makes the right buyer feel seen. And it gives the wrong buyer permission to leave without feeling like they wasted money.

Write one to three sentences that describe your exact buyer. Be specific enough that reading it feels personal.

"This is for you if you're building your first digital product business and you keep writing descriptions that feel like job postings."

"This is not for you if you're already running a six-figure store. This is the setup phase, not the optimization phase."

The second sentence is optional but useful. Pre-empting the wrong buyer saves you refund requests and negative reviews from people who bought the wrong thing.

**Section 4 -- The credibility line (6:30-8:00):**
You need one line that tells them why they should believe you. One line. Not a full bio. Not a list of credentials.

The most credible thing you can say is something specific and verifiable. "I've sold over 200 copies of this system in the last three months" is credible. "I've been doing this for years" is not.

If you don't have sales numbers yet, use specificity from your experience. "I tested this process across four different niches before it clicked" is credible because it's specific. "I know what I'm talking about" is not.

You're not trying to impress them. You're trying to give them one reason to trust that the thing you're describing is real.

**Section 5 -- The close (8:00-9:30):**
The last two or three lines of your description should do one thing: reduce friction at the decision point.

Name the price explicitly. "For $19, you get..." This frames the price as a transaction, not a threat. Most Gumroad descriptions don't do this and leave the price as a separate element the buyer has to process alone.

If you have a refund policy, state it here. Even a simple "If it doesn't help, message me" removes a layer of hesitation. People don't buy things they think they might regret. A stated refund policy converts fear into confidence.

End with a call to action. Not "buy now." Something specific. "Download it today and have your first revised description live by tonight." Specific CTAs perform better than generic ones because they give the buyer a mental image of what happens next.

**Section 6 -- Putting it together (9:30-11:00):**
The full formula in order: opening line that names the problem or outcome. What they get in specific terms. Who it's for. One credibility line. A close that names the price and removes friction.

Total length: 200 to 350 words. Anything longer and you're padding. Anything shorter and you're probably missing one of these components.

## Script 3: Why Your Product Isn't Selling on Gumroad (Honest Analysis)

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
You published the product. You shared it. A few people looked at the page. Nobody bought. I've been there. And I spent way too long blaming the platform before I started looking at the actual reasons. There are five real reasons products don't sell on Gumroad. I'm going to go through all of them honestly.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I want to be direct about something before we start. Most videos about "why your product isn't selling" will tell you to optimize your page, improve your copy, or run ads. Some of that is true. But some of the real reasons are harder to say. I'm going to say them anyway.

If you've been sitting on a product for more than a month with no sales, the answer is in this video. Which reason applies to you will depend on where you actually are in the process.

**Section 1 -- The traffic problem (1:30-3:30):**
The first and most common reason: you have no traffic. Zero or near-zero page views. This is not a conversion problem. This is a distribution problem.

Gumroad does not send you buyers. It processes your transactions. You send buyers to Gumroad. If you shared your product link once on one platform and then waited, that's not a marketing strategy.

Traffic has to be consistent. That means posting content regularly, putting the link in your bio or somewhere persistent, or running paid traffic if you're at that stage. A single post announcing your product is an announcement, not a promotion.

Check your Gumroad analytics. If you have under 50 page views, the problem is traffic, not conversion. Fix that first before you touch anything else on the page.

**Section 2 -- The wrong audience problem (3:30-5:30):**
The second reason: you sent traffic, but it was the wrong traffic. People who don't have the problem your product solves.

This is a mismatch between where you're promoting and who your product is for. If you're selling a budget system for people with ADHD and your main audience is business consultants, you will get clicks but not buyers. The people landing on your page are the wrong people.

Before you assume your page doesn't convert, ask: who actually saw this link. What do I know about those people. Does what I know match who this product is for.

The fix is not a new product. It's either finding better distribution channels for the existing audience, or getting clearer on who your current audience actually is and making something they'd buy.

**Section 3 -- The trust problem (5:30-7:30):**
The third reason: your page doesn't establish enough trust for a stranger to give you money.

This is about the state of your overall presence, not just the product page. If someone finds your Gumroad through a social media post, they're going to click your profile, look at your content history, check if you seem like a real person who does what they say they do.

If your profile is thin, your content is sparse, or there's a disconnect between what you post about and what you're selling, you'll get bounces. Not because the product is bad. Because the buyer can't validate that you're the right person to buy from.

The fix is longer-term: consistent content, a clear positioning, and visible work in your domain. There is no shortcut. But the good news is that every piece of content you post is compounding toward trust.

**Section 4 -- The price-to-clarity problem (7:30-9:00):**
The fourth reason: buyers don't understand clearly enough what they're getting relative to the price you're asking.

This is not always a pricing problem. It's sometimes a clarity problem dressed as a pricing problem. If someone looks at a $29 product and can't tell in 30 seconds exactly what they're going to have that they don't have now, they'll hesitate. Hesitation usually becomes a closed tab.

Go to your product page and read the description as someone who has never heard of you. Can you answer these three questions from the description alone: What is the exact format and length of what I'm getting. What specific problem does this solve. Why should I trust that this person knows what they're talking about.

If any of those three questions doesn't have a clear answer, your description needs work.

**Section 5 -- The product-market fit problem (9:00-10:30):**
The fifth reason is the one nobody wants to hear: the product solves a problem people don't want to pay to solve.

Some problems are urgent and specific enough that people will pay to solve them the moment they find the solution. Some problems are real but not painful enough to create buying urgency.

If you've fixed your traffic, your targeting, your trust signals, and your description, and you're still not seeing sales, it's time to ask honestly whether your product is in the first category or the second.

The signal I look for: do people talk about this problem unprompted in communities I'm part of. Not "do people have this problem" but "do people actively complain about it or ask for solutions." That's the level of urgency that creates buyers.

**Section 6 -- Where to start (10:30-11:30):**
Go through these five in order. Traffic first. Then targeting. Then trust. Then clarity. Then fit. Most people start at the end and rebuild their product when the actual problem was at the beginning.

Fix the first broken thing before moving to the next. The answers compound.

## Script 4: From First Product to $1,000: A Realistic Gumroad Timeline

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
The people who make $1,000 on Gumroad in their first month are the exception. The people who make $1,000 in their first six months and then keep going are the ones who build something real. This is a realistic breakdown of what that actually looks like, what the timeline usually is, and what moves the needle at each stage.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I'm going to break this into four phases. Each phase has a goal, a realistic time estimate, and the specific actions that matter most during it. I'm not going to tell you it's fast. It's not. I'm going to tell you exactly what to focus on so you don't waste time on the things that don't matter yet.

$1,000 total gross revenue is a meaningful early milestone. It tells you the product has real market pull, not just validation from people you know. It gives you enough data to know what to build next. And it takes most people somewhere between two and six months, depending on the audience size they're starting with.

**Section 1 -- Phase 1: Pre-launch (weeks 1-2) (1:30-3:30):**
Goal: publish a product that exists. Not a perfect product. A real one.

The mistake here is spending too long on the product and not enough time on the distribution plan. I've seen people spend a month building a course when a $17 PDF would have told them everything they needed to know about whether this audience buys.

Start simple. A focused PDF guide is enough for a first product. It should answer one specific question for one specific person.

During this phase: build the product, set up the Gumroad profile, and identify exactly where your first 100 visitors are going to come from before you hit publish. Not "I'll share it on social media." Specifically: which account, which platform, what content angle, how many times a week.

**Section 2 -- Phase 2: First sales (weeks 3-6) (3:30-5:30):**
Goal: ten sales. Not revenue, not viral reach. Ten real purchases from people who don't know you personally.

Why ten. Because ten purchases gives you enough data to see a pattern. If you've had ten sales, you know what description they responded to, where they came from, and whether any of them left reviews or sent you messages. That data shapes everything from here.

During this phase, the most important thing is volume of distribution attempts. Not ten posts. Forty. Across different formats, different angles, different platforms if necessary. You're finding out which version of your message resonates. Most won't. That's how this works.

Reply to every comment. Message every buyer once to ask if it helped. Not to sell them something else. To understand what they actually used and what they valued.

**Section 3 -- Phase 3: First $500 (weeks 7-14) (5:30-7:30):**
Goal: first $500 in gross revenue. This is usually a second product or a price adjustment on the first.

By the time you hit ten sales, you have real signal. You know what your buyers were looking for when they found you. You know what language they use to describe the problem. Use that to either raise your price on the existing product or build a second product that serves the same audience.

The fastest path to $500 is: one product that consistently converts at a price above $20, combined with a content output that's bringing in 200-400 page views per month. At a 3% conversion rate, which is achievable for a warm audience, that's six to twelve sales per month.

During this phase, don't build a third product. Deepen the distribution for what you have. Build an email list if you don't have one. Even 100 email subscribers who opted in because of your content are worth more than 5,000 social followers who followed for something unrelated.

**Section 4 -- Phase 4: First $1,000 (weeks 15-24) (7:30-9:30):**
Goal: $1,000 total. This is the credibility milestone.

At this point you have sales data, at least one review you can quote, and enough content history that new visitors can evaluate whether you know what you're talking about. That's a fundamentally different situation than week one.

The lever that moves you from $500 to $1,000 is usually one of two things: a price increase on a validated product, or a bundle that increases average order value.

If your $19 guide has sold 20 copies, test it at $29. If 20 people already paid $19 for it, a meaningful number will pay $29. The guide isn't worth less because you're further along. It's worth more because you can now prove it worked for real buyers.

**Section 5 -- What $1,000 actually means (9:30-11:00):**
$1,000 gross on Gumroad is not financial independence. I want to be clear about that. It's proof of concept. It means you found a real problem, built something people paid real money for, and figured out at least one distribution channel that works.

That proof of concept is what lets you confidently build the next product, raise prices, or start thinking about services or bundles. The first thousand is foundation, not finish line.

The people who stall out usually do one of two things: they give up after phase 2 because results are slow, or they skip phase 3 and jump to building more products before they've extracted every lesson from the first one.

Don't skip the phases. The lessons compound.

## Script 5: How to Build a Gumroad Bundle That Increases Your Average Order Value

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I doubled my average order value in three weeks. I didn't run ads. I didn't change any prices. I created a bundle. Not a complicated one. Two existing products packaged together with a single new landing page. This is how to do it and why most bundle attempts fail.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
Average order value is one of the three levers in any product business. More customers. More purchases per customer. More money per purchase. Most people focus entirely on the first one and ignore the third.

Bundles are the fastest way to increase per-purchase revenue without creating something entirely new. You already have the products. The work is in the positioning and the packaging. But most Gumroad bundles fail because they're just two products combined with a slight discount. That's not a bundle. That's a discount.

A real bundle is a positioned offer. The two or more products together tell a more complete story than either one alone. That's what I'm going to show you how to build.

**Section 1 -- Choosing what to bundle (1:30-3:30):**
The starting point is understanding the journey your buyer is on. Not what products you have. What sequence of problems your buyer solves.

If you have a product about finding profitable niches and a product about writing Gumroad descriptions, those two products serve two different stages of the same journey. Someone building their first digital product business needs both. Bundle them.

If you have a product about productivity and a product about eBay dropshipping, those are for different buyers at different stages of different goals. Don't bundle those. The bundle will confuse more people than it converts.

The test I use: can I write one sentence that explains why someone would need both products at the same time. If I can, the bundle works. If I'm reaching, it doesn't.

**Section 2 -- Pricing the bundle (3:30-5:30):**
There are two common bundle pricing strategies. Discount-based: take the combined price and reduce by 20-30%. Value-added: price the bundle at close to the full combined price but add something exclusive.

The discount-based approach works. It's straightforward. A $19 + $17 bundle at $29 is a clear value proposition. The buyer saves $7 and gets both things.

The value-added approach works better for higher price points. Instead of discounting, you add a small exclusive element to the bundle: a one-page reference sheet, a checklist, an email template, something that took you two hours to make but adds perceived completeness to the package. Now the bundle isn't cheaper. It's better.

I use the value-added approach for bundles above $40. Below that, a clean discount is simpler and converts well.

**Section 3 -- Writing the bundle page (5:30-7:30):**
The bundle page is its own product page. Don't just link to your two existing pages. Write a new description specifically for the bundle as a single offer.

The description should explain what the buyer has when they finish going through both products. Not "you get product A plus product B." You get: the ability to find a product idea, price it correctly, write a page that converts, and make your first sale. That's the outcome narrative. That's what the bundle page sells.

List what's included clearly. Price comparison matters here. Show the individual prices and the bundle price. "Normally $36, get both for $27" is more persuasive than just saying "$27" because it frames the discount explicitly.

**Section 4 -- Promoting the bundle (7:30-9:30):**
Bundles need their own promotion moment. Don't just add the bundle to your Gumroad store and expect buyers to find it. Announce it.

The announcement should be a piece of content, not just a link drop. Explain why you bundled these two specifically. What problem does having both solve that having one doesn't. That reasoning is your content angle and your sales argument at the same time.

Email your existing buyers of each individual product. Let them know the bundle exists. Offer them the difference in price as an upgrade. Someone who paid $19 for product A can get the bundle for the difference between their original price and the bundle price. That's a generous, fair offer. Most of them won't take it. Some will. Every one who does is essentially a sale with no acquisition cost.

**Section 5 -- Stacking bundles over time (9:30-11:00):**
Once you have two products and a bundle, you have the skeleton of a product ladder. Each new product you build either stands alone or becomes part of an existing or new bundle.

The mature version of this: three to five individual products, two or three bundle tiers at different price points, and a flagship bundle that packages the whole system at a price that makes individual products feel like obvious entry points.

You don't need all of that on day one. You need one bundle that works. Build that, learn what your buyers say about it, and use that language to build the next layer.
