## SCRIPT 1: "90 Days of Building in Public. Here's What Actually Happened."

**Format:** Talking head / vlog
**Target length:** 8-10 minutes
**Hook type:** Honest report, no vanity metrics

---

[HOOK]

90 days ago I had zero followers, zero sales, and 22 products sitting in a folder on my desktop.

I want to walk through what actually happened. Not the highlight reel. The actual numbers, the actual mistakes, and what month three feels like compared to what you think it will feel like going in.

---

[INTRO CARD]

"90 Days Building in Public. The Honest Version."

---

[SECTION: THE STARTING CONDITIONS]

So here is where I started.

Zero followers on every platform. Zero email subscribers. Zero revenue from digital products. And 22 products that had never been listed anywhere because I kept telling myself they were not ready.

I am a computer engineering student in Puerto Rico. I am not a content creator. I had never posted on Instagram with any consistency. I did not have a personal brand. I had a Google Drive full of ideas and a notebook full of plans that never became anything.

The reason I started was simple. I needed to prove to myself that you can build a real income stream as a student, with no audience, no budget, and no mentor. Build in public, document everything, see what happens.

---

[SECTION: MONTH ONE. THE SETUP PHASE.]

Month one was almost entirely setup.

I spent the first two weeks doing things that felt productive but were not producing anything. I redesigned the logo three times. I picked a color palette. I wrote a brand guide nobody was going to read.

This is the trap. You can spend weeks building infrastructure for an audience that does not exist yet.

What actually moved things: I listed the first product on Gumroad. That was it. Nothing else mattered until that happened. The logo was irrelevant until there was something to sell.

I also made a decision in month one that I think saved me a lot of time later. I stopped making content about the products and started making content about the process. Not "here is my AI productivity guide" but "here is what I learned building an AI productivity guide as a student with ADHD." The second framing is a story. The first one is an ad.

Numbers at the end of month one: 47 Instagram followers. Zero sales. One product listed.

---

[SECTION: MONTH TWO. DISTRIBUTION VS. CONTENT]

Month two is when I figured out the difference between being content-ready and being distribution-ready.

I had content. Plenty of it. What I did not have was a system for getting that content in front of people who would care about it.

Content-ready means you have the posts, the scripts, the products. Distribution-ready means you understand which platform surfaces your content to new people and under what conditions.

Instagram Reels showed my content to more non-followers in two weeks than three months of static posts had done. That was a meaningful data point. It changed where I put my time.

I also started tracking what actually drove traffic to my Gumroad page. Not likes. Not comments. Clicks. Almost all of it came from a single line in a post or reel that described a specific problem. Not "check out my product." More like "I built a one-page system for planning my week when I cannot focus and here is what it looks like."

Numbers at the end of month two: 340 followers. Two sales. Three products listed.

---

[SECTION: MONTH THREE. WHAT IT ACTUALLY FEELS LIKE]

Here is what nobody tells you about month three.

It does not feel like momentum. It feels like you finally understand the rules of the game, but you are still losing.

You are producing better content than you were in month one. Your systems work. Your products are solid. But the numbers are still small by any metric you would see in a "how I made $10,000 in 90 days" video.

The difference between people who keep going past month three and people who do not is not motivation. It is a system that makes showing up automatic regardless of how it feels.

That is what I had to build. Not a content calendar. A decision-making system. What gets posted this week. Why. What metric tells me if it worked. When I check that metric and when I stop checking it.

Numbers at end of month three: 1,100 followers. 14 sales. $247 revenue. Still not quit-your-day-job numbers. But real, verifiable proof of direction.

---

[SECTION: THE HONEST GAP]

The gap between where you start and where you want to be is not a motivation problem. It is a systems problem.

I was working hard in month one. I was just working on the wrong things. The work did not compound because it was not connected to anything. No audience funnel. No content that pointed to a product. No system for reviewing what worked.

When I built an operating system for the week, everything changed. Not because I worked more hours. Because the hours connected to each other.

## SCRIPT 2: "The 3-Hour Monday Habit That Runs My Entire Week"

**Format:** Screen recording / walkthrough
**Target length:** 6-8 minutes
**Hook type:** Routine reveal, productivity

---

[HOOK]

Every Monday morning I block three hours. I do not check messages during those three hours. I do not open new tabs. I do not answer anything.

By noon on Monday, I know exactly what I am building this week, where I am building it, how long it will take, and what good looks like when it is done.

Here is the full routine.

---

[INTRO CARD]

"3-Hour Monday. How I Run an Entire Week in One Morning."

---

[SECTION: WHY THREE HOURS ON MONDAY]

Most people start Monday by opening their inbox and reacting to whatever is loudest.

I used to do that. It meant by Tuesday I was already behind on what I actually wanted to build because I had spent Monday sorting other people's priorities.

The three-hour block is not about planning. It is about choosing. You are deciding, before anything else gets a vote, what this week is actually for.

---

[SECTION: HOUR ONE. THE REVIEW.]

The first hour is a review of last week. No more, no less.

I open three things: my git log, my Gumroad stats, and my Instagram analytics.

Git log shows me what I actually built last week. Not what I planned to build. What got committed. Sometimes there is a gap. Sometimes there is not. Either way, I need to see the real output, not the intention.

Gumroad stats show me if anything sold. Which product. When. What the traffic source was if I can tell.

Instagram analytics: reach, saves, profile visits. Saves are the most useful number. If someone saved a post, they thought it was worth coming back to. That is signal.

I write three sentences about last week. What shipped. What stalled. One honest observation about what the data says, not what I hoped it would say.

That is hour one.

---

[SECTION: HOUR TWO. THE THREE OUTPUTS.]

Most productivity systems ask you to write a task list. I stopped doing that.

A task list is a list of actions. An output is a thing that exists when the week is done. The distinction matters because outputs have a finish line. Tasks do not.

For this week I am going to set three outputs. Not ten. Not five. Three.

An output sounds like: "One published reel with a link to Gumroad." Or: "A working first draft of the AI security guide." Or: "A posted thread on X that explains the Behike Method in under 280 characters."

Each output gets a machine and a time slot. Machine means where does this get built, my laptop or another machine in my setup. Time slot means which two-hour block this week is reserved for it.

I write this directly into a plain text file called primer.md. No Notion. No Airtable. A text file I can open in one second on any machine.

That is hour two.

---

[SECTION: HOUR THREE. THE SYNC AND THE CALENDAR.]

The third hour is for removing obstacles before the week starts.

Are there any blockers on the three outputs I just set? Waiting on a file from somewhere? Need to install something? Need to make a decision about something that will stop me mid-build if I do not make it now?

I clear those. Or I flag them and adjust the output so it does not require that blocker.

I also update primer.md with the full current state: what project is active, what the three outputs are, what the next action is if I stop mid-session and come back cold.

This file is the handoff between past-Kalani and future-Kalani. It works whether it has been two days since I last opened it or two weeks.

---

[SECTION: WHAT THIS REPLACES]

This routine replaced:

Notion. I had a Notion workspace with 40 pages that I had not opened in three months. It was beautiful. It produced nothing.

Daily task lists. A list of 20 things gives you the illusion of a productive week before you have done a single thing.

Passive planning. Reading productivity content, watching "how I structure my week" videos, bookmarking systems. None of that is the same as choosing what you are building and committing to it.

## SCRIPT 3: "Why I Quit Notion and Built My Own System"

**Format:** Screen recording
**Target length:** 7-9 minutes
**Hook type:** Tool swap, opinion piece

---

[HOOK]

I used Notion for three years.

I built databases for my projects, my reading list, my content calendar, my ideas, my goals, my habits. I spent probably 40 hours over those three years building the system itself.

I quit Notion about six months ago. And I built more in the six months after quitting than I did in the three years using it.

Here is what I figured out.

---

[INTRO CARD]

"Why I Quit Notion. And What Actually Works."

---

[SECTION: THE APPEAL OF NOTION]

Notion is genuinely useful for certain things. Documentation. Team wikis. Project specs that multiple people need to read and edit.

I was using it as a one-person productivity system. And the problem is that Notion is optimized for organizing information, not for producing work.

When you are a one-person builder, the bottleneck is almost never "I cannot find what I need." The bottleneck is "I am not building the thing." Notion does not fix that. Sometimes it makes it worse because organizing feels like progress.

---

[SECTION: THE BEAUTIFUL DATABASE NOBODY USED]

I had a content database in Notion. Every post idea had a row. The row had: status, platform, tags, date, hook, body, CTA, linked product. It looked incredible.

I published maybe one post every three weeks from that database. Because the friction between "idea in Notion" and "post published" was enormous. Opening Notion, finding the right database view, changing the status, writing the copy in one field, copying it to Instagram, remembering to update the status again.

Compare that to what I do now: I send a voice message to my Telegram bot, it gets transcribed and saved to a daily file with a timestamp. If I want to post it, I open the file, copy the text, edit it once, post it.

The capture system that actually gets used beats the perfect system that does not.

---

[SECTION: WHAT I SWITCHED TO]

Three things replaced Notion.

First: plain markdown files. My project state lives in a file called primer.md. It is 200 lines. It tells me where every project is, what the next action is, what the open blockers are. I can open it in one second. I can edit it in any text editor on any machine. It syncs between machines over my local network.

Second: a Telegram bot I built called BehiqueBot. It captures everything I think of throughout the day. Text messages, voice messages, transcribed automatically. Filed by project or category. No friction. No decision about where to put it.

Third: three questions. Every day before I start building I answer these: What is the one thing I am shipping today. What might stop me. What does done look like.

That is the whole system.

---

[SECTION: THE REALIZATION ABOUT CAPTURE SYSTEMS]

Here is the thing I had to learn the hard way.

Capture systems do not produce work. They organize information about work. If you spend three hours a week maintaining a productivity system, those are three hours you did not spend building something.

The useful question is not "where does this idea live?" The useful question is "what is the next action on this idea?" A plain text file can answer that faster than any database.

---

[SECTION: WHEN TO USE NOTION]

I still use Notion for one thing: client deliverables. When I am building something for someone else and they need to review it, Notion is the right tool because it is shareable and readable.

For everything I am building for myself, plain text files and a Telegram bot are faster and create less maintenance overhead.

## SCRIPT 4: "I Have ADHD. Here's How That Changed How I Build."

**Format:** Talking head
**Target length:** 6-8 minutes
**Hook type:** Personal, vulnerability with solution

---

[HOOK]

I was diagnosed with ADHD at 19.

Not as a child, not in high school. At 19, in the middle of trying to figure out why I could spend six hours researching a topic I was interested in and then completely fail to complete a two-hour assignment that was due the next day.

I am not going to make this an inspiration video. I want to talk about the actual mechanics, because the mechanics changed how I build.

---

[INTRO CARD]

"ADHD and Building. The Actual Mechanics."

---

[SECTION: WHAT ADHD ACTUALLY IS IN PRACTICE]

ADHD in most content is framed as a superpower or a curse. Neither framing is useful when you are trying to ship something.

What it actually looks like in practice, for me:

Hyperfocus is real. When I am interested in something, I can work on it for hours without any effort. The problem is I cannot choose when it happens. If I am waiting for hyperfocus to start building, I will wait a long time. And when it does show up, it is usually on the wrong thing.

Context switching is expensive. When I stop working on something mid-task, coming back to it later costs significant mental energy. I have to rebuild the mental model of where I was. The cost is high enough that it is often easier to start something new than to return to something unfinished. This is why I have 40 half-built projects on my hard drive.

The starting problem is separate from the finishing problem. Starting a new project is easy when it is interesting. Getting to 80% is manageable. The last 20% is where almost everything dies because it stops being interesting and becomes execution.

The research loop is dangerous. I can spend three hours researching something I could have just built in one hour. The research feels like progress. It is not.

---

[SECTION: WHAT I CHANGED]

I worked with a psychologist on this. Not productivity YouTube. An actual person who studies attention and behavior.

Three things changed as a result.

First: I separate research mode from build mode and I do not mix them. Research happens on designated research days or research blocks. Build blocks are for shipping. If I am in a build block and I notice I am researching instead, I stop, write the question down, and return to building.

Second: I use externalized state instead of trusting my memory. My project state lives in a file, not in my head. When I return to a project, I read the file before I do anything. Two minutes of reading saves 30 minutes of re-orientation. This is why my primer.md file exists. It is not a productivity tool. It is a cognitive prosthetic.

Third: I set done conditions before I start. Not "work on the guide" but "write 500 words of section three of the guide." The done condition has to be observable. Did it happen or did it not. I need to be able to close the task with full certainty.

---

[SECTION: HOW ADHD CHANGES THE PRODUCT]

Here is the part that I think is actually interesting.

When you build something with ADHD, you are forced to solve the "I cannot get started" problem for yourself. And the solutions you find are genuinely better than the generic productivity advice because they have to actually work on a brain that resists artificial structure.

The Behike Method came directly from this. Every piece of it is designed around one question: what does the next action need to look like so that someone with high context switching costs can pick it up without needing to remember what they were doing?

The answer was always: smaller, more specific, and written down.

---

[SECTION: THE THINGS THAT DO NOT WORK]

For the record, these things do not work for me:

Long task lists. I stop reading at item four.

Motivation-based systems. "Remember your why" does not help when the executive function is already offline.

Flexible schedules. "Do it whenever you want this week" means it does not get done. Time-blocked specific slots work. Open-ended intentions do not.

## SCRIPT 5: "The One-Person Business Framework I Built After Dan Koe Made Me Rethink Everything"

**Format:** Talking head / explainer
**Target length:** 8-10 minutes
**Hook type:** Influence acknowledgment, original take

---

[HOOK]

I read Dan Koe's One Person Business framework about a year ago.

It changed how I thought about building a business as a student. Not because it told me what to do, but because it gave me a model to react against.

There are three things the OPB framework does not address for someone who is starting with zero audience and zero revenue. I want to walk through what those gaps are and what I built to fill them.

---

[INTRO CARD]

"After Dan Koe. What I Built When the Framework Did Not Fit."

---

[SECTION: WHAT THE OPB FRAMEWORK GETS RIGHT]

Quick version for anyone who has not read it.

The One Person Business model is: you build an audience by sharing what you know, you sell digital products and services to that audience, and you operate without employees or significant overhead. You are the product and the distribution.

What it gets right:

The model is real. One person, no team, meaningful revenue. It works. There are too many examples to argue with it.

The content-before-product sequencing makes sense in theory. Build audience, then sell. The audience makes distribution cheaper and trust is already there.

The focus on specific knowledge is correct. Generic knowledge does not sell. Specific experience does.

---

[SECTION: GAP ONE. THE AUDIENCE CHICKEN AND EGG.]

Here is where it breaks down for someone starting at zero.

The OPB framework assumes you build an audience first and then create products for that audience. That is fine if you have the time and runway to spend six to twelve months building an audience before you see any revenue.

If you are a student with no income, you need to see revenue signal much earlier than that. Not because you are impatient. Because signal tells you if you are building something real.

The fix I built: the reverse value ladder.

Instead of building an audience and then making a product, you make the cheapest product first. Something at $10 to $20 that is specific and useful. You describe that product clearly. You post content that addresses the exact problem the product solves. And you find your first buyers before you have an audience.

Three things happen when you do this. You get revenue signal early. You learn who your buyer is before you build anything else. And the product itself becomes content. Talking about building the product, what you learned, what surprised you, is already a content strategy.

You build the audience from the product, not before it.

---

[SECTION: GAP TWO. QUALITY RATE VS. SHIP RATE.]

The OPB framework is highly focused on quality. High-quality content, high-quality products, premium positioning.

This is correct advice for someone who is already established. It is the wrong advice for someone who is in month one.

In month one you do not know what quality looks like for your specific audience yet. You are guessing. And guessing slowly does not produce better guesses than guessing quickly.

The principle I built instead: ship rate over quality rate.

Posting ten pieces of content in a month and learning which two landed is more valuable than spending a month perfecting one piece. This is not "post bad content." It is "post your best-effort content consistently and let the market tell you what works."

Once the market tells you what works, then you optimize for quality within that category.

The error is trying to optimize for quality before you have signal. You are optimizing toward a target you cannot see yet.

---

[SECTION: GAP THREE. THE 30-DAY TEST BEFORE SCALE.]

OPB talks a lot about scaling systems, automating distribution, building once and earning multiple times.

What it does not address clearly enough is the decision of when to scale.

I see this pattern constantly with builders who are starting out. They build one thing, it does not immediately work, and they either abandon it and build something new or they try to scale it before proving it works.

The rule I built: 30-day test before scale.

Every product or content strategy gets a 30-day test period. I define in advance what success looks like at 30 days. If it hits the bar, I invest more. If it does not, I either change one variable and run another 30 days, or I stop and move on.

Scale is only for things that are already working at small scale. Everything else is still a test.

This prevents two expensive mistakes. Spending six months scaling something that was never working. And abandoning something that needed 30 more days to find its first real traction.

---

[SECTION: HOW THESE THREE FIT TOGETHER]

Reverse value ladder gives you signal early. Ship rate over quality rate gives you the volume to learn fast. Thirty-day test before scale tells you when to invest more and when to stop.

They are not separate principles. They are a sequence. Get signal, learn fast, invest based on evidence.

This is what the Behike Method covers. All three of these principles are in it, along with the weekly planning system I use to run this process without burning out.
