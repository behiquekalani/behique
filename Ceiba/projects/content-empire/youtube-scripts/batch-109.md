## Script 1: Growing on LinkedIn as a Solopreneur

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

LinkedIn is the only major platform where business content is not just tolerated but expected. Your competitors are not using it well. Most people post corporate fluff, engagement bait, or a photo of their feet at the beach captioned "grateful." That creates an enormous opening. The question is whether you take it.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

LinkedIn in 2026 is not the job-board platform people treated it as five years ago. It has become the highest-quality lead generation tool for solopreneurs, consultants, and one-person businesses, with distribution that outperforms most other platforms when you know how to use it.

The reason most solopreneurs do not grow on LinkedIn is not that they lack expertise. It is that they do not understand the format. LinkedIn rewards a specific type of content, distributed in a specific way, with a specific engagement pattern. Master those three things and growth follows.

---

**[SECTION 1: The Content Formats That Actually Grow]**

LinkedIn has five content formats. Three of them grow accounts. Two of them waste time.

The formats that grow: text posts, carousels, and long-form articles used strategically.

Text posts are the highest-reach format on LinkedIn. No link in the post. A strong first line that forces the reader to click "see more." A structured argument or story broken into short paragraphs. One key insight per post. This is the workhorse format.

Carousels, which LinkedIn calls documents, are the highest-save format. They work because they are visual, they scroll differently than the feed, and they pack a complete idea into a swipeable format. A good carousel teaches one concept in five to ten slides with clear, clean design.

Long-form articles work for SEO and for establishing deep authority on a specific topic. They do not get native feed distribution the way posts do, but they create a permanent record of your thinking that compounds over time.

The formats that waste time: polls, unless they are genuine research questions, and external links posted directly, which LinkedIn suppresses algorithmically.

---

**[SECTION 2: The Profile That Converts]**

Growing on LinkedIn is worthless if your profile does not convert visitors into connections and connections into leads.

Your headline is not your job title. It is your value proposition. "Founder at XYZ" tells a reader nothing useful. "I help B2B founders reduce client churn by fixing their onboarding process" tells them exactly why they should connect with you.

Your banner image is a billboard. It should reinforce your niche and make it immediately clear who you serve.

Your About section is a short sales page. Who you are, who you help, what transformation you create, and a clear call to action. Keep it under 300 words. Use short paragraphs.

Your featured section should contain your best content, your lead magnet, and a link to your product or offer. This is the most visible real estate on your profile after the headline, and most people leave it empty.

---

**[SECTION 3: The Engagement System That Compounds]**

Growth on LinkedIn is not just about publishing. It is about engagement cadence.

The algorithm rewards accounts that engage consistently and genuinely before and after they publish. This does not mean leaving spammy one-word comments on every post you see. It means choosing ten to fifteen accounts in your niche whose content you can engage with meaningfully, every day.

The ideal routine takes 20 minutes. Five minutes reading your feed and leaving two to three substantive comments on posts from creators you respect. Five minutes replying to comments on your own posts. Ten minutes writing or scheduling your next post.

That 20-minute routine, done consistently six days a week, compounds into significant reach over 90 days.

The key word is substantive. A comment that adds a perspective, challenges a point, or extends the argument is worth ten times more than "great post." It generates reply threads. It exposes you to the original poster's audience. It builds your reputation in ways that posting alone cannot.

---

**[SECTION 4: Turning Followers Into Revenue]**

LinkedIn followers who never see your offer are not an asset. They are a vanity metric.

The conversion path starts with your content. Every post, every carousel, every article should have a natural bridge to your offer. Not a hard sell at the end of every post, but a consistent directional pull toward what you do and what you sell.

The bridge is a lead magnet. Something free, specific, and immediately valuable that you mention in your content and link in your profile. When someone engages repeatedly with your content and then sees your lead magnet in your featured section, they are a warm lead. The email sequence does the rest.

One post per week can include a direct CTA to your offer or your lead magnet. Frame it as an offer to help, not a pitch. "If you want the full framework, it is in the free guide linked in my bio" is not a pitch. It is a service.

---

**[OUTRO — CTA]**

LinkedIn is the most underused platform for solopreneurs right now. The audience is there, the organic reach is still real, and the bar for quality content is low enough that showing up with a genuine perspective is enough to grow.

## Script 2: The n8n Automation Deep Dive

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

Most solopreneurs are doing manually what a properly built workflow could do automatically. Every time you copy-paste data between tools, every time you send the same type of email, every time you check the same dashboard every morning, you are doing a job that a machine should be doing. n8n is how you stop doing those jobs.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

n8n is an open-source workflow automation tool. Think of it as Zapier but without the usage limits, without the black-box architecture, and without the price tag that scales with your volume.

More importantly, n8n gives you access to code nodes, meaning you can write JavaScript directly inside your automations for anything the standard nodes do not cover. That flexibility is what separates it from every other automation tool in the market.

This deep dive covers how n8n works, the automations that deliver the highest ROI for solopreneurs, and how to build your first working workflow today.

---

**[SECTION 1: How n8n Works]**

n8n is built around the concept of nodes connected in a flow. Each node does one thing: it triggers an action, processes data, or sends data somewhere.

A trigger node starts the workflow. It could be a webhook, a schedule, a form submission, a new email, or a new row in a spreadsheet. The trigger is what wakes the workflow up.

Process nodes handle data transformation. They can filter, format, merge, split, or run custom JavaScript. This is where the real power lives.

Action nodes send data somewhere. They can create records in your CRM, send emails, post to social media, call an API, update a spreadsheet, or send a Telegram message.

You connect nodes visually by drawing lines between them. When the workflow runs, data flows from node to node according to your logic. You can branch the logic with conditional nodes, loop over lists with split-in-batches nodes, and handle errors with dedicated error branches.

The mental model is simple: if this happens, get this data, do this thing with it, send it there. Everything else is variation on that structure.

---

**[SECTION 2: The Five Automations Every Solopreneur Should Build]**

These five automations are not theoretical. They are the ones with the highest time savings and the lowest complexity to build.

Automation one: lead capture to CRM. When someone fills your contact form or downloads your lead magnet, n8n automatically creates a contact record in your CRM, tags them based on the form they filled, and triggers a welcome email sequence. No manual entry.

Automation two: social media republishing. You write one post. n8n formats it for Twitter, reformats it for LinkedIn, and schedules both. One input, two outputs, zero manual work.

Automation three: weekly analytics digest. n8n pulls data from your email platform, your Gumroad or Stripe account, and your website analytics every Sunday morning, formats it into a clean summary, and sends it to your inbox or a Telegram channel. You know your numbers without logging into anything.

Automation four: content research assistant. You drop a URL or keyword into a Telegram message. n8n triggers a web scrape, summarizes the content using an AI API call, and sends the summary back to you. Research in 30 seconds instead of 30 minutes.

Automation five: customer follow-up sequence. When someone buys a product, n8n logs the sale, creates a follow-up task in three days, and sends a check-in email if no review has been left. Retention and review collection, automated.

---

**[SECTION 3: Building Your First Workflow]**

The fastest path to your first working n8n workflow is a simple email-to-spreadsheet logger.

The trigger: a webhook that receives data from your email form.
The process: a Set node that extracts the name, email, and message fields.
The action: a Google Sheets node that appends a new row with those fields.

Build that first. It takes about 15 minutes. It teaches you how triggers work, how data flows between nodes, and how action nodes authenticate with external services.

Once that works, you extend it. Add an email send node that sends a confirmation to the submitter. Add a conditional node that routes high-priority inquiries to a Slack message. Add a CRM node that creates a contact automatically.

The skill compounds quickly. The tenth automation you build takes a fraction of the time the first one did. The systems you build in a month replace hours of weekly manual work indefinitely.

---

n8n is not a nice-to-have tool. For a solopreneur, it is infrastructure. The time you save with automations is time you redirect toward the work that actually requires your thinking.

## Script 3: How to Write a Viral Twitter Thread

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

A single Twitter thread in 2026 can generate more email subscribers, more product sales, and more meaningful attention than six months of random posting. The variable is not luck. It is structure. The threads that go viral follow a pattern that is learnable and repeatable.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

Twitter, now X, is still the fastest platform for distributing ideas to an audience that is specifically interested in reading them. The feed is text-first. The culture rewards depth when it is packaged correctly. And threads, specifically, are the format that compounds most effectively: they get shared, bookmarked, referenced, and found through search.

The difference between a thread nobody reads and a thread that hits 500 retweets is not quality of the underlying idea. It is the construction of the thread itself.

Here is the construction.

---

**[SECTION 1: The Anatomy of a High-Performing Thread]**

Every viral thread has the same skeleton.

Hook tweet: one or two sentences that create a knowledge gap. The reader needs to feel that they are missing something important and that they will get it by reading on. "Most people build content brands the hard way. Here is the shortcut: (thread)" is not a good hook. "I spent 90 days trying every content strategy I could find. Only three of them actually worked. They have one thing in common:" is a good hook.

The hook is 80 percent of the outcome. A weak hook means no one reads the body regardless of how good it is.

Thread body: a sequence of three to seven tweets that each deliver one distinct idea. Each tweet in the body should be able to stand alone as an insight. Each one should also pull the reader toward the next one.

Bridge tweet: a tweet in the middle of the thread, usually around tweet four or five, that re-hooks the reader. Briefly remind them what they are reading and raise the stakes of the conclusion.

Conclusion tweet: the payoff. The summary, the call to action, and the follow instruction. "If this was useful, follow me for one framework per day on building a one-person business."

---

**[SECTION 2: The Three Thread Formulas That Work]**

Formula one: the numbered list. "10 things I know now that I wish I knew when I started." This is the easiest format and the hardest to write well because every item needs to be genuinely good. If even two items are weak, readers lose trust in the whole thread.

Formula two: the story thread. "18 months ago I had no audience, no product, and no idea what I was doing. Here is what actually happened." The story thread builds emotional investment. The reader wants to know how it ends. The key is that the story must teach something concrete. A story without a takeaway is entertainment, not authority-building.

Formula three: the contrarian take. "Everyone says you need consistency to grow. I disagree. Here is what actually drives growth:" This formula generates the most engagement because it invites debate. The danger is taking a position so weak that the pushback undermines your credibility. Only use this formula when you genuinely believe the contrarian position and can defend it.

---

**[SECTION 3: Distribution and Amplification]**

Writing a great thread is necessary but not sufficient. You need to distribute it.

The first 30 minutes after posting determine whether a thread gets algorithmic distribution. In those 30 minutes, reply to the thread yourself with an extension of the last point. This keeps the thread active and signals engagement to the algorithm.

Engage with everyone who replies in the first hour. Real replies, not just likes.

Post the thread at peak activity time for your audience. For most business and creator-focused accounts, that is between 7am and 9am or 12pm and 2pm in the US Eastern timezone.

Repurpose the thread. Every thread you write can become a LinkedIn post, an Instagram carousel, a newsletter section, or a YouTube script. The research and thinking you put into writing the thread is not single-use material.

---

**[OUTRO — CTA]**

The thread format is the fastest way to build authority on Twitter and the most efficient way to distribute ideas across the creator economy. Master it and you have a content engine that generates leads, followers, and sales from a single piece of writing.
