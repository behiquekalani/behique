### Script 1 — How to Actually Use Claude AI (Beyond the Basics)

**Thumbnail concept:** Claude's UI on a screen, but with callouts pointing to specific features. Text overlay: "HOW TO USE CLAUDE PROPERLY." Clean, professional look.

**Hook (0-30s):**
Most people use Claude like a search engine. They ask a question, they get an answer, they move on. That barely scratches the surface. Claude is a reasoning system and when you know how to work with it properly, it functions more like a thinking partner than a tool. Here is how to actually use it.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This video is for people who use AI tools but feel like they are not getting the most out of them. Maybe you have tried Claude and gotten mediocre outputs. Maybe you are getting good outputs but inconsistently and you do not know why.

By the end of this video you will understand the difference between prompting for a task and prompting for a result, how context works in a Claude conversation, and how to set up persistent instructions that change your outputs significantly.

**Section 1 — Context Is Everything: How Claude Actually Reads Your Prompts (2-4min):**
Claude reads the entire conversation every time it generates a response. That means context builds. The longer and more specific the conversation, the more accurate and calibrated the outputs.

The mistake most people make is treating every message as a standalone request. "Write me a product description for a digital product about email marketing." Claude generates something generic because you gave it nothing to work with.

The better approach: before you ask for the output, give Claude the context it needs to generate something specific.

Tell it who you are. Tell it who your audience is. Tell it the tone you want. Tell it what you have already tried. Give it a sample of your writing if voice matters. Then ask for the output.

The same request with that context produces a completely different result. Not because Claude got smarter. Because you gave it the information it needed to be useful.

Think of Claude as a very capable contractor who can only work with what you give them. Bad brief, bad output. Detailed brief, useful output.

**Section 2 — The Prompt Structures That Work (4-7min):**
There are a few prompt structures that consistently produce better outputs.

The role prompt: "You are an expert in X. Your job is to Y. Here is what you know about my situation: [context]." This activates Claude's knowledge in a specific domain and frames the task clearly.

The iterative prompt: instead of asking for a finished output, ask for a draft, then improve it. "Write a first draft of this email. After I read it, I will tell you what to change." Drafts are always better than blank pages, and iteration produces better final outputs than trying to get everything right in one shot.

The constraint prompt: "Write a 5-tweet thread. Each tweet must be under 250 characters. No filler sentences. Tone is direct and confident." Constraints force Claude to make choices. Unconstrained requests produce mediocre average outputs. Constrained requests produce sharper outputs.

The reasoning prompt: "Think through this step by step before giving me your answer." For anything that requires logic or analysis, this prompt massively improves output quality because it slows down the generation process and forces sequential reasoning.

The document prompt: paste in your existing content and say "Based on this, help me do X." Claude can analyze, summarize, extract, transform, or extend any document you give it. This is one of the highest-leverage use cases.

**Section 3 — Setting Up a Productive AI Workflow (7-10min):**
The way to get consistent value from Claude is to build repeatable workflows around it.

Identify the three to five tasks you do repeatedly that involve writing, research, or analysis. For each one, write a prompt template that includes the context, the role, the constraints, and the format you want.

Store those templates somewhere accessible. I have a prompt library in Notion. When I need to run a recurring task through Claude, I open the template, fill in the variable parts, paste it in, and go.

This removes the cognitive load of figuring out how to prompt every time. The template has already done that work.

The second workflow principle: use Claude to improve Claude's outputs. After you get a first response, ask it to critique its own answer. "What is weak about this response. What would make it better." Then ask it to revise. This two-pass process reliably produces better outputs than a single prompt.

The third principle: use Claude for first drafts, not final outputs. Claude generates fast. Humans judge quality. Your job is to edit, not to write from scratch. Let Claude be the blank page filler and spend your time improving rather than starting.

### Script 2 — My Full AI Workflow for Content Creation

**Thumbnail concept:** A flowchart showing: idea input on the left, AI tools in the middle, final content outputs on the right. Text: "HOW I USE AI FOR EVERY PIECE OF CONTENT."

**Hook (0-30s):**
I use AI for almost every step of my content creation process. Not to replace writing, but to remove every part that is slow, repetitive, or mechanical. The result is that I can produce significantly more content than I could alone without it feeling like more work. Here is the full workflow.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This is not a generic "AI makes you faster" video. I am going to walk through the specific steps, the specific tools at each step, and the specific prompts I use. You can take this workflow and implement it today.

The content types I will cover: YouTube scripts, Twitter threads, newsletter issues, and product descriptions. Most of the workflow applies to all four.

**Section 1 — Ideation and Research (2-4min):**
Step one is finding what to create.

I start with a question: what problem did I solve or encounter in the last week that my audience also faces. I write that in one sentence.

Then I use Claude to expand it. Prompt: "I am creating content for solopreneurs and people building one-person businesses. I want to cover [topic]. What are the five most interesting angles I could take. For each angle, write a one-sentence hook that would make someone stop scrolling."

This gives me five angle options in about 30 seconds. I pick the strongest one or combine elements from two of them.

For research, I use Perplexity for factual questions and current data. I use Claude for synthesizing my own existing notes into something structured. The rule: Perplexity for external information, Claude for internal information.

**Section 2 — Drafting and Structuring (4-7min):**
For YouTube scripts: I write the outline manually first. Hook, three sections, CTA. This takes about 10 minutes. Then I give the outline to Claude and ask it to expand each section with specific content, using my voice and audience context. I review the draft, cut what does not land, rewrite transitions, and keep the best material.

The result is a draft that is 60% complete and needs about 30 minutes of my time to finish.

For Twitter threads: I use Claude almost entirely for the first draft. Prompt: "Write a 10-tweet thread about [topic]. Tone is direct, no fluff, no motivational language. Each tweet should contain one specific insight. Hook tweet should make a counterintuitive claim."

I then edit every tweet. AI-generated threads are usually 60-70% good. The remaining 30% requires human judgment about what is actually interesting versus what just sounds informative.

For newsletters: I start with the YouTube script or a voice note. I paste it into Claude and ask it to extract the three most actionable points and write them in newsletter format at roughly 150 words per point. That gives me the body of the newsletter. I write the intro and CTA manually.

Product descriptions: I give Claude the product, the audience, the price, and three real benefits. It writes five variations. I pick the closest one and edit.

**Section 3 — The Review and Polish Step (7-10min):**
AI-generated content has a recognizable quality problem. It is competent but flat. It uses phrases like "in today's world" and "it's important to note" and it rarely says anything surprising.

My review process removes this.

First pass: delete any sentence that uses a hedging phrase, a filler opener, or generic business language. Anything that sounds like it was written to sound like writing gets cut.

Second pass: add specificity. Replace any vague claim with a specific example, a number, or a concrete scenario. "AI can save you time" becomes "I cut my newsletter writing time from two hours to 45 minutes."

Third pass: check for voice. Does this sound like me. If not, rewrite the line until it does.

The review pass takes 20-30 minutes for a YouTube script, 10 minutes for a thread, 15 minutes for a newsletter. This is the part where your value as a human creator shows up. The AI provides structure and speed. You provide judgment and specificity.

The output after this process is consistently better than what I would produce writing from scratch, and faster.

### Script 3 — The Prompt Library System (How to Save and Reuse Your Best Prompts)

**Thumbnail concept:** A clean Notion page or database shown on screen with organized prompt categories. Text: "YOUR PROMPT LIBRARY. ZERO FRICTION." Dark background, clean typography.

**Hook (0-30s):**
Every time you figure out a good prompt, you are throwing away most of the value by not saving it. Finding the right prompt for a task can take 10-15 minutes of trial and error. If you do that same task again next week, you start from scratch. A prompt library solves this permanently. Here is how to build one.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
A prompt library is exactly what it sounds like: a collection of your best prompts, organized so you can find and use them in seconds.

The difference between someone who gets good results from AI consistently and someone who gets inconsistent results is usually not skill. It is documentation. The person getting consistent results has saved what works. The person getting inconsistent results is figuring it out fresh every time.

This video shows you how to build the system from scratch and how to populate it so it is actually useful.

**Section 1 — The Structure: How to Organize Prompts (2-4min):**
The most common mistake is organizing prompts by tool. "Claude prompts" and "ChatGPT prompts." The problem: when you are looking for a prompt, you know the task you need to do, not which tool you used. Organize by use case, not by tool.

The categories I use:

Content Creation: scripts, threads, newsletter issues, captions, product descriptions.
Business Operations: emails, proposals, contracts, follow-ups.
Research: summarize, extract, compare, analyze.
Learning: explain, simplify, teach me, quiz me.
Coding: write, review, debug, document.

Within each category, each prompt has four fields.

Name: a descriptive title that tells you what the prompt does.
Prompt: the full text with [brackets] for the variable parts you fill in.
Context notes: when to use it, what works well, what does not.
Last used: a date so you know if a prompt is still relevant.

This structure means you can find any prompt in under 15 seconds.

**Section 2 — Building the Library: Start With What You Use (4-7min):**
Do not start by building a comprehensive library. Start by capturing prompts you actually use.

For one week, every time you craft a prompt that produces a result you are happy with, copy it into your library. Remove the specific content and replace it with variables in brackets. Add a one-sentence note about what it is for.

By the end of week one you will have 10-20 prompts that cover your most common tasks. That is already more valuable than a 200-prompt library you built all at once and never used.

After two weeks you will start to see gaps. Tasks you do regularly but do not have a good prompt for. For each gap, run three to five test versions of a prompt until one produces consistently good output. Document that one.

Month two: review what you have. Delete prompts you have not used. Refine prompts that produce mediocre results. Add context notes to anything that has nuance.

The library is a living document. It gets better the more you use it, but only if you maintain it.

**Section 3 — The Meta-Prompt: Using AI to Improve Prompts (7-10min):**
One of the highest-leverage things you can do with a prompt library is use AI to improve your prompts.

The meta-prompt approach: take a prompt that is not working well and run this: "I have a prompt that is not producing the results I want. Here is the prompt: [paste prompt]. Here is an example of a weak output it produced: [paste output]. Here is what I actually want: [describe ideal output]. Rewrite the prompt to produce better results."

Claude will usually produce a significantly improved version. You can then test it, refine it further, and add the improved version to your library.

This approach also works for creating prompts for tasks you have not done before. Describe the task to Claude, ask it to write a prompt for that task, test the output, and iterate.

Using AI to write prompts that you then use with AI is one of the most efficient ways to expand your prompt library quickly.

One more structure worth adding to your library: the system prompt. Most AI interfaces let you add a persistent system prompt that applies to every conversation. This is where you put your identity context, your tone instructions, and your output format preferences. Once you set this up correctly, your baseline output quality goes up across all tasks without any additional prompting.
