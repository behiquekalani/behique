## Script 1: "I Set Up AI Systems for Businesses. Here's Exactly What I Do"

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Most videos about AI services show you slides. This one shows you the actual work. What I build, what tools I use, what I hand off when the project is done. No theory. Just what a real AI installation looks like from start to finish.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
My name is Kalani. I set up AI systems for small businesses. Not custom models. Not software development. AI installation: taking tools that already exist and connecting them to how a specific business already runs.

A client reaches out. They have a problem. Usually it is something repetitive that a person on their team is doing manually, every day, that a machine could do faster and more consistently. My job is to figure out what that system looks like, build it, and hand it over running.

Here is exactly what that looks like.

**Section 1 — The Discovery Call (1:30-3:30):**
Every project starts with one call. Twenty to thirty minutes. I come in with three questions.

What is the most repetitive thing your team does every week? How many hours does it take? What breaks when it does not get done?

I am not selling anything on this call. I am mapping the problem. The client does most of the talking.

By the end, I have a clear picture of one to three tasks that qualify for automation. Not every task they mention. The ones where the time cost is real, the process is consistent enough to automate, and the output matters to the business.

**Section 2 — The Scope Document (3:30-5:30):**
After the call, I send a one-page scope document. Not a proposal. A scope document.

It covers: what I will build, what it connects to, what they need to provide access to, the timeline, and the price.

One page. Plain language. No diagrams.

The scope document does two things. It shows the client I understood their problem. And it protects both of us by making clear what is in the build and what is not.

If the client comes back with questions, I answer them. If they want to change the scope, I update the document and the price. This process usually takes one to three emails before they sign off.

**Section 3 — The Build (5:30-8:30):**
This is where the actual work happens.

The tools I use most often: n8n for workflow automation, Telegram or WhatsApp Business API for client-facing chatbots, OpenAI or Claude for the AI layer, Airtable or Notion for structured data, and Zapier or Make for simpler connection jobs where n8n is overkill.

A typical mid-tier project ($1,500 to $2,500) involves: an intake form or trigger that starts the workflow, an AI step that processes or classifies the incoming data, an action that writes to wherever the client needs the output, and a notification so the client or their team knows something happened.

I build in a staging environment first. I test it with fake data. Then I test it with real data from the client's actual systems.

Most builds take three to seven days depending on complexity and how fast the client responds to questions.

**Section 4 — Delivery and Handoff (8:30-10:30):**
Delivery is not just sending a login.

I record a five to ten minute Loom walkthrough showing exactly how the system works, what triggers it, where the output goes, and what to do if something breaks.

I write a one-page "how to use this" doc. Basic. Enough for someone non-technical to understand what is running.

I do a live handoff call with the client if the project is over $1,000. We run through the system together. They ask questions. I answer them.

After the handoff, I offer thirty days of support for fixes or small adjustments. Not scope changes. Fixes. If something breaks in the first month, I repair it.

**Section 5 — What the Client Walks Away With (10:30-12:00):**
At the end of a project, the client has:

A working automation running in their actual environment. Documentation they can hand to anyone on their team. A recording they can rewatch without asking me. And my contact information for the support window.

The deliverable is not a demo. It is a live system they can use tomorrow.

That is what AI installation means. That is what clients pay for.

## Script 2: "Why Business Owners Pay $500-$2,500 for AI Setup Instead of Doing It Themselves"

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
$2,500 to set up a few automations sounds like a lot. Until you understand what a business owner is actually buying when they hire someone to do it. It is not the code. I will show you exactly what it is.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
This comes up every time I close a service project. Someone in the comments or in my DMs asks: why would a business owner pay that when they could just figure it out themselves?

It is a reasonable question. The tools are not that hard. YouTube has tutorials for all of them. Most of the software has a free tier.

Here is the actual answer. And it is not what most people assume.

**Section 1 — Time Has a Real Price (1:30-4:00):**
A business owner who runs a $200,000 per year operation works roughly 50 hours a week. Their effective hourly rate is around $75 to $100 per hour.

Setting up an AI automation from scratch, for someone who has never done it, takes 20 to 40 hours. That includes learning the tool, following tutorials, debugging errors, connecting APIs, testing, breaking things, fixing things, and testing again.

At $75 an hour, 30 hours of learning costs $2,250 in time. And at the end of it, they have a system that may or may not work, with no documentation and no one to call if it breaks.

Or they pay someone $1,500 and have a working system in a week with no interruption to their business.

The math is not complicated. Most business owners are not cheap. They are calculating.

**Section 2 — The Expertise Gap Is Real (4:00-6:30):**
There is a difference between following a tutorial and understanding what you built.

A tutorial gets you to a working demo. It does not get you to a production system that handles edge cases, fails gracefully, and integrates cleanly with tools the business already uses.

I have seen clients try to set things up themselves. The demos work. Then the first real piece of data comes through and the workflow breaks because the format was different from what the tutorial assumed.

Debugging that without knowing what you are looking at takes hours. For me it takes ten minutes.

That gap is real. It is not about intelligence. It is about pattern recognition that comes from doing this repeatedly.

**Section 3 — The Cost of Getting It Wrong (6:30-9:00):**
Businesses run on data. A broken automation is not just inconvenient. It is a trust failure.

If the customer inquiry intake breaks and nobody notices for three days, forty-seven customers did not get a response. If the invoice processing automation double-writes an entry, the accounting is wrong.

Business owners know this. That is why they pay for certainty, not just capability.

When I say "I've done this before, here is what breaks and how I prevent it," that is worth money. Not because I am special. Because they are buying the record of every mistake I already made.

**Section 4 — Opportunity Cost (9:00-11:00):**
This is the one most people do not talk about.

While a business owner is spending 30 hours learning n8n, they are not closing deals. They are not serving clients. They are not building the parts of the business that actually require their specific judgment.

Automation is a means, not an end. The goal is to free up high-value time. Spending high-value time to learn automation defeats the purpose.

The ROI calculation is not just "did the automation save time." It is "what could the owner have done with the time they spent learning, compared to just hiring someone."

Most of the time, the answer favors hiring.

**Outro (11:00-12:00):**
People do not pay $2,500 because they cannot figure it out. They pay $2,500 because they have already figured out it is the better use of their time.

If you are thinking about offering this as a service, that is the frame you need to sell from. You are not selling AI. You are selling time back to someone who has already calculated they do not have it to spare.

## Script 3: "How to Find Your First AI Service Client in 30 Days"

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
30 days. No cold email. No paid ads. No existing audience. Here is the exact sequence I would run if I were starting from zero today to land the first AI service client.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
This is not theory. This is the sequence that actually works for service businesses when you are starting with nothing.

The most common mistake is spending week one building a website and a portfolio before talking to a single person. That is the wrong order. The first step is a conversation, not a deliverable.

Here is the 30-day sequence.

**Section 1 — Days 1 to 7: The Warm Network Audit (1:30-4:00):**
Write down every business owner or manager you know personally. Family, friends, former coworkers, people from your neighborhood. Anyone who runs or manages something.

Do not filter by industry. Do not filter by size. Just write the list.

Now highlight the people you could call right now and have a real conversation with. Not a sales call. A conversation. People who would pick up if you called.

That is your first prospect list. Five to ten names is enough.

The outreach message is simple: "Hey, I've been doing some work helping small businesses cut down on repetitive tasks with AI. I know you deal with [thing you know about their business]. Would you be open to a 20-minute call? Just to see if there's anything useful there. No pitch."

Send that to every name on your list.

**Section 2 — Days 8 to 14: The Discovery Calls (4:00-7:00):**
You will get two to five calls from your initial list. Go into each one with the three-question audit.

What is the most repetitive thing your team does every week? How many hours does it take? What breaks when it does not get done?

You are not trying to close on this call. You are listening. Take notes. Ask follow-up questions. Let them talk.

At the end, say: "Based on what you described, I think I can automate that. Can I send you a scope document with what that would look like and what it would cost? No obligation."

Almost no one says no to a scope document.

**Section 3 — Days 15 to 21: The First Scope Document (7:00-9:30):**
Write the scope document within 24 hours of the call. One page. Plain language.

What you will build. What it connects to. What they need to provide. Timeline. Price.

For a first project, price at $499 to $999. Not because you are worth less. Because you need the case study more than you need the margin right now.

Be honest about this framing if they ask. "This is my first project in this specific setup. I am pricing it lower because I want to document the result."

Honest positioning builds trust faster than confident pricing you cannot yet back up.

**Section 4 — Days 22 to 30: Deliver and Document (9:30-11:30):**
Build it. Deliver it clean. Record the Loom walkthrough. Write the handoff doc.

Then, immediately after delivery, ask: "Can I share what we built here? A one-paragraph summary or a brief case study."

Most clients say yes. Some ask to see the draft first. Accommodate that.

That one case study is worth more than any portfolio page you could build in week one. It is real work, real client, real result.

Take that case study and post it. One post on Instagram. One post on LinkedIn if that is relevant to your market. One thread on X.

That is your entire marketing system for month two.

**Outro (11:30-12:30):**
Thirty days. Four to six conversations. One scope document. One delivered project. One case study.

That is the foundation. Not a course. Not a portfolio. Not a cold email sequence.

One real project changes everything about how you talk about what you do, how you price, and who takes you seriously.

Start the warm network audit today. Not tomorrow.

## Script 4: "The 5 AI Systems Every Small Business Needs Right Now"

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Five systems. Not tools. Not apps. Systems. These are the five automations I see missing from almost every small business I talk to, and what it costs them to not have them running.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I have done discovery calls with dozens of small business owners over the past year. Restaurants, real estate agents, freelancers, e-commerce stores, service providers.

The specific problems vary. The underlying gaps are almost always the same five.

Here they are, with the actual build behind each one.

**Section 1 — Customer Inquiry Intake (1:30-3:30):**
Most small businesses handle customer questions by hand. Someone reads the message, figures out the answer, types a reply.

For frequently asked questions, that is a waste of time and a source of inconsistency. The answer to "what are your hours" should not depend on who reads the message.

The build: a trained chatbot connected to whatever channel the business uses. Instagram DMs, WhatsApp, Telegram, or a website widget. The bot handles tier-one questions automatically. Anything it cannot answer gets flagged for a human.

Setup time: two to five hours. Tools: n8n plus OpenAI or Claude plus the relevant API.

What it saves: three to eight hours per week for businesses with moderate inquiry volume.

**Section 2 — Lead Follow-Up Sequences (3:30-5:30):**
Most businesses lose leads not because the lead was not interested. Because nobody followed up in time.

A lead who does not hear back within an hour is 10 times less likely to convert than one who hears back in five minutes. Most small businesses are at 24 to 48 hours.

The build: when a lead submits a form, sends a DM, or clicks a specific link, a workflow triggers. The first message goes out immediately. A second message goes out 24 hours later if there is no response. A third at 72 hours.

All automated. Personalized by the data in the form submission. Stops when the lead responds.

Tools: n8n or Make plus the CRM or form tool the business already uses.

**Section 3 — Content Repurposing Pipeline (5:30-7:30):**
A business owner who records one 10-minute video should not be doing anything to turn that into short clips, captions, or a summary.

The build: video gets uploaded to a trigger folder. Whisper transcribes it. The transcript goes to an AI prompt that extracts five key points, writes three short captions, and produces a summary. Output lands in Notion or Airtable, ready to post.

No editing. No rephrasing. Just structured output from raw content.

This one is especially valuable for service businesses where the owner is the face of the brand but does not have time to produce content consistently.

**Section 4 — Invoice and Receipt Processing (7:30-9:30):**
Manual bookkeeping is one of the most consistent sources of lost time I find in small business audits.

Receipts get stored in email. Invoices get downloaded to a desktop folder. Someone enters them into a spreadsheet at the end of the month.

The build: receipts forwarded to a specific email address trigger a workflow. The attachment goes through a parsing step that extracts vendor, amount, date, and category. That data writes to a Google Sheet or Airtable row automatically.

Not accounting software. A structured intake pipeline that feeds whatever system the business already uses.

Setup time: three to six hours. What it saves: two to four hours per week and a significant reduction in data entry errors.

**Section 5 — Post-Sale Onboarding and Check-Ins (9:30-11:30):**
This one generates the most visible results for service businesses.

When a client pays, they need three things immediately: confirmation that the payment worked, clear next steps, and a sense that they made the right decision.

Most businesses handle this manually or not at all.

The build: payment triggers a workflow that sends a confirmation, delivers any onboarding materials, schedules a check-in reminder for 7 days out, and flags the client in the CRM for a personal follow-up.

That sequence runs without anyone touching it. The client feels taken care of. The business owner has one less thing to remember.

**Outro (11:30-12:30):**
Five systems. Inquiry intake, lead follow-up, content repurposing, receipt processing, and post-sale onboarding.

None of them require custom software. All of them run on tools that exist today. All of them save real hours every week.

## Script 5: "Building a Service Business vs. Selling Products: What I Learned After Doing Both"

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I have done both. Digital products on Gumroad. Custom AI service projects for clients. The income math is different. The lifestyle math is different. The growth path is different. Here is an honest breakdown of what I found.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
This is not a "which one is better" video. Neither is universally better. But most people building online income pick one without understanding what they are actually signing up for.

I want to walk through what each model actually looks like from the inside, because the pitch version of both is misleading.

**Section 1 — How Services Actually Work (1:30-4:30):**
A service business is a time-for-money business at the start. That is the honest frame.

You trade hours for a fixed fee. You scope the project, you build it, you deliver it. The client pays.

The advantages are real and underrated:

You learn fast. Every project is market research. Clients tell you exactly what problems they have, in their own words, with a dollar figure attached.

Feedback is immediate. You know within two weeks whether your work solved the problem. That is much faster signal than product sales.

Cash flow is predictable in a way product sales are not. A booked client is confirmed revenue. A product sale is probabilistic until your audience is large.

The disadvantages are also real. Your income ceiling is tied to your hours. Taking a vacation means not working, which means not earning. Bad-fit clients can make your life difficult. Scope creep is a constant risk if you do not manage it early.

**Section 2 — How Products Actually Work (4:30-7:30):**
A product business is an audience-for-revenue business. That is the frame most people miss.

You do not sell products. You sell to an audience. Without the audience, the product sits there.

The upside everyone knows: a product can sell at 3am without you doing anything. You build it once and it has the potential to sell indefinitely.

What people do not tell you: building the audience takes longer than building the product. Most product businesses take 6 to 18 months before the revenue becomes meaningful. During that time, you are producing content, building trust, testing pricing, and iterating on the product itself.

The $2,500 service project that took you a week gave you more real income in one transaction than most product sellers see in their first three months.

That is not a knock on products. It is context that the pitch version leaves out.

**Section 3 — The Lifestyle Math (7:30-9:30):**
Services give you income stability. Products give you income flexibility. They are not the same thing.

With services, you know what you are making next month because you have the project booked. You can plan. You can budget. That stability is valuable, especially early on.

With products, you do not know what you are making next month unless your audience and conversion rates are predictable. That uncertainty is uncomfortable until you have enough volume and data to read the patterns.

What I found in practice: services fund the lifestyle while products build the asset base. They are not competing. They are sequential.

The mistake is trying to run both at full intensity simultaneously when you are starting out. You end up doing neither well.

**Section 4 — Recurring Revenue Changes Everything (9:30-11:00):**
The real goal in services is not project income. It is retainer income.

A client who pays you $500 per month for ongoing support, monitoring, and updates is worth more over a year than a one-time $2,500 project. And they require less sales work because the relationship is already established.

Getting from project work to retainers is a shift in how you frame the handoff. Instead of ending the engagement at delivery, you position ongoing support as the natural next step.

"The system is live and running. I offer a support plan that covers monitoring, updates, and any adjustments as your business changes. Here is what that looks like."

That conversation, done consistently, is how a service business builds recurring revenue without building a product.

**Outro (11:00-12:00):**
Services gave me faster income, faster learning, and faster market signal. Products gave me leverage that does not depend on my time.

The sequence I would recommend: start with services. Get paid to learn. Collect the patterns. Then use what you learn to build products the market actually wants.

Neither path is passive. Neither path is easy. But one gives you a foundation the other can grow from.
