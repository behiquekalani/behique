### Script 1 — How to Build a Second Brain with Notion

**Thumbnail concept:** Notion interface screenshot showing a clean dashboard. Text overlay: "My entire brain. One system." Dark minimal aesthetic.

**Hook (0-30s):**
The average knowledge worker spends 20 percent of their week searching for information they already have. That is one full day every week spent looking for things you already know. The solution is not working harder. It is building a system that stores and surfaces your knowledge when you need it. That is what a second brain is, and Notion is the best tool I have found to build one.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This video is for anyone who feels like they are constantly re-learning things they have already figured out, losing track of ideas, or drowning in half-finished notes that never turn into anything. A second brain is not a productivity fad. It is a practical system for capturing, organizing, and using the knowledge you accumulate. By the end of this video you will have a clear framework for building one in Notion, even if you have never used Notion seriously before.

**Section 1 — What a Second Brain Actually Is (2-4min):**
The concept was popularized by Tiago Forte in his book Building a Second Brain. The core idea is simple: your biological brain is for generating ideas, not storing them. When you try to hold everything in your head, you create cognitive load that limits creativity and decision-making. Offloading storage to an external system frees your brain to do what it does best.

A second brain has four functions. It captures ideas and information from any source. It organizes that information in a way you can find it again. It distills key insights from raw material. And it expresses those insights through projects, content, and decisions.

Notion works well for this because it is flexible enough to handle all four functions. It can be a capture tool, a database, a project manager, and a publishing space all in one. The downside is that flexibility requires some setup. This video gives you the setup.

**Section 2 — The Notion Second Brain Architecture (4-7min):**
The architecture I recommend is based on Forte's PARA method: Projects, Areas, Resources, and Archive.

Projects are things with a deadline and a defined outcome. A project ends when the outcome is achieved. "Launch the new product" is a project. "Write blog posts" is not, that is an area.

Areas are ongoing responsibilities with no end date. Your health, your finances, your business operations. Areas do not get completed. They get maintained.

Resources are topics you want to learn about or reference regularly. Not tied to a specific project or area, just knowledge you want to have access to. Swipe files, research, reading notes, templates.

Archive is where everything goes when it is no longer active. Old projects, inactive areas, outdated resources. It is not deleted, just filed away so it does not clutter your active workspace.

In Notion, create a top-level page for each of these four categories. Everything you add goes into one of these four buckets. This creates a consistent filing logic that becomes automatic over time.

The most important habit is a weekly sort. Once a week, go through your inbox or capture area and sort everything into the right bucket. This prevents the "pile of unsorted stuff" problem that kills most personal knowledge management systems.

**Section 3 — Making Your Second Brain Useful (7-10min):**
A second brain is only valuable if you can retrieve information when you need it. The two retrieval mechanisms in Notion are search and structure.

Search works when you have good, consistent naming conventions. Be specific with page titles. "Marketing ideas" is hard to search. "Marketing ideas for digital product launch Q2 2026" will surface exactly when you need it.

Structure works through databases and linked views. If you have a projects database, you can filter and sort by status, deadline, or category. Linked views let you see the same database from multiple angles without duplicating data.

Tags add a third layer of retrieval. Tag your notes with relevant topics so you can pull up everything related to a concept even if it is spread across multiple projects and areas.

The habit that makes everything work is what Forte calls progressive summarization. When you first capture something, you keep the full text. The next time you revisit it, you bold the most important sentences. The time after that, you highlight the key phrases. Over time, the most valuable information rises to the top without any extra work.

This makes revisiting old notes much faster and turns your archive into a genuinely useful resource rather than a graveyard of forgotten ideas.

**CTA + Outro (10-12min):**
Start with just one thing today. Create your four top-level folders in Notion: Projects, Areas, Resources, Archive. Then add one page to each that reflects something real in your life right now. That is the whole setup for day one.

### Script 2 — The Weekly Review Ritual That Keeps Your System Running

**Thumbnail concept:** Calendar page with Sunday circled. Simple notepad with three bullet points visible. Text: "60 minutes. Entire week clear."

**Hook (0-30s):**
Every productivity system eventually breaks down for the same reason: the person stops doing their maintenance ritual. The weekly review is that ritual. It is the one habit that keeps every other system, your second brain, your task list, your calendar, all of it, running cleanly. Most people skip it because it feels optional. It is not optional.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This video is about the weekly review: what it is, why almost everyone's version of it is too complicated, and a simplified version that takes 60 minutes or less. I have done some version of a weekly review every week for years. The weeks I skip it, I always feel it. The weeks I do it, I start Monday with clarity instead of anxiety. By the end of this video you will have a process you can run this Sunday.

**Section 1 — Why the Weekly Review Exists (2-4min):**
David Allen introduced the weekly review in Getting Things Done as the mechanism for keeping your trusted system trustworthy. The core idea: your brain cannot relax about something until it trusts that the system is handling it. If your system gets stale and you stop believing it is current, your brain starts trying to hold everything in memory again. That is where stress comes from.

The weekly review is how you keep the system current. You close open loops, update projects, clear your inbox, and set intentions for the coming week. When you do this consistently, you never have a Sunday night dread about Monday because you already know what Monday looks like.

The reason most people's weekly reviews fail is they are too long and too elaborate. If your review takes two hours and feels like a burden, you will skip it when you are tired, which is exactly when you need it most. The goal is a review that is thorough enough to keep your system current and fast enough to do consistently.

**Section 2 — The 60-Minute Weekly Review Process (4-7min):**
This review has five stages.

Stage one: collect (10 minutes). Gather everything that is not yet in your system. Receipts in your wallet, notes on paper, photos of whiteboards, voice memos, screenshots. Get it all into one place so nothing is floating outside your system.

Stage two: process (15 minutes). Go through your inboxes. Email, Notion capture inbox, Telegram saved messages, physical inbox. For each item: decide what it is, decide what action is required, and put it in the right place. This is not doing the tasks. It is deciding where they live.

Stage three: review (20 minutes). Go through your active projects list. For each project, ask: what is the next action? Is the project moving? Is anything blocked? Update statuses. Add tasks to your task manager where needed. This is the most important stage.

Stage four: clean and organize (10 minutes). Archive completed projects. Clear your desktop. Empty your downloads folder. Organize your notes. This is the maintenance pass that keeps your system from accumulating clutter.

Stage five: set intentions (5 minutes). Look at next week's calendar. Identify your three most important outcomes for the week. Write them somewhere visible. These are not tasks. They are the three things that, if accomplished, would make the week a success regardless of everything else.

**Section 3 — Building the Habit (7-10min):**
The weekly review should have a fixed time. Sunday morning or Friday afternoon work well for most people. The key is consistency. If your review moves around every week, it is much easier to skip.

Protect the time. Put it in your calendar. Treat it like a meeting with yourself that does not get rescheduled. One hour a week to have full clarity on your life and work is one of the best returns on time you can find.

If you miss a week, do not try to catch up by doing an extra long review. Just do the standard review as if everything is current. Missing one week is fine. Missing three in a row means your system is breaking down and you need to simplify it.

Build a checklist for your review. Not a long elaborate one. Five to seven items. Go through the same steps in the same order every week. Consistency reduces friction because you are not deciding how to do the review, you are just doing it.

Over time, the weekly review changes how you operate throughout the week. Because you know Sunday is coming, you are more likely to capture things as they happen rather than letting them pile up.

This Sunday, block 60 minutes. Run through the five stages. Do not overthink the process. The goal is to end the session feeling clear.

### Script 3 — Building a Knowledge Base You Will Actually Use

**Thumbnail concept:** Clean bookshelf graphic. Books labeled with topics. Text overlay: "Stop losing what you learn." Minimal aesthetic.

**Hook (0-30s):**
You have read hundreds of articles, books, and threads. You have watched hours of tutorials. And yet, when you try to recall a specific framework or piece of information you know you saw somewhere, it is gone. The problem is not your memory. It is that you never built a system to store and retrieve what you learn. Here is how to fix that.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
A knowledge base is a searchable, organized repository of the things you learn, the decisions you make, and the insights you want to keep. It is different from a second brain in that it is less about capturing ideas and more about curating knowledge you have vetted and found valuable. This video is about how to build a personal knowledge base that grows with you and becomes more useful over time, not a graveyard of half-read articles and forgotten notes.

**Section 1 — The Problem with Most Personal Knowledge Systems (2-4min):**
Most people save too much. They have bookmarks they never open, saved tweets they never revisit, and Notion pages they have not touched in months. The result is a storage problem, not a knowledge problem. More is not more.

The key insight is that your knowledge base should contain only what you have already processed and found valuable. Not raw captures. Not "save for later." Only things you have thought about, summarized in your own words, and decided are worth keeping.

This changes how you approach consuming content. Instead of trying to save everything interesting, you ask a different question: is this worth processing? If yes, you read it fully, summarize the key idea in your own words, and add it to your knowledge base with a note about why it matters to you.

If no, you let it go. The absence of clutter is what makes a knowledge base searchable and useful.

**Section 2 — The Structure That Works (4-7min):**
A knowledge base has three layers.

Layer one: atomic notes. These are single-concept notes that capture one idea or insight. They are short, usually under 200 words, titled with the specific concept they contain. "Value based pricing," "How habit loops work," "The discovery call question that changes everything." Each atomic note links to other notes where relevant.

Layer two: index notes. These are map-of-content pages that link to related atomic notes around a theme. "Everything I know about pricing" or "My notes on productivity systems." Index notes are not summaries. They are navigation aids that help you find clusters of related knowledge.

Layer three: MOCs (maps of content) or topic hubs. These are higher-level pages that bring together your index notes and point toward projects or content you are creating from the knowledge. This is where your knowledge base connects back to your actual work.

The navigation structure is what separates a useful knowledge base from a pile of notes. Build the index notes as you go. Every time you add five or six notes on a topic, create an index note that links them together.

**Section 3 — The Input-Output Habit (7-10min):**
A knowledge base becomes valuable when it has two directions: input and output.

Input is everything you add. New notes, updated notes, new connections between ideas. The rule for input is always to write in your own words. Pasting a quote is storage. Summarizing in your own words is learning. Your knowledge base is for the latter.

Output is how you use what is stored. When you write content, create a product, or solve a problem, start by searching your knowledge base first. You will often find that you already have most of the material you need. The act of creating from your notes is also how you discover gaps in your thinking, which drives more quality input.

The habit that makes this work: every time you learn something worth keeping, spend five minutes adding it to your knowledge base before you close the article or video. Not "I will do it later." Right now, five minutes, done.

This habit, compounded over a year, creates a genuinely powerful resource. After 12 months of consistent input, your knowledge base contains hundreds of processed, connected insights that you can draw on for content, decision-making, and creative work.

**CTA + Outro (10-12min):**
Start your knowledge base this week with just 10 notes. Pick 10 ideas, frameworks, or insights you have encountered in the past month that you actually found useful. Write each one in your own words. Title it with the concept. That is your starting point.
