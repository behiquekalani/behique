### Script 1 — How to Price Your Digital Product

**Thumbnail concept:** Three price tags: $9, $47, $197. Arrow pointing to $47. Bold text: "Price it like this." Clean white background.

**Hook (0-30s):**
Pricing a digital product is not about picking a number that feels comfortable. Comfort pricing almost always means underpricing. The goal is to price based on value delivered, not on how long it took you to make or what competitors are charging. I am going to show you a framework that takes the guesswork out of pricing and helps you find the number that makes sense for your market.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Pricing is one of the most overthought decisions in digital product businesses. People spend weeks agonizing over whether to charge $27 or $37, then copy a competitor's price and never revisit it. In this video I give you a structured framework for arriving at a price that reflects the value of your product, not just the time you spent making it. You will also understand why the same product can legitimately sell for five to ten times more depending on how it is positioned.

**Section 1 — The Problem with Cost-Based Pricing (2-4min):**
Most first-time product creators price based on cost: either the time they spent creating the product, or the cost of whatever tool or service they used to make it. This is a logical instinct and it produces consistently bad prices.

The problem is that cost has nothing to do with value. A checklist that takes someone from confusion to clarity on a high-stakes decision might take you two hours to make and is worth $97 to the right buyer. A 200-page ebook that took you three months to write might be worth $7 to someone who only needed one chapter.

What matters to the buyer is not how long you worked. It is: does this solve my problem, and does the price match the stakes of that problem? A template that saves a freelancer three hours of work per week is worth a lot more than its production cost.

**Section 2 — The Value-Based Pricing Framework (4-7min):**
Step one: define the specific outcome your product delivers. Not features. Outcome. "This guide teaches you how to build an email list" is a feature. "By following this guide, you will have your first 100 email subscribers within 30 days" is an outcome.

Step two: calculate the value of that outcome to your buyer. What is 100 email subscribers worth to someone building a digital product business? If one subscriber converts to a sale every 20 opens, and the product costs $47, then 100 subscribers is worth $235 in expected revenue. That makes a $47 price for the guide very easy to justify, because the ROI is obvious.

Step three: find the ceiling and the floor. The ceiling is the value of the outcome. The floor is slightly above what would make it feel too cheap to be trustworthy. In most digital product niches, there is a minimum credibility threshold. A $1 product signals low quality. A $7 product can work for impulse purchases. Anything requiring real commitment from the buyer should usually start at $19 minimum.

Step four: test. Price is a variable. Pick a number that feels right based on the framework, launch, and observe conversion rates. If everyone who sees the sales page buys, you are probably underpriced. If no one buys and you are getting traffic, there is either a value problem or a price problem. Raise the price before assuming the value is wrong.

**Section 3 — Price Anchoring and the Value Stack (7-10min):**
Anchoring is the practice of placing your product price next to a higher reference point so it feels like good value.

The most common version is the comparison. "A 1-hour consultation with me costs $200. This guide delivers the same framework for $47." This anchoring works because it gives the buyer a mental reference for the price.

The value stack is related. Instead of listing what the product is, list everything it includes and assign individual values to each component. "You get the main guide (worth $47), the implementation checklist (worth $27), the email swipe file (worth $37), and a bonus training video (worth $57). Total value $168. Your price today: $67."

Whether or not anyone would actually buy those components separately is somewhat irrelevant. The stack communicates abundance and makes the actual price feel like a deal.

Pricing tiers also unlock more revenue without requiring new products. A base tier with the core product, a standard tier that adds a bonus, and a premium tier that adds a personal element like a review or a live call. Tiers allow different buyers to self-select based on their situation and budget.

**CTA + Outro (10-12min):**
Take your current product or the one you are building, and go through the four steps right now. Define the specific outcome, calculate its value to your buyer, find your floor and ceiling, and pick a number to test. Do not agonize over it. You can change the price later. What you cannot do is generate data without launching.

### Script 2 — The Value Stack: How to Make Any Product Feel Like a Deal

**Thumbnail concept:** Shopping cart with many items. Price tag crossed out at $200. New price: $67. Text: "Stack > discount."

**Hook (0-30s):**
Discounting is the laziest way to increase conversions. You train your buyers to wait for sales, and you condition yourself to believe your product is not worth full price. The value stack is the smarter alternative. You keep the price, increase the perceived value, and your conversion rate goes up without reducing your margin.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Every top-performing digital product sale page uses a value stack. You have seen it even if you did not register it consciously. The long list of everything included, with individual prices listed next to each item, culminating in a total that is far higher than the actual price. This video breaks down exactly how to build a value stack, how to price the components, and why it works even when buyers know the individual prices are somewhat notional.

**Section 1 — The Psychology Behind the Value Stack (2-4min):**
The value stack exploits a well-documented psychological phenomenon called the principle of contrast. When you show a high number before a lower one, the lower one feels more favorable than if it had been shown alone.

A product priced at $97 feels expensive in isolation. The same product priced at $97 after showing that it includes $400 worth of components feels like a bargain.

This is not manipulation. The components genuinely have value. The framework teaches real skills. The templates save real time. The checklist prevents real mistakes. Assigning individual values to these components is not fiction. It is helping the buyer understand what they are getting.

The second reason the value stack works is that it changes the mental comparison. Instead of comparing your $97 product to a competitor's $87 product, the buyer is now comparing $97 to $400. That comparison is much more favorable.

**Section 2 — How to Build Your Value Stack (4-7min):**
A good value stack has four to six components. Too few and it feels thin. Too many and it starts to feel like you are padding.

Component one: the core product. This is the main thing they are buying. Assign it a price that reflects its standalone value. Usually the full price of the offer or slightly above it.

Component two: the primary bonus. Something that directly enhances the core product. If the core is a course on email marketing, the primary bonus might be a swipe file of 30 proven subject lines. Assign it a value that feels realistic if it were sold separately.

Component three: the implementation aid. A checklist, template, or worksheet that makes the core product easier to use. This is often the highest-perceived-value component because it reduces the work the buyer has to do.

Component four: the context bonus. Something that addresses the next step after the core product. If the core is about getting clients, the next step might be about pricing. A bonus guide on pricing follows naturally and extends the value of the purchase.

Optional component five: access or community. A live Q and A, a private community, an email review. Access to you as a person is the highest-value component you can offer and can dramatically increase your overall package value.

Add up all the individual values. Your actual price should be 30 to 60 percent of that total. This creates the contrast that makes the price feel right.

**Section 3 — Where to Put the Value Stack (7-10min):**
The value stack lives on your sales page, typically in the middle to lower third, after you have established the problem and your credibility, but before the final call to action.

The format: headline that says something like "Here's everything you get." Then list each component with its name, a one-sentence description, and its individual value in parentheses. At the bottom: "Total value: $X. Your price today: $Y."

The conversion formula: the more specific each component description is, the more the value feels real. "Bonus: extra tips" is worthless. "Bonus: 30-email subject line swipe file, collected from 90-day campaign with 42% average open rate" is specific enough to feel credible and valuable.

Test your value stack by asking someone who is your ideal buyer to read your sales page and tell you which component they find most valuable. Their answer will surprise you, and it tells you which component to lead with or emphasize most.

**CTA + Outro (10-12min):**
Audit your existing sales page or product description today. Does it have a value stack or does it just describe the product. If there is no stack, add one. Pick three to four components, assign honest values, and create the contrast.

### Script 3 — Launching Without an Audience

**Thumbnail concept:** Megaphone graphic. Nobody listening graphic on one side. First sale notification on the other. Text: "Zero followers. First sale."

**Hook (0-30s):**
The most common reason people delay launching their digital product is that they do not have an audience yet. They wait until they have 1,000 followers. Then 5,000. Then they never launch because the audience never feels big enough. The reality is that you do not need an audience to make your first sale. You need a distribution strategy. Here is one that works from zero.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This video is for anyone who has a product idea or a finished product and is waiting for the right moment. The right moment is now. Not because of some motivational reason, but because of a practical one: getting your first 10 to 20 sales requires almost no audience. It requires access to places where your potential buyers already are. This video maps out those places and gives you a concrete launch plan you can run this week even with zero followers.

**Section 1 — Why Audience Size Is the Wrong Metric (2-4min):**
Audience size is a proxy for distribution. What you actually need is to put your product in front of people who already have the problem it solves. A large audience is one way to do that. It is not the only way.

The people who buy your first 10 products are almost never your followers. They are people who found you through a specific piece of content, a specific community post, or a specific search result. The channel does not require a following. It requires that one person, then another, then another, found something relevant to their situation.

Your first goal is not 1,000 customers. It is 1 customer. Then 10. Then 100. The path to each of those milestones is different and none of them require a large built-in audience if you use the right channels.

**Section 2 — Five Channels That Work Without an Audience (4-7min):**
Channel one: niche communities. Reddit, Facebook groups, Discord servers, Slack communities. Every niche has places where people gather online. Find three to five that contain your ideal buyer. Participate genuinely for two weeks before mentioning your product. When you do mention it, frame it as a resource, not a sale. "I built this because I had this exact problem" performs far better than "buy my product."

Channel two: content on platforms with algorithmic discovery. YouTube, TikTok, Pinterest, and now LinkedIn all have algorithms that can surface your content to people who have never heard of you. One piece of content that addresses a specific problem can drive hundreds of visitors to your product page without any followers.

Channel three: partner distribution. Find five to ten people with small audiences in adjacent niches. Offer them your product for free in exchange for an honest review or mention. Small newsletter writers and micro-influencers often say yes because the product is relevant to their audience.

Channel four: personal network outreach. Not a mass email blast. Targeted messages to 20 to 30 people you know who match your buyer profile. "I just launched something I think would be useful for you" is not spam. It is a recommendation from someone they know.

Channel five: SEO-focused free content. Write one piece of content specifically targeting a long-tail search query that your buyer types when they are looking for what you offer. This takes longer to pay off but requires no promotion and compounds over time.

**Section 3 — The Zero-Audience Launch Plan (7-10min):**
Day one to three: prepare. Finish the product if you have not. Set up the simplest possible sales page. You do not need a full website. Gumroad or Lemon Squeezy and a clear one-page description will do. Have your payment processing working and confirmed.

Day four to seven: community priming. Join three niche communities. Introduce yourself with one honest post about your background and what you are working on. Add value by answering existing questions. No product mention yet.

Day eight: the soft launch. Post in those communities with the framing: "I just finished building X. I think it is relevant for people dealing with [specific problem]. Here is the link if you want to check it out." Short, direct, no hard sell.

Day nine onward: content distribution. Publish one piece of content on YouTube, LinkedIn, or wherever your buyer spends time that addresses the core problem your product solves. End the content with a mention of the product.

Day 14: partner outreach. Send five messages to potential partners offering the product free in exchange for an honest mention if they find it useful.

This plan can produce your first five to ten sales within two weeks from a standing start. Not guaranteed, but achievable.

The only thing standing between you and your first sale is the decision to ship. Pick a launch date this week. Tell one person. The external commitment will make you follow through.
