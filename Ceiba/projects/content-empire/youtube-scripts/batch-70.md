### Script 1 — The Solopreneur Content Strategy (Simple, Repeatable, Actually Sustainable)

**Thumbnail concept:** A simple content pyramid diagram. Three tiers labeled: long-form, short-form, conversational. Clean white background with bold color fills per tier.

**Hook (0-30s):**
Most solopreneurs are either posting every day and burning out or posting nothing and wondering why they are not growing. There is a third option, and it is what actually works. A content strategy that is simple enough to maintain alone and structured enough to compound over time. I am going to show you mine.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This is for solopreneurs, freelancers, or anyone building a one-person business who knows they need to create content but does not have a team or a full-time schedule to dedicate to it.

The strategy I am sharing works with as little as three hours per week. The output is more than enough to build an audience, drive traffic to your products, and grow an email list.

The key is thinking about content as a system, not a schedule.

**Section 1 — The Content Pyramid (2-4min):**
Every piece of content you create should have a purpose in a hierarchy.

At the top is long-form content. One piece per week. This is your anchor: a YouTube video, a newsletter issue, or a long Twitter thread. Long-form builds depth, trust, and SEO over time. It takes the most effort and produces the most leverage.

In the middle is short-form content. Three to five pieces per week derived from your long-form. These are Reels, TikToks, quote graphics, or short tweets. Short-form builds reach and brings new people into your world.

At the bottom is conversational content. Replies, comments, DMs. This is where relationships form. You do not need to schedule this, but you do need to do it. Twenty minutes per day, three days per week.

The entire system flows from one anchor piece per week. Everything else is derived. You are not creating from scratch every time.

**Section 2 — The Weekly Production Schedule (4-7min):**
Here is what the week looks like in practice.

Monday: Create your anchor content. For me this is either a YouTube script or a newsletter draft. I dedicate two hours to this one thing.

Tuesday: Record or finalize the anchor piece.

Wednesday: Repurpose. Take the three best points from the anchor and turn each one into a short-form post. This takes about an hour if you have a repurposing template.

Thursday: Schedule the short-form posts using Buffer or a similar tool.

Friday: Engagement day. Reply to comments, engage with people in your niche, follow up on any DMs.

That is the week. About five hours total. One anchor, five short-form pieces, consistent engagement.

The trap to avoid is creating content that has no connection to your products or offers. Every piece of content should, directly or indirectly, lead somewhere. To a lead magnet, a product, or an email list opt-in.

**Section 3 — The Growth Mechanism (7-10min):**
Content alone does not grow an audience. Distribution does.

After you post your anchor content, put it in front of people who do not follow you yet. This is the step most people skip.

For YouTube: optimize the title and thumbnail for search. One video on a specific topic can bring in organic traffic for years.

For newsletters: share the issue link on Twitter and LinkedIn the day it goes out. Mention relevant communities.

For short-form: use platform-specific strategies. On Reels, use trending audio. On TikTok, post daily for the first 30 days to understand what your audience responds to.

The content strategy compounds when you do it consistently. Month one you might get ten new followers. Month six you might get 500 from a single video. The growth is not linear, but it is real.

The number one reason people fail at content is inconsistency. Not quality. Not platform choice. They quit before the compound effect kicks in.

One piece of long-form content per week, every week, for 12 months. That is the strategy. It is not complicated. It is just consistent.

### Script 2 — Why I Batch All My Content (And Why You Should Too)

**Thumbnail concept:** A calendar with one day heavily marked in red: "CONTENT DAY." The other days are clear. Text overlay: "One day. A month of content."

**Hook (0-30s):**
I do not create content daily. I create it all at once, once per week or once every two weeks. This is called batching and it is one of the highest-leverage changes I have made to how I run this business. Here is exactly how I do it.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Content creation is not just writing or recording. It is the cognitive warm-up before you start, the decisions about what to create, the switching between creation mode and editing mode. All of that overhead adds up.

When you create content every day, you pay that overhead every day. When you batch, you pay it once.

This video explains the batching system I use, why it works psychologically and practically, and how to set it up even if you feel like you need fresh inspiration every day to create anything good.

**Section 1 — The Psychology of Batching (2-4min):**
Your brain has two modes that are relevant here: creative mode and execution mode.

Creative mode is generative. You are making decisions, finding angles, writing first drafts. It is high energy and it does not last indefinitely.

Execution mode is mechanical. Recording, editing, scheduling. Lower cognitive load, more repeatable.

When you create daily, you are switching between both modes every single day. That context switching is expensive. You lose 20-30 minutes each time just getting back into flow.

Batching works by grouping all your creative work into one session and all your execution work into another. One day of writing. One day of recording. One day of editing and scheduling. You stay in the same mode for longer periods and do both work types better.

There is also a momentum effect. Once you are in creative mode and you have written one good piece, the second one comes faster. The third one faster still. The first one is always the hardest. Batching means you only pay that startup cost once.

**Section 2 — How to Structure a Batch Day (4-7min):**
Here is what a bi-weekly content batch session looks like for me.

Morning, first two hours: I outline everything I want to create for the next two weeks. Script outlines for videos, thread ideas, newsletter topics. I use a simple list. I do not start writing yet.

Next two hours: I write all the short-form content first. Tweets, captions, short-form scripts. These are faster to write once your brain is in writing mode. Getting through them builds momentum.

After that: the long-form anchor pieces. By now I am in flow and the writing comes faster than it would cold.

End of session: organize everything into a folder structure. Video scripts in one folder. Newsletter drafts in another. Social posts in a scheduling queue.

The following day is execution: record videos, schedule social posts, send newsletters.

The whole content pipeline for two weeks is completed in one full day plus one morning. That is roughly 10 hours per two weeks, or five hours per week. The math is similar to daily creation but the quality and the mental load are completely different.

**Section 3 — Solving the Inspiration Problem (7-10min):**
The most common objection is: I can not create in advance because I do not know what I will be inspired by two weeks from now.

This objection is real but it is solvable. The solution is an idea capture system.

Throughout the week I drop ideas into a single note in Notion. Just fragments: an observation, a question someone asked me, a problem I solved. No pressure to develop them. Just capture.

When batch day comes, I open that note and I have 15-20 fragments. Most are weak. Three or four are strong enough to build content around. Those become my scripts for the next batch.

The inspiration is not missing. It was just uncaptured. Build the capture habit and the batch day goes from intimidating to mechanical.

One more tool that helps: an outline template. Every piece of content I create starts with the same three-part structure: what is the problem, here is the framework, here is the action. Having a template means I am never starting from a blank page. I am filling in a structure.

### Script 3 — How to Turn One YouTube Video Into 10 Pieces of Content

**Thumbnail concept:** One video thumbnail on the left, then arrows exploding outward to 10 different platform icons: Twitter, Instagram, TikTok, newsletter, LinkedIn, etc. Text: "1 VIDEO = 10 POSTS."

**Hook (0-30s):**
Most people spend two hours making a YouTube video and then move on. They leave 90% of the value on the table. Everything you need for a month of social content is already inside that one video. I am going to show you exactly how to extract it.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This system is called content repurposing. It is not new, but most explanations of it are vague. I am going to give you a specific process with specific outputs so you know exactly what to make from a single video.

This works for YouTube videos, podcast episodes, or long newsletter issues. Any long-form content becomes the raw material for everything else.

By the end of this video you will have a 10-piece content list you can apply to any video you create starting today.

**Section 1 — The Framework: Extract Before You Repurpose (2-4min):**
Before you repurpose anything, you extract.

Watch or read your long-form content and pull out these four things:

One: the core argument. The one claim the whole piece is built around.
Two: three to five supporting points. The evidence or steps.
Three: one surprising or counterintuitive fact.
Four: one practical takeaway someone can use immediately.

Write these down as notes before you start repurposing. They are your raw material.

Most people try to repurpose by skimming the content and reformatting it. That produces weak derivatives. Extracting first forces you to understand the content at a structural level, which produces better repurposed pieces.

**Section 2 — The 10 Content Pieces (4-7min):**
Here are the 10 pieces you get from one video.

Piece 1: Twitter thread. Turn the core argument and three supporting points into a thread. The hook is the core argument. Each tweet after is one supporting point explained in one to three sentences. End with the takeaway.

Piece 2: Quote graphic. Take the most quotable line from the video and turn it into a clean graphic for Instagram or LinkedIn. This takes five minutes in Canva.

Piece 3: Short-form clip. Pick the 60-90 second segment where you make your most compelling point. This becomes a Reel or TikTok with captions. No additional recording needed.

Piece 4: Newsletter paragraph. The core argument condensed into 150 words becomes a section in your next newsletter issue.

Piece 5: LinkedIn post. The practical takeaway formatted as a standalone tip. LinkedIn rewards direct, actionable content.

Piece 6: Second short-form clip. The surprising or counterintuitive fact usually makes a strong standalone clip. People share things that surprise them.

Piece 7: FAQ post. What questions does the video raise? List three of them and answer each in one sentence. This works well as a carousel or a thread.

Piece 8: Poll. Turn the core argument into a question. "Do you batch your content or create daily?" Polls drive engagement and give you data on your audience.

Piece 9: Behind the scenes. A single screenshot or photo from your creation process with a one-sentence caption. "Working on this video for three hours. Thread coming Wednesday."

Piece 10: Email to your list. A shorter, more personal version of the video for subscribers. Reference the video at the end and link to it.

That is ten pieces from one video. At least three of them require no additional writing, just clipping and formatting.

**Section 3 — The Production System (7-10min):**
Here is how to make this repeatable without it taking too long.

First: use an AI tool to speed up the extraction step. Paste your video script or transcript into Claude and ask it to pull the core argument, three supporting points, the most counterintuitive claim, and the strongest takeaway. Thirty seconds versus ten minutes of manual reading.

Second: use templates for every format. A Twitter thread template. A LinkedIn post template. A Reels caption template. Templates mean you are filling in blanks, not writing from scratch.

Third: schedule all 10 pieces at once using a scheduling tool like Buffer. One session of scheduling covers you for one to two weeks depending on your posting cadence.

The entire repurposing session after a video is done should take 60 to 90 minutes. Script extraction with AI: 10 minutes. Writing the thread and LinkedIn post: 30 minutes. Clipping short-form segments: 20 minutes. Scheduling everything: 20 minutes.

One video. One 90-minute session. Ten pieces of content.
