## SCRIPT 1: The One-Person Media Company (Full System)

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

The one-person media company is not a trend. It's the most viable business structure for anyone who builds things independently in 2026.

But most people who talk about it describe the concept without describing the system. Concept without system is motivation without direction.

This video is the full system.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-2:00]**

Dan Koe, Justin Welsh, and a handful of others made the one-person media company concept widely known. The idea is simple: one person creates content across multiple platforms, builds an audience, and monetizes through products and services. No employees, no overhead, full leverage.

What's less discussed is the operational infrastructure behind it. How the content actually gets produced consistently. How the platforms connect to each other. How the products align with the content. How to build this without burning out in month three.

Today I'm covering the full stack.

- The content flywheel: how one piece of content becomes many
- The platform hierarchy: where to focus and why
- The product layer: what to build and when
- The operating system: the minimal infrastructure you actually need
- The numbers: what to expect and when

This is the complete picture.

---

**[SECTION 1: The Content Flywheel — 2:00-5:00]**

The content flywheel is the operational core of the one-person media company. It solves the hardest problem in content creation: producing enough content across enough platforms without spending 60 hours a week doing it.

The flywheel works on a simple principle: create once, distribute many.

Here's the structure. Once per week, create one long-form piece of content. This is your primary asset. For most people this is a YouTube video or a long-form newsletter. This piece takes the most time and carries the most depth.

From that one piece, extract:

- Three to five Twitter/X posts (individual insights or quotes)
- One or two LinkedIn posts (the business angle)
- Two or three short-form video clips (for YouTube Shorts, TikTok, or Reels)
- One email newsletter issue

A single YouTube video, if scripted intentionally, contains enough material for an entire week of social content. The video is not just a YouTube asset. It's the raw material for everything else.

The key is designing your primary content with repurposing in mind. Scripts that contain distinct, self-contained insights repurpose well. Essays and rambling conversations repurpose poorly.

When you build this system, one hour of primary content creation becomes five to six hours of distributed content across platforms. You didn't work five extra hours. You worked the same hour more intelligently.

---

**[SECTION 2: The Platform Hierarchy — 5:00-8:00]**

The one-person media company does not require you to be everywhere. It requires you to be strategic about where your primary content lives and where your distribution goes.

The hierarchy works like this.

**Primary platform** (where depth lives): YouTube or long-form newsletter. These are the highest-trust, highest-conversion formats. They're where you teach, demonstrate expertise, and build the relationship that converts to sales. Choose one to do well, not both to do adequately.

**Distribution platforms** (where reach comes from): Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Instagram, TikTok. These are not where you build depth. They're where you distribute clips and ideas from your primary platform and drive people back to it. Treat these as top-of-funnel, not as core business assets.

**Owned platform** (the floor): your email list. This is the only distribution channel you actually own. Platform algorithms change, accounts get restricted, reach fluctuates. Your email list cannot be taken from you. Everything in your business should be optimizing for this.

The common mistake is treating all platforms equally and spreading energy thin. Pick one primary platform. Build it well. Let the distribution platforms serve it. Build the email list from it.

---

**[SECTION 3: The Product Layer — 8:00-11:00]**

Content without products is a hobby. Products without content have no distribution. The product layer is where the one-person media company becomes a business.

The product ladder for a one-person media company looks like this.

**Entry product ($17-$47):** A focused guide, template, or toolkit that solves one specific problem your audience has. Low barrier to entry. Tests what your audience will pay for. Builds your buyer list.

**Core product ($97-$297):** A more comprehensive system, course, or framework. This is the product you're known for. It solves the central problem your content addresses. If your content is about building a content business, your core product is a complete system for building a content business.

**High-ticket offer ($500+):** Consulting, coaching, done-for-you services, or a high-value community. This serves the buyers who want direct access to you. Not everyone builds this layer, but it's where the highest per-unit revenue is.

**Recurring revenue ($15-$50/month):** A membership, paid newsletter tier, or software product. This creates the financial floor that makes everything else less stressful.

Build these layers incrementally. Entry product first, in the first 3 months. Core product by month 6-9. Recurring revenue when you have 1,000+ email subscribers. High-ticket when you have proof of results from your core product.

---

**[SECTION 4: The Operating System — 11:00-13:00]**

The minimal infrastructure for a functioning one-person media company.

**Content management:** Notion, Obsidian, or a similar knowledge system. You need one place where all content ideas, scripts, research, and performance data live. Not five tools. One.

**Email platform:** Beehiiv, ConvertKit, or Kit. Your email list is your most important asset. Invest in a real platform from day one. Free tiers work for the first 500-1,000 subscribers.

**Product delivery:** Gumroad, Lemon Squeezy, or Podia. Pick one and don't overthink it. The product delivery platform matters less than the product itself.

**Analytics:** YouTube Studio, your email platform's analytics, and a simple spreadsheet for tracking revenue. Check analytics weekly, not daily. Daily analytics checking is a distraction from creation.

**Automation:** One automated welcome sequence for new email subscribers, one automated delivery system for digital products. These two automations handle the most time-sensitive tasks without ongoing attention.

The total monthly cost of this infrastructure: under $50. The barrier to starting is not the tools. It's the decision to start.

---

**[SECTION 5: The Numbers — 13:00-15:00]**

What to expect and when, based on honest observation.

Months 1-3: Zero revenue, probably. This is the learning and building phase. You're developing your content voice, your production process, and your first product. Revenue is not the metric. Process is the metric.

Months 3-6: First sales. If you've launched your entry product and you're driving traffic consistently, you should see your first few hundred dollars in this window. Realistic range: $0-$500/month.

Months 6-12: Product-market fit. By now you know what your audience buys. The core product is launched or in development. Email list is growing. Monthly revenue range: $500-$3,000.

Year 2: The compounding phase. Your older content is driving consistent traffic. Your email sequence is converting subscribers automatically. The systems are running. Monthly revenue range: $2,000-$10,000 depending on niche, offer quality, and consistency.

These numbers are not guarantees. They're patterns. The variance depends primarily on niche selection, offer quality, and consistency of content output.

The one-person media company is not a get-rich-quick path. It's a build-it-once, leverage-it-forever path. The early investment of time and consistency pays compounding dividends for years.

## SCRIPT 2: How to Make Money with Digital Downloads

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

Digital downloads are the simplest business model in the creator economy. No shipping, no inventory, no customer service beyond the occasional email. You build something once and sell it indefinitely.

Most people make it more complicated than it needs to be.

Here's the complete picture in under 15 minutes.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

I've been building and selling digital products for over a year. The patterns I'm covering today are based on what actually sells, not what sounds good in a marketing course.

Here's what we're covering:

- What types of digital downloads actually sell
- How to set up the minimal infrastructure
- Pricing strategy for digital downloads
- The marketing channels that generate consistent sales
- How to scale from first sale to ongoing passive income

---

**[SECTION 1: What Types of Digital Downloads Actually Sell — 1:30-4:00]**

Not all digital downloads sell equally. The ones that sell consistently share one characteristic: they save the buyer meaningful time or give them something they couldn't easily build themselves.

**Templates** are the highest-converting digital download category. Notion templates, Google Sheets templates, Canva templates. People buy templates because building from scratch is slower than customizing something that's already done. A well-designed Notion workspace template for content creators can sell for $15-$47 and has essentially zero marginal cost.

**Guides and ebooks** sell when they solve a specific, high-urgency problem. A 2,000-word PDF on "how to set up your first Gumroad store in one afternoon" will outsell a 200-page comprehensive guide to digital business. Specificity and urgency drive downloads, not length.

**Swipe files and resource lists** are underrated. A curated list of 50 hook formulas for YouTube, or 30 email subject lines that convert, or the 15 tools a solopreneur actually needs, provides immediate value that buyers can use today. Low price point, high volume.

**Mini-courses** are a digital download when they're delivered as downloadable video files or a simple linked video collection. Not a full course platform, just recorded lessons. Lower production bar, faster to market.

The products that fail: overly generic ebooks on broad topics, product names that don't immediately communicate what they do, and anything that requires ongoing maintenance to stay useful.

---

**[SECTION 2: Minimal Infrastructure Setup — 4:00-6:00]**

The infrastructure for selling digital downloads is simpler than most people think.

You need: a product delivery platform, a payment processor (built into the platform), a landing page for each product, and an email list to market to.

For the platform: Gumroad is the easiest starting point. Zero upfront cost, they take 10% on free accounts or a flat fee on paid. You can have a product live in under an hour.

For the landing page: Gumroad has built-in landing pages. They're basic but functional. For higher conversion rates, build a standalone landing page with Carrd or a simple HTML page. But don't let landing page perfection delay your first launch.

For the email list: add every buyer to your email list automatically through Gumroad's integration with ConvertKit or Beehiiv. Your buyer list is your most valuable marketing asset.

Spend less than two hours on setup. Every hour on infrastructure is an hour not spent on the product or marketing.

---

**[SECTION 3: Pricing Digital Downloads — 6:00-8:30]**

Three pricing tiers that work for digital downloads.

**Tier 1 ($7-$17):** Entry price, impulse purchase range. Buyers need almost no consideration time. Best for: single-purpose templates, swipe files, resource lists, short guides. Volume is the play here. 100 sales at $17 is $1,700.

**Tier 2 ($27-$67):** Mid-range, considered purchase. Buyer thinks briefly but doesn't need to sleep on it. Best for: comprehensive templates, structured guides, mini-courses, tool bundles. This is the sweet spot for most first digital products.

**Tier 3 ($97-$297):** High-consideration purchase. Buyer is making a real investment. Best for: complete systems, full courses, template suites, detailed playbooks. Requires more trust and better sales copy than the lower tiers.

Price test by launching at Tier 2 and watching conversion rate. If conversion rate is over 3%, the price is right or too low. Under 1%, the product is either not solving a felt need or the sales page isn't communicating the value.

---

**[SECTION 4: Marketing Channels That Generate Consistent Sales — 8:30-11:00]**

The three highest-converting marketing channels for digital downloads, in order of reliability.

**Channel 1: Your email list.** Existing subscribers who know your work convert at 2-8% on a new product announcement. This is 10-50x the conversion rate of cold traffic. Every new subscriber who comes from your content is a potential buyer. Prioritize list growth above all other marketing activities.

**Channel 2: Pinterest.** Pinterest pins that link to your product page drive long-term passive traffic. Create 5-10 pins per product with different angles (problem-focused, benefit-focused, visual preview of the product). Pin consistently for 60 days and let the traffic compound. Pinterest traffic is slower to build than social media but doesn't decay the way algorithm-based traffic does.

**Channel 3: YouTube.** If you're making content on topics related to your digital products, every video is a product ad. A tutorial video that uses your Notion template as the example converts viewers who watch the tutorial into buyers who want the template. This is the most leveraged marketing because the content serves both educational and commercial purposes simultaneously.

Secondary channels worth experimenting with: Reddit (high intent, be careful not to spam), TikTok (high volume, lower conversion, fast audience growth), and X (slower build, good for network effects).

---

**[SECTION 5: Scaling from First Sale to Ongoing Passive Income — 11:00-13:00]**

The scale path for digital downloads is product depth plus distribution breadth.

Product depth: after your first product succeeds, build the companion products. If your content creator Notion template sells well, build the YouTube video tracker template, the content calendar template, and the email sequence framework. Each new product serves the same buyer at a different stage of their journey.

A library of 5-8 related digital products dramatically increases average order value. Bundle them. Offer the bundle at a 30% discount versus buying individually. Buyers who are already sold on one product are the easiest people to sell a second to.

Distribution breadth: expand the marketing channels over time. If Pinterest is working, double down. If YouTube is driving sales, build more tutorial content. Follow what works before adding new channels.

The passive income reality: digital downloads are not truly passive in the set-it-and-forget-it sense. They require ongoing marketing attention. But the production work is one-time. The leverage ratio between your input time and output revenue improves every month as your content library and email list grow.

After 12 months of consistent building, a digital downloads business with 5-8 products, a 2,000+ email list, and consistent content marketing should generate $1,500-$5,000/month with roughly 10-15 hours per week of active work.

## SCRIPT 3: The 6-Month YouTube Growth Plan

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

Most YouTube growth advice is useless because it's not attached to a timeline. "Be consistent" is not a plan. "Post good content" is not a strategy.

A real growth plan has phases, milestones, and decision points. Here's the one I'd follow if I were starting a YouTube channel today with the goal of reaching 1,000 subscribers in six months.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

Six months is enough time to build a real foundation on YouTube. Not viral fame. A real foundation with consistent traffic, a growing email list, and a channel that the algorithm has learned to recommend.

1,000 subscribers in six months is achievable for most channels with correct strategy and consistent execution. Some channels reach it faster. Some take longer. The plan I'm laying out is based on what I've observed works across content niches, not just one example.

Here's the six-month roadmap.

---

**[SECTION 1: Month 1 — Infrastructure and Baseline — 1:30-4:00]**

Month 1 is not about growth. It's about building the infrastructure correctly so that when growth comes, it compounds.

Week 1: Set up your channel completely. Channel art, profile picture, channel description with clear value proposition, email signup link in bio. Create your first three videos on topics you know deeply and that have some search volume. Publish all three in the first week.

This gives the YouTube algorithm initial data about your content and audience. Three videos in week one also means you're not building an empty channel, which new visitors judge poorly.

Week 2-4: Publish one video per week. Start your email list. Add a CTA to every video description. The goal at the end of month 1 is: 12 videos published, email list started, basic analytics understanding established.

The metric to track in Month 1: average view duration (watch time percentage). Not subscriber count. If your average view duration is below 40%, you have a hook or structure problem to fix before scaling.

---

**[SECTION 2: Month 2 — Content Optimization — 4:00-6:30]**

By month 2 you have enough data to see what's working. Look at your YouTube analytics.

Which video has the highest click-through rate? That thumbnail and title formula is working. Replicate it.

Which video has the highest watch time percentage? That topic and structure is holding attention. Make more like it.

Which video is driving the most subscriber conversions? That topic has the highest viewer intent for your channel specifically.

In month 2, use these data points to consciously optimize. Stop making videos in formats that underperformed in month 1. Double down on what showed promise.

Also in month 2: improve your scripting. Review your month 1 videos. Where do viewers click off on the audience retention graph? That's where your script is losing them. Fix the pattern in month 2.

---

**[SECTION 3: Month 3 — SEO Compound Start — 6:30-8:30]**

Month 3 is when you start building the SEO layer deliberately.

Use TubeBuddy or VidIQ to identify keywords in your niche with moderate search volume and low competition. "How to start a YouTube channel with no equipment" has search volume. "How to start a YouTube channel" is too competitive for a new channel to rank for.

Create two or three search-focused videos per month alongside your regular content. These videos are designed to rank in YouTube search results over the next 6-18 months. They take time. But you plant the seeds in month 3 and harvest them in months 6-12.

Your channel should have 12-15 videos published by the end of month 3. Your email list should have 50-150 subscribers depending on how aggressively you're promoting it.

At the 90-day mark, do a full content audit. Rewrite the descriptions of your best videos to include primary keywords. Add chapters to every video over 5 minutes. Update the CTAs in older video descriptions to point to your email signup.

---

**[SECTION 4: Month 4 — Product and List Building — 8:30-10:30]**

Month 4 is when you launch your first product.

By now you know what your audience needs. Comments, email replies, and your analytics data have told you. Build the minimal version of that product and launch it to your email list first.

Email your list with a simple announcement. Not a long sales pitch. "I built this because [your audience] keeps asking about [problem]. Here's what it covers and where to get it."

Add the product link to every new video description and to the most visited older video descriptions.

From this point forward, every video has a dual purpose: grow the audience and drive product sales. Those goals reinforce each other.

Month 4 subscriber goal: 300-500 subscribers. If you're behind this, the likely causes are hook quality, topic selection, or inconsistency. All three are fixable.

---

**[SECTION 5: Months 5-6 — Compound Growth Phase — 10:30-13:00]**

Months 5 and 6 are when the system starts to compound.

Your SEO videos from month 3 are starting to get consistent search traffic. Your email sequence is converting new subscribers automatically. Your product is making sales.

In these two months, continue publishing weekly. Add one significant collaboration: a cross-promotion with another creator in a complementary niche, a guest appearance, or a video that references a larger creator's work in a way that gets you into their audience.

The subscriber velocity should be noticeably higher in months 5 and 6 than it was in months 1-3. This is not luck. It's the compound effect of consistent quality content, improving SEO, and a growing audience that shares your content.

At month 6, review the full six months. Calculate: total revenue from the product, email list size, average watch time, subscriber count. More importantly, review whether the content direction still feels right. Are you making videos you're proud of? Are you attracting the audience you wanted?

If yes: build the year 2 plan. If no: diagnose what drifted and correct it before the second six months.

---

**[OUTRO — 13:00-14:00]**

Six months. One video per week. Clear milestones at 30, 60, 90, and 180 days. Product launched at month 4. SEO layer started at month 3. Email list built from day one.

That's the plan. It's not complicated. It's just consistent.

The creators who don't reach 1,000 subscribers in six months almost always failed on one of two things: they didn't publish consistently, or they didn't optimize from the data they had.

Both are fixable if you decide to fix them.
