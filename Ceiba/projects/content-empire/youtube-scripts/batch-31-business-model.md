[B-roll: single book on a shelf, empty]

Twenty-five products. Most under $20. All of them serving the same audience. That is the model.

## Script 31-2: "How digital product bundles change the unit economics completely"

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**
[B-roll: single product at $14.99 vs. bundle badge showing $149.99 with $507 crossed out]
A single $14.99 product is a transaction. A $149.99 bundle is a relationship. The economics of those two things are not comparable.

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**
[B-roll: Gumroad product page, bundle listing with value breakdown]
Bundles are misunderstood by most digital product creators. They think bundles are just products grouped together with a discount. That is surface level. The real function of a bundle is to change how a buyer perceives value, eliminate comparison shopping, and increase average order value without increasing traffic.

I will break down how bundles work, how I price them, and why the Complete Bundle at $149.99 generates more revenue per sale than any individual product I sell.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45 | The psychological mechanics of a bundle]**
[B-roll: a product page with individual prices listed, then bundle price beneath]
Timestamp: 0:45

When a buyer looks at 25 individual products totaling $507, and sees a bundle at $149.99, their brain does not calculate a 70% discount. It registers a verdict: this is obviously the right choice.

The bundle removes the decision of which products to pick. Decision fatigue is real. When a buyer has to choose between 25 products, many of them do not choose anything. The bundle collapses the choice to one: buy everything or buy nothing.

This is the core mechanics. The bundle is not a discount. It is a decision tool.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:00 | Bundle pricing math]**
[B-roll: spreadsheet, margin calculations]
Timestamp: 2:00

Profit margin on a $14.99 product after platform fees is roughly $13.50. Sell 100 copies: $1,350.

Profit margin on the $149.99 bundle after fees is roughly $135. Sell 10 copies: $1,350. Same revenue with 10 transactions instead of 100.

Fewer transactions means fewer support requests, fewer refund claims, lower operational load. The bundle creates the same revenue with a fraction of the volume.

Now compound that: if the bundle converts at even 2% of total traffic and the individual product converts at 3%, the bundle wins on revenue per visitor because of the order value difference.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 3:30 | How to build the bundle architecture]**
[B-roll: diagram of 4 bundle tiers on whiteboard or Notion]
Timestamp: 3:30

You need more than one bundle. One bundle for beginners, one for builders, one for operators, one for everything.

My structure: Starter Bundle at $29.99 (3 products). Builder Bundle at $49.99 (5 products). Automation Bundle at $59.99 (4 focused products). Complete Bundle at $149.99 (25 products).

Each tier serves a different buyer. The Starter Bundle captures the person who is not sure yet. The Complete Bundle captures the buyer who is already committed and wants everything now. You need both ends of that spectrum.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 5:00 | What to include in a bundle]**
[B-roll: product selection process, drag and drop into bundle]
Timestamp: 5:00

Bundles should tell a story. The Automation Bundle includes n8n Automation Pack, AI Agent Installer Kit, Social Media Pipeline Guide, and AI News Tracker Guide. Those four products serve one type of person: someone who wants to automate their workflow.

The story is: here are four tools that work together. The buyer does not have to figure out how to combine them. You did that for them.

Do not bundle randomly. Bundle thematically. Buyers should look at the bundle contents and think, this person understood exactly what I need.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 6:30 | Bundles reduce churn and increase trust]**
[B-roll: email inbox, customer reply, 5-star review text]
Timestamp: 6:30

When a buyer purchases the Complete Bundle, they are not treating this like a $5 impulse buy. They made a considered decision. That buyer reads the products. They get results. They leave reviews. They come back for future products.

Bundle buyers have a 3x higher lifetime value in my data than individual product buyers. The bundle price filters for committed buyers.

This is counter-intuitive. You would expect the higher price to reduce trust. It increases it. The buyer who spends $149.99 is more invested in getting value than the buyer who spent $4.99.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 8:00 | How to introduce bundles without confusing your store]**
[B-roll: store page with clear bundle section, call-to-action buttons]
Timestamp: 8:00

The biggest mistake with bundles is burying them. Most creators list the individual products prominently and put the bundle at the bottom as an afterthought.

Flip that. Lead with the bundle. The individual products are the fallback for buyers who cannot commit to the full price yet.

In my store, the Complete Bundle is the first thing visible. The CTA on every individual product page points back to the bundle. Every piece of content mentions the bundle exists.

The bundle is the main offer. Individual products are the entry points.

---

**[CONCLUSION — 9:30]**
[B-roll: revenue dashboard, bundle conversion stats]
Individual products build the catalog. Bundles capture the value the catalog creates. You need both, but you have to run them in the right order.

Build the catalog first so the bundle has enough content to feel substantial. Then introduce the bundle as the primary offer. Let the catalog be the proof and the bundle be the close.

## Script 31-3: "Fear funnel explained: the 5 stages before someone buys"

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**
[B-roll: customer journey diagram, five stages labeled]
Nobody buys a product the moment they see it. There are five mental stages between first impression and purchase. Most creators market to stage five. Nobody is there yet.

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**
[B-roll: sales funnel graphic, then close-up on top stage labeled "AWARENESS"]
The fear funnel is a model for understanding the psychological distance between a buyer and a purchase. It is not about manipulation. It is about meeting people where they actually are instead of where you assume they are.

I built the fear funnel into my entire content strategy. Every piece of content I publish serves one of five stages. This is how it works.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45 | Stage 1 -- Unaware]**
[B-roll: person scrolling mindlessly, no intent signal]
Timestamp: 0:45

The unaware buyer does not know they have a problem. They have not searched for a solution. They are not looking at your product.

To reach this person, your content cannot talk about your product. It has to talk about a reality they already live in. A video called "why most side projects fail before they start" reaches this person. A video called "my $14.99 Niche Sniper guide" does not.

Stage one content is problem-awareness content. You are naming the reality before you name the solution.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:00 | Stage 2 -- Problem-Aware]**
[B-roll: person searching on Google, looking frustrated]
Timestamp: 2:00

The problem-aware buyer knows they have an issue but does not know what kind of solution exists. They are searching "how to find products to sell online" but they do not know that a product research system is the answer.

Content for stage two names the problem explicitly and begins to frame the category of solution without pitching anything specific. A video on "the three reasons your product research is failing" is stage two content.

You are bridging from problem to solution category. Not product, category.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 3:30 | Stage 3 -- Solution-Aware]**
[B-roll: comparison chart, person reading reviews]
Timestamp: 3:30

Stage three is where most creators start their marketing. The buyer knows solutions exist and is evaluating them. They are comparing your product to alternatives.

Here, you can name your product directly. But the framing is still about them, not you. Not "my Niche Sniper guide is great" but "here is how a systematic product research process changes your hit rate."

Stage three content is comparison and differentiation content. You are answering the question: why this over everything else.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 5:00 | Stage 4 -- Product-Aware]**
[B-roll: product page open in browser, person reading description carefully]
Timestamp: 5:00

Stage four is when the buyer knows your product specifically and is doing final due diligence. They have visited your page. They know the price. They are looking for confirmation that the purchase is correct.

Content for stage four is social proof, specifics, and results. A walkthrough of the product. A case study of someone who used it. A response to the most common objection.

The fear in stage four is: will this actually work for me. Your job is to answer that directly.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 6:30 | Stage 5 -- Most Aware]**
[B-roll: checkout page, cursor hovering over buy button]
Timestamp: 6:30

Stage five is the buyer who is ready to purchase and is looking for a reason to do it now. They need a trigger, not more information.

Triggers at stage five: a time-limited discount, a bonus added to the purchase, a simple one-line reminder that the problem they have is not going away.

Do not confuse stage five with stage one. Most email broadcasts and Instagram posts are written for stage five buyers. But most of your audience is at stage one or two. That mismatch is why conversion rates are low.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 8:00 | How to build content across all five stages simultaneously]**
[B-roll: content calendar, five categories labeled]
Timestamp: 8:00

The practical implementation: map every piece of content to a stage before you create it. Your YouTube videos should mostly be stages one and two because that is where cold audiences live. Your email list should be stages three and four. Your product pages are stage five.

For my content calendar: 50% is stage one and two content for organic reach. 30% is stage three and four content for buyers already in the ecosystem. 20% is direct product content and CTAs.

Balance those proportions and you stop talking to a room that only contains people who already agree with you.

---

**[CONCLUSION — 9:30]**
[B-roll: funnel diagram with all five stages completed]
The fear funnel is not a tactic. It is a map. Once you know which stage each piece of content serves, you can stop guessing why some posts convert and others get ignored.

Most ignored posts are great stage five content delivered to a stage one audience. Nothing wrong with the content. Wrong timing, wrong stage.

## Script 31-4: "The value ladder from free to $500: how to structure your product line"

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**
[B-roll: staircase shot from below, each step labeled with a price point]
A random product line is a collection of things you felt like making. A value ladder is a system that converts strangers into long-term buyers one step at a time. Those are not the same thing.

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**
[B-roll: product grid organized by price tier, left to right]
The value ladder is one of the oldest frameworks in direct response marketing. DotCom Secrets codified it. Every successful digital product business uses some version of it. Most creators think they are doing it but are actually just selling things at different prices.

A real value ladder has a specific logic: each tier prepares the buyer for the next tier. Every rung creates the belief necessary to climb to the next one. This is what mine looks like.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45 | The free tier: lead magnets that do real work]**
[B-roll: download button, email opt-in form]
Timestamp: 0:45

The first rung is free and it has to be genuinely useful. A bad lead magnet is a PDF that promises 10 tips and delivers generic advice. A good lead magnet solves one specific problem completely.

My free products: 5 AI Tools for Builders, AI Safety Checklist, Business Automation Audit. Each one solves a narrow problem completely. No fluff. No "for more, buy X."

When someone downloads a free product and it actually helps them, they trust you. That trust is the only thing that makes the next rung work.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:00 | The entry tier: $4.99-$9.99]**
[B-roll: hand reaching for wallet, small product display]
Timestamp: 2:00

The entry tier removes the last remaining friction for a new buyer. The price is low enough that it is not a real financial decision. The buyer is deciding whether they trust you enough to transact at all.

At this tier, the product must over-deliver relative to the price. The AI News Tracker Guide at $4.99 gives someone a complete automated news pipeline. The buyer expects maybe a useful tip. They get a full system. That gap between expectation and delivery is what converts them to the next tier.

Do not use this tier to sell stripped-down versions of higher-priced products. Make it complete for a narrow use case.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 3:30 | The core tier: $14.99-$29.99]**
[B-roll: product stack, multiple guides displayed]
Timestamp: 3:30

This is where most of the revenue lives. Buyers have already purchased an entry product and trust you. The price is still accessible but the scope is larger.

At this tier, the product is solving a problem the buyer has already identified as important. Niche Sniper at $14.99 solves product research. Solopreneur OS at $12.99 solves business organization. The buyer already knows they have these problems. You are not convincing them the problem exists.

Conversion rates at this tier are higher than entry tier for repeat buyers. First-time buyers still need the entry tier first.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 5:00 | The premium tier: $49.99-$99]**
[B-roll: premium product display, Ghost Writer Kit packaging]
Timestamp: 5:00

Premium products require a different kind of trust. The buyer is not just trusting that you can explain a concept. They are trusting that your system, tool, or framework can change something meaningful for their business.

Ghost Writer Kit at $99 is a voice cloning and content generation system. Ceiba Sanctuary at $49.99 is a browser wellness application. These are not guides. They are tools.

At this tier, you are selling outcomes, not information. The buyer is paying for a system that does something, not for knowledge about how to do it themselves.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 6:30 | The bundle tier]**
[B-roll: Complete Bundle page, $507 value indicator]
Timestamp: 6:30

Bundles sit outside the standard ladder because they serve multiple buyer stages simultaneously. The Complete Bundle at $149.99 captures the buyer who wants everything and does not want to make decisions.

The bundle tier also creates an anchor. When a buyer sees 25 products valued at $507 available for $149.99, every individual product looks like a better deal by comparison. The bundle sets the reference price for the entire catalog.

This is not manipulation. It is context. The buyer now understands the scope of what you have built.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 8:00 | The service tier: $500+]**
[B-roll: calendar with one-on-one call slot, consultation page]
Timestamp: 8:00

The top of the ladder is not a product. It is a service. This is where the economics shift entirely. One client at $500 equals 100 sales of a $5 product.

For Behike, the service tier is custom automation builds and AI consulting for solo businesses and small teams. The buyer at this tier has already been through the product ladder. They have done the DIY work. Now they want someone to do it for them.

The service tier also validates the product line. If clients are hiring you for custom work based on your guides and tools, your products are working. The service tier is the proof.

---

**[CONCLUSION — 9:30]**
[B-roll: ladder visualization, each tier labeled and connected]
The value ladder is not a design exercise. It is a conversion architecture. Each tier has a job: create trust, deliver value, prepare the buyer for the next level.

Most creators stop at the free or entry tier and wonder why nobody buys. The ladder works because each step creates the belief necessary for the next step. Skip the rungs and the buyer cannot climb.

## Script 31-5: "Solo business vs. agency: the math that made me choose one person"

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**
[B-roll: single desk setup vs. open office with multiple people]
An agency scales by hiring. A solo business scales by systems. Those two paths require completely different skills, different capitals, and different tolerances for stress. I chose one. Here is the math behind it.

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**
[B-roll: Kalani's desk, single laptop, quiet workspace]
I am a CS student building a digital product business alone. I have thought seriously about building an agency. I know people running them. I understand the model. I chose not to.

This is not a video about which model is better in general. It is about which model fits a specific profile. By the end, you will know which profile you have and which path the math supports.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45 | The agency model: what it actually requires]**
[B-roll: org chart, team messaging app, project management board]
Timestamp: 0:45

An agency's primary asset is people. Clients pay for time, expertise, and execution delivered by a team. Revenue scales with headcount, but so does complexity.

To run an agency profitably, you need client acquisition, account management, quality control, team coordination, and HR. None of those are trivial. Most agency owners spend more time managing people and processes than doing the work they are good at.

The agency model works if you are good at sales, delegation, and systems management simultaneously. Most people are good at one of those three.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:00 | The solo model: what it actually requires]**
[B-roll: single person coding, writing, recording, building]
Timestamp: 2:00

A solo business scales through leverage. The leverage comes from products, content, and automation. You build something once and it generates value repeatedly.

The solo model requires a different set of skills: the ability to create valuable things, distribute them through content, and systematize the operations behind them. No people management. No client account juggling.

The constraint is your personal capacity. You cannot work more hours to grow faster. You have to build systems that work without you working.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 3:30 | Revenue ceiling comparison]**
[B-roll: income chart, ceiling line on agency vs. solo]
Timestamp: 3:30

Agency revenue ceiling: theoretically unlimited. A large agency can do $10M annually. But the complexity scales with revenue. A $10M agency is a real company with real company problems.

Solo revenue ceiling: $50K-$500K annually for most operators, with outliers going higher. Dan Koe runs a multi-million dollar solo business. But those are outliers built over years.

For someone starting now, the relevant question is not the ceiling. It is the floor. A solo digital product business can generate $2K-$5K monthly within one year with consistent execution. An agency cannot make that promise at startup phase.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 5:00 | Capital requirements]**
[B-roll: startup cost breakdown, spreadsheet comparison]
Timestamp: 5:00

Agency startup costs: domain and website (minimal), but also contract labor or salaries, project management tools, CRM software, legal documentation for client contracts. Realistically $2K-$10K to operate professionally from day one.

Solo startup costs: domain, hosting, Gumroad or Shopify fee (free or $30/month), content creation tools. Total startup budget under $200.

I launched my entire product catalog for under $100. The capital efficiency difference is significant when you are a student in Puerto Rico who does not have a venture budget.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 6:30 | Stress profile comparison]**
[B-roll: empty inbox vs. busy Slack channel, contrast]
Timestamp: 6:30

Agency stress: client expectations, team performance, cash flow tied to invoice timing, and the constant pressure of payroll. If you have three employees and your biggest client leaves, you have a crisis.

Solo stress: distribution pressure (you have to post consistently), product quality (your reputation is directly attached to your work), and the psychological isolation of building alone.

Both models are stressful. The stresses are completely different. Pick the stress that matches your tolerance, not the one that sounds less bad in the abstract.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 8:00 | When to choose each]**
[B-roll: decision tree diagram, two paths labeled]
Timestamp: 8:00

Choose the agency model if: you have existing client relationships you can convert, you are good at selling and delegating, you can front the capital, and you want to build something you can eventually sell or step back from.

Choose the solo model if: you are starting with limited capital, you want the highest possible profit margins, you prefer building products over managing people, and you are willing to invest in content distribution for years.

I chose solo because the capital fit, the skill fit, and the stress profile fit. Not because it is superior in some abstract sense.

---

**[CONCLUSION — 9:30]**
[B-roll: solo workspace, clean, focused]
The solo model is not for everyone. It rewards a specific type of builder: someone who can create value through content and products, distribute consistently, and operate without the validation that comes from a team.

But if that profile is yours, the math is compelling. Low startup cost, high margins, no payroll, total ownership of the output.

That is why I chose one person. The math supported it.
