---

[HOOK]

You don't need a million followers. You don't need to be on TikTok. You don't need to spend $5,000 on assets.

You need to know how Roblox monetization actually works. Most guides skip this part entirely.

[INTRO CARD: "Roblox. Build. Monetize. No fame required."]

---

[THE REALITY CHECK . 60 seconds]

Most Roblox advice is about going viral. Make a great game, it blows up, you make millions. That's survivorship bias.

The builders actually making consistent money on Roblox are mostly invisible. Small games with loyal playerbases. Games with tight gameplay loops that keep people coming back. Games with smart Developer Product designs.

You don't need 100,000 concurrent players. You need 500 players spending 100 Robux each month. That's 50,000 Robux. At $0.0035 per Robux on the Developer Exchange, that's $175 a month. Not life-changing. But real.

Scale that to 5,000 players and you're at $1,750 a month. For a game you built once.

---

[THE THREE MONETIZATION PATHS . 2 minutes]

[SCREEN: Roblox Studio]

Path 1: Developer Products.

These are one-time purchases inside your game. A potion that heals your character. A speed boost. A cosmetic item. Players can buy them multiple times. No subscription needed.

[DEMO: show creating a Developer Product]

Price these low. 25-50 Robux. You want the impulse buy. The player who's stuck on a level and thinks "25 Robux is worth it to keep going."

Path 2: Game Passes.

Single purchase, permanent benefit. VIP status. Double XP. Special areas. Players buy these once and keep the benefit forever.

[DEMO: show creating a Game Pass]

Price these at 100-500 Robux depending on the value. The permanent perk justifies a higher price point.

Path 3: Premium Payouts.

You don't have to charge anything. Roblox automatically pays developers when Premium members play their games. Set this up correctly and you earn money on every play session from Premium users.

No purchase required from the player. Roblox pays you directly.

---

[THE KEY MECHANIC . 90 seconds]

The mechanic that makes all three work: retention.

If a player leaves after 5 minutes, they don't spend Robux. If they come back for 200 hours, they do.

Retention is about game feel. Feedback loops. Progress systems that always show the player there's something more to reach.

This is what Chapter 4 of the Roblox Builder's Guide covers: the retention mechanics that top-grossing games actually use. Checkpoints. Leaderboards. Daily challenges. Progression systems.

[SCREEN: diagram of retention loop]

The loop looks like this: Player achieves something small. Game rewards them. Player is shown the next thing to achieve. Player keeps playing.

That loop is not complicated. But it has to be designed intentionally. Most beginner games skip it and wonder why no one comes back.

## SCRIPT 2: "I Made a Roblox Game That Actually Keeps Players Coming Back"
**Product:** Roblox Builder's Guide (close look on retention mechanics)
**Format:** Screen recording
**Length:** 7-9 minutes
**Hook type:** Tutorial, framework reveal

---

[HOOK]

Most Roblox games die in the first week. Players try it once and never return.

Here's the mechanic that fixes that. And it's simpler than you think.

---

[THE CORE PROBLEM]

Roblox has millions of games. Most get zero players. The ones with consistent players have one thing in common: they always give the player something to come back for.

This is not an accident. It's designed.

---

[THE FOUR RETENTION MECHANICS . 4 minutes]

[SCREEN: Roblox Studio]

**Mechanic 1: Daily Rewards**

Each time a player logs in, give them something small. A coin. A badge. A cosmetic item.

The psychological principle is called a variable reward schedule. The player doesn't know exactly what they'll get. That unpredictability is more motivating than a predictable reward.

[DEMO: show the DataStore code for tracking login streaks]

This takes about 50 lines of Lua. I'll show you the structure.

**Mechanic 2: Visible Progress**

The player should always know two things: what they achieved, and what comes next.

Leaderboards handle the "what you achieved" part. They also create social competition. A player who sees their name at #47 and knows someone at #46 will keep playing to move up.

[DEMO: show leaderboard setup in Studio]

The "what comes next" part is a progress bar. Small. Always in the corner. Always showing the next milestone. This is how mobile games make you play for 6 hours without noticing.

**Mechanic 3: Checkpoint Systems**

Nothing kills retention faster than losing progress. If a player dies and has to restart a 10-minute section, most of them close the game.

[DEMO: show checkpoint code in Lua]

Checkpoints are simple: save the player's position at key moments. When they respawn, put them at the last checkpoint, not the beginning. Five minutes of code. Huge difference in retention.

**Mechanic 4: Collectibles and Discovery**

Put things in your world that reward exploration. Hidden areas. Easter eggs. Items that aren't mentioned in the game description.

Players who discover something unexpected tell other players. That organic word-of-mouth is the cheapest marketing you'll ever get.

## SCRIPT 4: "Build Your First Roblox Game in One Weekend (Actually)"
**Product:** Roblox Builder's Guide
**Format:** Tutorial / challenge
**Length:** 7-8 minutes
**Hook type:** Weekend project challenge

---

[HOOK]

One weekend. Zero experience required. A published Roblox game.

I'm going to show you the path. Not the theory. The actual steps.

[INTRO CARD: "One Weekend. One Game. Here's how."]

---

[THE SETUP . 60 seconds]

Download Roblox Studio. It's free. Mac and Windows.

Open it. Select "Baseplate" as your starting template. This gives you a flat surface to build on.

You're already in Studio. That's the hardest part for most people: actually opening it.

---

[SATURDAY MORNING: The Idea . 30 minutes]

Pick something simple. I mean genuinely simple.

Obby (obstacle course): A series of platforms the player has to cross. Players fall, they respawn, they try again. Every Roblox player understands this format instantly.

Pick a theme. Space. Underwater. Desert. The theme is just the visual style. The gameplay is the same.

Write down 10 obstacles you want in the game. Be specific: "a spinning beam," "a moving platform that goes left and right," "a jump pad that sends you flying."

That's your weekend scope. 10 obstacles. One theme. Don't add more.

---

[SATURDAY AFTERNOON: Building . 3 hours]

[SCREEN: Roblox Studio]

Place basic parts. Resize them. Connect them to form your obstacles.

The Studio toolbar: Parts tab for basic shapes. Model tab for grouping. Constraints for physics.

[DEMO: build 3 obstacles live]

Obstacle 1: A beam that rotates. Create a part. Go to Properties. Set Anchored to True. Write 5 lines of Lua that rotate it.

Obstacle 2: A platform that moves. Same concept. Lua script, TweenService to move it back and forth.

Obstacle 3: A kill floor. Any part can be a kill floor. Create a Script inside the part. When the player touches it, teleport them back to the start.

---

[SATURDAY EVENING: Scripting . 2 hours]

Lua is the scripting language for Roblox. It reads like English.

You need to know three things to make a complete game:
1. How to detect when a player touches something (events)
2. How to move things (TweenService)
3. How to store player progress (DataStores)

[DEMO: show a basic script with these three elements]

Chapter 2 of the Roblox Builder's Guide covers Lua from variables up. If you want the full language foundation, it's there.

---

[SUNDAY: Polish and Publish . 2 hours]

Lighting: go to Lighting in the Explorer. Change the ambient color to match your theme. This takes 5 minutes and makes the game look 10x better.

UI: add a "Game Complete" GUI that appears when the player finishes. Players need feedback that they won.

Testing: press Play in Studio. Play through the whole game. Fix anything that breaks.

Publishing: File > Publish to Roblox As. Give it a name and description. Add a thumbnail. Set it to public.

Your game is live.

---

[CLOSE]

That's the weekend build. Not a polished masterpiece. A real, published, playable game.

Start there. Build the second one faster. Build the third one with real retention mechanics.

## SCRIPT 5: "3D Clothing for VTubers: Start to Final Asset"
**Product:** 3D Fashion Design Guide
**Format:** Screen recording tutorial
**Length:** 8-10 minutes
**Hook type:** Tutorial, niche expertise

---

[HOOK]

VTubers are spending thousands of dollars on custom outfits. The people making those outfits are not fashion designers. They're 3D artists who learned a specific pipeline.

Here's how the pipeline works.

[INTRO CARD: "VTuber clothing. Marvelous Designer to Unreal. Full pipeline."]

---

[THE MARKET CONTEXT . 60 seconds]

VTubing is a $100 million+ market. Every VTuber needs content, and a meaningful part of that content is outfit variety.

The people who design those outfits work as freelance 3D artists. An original VTuber outfit costs $500-3,000 depending on complexity.

The tools to build them are available to anyone. The pipeline is learnable. Most people just don't know where to start.

---

[THE FOUR TOOLS . 4 minutes]

[SCREEN: Marvelous Designer]

Tool 1: Marvelous Designer

This is the cloth simulation software. You design garment panels, assign fabric properties (weight, stretch, stiffness), and simulate how the cloth drapes on a mannequin.

It handles the physical behavior of fabric better than any other tool. Drape, wrinkle, how a collar sits. Marvelous makes these realistic without manual sculpting.

[DEMO: create a simple top in Marvelous Designer]

Tool 2: ZBrush

After simulation, you bring the mesh into ZBrush to add fine details. Stitching. Surface texture. Small decorative elements. The seams and folds that make fabric look real up close.

[DEMO: show ZBrush detailing briefly]

Tool 3: Retopology

Game-ready assets need clean geometry. The simulated mesh from Marvelous is high-poly. You manually retopologize it to create a lower-poly version that animates correctly.

Most VTuber rigs are rigged to specific bone structures. The mesh needs edge flow that accommodates those deformations.

Tool 4: Texturing in Substance Painter

Diffuse map: the base color.
Normal map: the surface detail without the polygons.
Roughness map: how much the material reflects light.

VTuber outfits often use stylized textures rather than photorealistic ones to match the art style of the character design.

---

[THE EXPORT]

Export as FBX for most rigs. The exact settings depend on the VTuber's rigging software: VRoid, VTube Studio, or custom Unity/Unreal setups.

Chapter 5 of the 3D Fashion Design Guide covers Unity and Unreal export specifically. Including LOD setup, which determines how the outfit looks at different distances.
