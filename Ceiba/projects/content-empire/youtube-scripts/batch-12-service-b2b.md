"AI Agent for a Local Business. The Real Walkthrough."

---

[SECTION 1: THE PROBLEM]

Before I show you the build, you need to understand the actual problem.

The salon owner had 47 unread DMs when we first talked. Not because she was ignoring them. Because she had three stylists to manage, a walk-in floor to run, and clients in the chair. Responding to messages was something she did in the gaps, and the gaps were getting smaller.

The cost of those delayed responses was real. She knew it.

A booking inquiry that sits unanswered for four hours often goes to the next salon on the list. Not because the client is impatient. Because they need to know if they can get an appointment before they make other plans. Speed is the whole product in service business scheduling.

She was losing bookings she never knew she lost.

That is the problem I was hired to solve.

---

[SECTION 2: THE AUDIT]

The first thing I do before building anything is a 30-minute audit.

I am not selling during this call. I am diagnosing. I ask: what are the ten most common messages you receive? What is your current booking process? What happens when you are unavailable?

For this salon, the answers were consistent. Eighty percent of DMs fell into four categories.

"What are your hours?" "Do you have availability this week?" "How much is a balayage?" "Can I reschedule my appointment?"

Those four questions were consuming most of her two hours per day.

None of them required a human to answer. The information existed. It just was not connected to anything that could respond automatically.

---

[SECTION 3: THE BUILD]

Here is exactly what I built.

The core is a chatbot connected to her Instagram DMs. When a message comes in, the bot classifies it. Is this a booking question? A pricing question? A scheduling change? A general inquiry?

For booking questions, the bot responds with her current availability window and a link to her booking system.

For pricing questions, it pulls from a pricing FAQ I structured with her during the audit. Current prices. What each service includes. How long each service takes.

For scheduling changes, it captures the request and sends a notification to her phone. A human still handles those. But now she is notified immediately instead of discovering the message two hours later.

For general inquiries it cannot classify, it tells the client a response is coming within two hours and flags the message for human follow-up.

The whole system took five days to build and test.

---

[SECTION 4: THE NUMBERS]

Here is the before and after.

Before: two to three hours per day on DMs. Average response time: 2.4 hours. Estimated missed bookings per week: the owner believed it was three to five, but she had no data.

After: 20 minutes per day on DMs. Those 20 minutes are only the messages the bot flagged for human review. Average response time for automated messages: under 60 seconds. In the first month she tracked four bookings that explicitly came through the automated flow during hours she would have been unavailable to respond.

That is not a complete ROI picture. But four additional bookings at an average service value of $120 is $480 in the first month. The setup cost $997.

The math pays off in the second month. Every month after that is profit on the automation.

---

[SECTION 5: WHAT ACTUALLY MATTERED]

The technical build was not the hard part.

The hard part was the audit. Getting the actual list of common questions. Building the pricing FAQ with her so it was accurate. Setting the right tone for the bot responses so they sounded like her business and not like a corporate help desk.

The build is repeatable. The audit is the work that makes the build matter.

There are 33 million small businesses in the United States. The majority of them were built around the owner doing everything manually. Booking, customer communication, follow-up, data entry. All of it by hand because there was no other option five years ago.

The option now exists. But most of these businesses have no idea. They are not following AI news. They are not on Twitter. They are running a salon or a plumbing company or a real estate office. AI is something they have heard about but have not connected to their specific daily problems.

The signal I look for is bad process plus no tech person.

No tech person looks like this: their website was last updated in 2019. Their social media is inconsistent. They are using a Gmail address for their business contact, not a domain email.

I find businesses on Google Maps. I search the niche plus the city. I filter by review count, looking for businesses with enough reviews to indicate real volume but with reviews mentioning the problems I solve.

I build a list of 20 to 30 businesses per batch. Each one gets researched for two minutes. Does it have the signals? If yes, it goes on the list.

[SECTION 4: THE APPROACH]

The first contact is not a pitch.

The single biggest mistake I see from people trying to sell services is leading with what they offer. The business owner does not care what you offer until they believe you understand their problem.

My first message to a business is about a specific thing I noticed. Not "I offer AI automation." Specifically: "I noticed your Google reviews mention slow response times on messages. I build systems that handle that automatically. Thirty minutes to show you what that looks like for your type of business."

I am showing them I did research. I am naming their specific problem. I am asking for 30 minutes, not a sale.

The response rate on this approach is significantly higher than generic outreach. I will cover the exact numbers in another video.

[SECTION 5: THE PIPELINE]

Once you have a list of 20 businesses and a first message, the pipeline is simple.

Send the message. Wait three days. Follow up once if no response. If still no response, move on. Do not chase.

Of every 20 businesses I contact with targeted outreach, I expect two to four to respond and book an audit call. Of those audit calls, one to two become paying clients.

One paying client at $997 per project covers 20 outreach attempts. The second client is profit.

Not a course about cold outreach. Not a hypothetical. I actually sent them. I want to show you exactly what I sent, what the responses looked like, and the one thing that changed the response rate from near zero to something worth talking about.

---

[INTRO CARD]

"20 Cold DMs. What Actually Happened."

---

[SECTION 1: THE FIRST BATCH]

The first batch was bad.

I sent eight messages in the first week. The message was something like: "Hi, I build AI automation for small businesses. It can save you time on customer communication and scheduling. Would you be open to a quick call?"

Zero responses.

I know why now. That message is about me, not them. It describes a capability, not a problem. There is no reason for them to respond because I have not given them a reason to believe I understand anything specific about their business.

---

[SECTION 2: THE SHIFT]

The shift happened when I changed the structure of the message.

Instead of starting with what I offer, I started with something specific I noticed about their business.

The new structure: one sentence about what I observed. One sentence naming the problem it suggests. One sentence describing the result I can deliver. One question asking for 30 minutes.

Example: "I looked at your Google reviews and a few of them mention slow response times on messages. I build chatbots that respond automatically within 60 seconds, 24 hours a day. Would 30 minutes to show you what that looks like for a salon make sense this week?"

That message is 52 words. It is specific. It is about them. It asks for very little.

---

[SECTION 3: THE RESULTS]

I sent 12 messages with the new structure.

Four responded. Three booked audit calls. One became a paying client.

That is a 33% response rate on the second batch versus 0% on the first batch.

The only variable that changed was the message structure. Same niche, same city, same price point, same offer.

Specificity is the mechanism. When a business owner reads a message and thinks "they actually looked at my business," the response rate changes.

---

[SECTION 4: WHAT DIDN'T WORK]

I want to be honest about what did not work.

Following up more than once made things worse, not better. A second message got two responses, both negative. I do not follow up more than once now.

Sending to businesses with no public pain signals was wasted effort. If their reviews are fine and their response times look good, they are not feeling the problem. They are the wrong prospect right now.

Generic platforms like Upwork and Fiverr attracted the wrong inquiries. Clients looking for the cheapest possible option, not clients with a real problem they want solved. I stopped spending time there.

---

[SECTION 5: THE CURRENT PROCESS]

Here is the current outreach process.

Monday: research 20 businesses in the target niche. Identify the specific pain signal for each one.

Tuesday: write and send 20 messages, each personalized to the specific signal.

Friday: one follow-up to non-responders who opened the message.

Week two: start a new batch. Move on from non-responders.

I run this on about two hours per week. It is not a full-time sales process. It is a consistent pipeline that produces one to two audit calls per week.

The proposal answers every question a business owner has before they say yes: what are you building, when will it be done, how much does it cost, and what happens if something breaks.

Once the proposal is signed and the deposit is paid, the build starts.

The goal of this call is that the client finishes it feeling like they own the system, not like they are dependent on me to keep it running.

The first 30 days after delivery are the support window.

In this period I fix bugs at no additional cost. I make reasonable adjustments based on how the system performs in the real environment. I answer questions quickly.
