## USE CASE 1: CLEAN UP MY INBOX (0:30 - 3:30) [SHORT 2 + SHORT 3]

*[Cut to screen recording. Gmail open. 347 unread emails visible.]*

Alright, first one. My inbox is a disaster. I've been ignoring it for two weeks. Look at this. 347 unread.

Here's what I told it:

> "Open Gmail. Sort my unread emails by importance. Draft replies to the top 3. Archive everything from newsletters I haven't opened in 30 days."

That's it. That's the whole prompt. Plain English.

*[Screen recording: Claude Cowork opens Gmail, starts scrolling through emails, categorizing.]*

Watch. It's reading my emails. Not summarizing them in a chatbot window somewhere. Actually reading them. In Gmail. On my screen. I can see everything it's doing.

It found the 3 most important ones. A professor asking about my project deadline. A client follow-up. And a shipping confirmation I actually needed to act on.

*[Screen shows draft replies appearing.]*

Now look at these drafts. It's not writing corporate nonsense. It pulled context from the email thread and wrote replies that sound like me. The professor one even referenced the specific assignment. I just had to read them, tweak one line, and hit send.

Here's the honest part. When it got to the newsletter archiving, it stopped. It said: "I found 47 emails matching this criteria. Want me to archive all of them?"

Good. I want that control. This isn't autopilot. It's a collaborator that asks before it does something permanent.

I said yes. 47 emails gone. Inbox went from 347 to under 30 in about two minutes.

*[Before/after split: messy inbox vs clean inbox.]*

Two minutes. No coffee even got cold.

## USE CASE 2: RESEARCH AND MAKE A DOC (3:30 - 7:00) [SHORT 4]

*[Cut back to Kalani on camera.]*

Second task. This one used to eat my whole morning.

I needed to compare AI tools for content creation. Not for me. For a client. They wanted a breakdown with pricing, features, the whole thing. Formatted. Professional.

Here's what I said:

> "Research the top 5 AI tools for content creation in 2026. Open each website, compare pricing and features, then create a Google Doc with a comparison table."

*[Screen recording: Browser opens. Multiple tabs appear.]*

Watch the tabs. It's opening each tool's website. Reading the pricing pages. Not pulling from some cached training data. Actually going to the websites. Right now. Live pricing.

*[Screen shows it navigating pricing pages, scrolling through feature lists.]*

It checked Runway, Kling, Veo 3, CapCut, and Sora. Went to each pricing page. Read the feature breakdowns. Compared the free tiers vs paid.

*[Screen switches to Google Docs. A formatted document appears.]*

Then it opened Google Docs and started building. Headers. A comparison table with columns for each tool. Pricing tiers. Key features. Limitations. And at the bottom, a recommendation section.

The table alone would have taken me 30 minutes to format. The research, probably an hour and a half on top of that.

Total time: just under 3 minutes.

*[Kalani on camera.]*

I'm not exaggerating. I timed it. Two hours of work compressed into 3 minutes. And the doc was cleaner than what I would have made manually because it was consistent. Same format for every tool. No sections where I got lazy and wrote less.

That's the thing people miss. It's not just faster. It's more consistent. Because it doesn't get tired on tool number 4 and start cutting corners.

## USE CASE 3: FILL OUT FORMS (7:00 - 9:30) [SHORT 5]

*[Kalani on camera. Slightly more serious tone.]*

Third one. And this is the one where I need to be real with you about something.

I had a scholarship application open. Long form. Name, address, GPA, extracurriculars, essays, references. The kind of thing that takes 45 minutes and drains your soul.

Here's what I said:

> "Open this scholarship application. Use the info from my resume to fill in every field. Don't submit yet, let me review."

That last part matters. I'll come back to it.

*[Screen recording: Application form visible. Fields start filling in.]*

It opened my resume first. Read through it. Then went to the application and started filling fields. Name. Address. University. GPA. It handled the dropdowns. Found "University of Puerto Rico" in the list. Selected the right campus. Got the graduation year right.

*[Screen shows it navigating to the essay section.]*

For the short-answer questions, it used bullet points from my resume and expanded them into actual paragraphs. Not generic filler. It pulled from my specific projects and experiences.

The extracurriculars section? It formatted them exactly how the form wanted. Organization name, role, dates, description. All from my resume.

Ninety seconds. The whole form. Done.

*[Kalani on camera.]*

But here's what I want you to hear. I said "don't submit." And it didn't. It filled everything in and then said: "Ready for your review. I haven't submitted anything."

Always do this. Always say "don't submit." Always say "let me review." This is a collaborator, not autopilot. You are the pilot. It's the co-pilot.

I reviewed every field. Changed one date. Tweaked a sentence in the essay. Then I hit submit myself.

That's the right way to use this. You save 45 minutes of typing. But you keep 100% of the control.
