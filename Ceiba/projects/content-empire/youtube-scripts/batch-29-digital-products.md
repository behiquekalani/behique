Twenty-five digital products. Six weeks. One person with a laptop and a course load.

I'm a computer engineering student in Puerto Rico. I don't have a team. I don't have a large budget. I have focused blocks of time and a build system that I iterate on constantly.

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45-2:30]**
**[00:45] The system starts with a product map, not an idea**

[B-roll: product map document, categories and price points visible]

Most people start with one product idea and try to flesh it out. That approach produces one product after a lot of effort.

I started with a map. I listed every category of problem my audience has, then filled in product slots at different price points. Free lead magnet. $4.99 entry point. $9.99-$14.99 core. $29.99-$99 premium.

**[02:30] Format selection: the fastest formats first**

[B-roll: product format list: guides, templates, cheat sheets, web apps]

Not all product formats take the same time to build.

A cheat sheet takes two hours. A PDF guide takes six. A template takes four. A web app takes eight to twelve.

I started with cheat sheets and templates. They built fast, gave me real products on Gumroad, and created momentum.

The heavier formats, the web apps and course-style guides, came later when the system was running smoothly.

[B-roll: order of builds on a timeline, lighter products first]

Sequence matters. Don't start with your hardest product.

I work section by section. The result feels built, not generated.

[B-roll: editing a section in real time, specific word changes]

The edit pass is mine. That's where the product becomes Behike's, not just an AI document.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 6:00-7:45]**
**[06:00] Parallel tracks: building while publishing**

[B-roll: two tasks running side by side, one being built, one being published]

By week two I had a rhythm: build in the morning, publish in the afternoon.

Background agents handled overnight tasks: generating cover copy, formatting listings, creating landing page sections. I reviewed in the morning and approved what was usable.

[B-roll: morning review session, quick file scan, approvals]

[B-roll: product review checklist, five criteria]

Listing copy. Getting the hook right took iteration. I built a swipe file of my own listings that worked and used those as models.

Category clarity. Some products took too long to scope because I hadn't decided the format early enough.

My $4.99 product and my $99 product both sell. They sell to completely different people for completely different reasons.

---

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**

[B-roll: Gumroad dashboard, price column visible across multiple products]

I have products priced from $4.99 to $149.99. Each price point is intentional. Each one attracts a different buyer with different expectations.

**[00:45] Who buys a $4.99 product**

[B-roll: entry-level product page, short description, simple CTA]

The $4.99 buyer is not cheap. They're testing you.

They don't know you yet. They're not sure if your approach fits them. They're willing to risk five dollars to find out.

What they expect at $4.99: something focused, fast to consume, immediately usable. They don't expect a course. They expect a tool.

[B-roll: single-page PDF, tight format, clear outcome]

If you give them that, they become the buyer who eventually spends $99 with you. If you give them a bloated PDF, they don't come back.

The $4.99 product is a trust-builder, not a revenue source.

**[02:30] Who buys a $99 product**

[B-roll: Ghost Writer Kit product page, longer description, deliverables list]

The $99 buyer has already done their research. They've read your content. They've seen your thinking. They're not testing you anymore.

**[SECTION 3 — 4:15-6:00]**
**[04:15] What $9.99 and $14.99 actually do**

[B-roll: mid-range product pages, AI Prompt Cheat Sheet, Niche Sniper]

The middle tiers are where most volume happens.

$9.99 has low enough friction that people don't agonize. High enough that they respect it. This is the tier for cheat sheets, templates, and reference guides.

[B-roll: conversion funnel diagram, wide at top for $4.99, narrow for $99]

Honest numbers from my catalog.

At $4.99: high conversion from traffic, low average order value, high repeat buyer rate.

At $99: low conversion from cold traffic, high conversion from warm audience, higher satisfaction and lower refund rate.

The mistake most people make: optimizing the $99 product for cold conversion. That's not what $99 is for. It closes warm audiences.

Your job at $99 is not to convince. It's to deliver for the people who already believe in you.

**[SECTION 5 — 7:45-9:30]**

[B-roll: catalog price ladder visible, $4.99 at bottom, $149.99 bundle at top]

[B-roll: product changelog, new additions documented]

## SCRIPT 3
### "Why most digital products don't sell (it's not traffic)"

**Target length:** 1,000-1,200 words
**Product tie-in:** Behike Method v2 ($19.99) — behike.shop
**Thumbnail concept:** Product listing with zero sales. Sad face icon crossed out. Text: "It's not traffic."

---

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**

[B-roll: Gumroad analytics page, zero sales on a product]

The traffic came. People visited the page. Zero sales.

It's not a traffic problem. Here's what's actually wrong.

---

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**

[B-roll: laptop, Gumroad dashboard, product page visible]

I've seen this pattern in my own catalog and in dozens of other creators' setups. A product that should sell sitting at zero or near-zero, with traffic that confirms people are seeing it.

The instinct is always "I need more traffic." That's usually the wrong answer.

The real problem is almost always one of three things.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45-2:30]**
**[00:45] Problem one: the hook doesn't match the product**

[B-roll: product listing with vague headline, then revised with specific headline]

The hook is the first thing a visitor reads. It's the headline, the first sentence of the description, the product title.

Most digital product hooks describe the product. "A 30-page guide to email marketing." That's a description. A description doesn't sell.

A hook that sells makes a specific claim about the buyer's situation. "Write your first email sequence in one afternoon using this template." Same product. Completely different framing.

[B-roll: A/B comparison of two listing headlines, one vague, one specific]

Vague hooks convert poorly regardless of traffic. The audience can't tell if the product is for them.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:30-4:15]**
**[02:30] Problem two: the audience is wrong, not small**

[B-roll: audience targeting diagram, broad vs. specific]

More traffic from the wrong audience produces more zero-sales pages.

The question is not "how many people saw this." The question is "did the right people see this."

[B-roll: content that attracted wrong audience vs. right audience side by side]

If your product is for solopreneurs who want to automate content and your traffic comes from people who want to become influencers, you will never convert well. The intent doesn't match.

The fix is upstream. Look at the content that drove traffic. Does it attract the person who would buy your product, or just someone in the general area.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 4:15-6:00]**
**[04:15] Problem three: no clear next step for the buyer**

[B-roll: product page with weak CTA, then strong CTA comparison]

Even when the hook works and the right audience finds you, a weak CTA kills conversion.

"Buy now" is the weakest CTA possible. It tells the buyer nothing about what happens next.

A strong CTA for a digital product answers two questions: what happens immediately after purchase, and what does success look like.

[B-roll: strong product CTA example, written on screen]

"Download instantly. Use the template inside to build your first automation in 30 minutes." That CTA closes. The buyer can see the outcome before they buy.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 6:00-7:45]**
**[06:00] The role of social proof (and why it's not what you think)**

[B-roll: testimonial section on a product page, one specific quote]

New creators focus on getting lots of reviews. That's not the most important thing.

One specific, credible testimonial outperforms ten generic ones. "This saved me six hours of work" is worth twenty "love this product" reviews.

Specificity is what makes social proof believable. Generic praise sounds like it was written by the creator. Specific outcomes sound like a real buyer.

[B-roll: specific testimonial example compared to generic one]

If you have zero reviews, lead with your own use case. "I built this because I needed it. Here's what I built it to do." That's more credible than manufactured social proof.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 7:45-9:30]**
**[07:45] The listing as a conversation, not a pitch**

[B-roll: Gumroad listing scroll, section by section]

The best product listings I've seen read like a conversation between the creator and a specific buyer.

They name the problem. They describe what it was like to have the problem. They explain what they built to solve it. They show the outcome. They explain exactly what the buyer receives.

That structure is not a sales formula. It's just how one person talks to another person about something they built.

[B-roll: listing rewrite, before and after sections on screen]

When your listing sounds like a pitch, buyers feel sold to. When it sounds like a conversation, they feel informed.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 9:30-10:45]**
**[09:30] The one-hour listing audit**

[B-roll: notebook with audit checklist]

When a product isn't selling, I do a one-hour audit.

First 20 minutes: read the listing out loud. Anywhere it sounds like marketing copy, rewrite it to sound like you talking to someone.

Next 20 minutes: look at the traffic source. Is this the right audience for this product.

Last 20 minutes: look at the hook and CTA. Run both through the test: does the hook describe the buyer's situation, does the CTA show them the outcome.

[B-roll: changes being made to listing after audit]

Most of the time, something changes in that hour and the listing improves. Not always dramatically. But enough to move.

---

## SCRIPT 4
### "How to write a Gumroad listing that actually converts"

**Target length:** 1,000-1,200 words
**Product tie-in:** Behike Method v2 ($19.99) — behike.shop
**Thumbnail concept:** Clean split screen. Left: bad listing. Right: good listing. Text: "Rewrite it like this."

---

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**

[B-roll: cursor hovering over a Gumroad buy button]

The product is good. The listing is killing it.

Here's how to rewrite it in one hour.

---

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**

[B-roll: Gumroad product editor open, description field visible]

A Gumroad listing is usually 200-400 words. It has a title, a short description, maybe a few bullet points, and a buy button.

Most listings waste that space with features. What you get. What's included. How many pages.

Features don't convert. Outcomes do. This video shows you exactly how to write for outcomes.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45-2:30]**
**[00:45] The title: specificity over cleverness**

[B-roll: title brainstorming on screen, drafts visible]

A Gumroad title has one job: tell the right buyer this is for them.

Bad title: "The Complete Email Marketing Guide." That's a category, not a product.

Good title: "Email Welcome Sequence Template: Your First 5 Emails, Done in One Afternoon." That's a specific deliverable with a time expectation.

[B-roll: before and after title comparison]

The test I use: read the title and ask if you could find ten other products with the same title. If yes, it's too generic. Make it specific enough that it describes exactly one product.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:30-4:15]**
**[02:30] The first sentence: the hook**

[B-roll: Gumroad listing, first sentence highlighted]

The first sentence of your description is read by everyone who visits the page. Everything after it is read by the people the first sentence kept.

The first sentence should describe the buyer's current situation. Not the product. The buyer.

"You've been putting off setting up email because the whole thing feels like a system you don't have time to build." That's a first sentence for someone who will buy an email template.

[B-roll: first sentence rewrite example, cursor typing]

It says nothing about the product yet. It just tells the right buyer: "I know what you're dealing with."

---

**[SECTION 3 — 4:15-6:00]**
**[04:15] The problem section**

[B-roll: problem description in listing, 2-3 short paragraphs]

After the hook, describe the problem specifically. Not vaguely.

Bad: "Email marketing is hard and time-consuming."
Good: "Most people write the first email fine. It's the sequence that breaks down. Email three feels redundant. Email five has no call to action. By email seven, you've run out of things to say."

[B-roll: detailed problem description on screen, specific scenarios]

The more specific the problem description, the more the right buyer feels seen. The buyer who doesn't have that problem stops reading. That's a good thing. You're filtering for buyers, not maximizing pageviews.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 6:00-7:45]**
**[06:00] The solution section**

[B-roll: solution description following problem, two short paragraphs]

The solution section is short. Two paragraphs max.

First paragraph: what you built and why. "I built this template for myself when I was setting up my first store. It took me three iterations to get a sequence that converted. This is the third version."

Second paragraph: what the buyer gets. "Five email templates, pre-written. A brief for each one explaining what it does and why it works. A timing guide."

[B-roll: deliverables list on product page]

Short. Specific. No adjectives that don't do work.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 7:45-9:30]**
**[07:45] The outcome statement**

[B-roll: outcome section at the bottom of a listing]

After the solution, one sentence about the outcome. Just one.

"If you follow this sequence, you'll have a working five-email welcome series by the end of the day."

That sentence does three things. It gives a specific result. It specifies a timeframe. It puts the condition on the buyer doing the work, not on the product being magic.

[B-roll: outcome sentence highlighted in red, set apart from the rest]

Honest outcome statements build trust. Overpromised outcomes get refund requests.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 9:30-10:45]**
**[09:30] The buy button text and CTA**

[B-roll: Gumroad checkout button, default text vs. custom text]

Most Gumroad stores use the default "I want this" button text. That's fine, but you can improve it.

I use text that reinforces the outcome: "Get the template" or "Download the sequence." Those phrases remind the buyer what they're about to receive, not just that they're making a purchase.

[B-roll: checkout flow from product page to confirmation]

The CTA at the bottom of your description should be even more specific: "Download instantly and complete your first sequence today." It's a micro-commitment. The buyer can picture themselves doing it.

---

Behike Method v2 has the full listing framework plus templates for each section. Title, hook, problem, solution, outcome, CTA.

## SCRIPT 5
### "Bundles: why grouping products changes the economics completely"

**Target length:** 1,000-1,200 words
**Product tie-in:** Complete Bundle ($149.99) — behike.shop
**Thumbnail concept:** Individual products scattered. Then grouped with a bracket and one price. Text: "This is where revenue lives."

---

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**

[B-roll: bundle product page, individual product values listed, bundle price below]

Twenty-five individual products. $507 total if you bought them one by one.

One bundle: $149.99. The economics of this are worth understanding.

---

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**

[B-roll: product grid, then bundle card at the end of the row]

Bundles are not a discount strategy. They're a different product with a different buyer and a different job.

The buyer who purchases a bundle is not the same person who buys one product. The bundle buyer is committing to using the whole system. The individual buyer is solving a specific problem.

Understanding that distinction is what makes bundles profitable.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45-2:30]**
**[00:45] Why bundles work psychologically**

[B-roll: buyer decision tree, one item vs. multiple items, cognitive load comparison]

When you buy one product, you evaluate one purchase. When you buy a bundle, you evaluate one purchase against an aggregate value.

The cognitive math works in your favor as the seller. The buyer sees: twenty-five products individually would cost $507. The bundle is $149.99. That's a 70% reduction. The perceived value is enormous.

[B-roll: price comparison highlighted in listing]

What the buyer is actually getting is not just the products. They're getting permission to stop deciding. One purchase covers everything. That relief has value.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:30-4:15]**
**[02:30] How bundles change your average order value**

[B-roll: Gumroad analytics, order value distribution]

Without bundles, my average order value was around $14. Most buyers were picking up one product in the $9.99-$19.99 range.

After adding the Complete Bundle, my average order value went up significantly. Not because I sold more individual products. Because some buyers chose the bundle instead of deciding which single product to start with.

[B-roll: bundle vs. single product purchase comparison, revenue per transaction]

The bundle converts a "where do I start" decision into a single purchase. That's valuable for the buyer and for your revenue.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 4:15-6:00]**
**[04:15] How to price a bundle honestly**

[B-roll: pricing calculation on screen, adding up individual values]

Bundle pricing has two constraints.

The first: the bundle price should be a clear discount from the sum of individual prices. The buyer needs to feel the math immediately. If the bundle saves 20%, most people will just buy the most relevant product individually. If it saves 60-70%, the bundle becomes obviously the better choice.

The second: the price should still feel proportional to the value. A bundle priced at $149.99 needs to contain products that justify that number. If someone pays $149.99 and feels the quality is $30 worth of PDFs, you've damaged trust for future sales.

[B-roll: bundle listing with individual values and total comparison]

I price my bundles at about 70% off the sum. That's the threshold where the math is undeniable.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 6:00-7:45]**
**[06:00] Themed bundles vs. complete bundles**

[B-roll: Automation Bundle vs. Complete Bundle product pages side by side]

Not every bundle should be "everything." Themed bundles often convert better for specific audiences.

My Automation Bundle groups n8n Automation Pack, Claude Code Course, AI Agent Installer Kit, and AI Employee Guide. $59.99. It's for one type of buyer: someone who wants to automate their workflow.

That buyer might not want my Roblox Builder's Guide. The Complete Bundle includes it. The Automation Bundle doesn't.

[B-roll: focused buyer persona diagram next to themed bundle]

Match the bundle to the buyer's identity, not just the product category. "All my automation tools" is clearer to the right buyer than "everything I've ever made."

---

**[SECTION 5 — 7:45-9:30]**
**[07:45] When to introduce a bundle**

[B-roll: product catalog timeline, bundle added at six products]

I waited until I had at least six products before creating my first bundle.

With fewer than six, the bundle looks thin. The buyer sees the math but doesn't feel the depth.

[B-roll: catalog growth chart, bundle introduced at specific milestone]

With six or more, the bundle starts to feel like a complete system. That's when the "one purchase covers everything" value proposition becomes real.

Introduce the bundle as soon as you have enough products to make it genuinely broad. Don't wait until you have 25.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 9:30-10:45]**
**[09:30] The bundle as a catalog anchor**

[B-roll: product page, bundle listed prominently at the top]

Having a Complete Bundle changes how people browse your catalog.

Before bundles, buyers evaluate each product independently. After bundles, they evaluate individual products against the bundle. "Is this one product worth $19.99, or should I just get the full thing for $149.99."

[B-roll: buyer evaluation flow with bundle as reference point]

Some people buy the individual product. Some people upgrade to the bundle. Both outcomes are better than the buyer leaving with nothing because they couldn't decide.

The bundle is an anchor. It gives every other product in your catalog a reference point.
