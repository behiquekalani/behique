### SECTION 1: Why Standard Routines Fail ADHD Brains (0:45-2:45)

[B-roll: long morning routine checklist, person looking overwhelmed, clock showing 9am]

**[Timestamp card: 0:45 — Why Standard Routines Fail]**

Standard morning routines are built on the assumption that motivation is a reliable resource. For most people, a strong routine generates momentum. You do one thing, it leads to the next, and by the time you sit down to work you feel ready.

ADHD brains do not follow that chain reliably. Dopamine dysregulation means that starting a chain is the hard part. The first task is the highest-resistance moment. If that first task requires any decision-making, you lose.

The other problem is length. Most productivity routines are long. Meditation, journaling, exercise, cold shower, reading. Each item requires a transition. Transitions are where ADHD brains stall. The longer the routine, the more transitions, the higher the failure rate.

A routine that works for an ADHD brain has three properties. It is short. It is fixed, meaning no decisions about what comes next. And it ends with you already doing real work, not preparing to do real work.

---

### SECTION 2: The Three-Phase Structure (2:45-5:00)

[B-roll: simple phone alarm setup, glass of water, laptop opening]

**[Timestamp card: 2:45 — The Three-Phase Structure]**

My morning routine has three phases. Total time: 18 to 22 minutes.

Phase one is activation. 5 minutes. This is the bridge between sleep and function. The goal is not to be productive. The goal is to be awake. I drink a full glass of water before I touch my phone. I do not check notifications. I do not open social media. The water is the trigger. It is physical, immediate, and requires no decision. After the water, I put on the same playlist I always use for mornings. Same playlist every day. The music is the cue that tells my brain what context we are in.

Phase two is orientation. 8 minutes. I open Behike OS, my one-tab operating system. I read my sprint goal, which I wrote the previous Sunday. I read my three tasks for today. I do not add to them. I do not reorganize them. I read them. Then I do a two-minute physical movement. Not a full workout. Two minutes. Jumping jacks, walking around the room, anything that gets blood moving. This is not about fitness. It is about reducing the physical drag that makes the brain feel slow.

Phase three is the start. 5 minutes. I open the first task and start working on it. Not planning it. Not organizing notes about it. Starting it. The timer runs for 25 minutes once I begin. By the time the timer starts, the routine is over.

That is the whole thing. No journaling. No meditation. No cold plunge. Those things are fine if you like them, but they are not required for a functional morning.

---

### SECTION 3: The Phone Protocol (5:00-6:30)

[B-roll: phone face-down on a desk, phone in another room, notification settings screen]

**[Timestamp card: 5:00 — The Phone Protocol]**

The phone is the largest threat to a functioning ADHD morning. Not because you lack discipline. Because the phone is engineered by teams of engineers specifically to capture attention. You are not competing with your own laziness. You are competing with billion-dollar behavioral science.

The protocol is simple. The phone does not come off the charger until phase two ends. That is approximately 13 minutes after waking. Not an hour. Not two hours. Thirteen minutes.

During those 13 minutes the phone stays in a different room, or face-down on the far side of the desk. The charger is not next to the bed.

If you need an alarm, use a separate alarm clock. They cost less than ten dollars. The investment is worth it.

After phase two, the phone comes on. You have five minutes to handle any urgent notifications before the first Pomodoro starts. Urgent means: someone is in danger, or a paying customer has a critical issue. Everything else waits until the first break.

This sounds extreme. It is not. You are giving yourself 18 minutes before the world starts demanding things. That is the minimum viable barrier between sleep and reactive mode.

---

### SECTION 4: Handling the Days When It Breaks (6:30-8:30)

[B-roll: person waking up late, messy room, looking frustrated then refocusing]

**[Timestamp card: 6:30 — When It Breaks]**

The routine will break. Late nights happen. You wake up at 11am. You already checked your phone before your feet hit the floor. The chain is broken before it started.

This is where most systems fail permanently. The person misses one day, decides the system does not work, and abandons it. This is a pattern, not a personal failure.

The rule for broken mornings is: run a compressed version. Not zero, not the full version. Compressed.

Compressed means: water, open the OS, read the three tasks, start the timer. Two minutes total. That is the floor.

The reason this works is continuity. The brain tracks streaks and patterns. A two-minute compressed version keeps the pattern alive. It signals to your system that this is what mornings look like, even when they are imperfect. A perfect system you abandon is worth nothing. An imperfect system you maintain is worth everything.

Write the compressed version on a sticky note and put it somewhere visible. On bad mornings, you do not think. You read the sticky note.

---

### SECTION 5: The Night-Before Setup (8:30-10:00)

[B-roll: person at desk at night, writing three tasks on paper, closing laptop deliberately]

**[Timestamp card: 8:30 — Night-Before Setup]**

The morning routine starts the night before. This is not metaphorical.

Every evening, before I close the laptop, I do three things. I write down tomorrow's three tasks. I set the OS sprint goal if it needs updating. And I set out whatever physical items I need for the morning, whether that is my notebook, a specific piece of work, or a reminder.

This matters because ADHD working memory is unreliable. You cannot count on remembering what you decided to do. The written task list from the night before removes that dependency. When you sit down in the morning, the decision is already made. You execute. You do not plan.

The night-before setup takes less than five minutes. It is the single highest-leverage action in the entire system.

### SECTION 1: The Psychology of Setup Work (0:45-2:45)

[B-roll: brain scan graphic, dopamine meter animation, Notion template marketplace]

**[Timestamp card: 0:45 — The Psychology of Setup Work]**

There is a name for what happens when you spend hours building a productivity system instead of doing actual work. It is called productive procrastination.

The brain experiences setup work as meaningful activity. You are making decisions, creating structure, solving problems. Dopamine fires. It feels like progress. But the output is a system, not the thing the system is supposed to produce.

This is particularly common in people who have ADHD, creative tendencies, or high openness to experience. The novelty of a new system is itself rewarding. The first three days of any new productivity framework feel good because you are still in setup mode. Reality arrives on day four.

The trap is that when the system stops feeling good, the easy move is to blame the system. Add a new feature. Try a different app. Rebuild. The rebuild gives you another three-day dopamine window. The cycle repeats.

This is not a discipline problem. It is a reward structure problem. The system has been accidentally optimized to reward building instead of shipping.

---

### SECTION 2: Five Signs Your System Is the Problem (2:45-5:00)

[B-roll: checklist on screen, each item being checked with recognition]

**[Timestamp card: 2:45 — Five Signs Your System Is the Problem]**

Here are five specific signs that your productivity system is working against you.

Sign one: you spend more time updating the system than executing tasks from it. If your daily review takes 30 minutes and your first work session takes 25, the ratio is inverted.

Sign two: you have more categories than active projects. Categories create the illusion of structure. If you have 12 Notion tags but only two active projects, ten of those tags are theater.

Sign three: you use the system to avoid starting the hardest task. You update your habit tracker. You reorganize your project boards. You archive old tasks. All legitimate system work. All happening because the hard task is on the list and you are not touching it.

Sign four: you feel productive at the end of the day but cannot name three things you shipped. Feeling productive and being productive are different measurements. The system should produce tangible outputs, not good feelings.

Sign five: you have rebuilt the system more than twice in the last six months. Each rebuild resets your accountability. You cannot look back at what you did last quarter because the old system is gone. Rebuilding is a way of erasing accountability without acknowledging that is what you are doing.

---

### SECTION 3: The Minimum Viable System (5:00-7:00)

[B-roll: blank Notion page, then three simple items being added, nothing more]

**[Timestamp card: 5:00 — The Minimum Viable System]**

The productive system is the smallest system that generates consistent output.

Here is what that looks like in practice.

One page. Not a workspace. One page.

Three items on that page: today's goal, today's three tasks, and current sprint focus. That is the entire system for daily operation.

A separate archive page holds completed work. You move things there once a week. The main page never gets longer.

No habit trackers. No dashboards. No databases unless a database is directly required by an active project.

This feels insufficient. It is not. The purpose of the system is to answer one question when you sit down to work: what should I do right now? A one-page system answers that question in five seconds. An elaborate system makes you navigate to find the answer, and navigation is an opportunity to get distracted.

The minimum viable system is uncomfortable because it offers nowhere to hide. There is no organizing to do. There is only the work.

---

### SECTION 4: How to Audit Your Current System (7:00-9:00)

[B-roll: person going through their Notion, deleting sections, simplifying]

**[Timestamp card: 7:00 — Audit Your Current System]**

Run this audit on your current system. It takes about 20 minutes.

Step one: list every section, database, or page in your current system. Write them all down.

Step two: for each item, answer one question. In the last 14 days, did this section contribute directly to an output? Not to planning. Not to organizing. An actual output. Something shipped.

Step three: archive everything that did not contribute. Do not delete it. Archive it. The psychological resistance to deleting is real. Archiving respects the work that went into building those sections without letting them clutter your active workspace.

Step four: what remains is your actual system. Whatever answered yes to step two. This is what your brain actually uses when it is working.

Step five: rebuild only from what remains. Do not add anything new for 30 days.

Most people who run this audit find that two to four sections from their entire system are actually active. Everything else was maintenance theater.

[B-roll: before and after comparison of simplified system]

---

### SECTION 5: Building a System That Stays Simple (9:00-10:30)

[B-roll: handwritten rule on sticky note, calendar reminder on phone, simple workspace]

**[Timestamp card: 9:00 — Keeping It Simple]**

Simplicity is not a starting condition. It is a discipline you maintain.

Three rules keep the system small.

Rule one: one in, one out. Before adding anything new to the system, you remove something of equal size. A new tracker comes in, an old one goes to archive. This creates a cost for addition that did not exist before.

Rule two: the Sunday rule. Every Sunday, the system gets ten minutes. You update the sprint goal if needed. You clear completed items. You do nothing else to the system. No redesigns. No new sections. Ten minutes, then close.

Rule three: the embarrassment test. Before adding anything to your system, ask whether you would be embarrassed to show it to someone who ships a lot. If a high-output founder looked at this new section and thought "that is a waste of time," it probably is.

These rules are not strict. But they create friction for the impulse to build instead of ship. That friction is the point.

The foundation of a content machine is capture. Ideas that are not captured are not content. They are noise.

The capture layer needs to have one property: zero friction. If capturing an idea takes more than ten seconds, you will not do it consistently.

Free tool: Telegram. Specifically, a private Telegram channel you send messages to yourself. You can send text, voice messages, images, links. Everything goes in chronologically. It is searchable. It syncs across devices. It costs nothing.

The voice note is important for ADHD brains specifically. If the idea comes in the shower, while driving, or mid-conversation, a voice note is faster than typing. Telegram transcribes voice notes automatically in newer versions.

Once a week, you triage the channel. You look at everything that came in during the week and decide: is this an idea worth developing? The ones that survive triage move to the content calendar. The rest stay in the Telegram archive as reference.

Do not triage daily. The daily volume is too low to be worth the context switch. Once a week, 20 minutes. That is the capture workflow.

### SECTION 3: The Planning Layer (4:30-6:30)

[B-roll: Notion content calendar, simple three-column board, monthly view]

**[Timestamp card: 4:30 — Planning Layer]**

The planning layer is where ideas become scheduled content.

Free tool: Notion free tier. Specifically, a simple board with three columns: Idea, In Production, Scheduled.

Each idea that survives the weekly triage becomes a card. The card has four fields: topic, format, platform, and target date. Nothing else. No elaborate tags, no status systems, no priority matrices.

The board is not a project management system. It is a visual representation of what is moving. Cards move from Idea to In Production when you start creating. They move to Scheduled when they are ready to post. Once posted, they move to a separate archive board. The main board never has more than two weeks of content on it.

[B-roll: Notion board being updated, cards moving forward]

The planning session happens once a week, the same day as the capture triage. You triage ideas, then immediately plan the next week's content. This is one session, not two. The total time is about 30 minutes.

The rule is: you do not start creating any piece of content that is not on the board. If the idea is not planned, it is not in production. This prevents reactive content creation, which is the main reason most solo creators produce inconsistently.

### SECTION 4: The Creation Layer (6:30-9:00)

[B-roll: CapCut timeline, Canva design being created, simple screen recording setup]

**[Timestamp card: 6:30 — Creation Layer]**

The creation layer is where content gets made. This is the highest-cost step in terms of time and energy. The system is designed to make this layer as efficient as possible.

Free tools: CapCut for video editing, Canva free tier for graphics, OBS Studio for screen recording.

The efficiency principle is batching. You create all content for one week in a single session. Not scattered across the week. One session.

The session structure is this. First 30 minutes: write all scripts or outlines for the week. You are in writing mode. Do not edit while writing. Second 30 minutes: record all video content. Same setup, same lighting, same energy. Third 30 minutes: edit everything. You are in editing mode. Do not shoot while editing. Final 20 minutes: create graphics and thumbnails.

[B-roll: timer counting through each phase, final batch of content files visible]

Batching works because mode-switching is expensive. Every time you switch from writing to recording to editing, your brain needs time to recalibrate. Batching eliminates most of those switches. You are in one mode for an extended period, which is faster and produces better work.

For a four-hour-per-week content schedule, one creation session plus one planning and triage session covers the whole week.

### SECTION 5: The Distribution Layer (9:00-11:00)

[B-roll: Buffer scheduling interface, posts being queued, calendar showing scheduled times]

**[Timestamp card: 9:00 — Distribution Layer]**

The distribution layer is where content gets published. This is the mechanical part of the workflow. Zero creative energy required.

Free tool: Buffer free tier. It handles scheduling for Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, Facebook, and X from one interface. The free plan covers three channels with up to ten scheduled posts each. For a solo creator starting out, that is sufficient.

The distribution workflow is the same day as the creation session. After creation, upload everything to Buffer and schedule it. This takes about 20 minutes. The week's content is now on autopilot.

[B-roll: Buffer queue filled with scheduled posts, then closing laptop]

The one rule that makes distribution sustainable is consistency over volume. It is better to post three times per week for six months than to post seven times per week for three weeks. The algorithm rewards sustained presence more than short-term volume.

Pick a number you can maintain in four hours per week. For most solo creators, that is three to four posts per week on one platform, two to three on a second. Start there. Increase only when the system feels comfortable at the current volume.

### SECTION 6: The One Rule That Makes Everything Work (11:00-12:30)

[B-roll: calendar with consistent posting marked, the word "ship" written on a sticky note]

**[Timestamp card: 11:00 — The One Rule]**

Every layer of the system depends on one rule. It is not complicated.

Never miss two in a row.

Miss one week? Fine. Life happens. But two misses in a row creates a break in the habit loop that is increasingly difficult to restart. One miss is a disruption. Two misses is a new default.

The system does not need to be perfect. It needs to be continuous. Continuous mediocre output will beat occasional brilliant output over a 12-month period. Consistency is the only compounding asset in content creation.

When the week is genuinely too full, activate the minimum viable version. One piece of content, any format, any topic. Post it. The streak lives.

This rule, held firmly, makes the entire system work. Without it, the system is decoration.
