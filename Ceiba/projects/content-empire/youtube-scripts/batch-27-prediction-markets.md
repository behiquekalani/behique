## SCRIPT 1
**Title:** How Prediction Markets Work (And Why They Are More Accurate Than Polls)
**Target Length:** 7-8 minutes
**Hook Type:** Contrast hook

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:45)

In the months leading up to a major election, you will see two different sets of numbers.

The polls will show one thing.
Prediction markets will show something slightly different.

And by the end of election night, one of them will have been more accurate.

It is almost always the prediction markets.

This is not a coincidence. There is a structural reason for it. And once you understand that reason, it changes how you evaluate any source of information about uncertain events.

Nothing in this video is financial advice. This is about understanding how prediction markets work as an information tool.

---

### FRAMEWORK (0:45 - 4:00)

**What a prediction market actually is:**

A prediction market is a system where people buy and sell contracts tied to the outcome of future events. If you believe an event will happen, you can buy a contract that pays out if it does. If you think it will not happen, you can sell or bet against it.

The price of that contract at any given moment reflects the collective belief of everyone participating about how likely that outcome is.

If a contract is trading at 70 cents on the dollar, that represents roughly 70% probability according to the market's collective judgment.

That is the mechanism. Now here is why it tends to be more accurate.

**The incentive difference:**

When someone answers a poll, there is no cost to being wrong. They answer based on what they want to happen, what they assume will happen, or what they think sounds reasonable. There is no feedback loop. No consequence.

When someone participates in a prediction market, being wrong costs them directly. Being right earns them. This creates a different relationship with their own beliefs.

Before taking a position, a careful participant will research. They will look for information others might have missed. They will think more carefully about base rates, about what variables actually matter, about how similar events have resolved historically.

The result is that prediction market prices aggregate information from people who have done real analytical work and are accountable for their conclusions.

This is called the wisdom of crowds, but with an important caveat: it works best when participants have skin in the game and access to real information. When those conditions are met, the aggregated signal is usually better than expert opinion.

**What the research shows:**

Academic research on prediction markets has found they tend to outperform polls, expert panels, and media analysts on questions with measurable outcomes. The margin is not huge in every case, but it is consistent.

Philip Tetlock's work on forecasting, which became the basis for his book "Superforecasting," found that a small group of trained forecasters could consistently outperform both polls and expert opinion. The key traits they shared: they checked base rates, updated their views frequently with new information, and held their beliefs in probabilities rather than certainties.

Prediction markets institutionalize those habits at scale.

---

### EXAMPLES (4:00 - 6:00)

**Example 1: Election forecasting**

During most modern elections, prediction markets have been faster to price in shifts in candidate probability than polling averages. When a significant event happens, like a major debate or an unexpected endorsement, market prices update within hours. Polling averages take days to catch up because polls take time to conduct and publish.

The real-time nature of markets is part of their advantage.

**Example 2: Economic events**

Markets have a track record of pricing in economic outcomes like interest rate decisions and GDP shifts before official announcements. This is because participants who have done the analytical work act on their beliefs, and those actions move prices.

The market price is essentially a live, crowd-sourced analysis that has been bet on.

**Example 3: Scientific questions**

Some prediction markets run on scientific questions, like the probability that a given study will replicate, or whether a specific drug will receive regulatory approval. Researchers have found that these markets are reasonably well-calibrated compared to expert estimates.

---

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION (6:00 - 7:30)

How do you use this as an information consumer?

First, treat market prices as one signal among several. They are not infallible. They can be wrong. They can be manipulated by large actors in thin markets. They reflect the information available at the time.

But they are a faster, more incentive-aligned signal than most news sources. When you are trying to understand the probability of an uncertain event, checking what the market is pricing can be more informative than reading commentary from people with no stake in the outcome.

Second, studying how prediction markets work trains you to think probabilistically. The goal is not to participate in these markets. The goal is to absorb their way of reasoning.

Ask yourself when forming any opinion about an uncertain event: what probability would I put on this? What base rate am I starting from? What would change my view?

Those questions make you a better thinker whether you ever open a market account or not.

## SCRIPT 2
**Title:** The Research Framework for Evaluating Uncertain Events
**Target Length:** 7-8 minutes
**Hook Type:** Problem hook

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:45)

Most people do not have a research process.

They have a reaction process.

They encounter an uncertain event, they have an immediate gut response, they look for information that confirms it, and they call that research.

The problem is not that they reach wrong conclusions. The problem is that their process does not get better over time.

If you do not have a defined framework, you cannot improve it. You cannot identify where your analysis broke down.

This video is a 6-step framework for evaluating uncertain events more accurately. Nothing in this video is financial advice.

---

### FRAMEWORK (0:45 - 5:00)

**Step 1: Establish the base rate**

Before anything else, ask: historically, how often does this type of situation resolve in each possible direction?

This step alone corrects for a huge percentage of forecasting errors. People treat every event as unique. Most events are not as unique as they appear. Similar situations have happened before. Look at how they resolved.

If you are trying to evaluate whether a company will recover from a product recall, find base rate data on how often companies recover from product recalls, and how long it takes.

If you are evaluating a political event, find historical data on how similar events have resolved.

Ground your analysis in what usually happens before you argue for why this case is different.

**Step 2: Identify the key variable**

Most uncertain situations have one or two factors that are doing most of the predictive work.

Before you go broad in your research, spend time identifying what matters most. Otherwise you diffuse energy across 20 factors when 2 of them explain 80% of the outcome.

Ask: if I had to pick the single factor that will most determine how this resolves, what would it be? Start there.

**Step 3: Go to primary sources**

News articles are someone else's interpretation of original data.

If you are researching a company, read the actual filings, not just the coverage of them. If you are researching a policy, read the actual text, not just the editorial commentary.

Primary sources take more time. They also contain information that gets filtered out, mischaracterized, or de-emphasized in secondary coverage.

**Step 4: Map the incentives**

Every person and institution that produces information has an incentive structure that shapes what they say.

This does not mean everyone is lying. It means that what a source emphasizes, downplays, or omits is influenced by what they benefit from.

When you evaluate a source, ask: what does this person or organization stand to gain from this particular framing? That tells you where to apply extra scrutiny.

**Step 5: Assign a probability, not a verdict**

This is the step most people skip. Instead of concluding "X will happen," conclude "I think X is 65% likely based on what I found."

This matters for two reasons.

First, it keeps you epistemically honest. It forces you to hold your view as a probabilistic belief rather than a certainty.

Second, it creates a measurable outcome. You can track whether your 65% beliefs are right about 65% of the time. That is how you calibrate.

**Step 6: Set a revision trigger in advance**

Before you close your analysis, write down what new information would cause you to update your view and by roughly how much.

This does two things. It prevents you from anchoring too hard on your initial conclusion. And it keeps you alert to incoming information that is actually relevant versus noise that just confirms what you already think.

---

### EXAMPLES (5:00 - 6:30)

Let me walk through how this looks applied to a simple uncertain question.

Suppose you are trying to evaluate whether a new policy will pass in the next 6 months.

Step 1: What is the base rate of similar policies passing in comparable political environments? Start there.

Step 2: What is the single most important variable? Probably the positions of 3-5 key deciding votes. Focus research there.

Step 3: Go read actual statements from those people, not just coverage of what they allegedly said.

Step 4: Who has an interest in this passing or failing? Which sources covering this story benefit from particular framing?

Step 5: Based on all of that, "I think this has roughly a 45% chance of passing in the next 6 months."

Step 6: "If two of the key swing votes publicly change position, I would revise to 70%. If the lead sponsor withdraws support, I would revise to 20%."

That is a complete analysis. It took more work than reading three headlines. It is also far more actionable.

---

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION (6:30 - 7:30)

You do not need to apply this framework to every opinion you hold. That would be exhausting.

Apply it to decisions that matter. Career choices. Major purchases. Anything where being systematically wrong has real costs.

The more you practice it on lower-stakes questions, the faster and more automatic it becomes on high-stakes ones.

Keep a decision journal. Not every day. Just when you make a meaningful decision or form a strong view. Log the base rate you started from, the key variable, the probability you assigned, and the revision trigger.

Review it every few months. You will learn more about your own thinking patterns from that log than from any course on decision-making.

## SCRIPT 3
**Title:** Base Rate Thinking: The Mental Model That Changes How You Analyze Anything
**Target Length:** 7-8 minutes
**Hook Type:** Claim hook with immediate evidence

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:45)

Daniel Kahneman called neglect of the base rate one of the most consistent and costly errors in human judgment.

And yet almost no one checks base rates before forming an opinion.

It is the simplest, most foundational thing you can do to improve your analytical accuracy. It is almost never done. And once you understand why it matters, you will not be able to stop noticing when people skip it.

Nothing in this video is financial advice. This is about a thinking tool that applies to almost any domain.

---

### FRAMEWORK (0:45 - 3:30)

**What the base rate is:**

The base rate is the historical frequency of a particular type of outcome across a reference class of similar situations.

Before you analyze what makes this specific situation unusual, you need to know what usually happens in situations like it.

That is the base rate. The default. What happens absent a compelling reason to expect something different.

**Why people ignore it:**

Human thinking is narrative-based. When we encounter a specific situation, we automatically start constructing a story about it. That story involves the particular details of this case: the specific people, the specific circumstances, the specific factors that make this feel unique.

Narratives are engaging. Base rates are boring.

The irony is that base rates are almost always more predictive than the narrative details that feel important.

**The base rate neglect error:**

When someone asks you to evaluate a startup's chances of success and tells you about the team, the market size, and the product, your brain constructs a detailed picture of that specific company.

The base rate answer is: most startups fail. The majority fail within 5 years. That fact is more predictive than almost any specific detail about any individual startup.

This is not pessimism. It is calibration.

Good analysts do not ignore the specific details. They start with the base rate and then ask: what makes this case different enough to justify departing from the base rate? How much?

---

### EXAMPLES (3:30 - 5:30)

**Example 1: Project planning**

The planning fallacy, another concept from Kahneman, describes the systematic tendency to underestimate how long projects will take and how much they will cost.

The fix is to apply the base rate. Instead of asking "how long do I think this project will take?", ask "how long have similar projects actually taken in practice?"

When you look at the base rate for projects of that type, size, and complexity, your estimate becomes far more accurate.

Teams that use this method, called reference class forecasting, consistently outperform teams that rely only on detailed internal analysis.

**Example 2: Medical diagnosis**

When a doctor encounters a patient with ambiguous symptoms, starting with base rates means first considering what the most common causes of those symptoms are in patients of that profile.

Rare diagnoses are exciting narratives. Common diagnoses are usually correct.

"When you hear hoofbeats, think horses before zebras" is base rate thinking in medical form.

**Example 3: Forecasting outcomes in any uncertain domain**

Whether you are trying to evaluate a business decision, a career move, or a complex event you are trying to understand, the first question is always: in historically similar situations, what has the distribution of outcomes looked like?

If 80% of similar situations resolved in direction A, you need a compelling specific reason to bet heavily on direction B.

Maybe you have that reason. But starting from the base rate prevents you from inventing a compelling story about B when the evidence does not actually support departing from the base rate.

---

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION (5:30 - 7:00)

How to apply base rate thinking:

1. When forming any prediction, identify the reference class first. What category of situation is this? What group of similar events can you look at?

2. Find the historical distribution of outcomes for that reference class. Numbers if possible. General patterns if numbers are not available.

3. Start your estimate from that distribution. Then add the specific details of this case and ask: how much should these specific factors move me away from the base rate?

4. Be conservative about how much weight you give to specific narrative factors. They feel compelling. They are usually less predictive than you think.

The goal is not to be cynical or to ignore specific details. The goal is to sequence your analysis correctly.

Base rate first. Specific details second. That order prevents a lot of errors.

## SCRIPT 4
**Title:** Why I Track My Prediction Accuracy (And What I've Learned)
**Target Length:** 7-8 minutes
**Hook Type:** Personal story hook

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:45)

About a year ago I started keeping a log of my predictions.

Not to prove anything. Not to post about it. Just to see if my thinking was actually as good as I assumed it was.

It was not.

The gap between my confidence in my views and my actual accuracy was uncomfortable to look at. But it was also one of the most useful things I have ever done for my thinking.

This video is about what I found, why it matters, and how you can do the same thing.

Nothing here is financial advice. This is about cognitive self-assessment.

---

### FRAMEWORK (0:45 - 3:30)

**Why most people do not track predictions:**

There is no social reward for honest self-assessment.

When you are right about something, you remember it. You might bring it up in conversation. You build a self-image around being someone with good judgment.

When you are wrong, you have a built-in suite of explanations ready. "I had no way of knowing." "The situation changed." "That was a one-off."

This is confirmation bias operating on your own track record.

You do not have accurate data on how good your judgment actually is. You have a narrative that makes you feel like your judgment is good.

The only way to break that is to actually track predictions over time.

**What calibration means:**

A well-calibrated person is right about 90% of the time on things they say they are 90% sure about. Right about 70% of the time on things they say they are 70% sure about.

The confidence matches the accuracy.

Most people are poorly calibrated. They are overconfident on uncertain questions and sometimes underconfident on questions where the evidence is actually strong.

Knowing your calibration pattern is useful because it tells you how to adjust. If you tend to be overconfident in fast-moving situations, you can apply a correction factor. If you tend to be underconfident in domains where you actually have expertise, you can trust yourself more.

---

### EXAMPLES (3:30 - 5:30)

**What I found about my own calibration:**

When I reviewed my first 3 months of logged predictions, I found two consistent patterns.

First, I overestimated how much single events would change ongoing trends. Something that seemed like a major shift would happen, and I would significantly revise my probability for a related outcome. In most cases, the trend continued anyway. The event mattered less than I thought.

Second, I underestimated momentum. Things that were clearly going in a direction tended to continue in that direction longer than I expected. I kept expecting reversals that were slower to come than I had predicted.

Both of those are correctable patterns once you can see them.

For the first one, I started checking base rates on how much similar events historically disrupted similar trends before revising my views.

For the second one, I started building in a "trend continuation" prior that is harder for a single piece of news to override.

**The process:**

The log itself is simple. Before I close my analysis on any uncertain question I care about, I write:

- The question, as precisely as I can state it
- My probability estimate
- The 2-3 key factors I am basing it on
- What would cause me to revise significantly
- The date I expect to have a clear outcome

Then I check back when the outcome is known and score myself.

Anything above 75% that I logged as high-confidence and was wrong about gets extra scrutiny. I want to understand why that gap happened.

---

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION (5:30 - 7:00)

You do not need special tools. A simple note or spreadsheet works.

The discipline is what matters. Force yourself to commit to a probability before you know the outcome. That commitment is the whole mechanism.

If you never commit, you can always say "I knew it was uncertain." That is not a prediction. That is a hedge.

Predictions that have real probability numbers attached to them are honest. They put something on the line. And they give you data.

Start with something small and short-term. A weekly or monthly outcome you have an opinion about. Assign a probability. Write it down. Check back.

After 6 months, look at all your logged predictions and see if your confidence levels matched your accuracy.

That data is worth more than any framework someone can give you, because it is personalized to how you actually think.

---

Not because you will suddenly become right about everything.
Because you will start to understand the texture of your own uncertainty.

## SCRIPT 5
**Title:** Information Edge: What It Is and How to Find It Before the Market Does
**Target Length:** 7-8 minutes
**Hook Type:** Reframe hook

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:45)

When people hear "information edge," they usually think it means having access to information that no one else has.

Insider knowledge. Private data. Exclusive sources.

That is one type of edge. But it is not the only type, and it is not the most accessible one.

The more interesting edge is not about what information you have.
It is about how you process the same information that everyone else already has access to.

Nothing in this video is financial advice. This is about information skills that apply to any domain where you are trying to think more accurately than the average person.

---

### FRAMEWORK (0:45 - 4:00)

**The three types of information edge:**

**Type 1: Data edge**
You have information others do not have. This is the least scalable type because it requires unusual access, and for anyone operating outside of institutional contexts, it is hard to come by through legitimate means.

**Type 2: Analytical edge**
You process the same public information that everyone has access to, but you do it more carefully, more systematically, or through a better framework.

This is accessible to anyone willing to build the habit.

**Type 3: Timing edge**
You identify the implication of publicly available information before most people have processed it.

This is partly about speed and partly about having your analytical framework already in place so you can apply it quickly when new information arrives.

The most sustainable edge for most people is a combination of Type 2 and Type 3.

**What better processing looks like:**

Most information consumers stop at the first plausible explanation for an event.

Someone with an analytical edge keeps asking questions after the first explanation.

Why is this happening now and not earlier?
Who benefits from this framing?
What does the base rate say about situations like this?
What would have to be true for the obvious explanation to be wrong?
What are the second-order implications if the obvious explanation is correct?

These questions take more time. They also produce more accurate conclusions.

**The synthesis advantage:**

One underrated source of information edge is reading across domains.

Most people specialize their information consumption. They read deeply in one area and assume the frameworks from that area apply everywhere.

The unusual connections, the insights that come from applying a model from one field to a question in a completely different field, tend to be underpriced because fewer people make those connections.

Base rate thinking from actuarial science applies to startup evaluation. Concepts from epidemiology apply to how ideas spread. Game theory from economics applies to negotiations in any context.

The more domains you understand at a framework level, the more unusual connections you can find. That is a source of insight that is genuinely rare.

---

### EXAMPLES (4:00 - 6:00)

**Example 1: The early recognizer**

In any domain, there are moments when information is publicly available but has not yet been widely processed and priced in.

A new research paper publishes on a topic most people will not read for months. A regulatory filing surfaces a detail that most analysts will overlook. A company's hiring patterns signal a strategic shift that will not be reported for a year.

The edge is not in having secret access. It is in doing the analytical work that others are not doing yet.

**Example 2: The framework applier**

Someone trained in systems thinking can look at an organization's public behavior and identify bottlenecks or failure modes that are not obvious to people without that framework.

Someone with base rate training can look at a confident prediction being made by a pundit and immediately ask: what is the base rate for this type of situation, and is this prediction departing from that base rate with sufficient justification?

The frameworks are public. The skill is applying them consistently.

**Example 3: The tracker**

As covered in the last video, someone who tracks their own prediction accuracy over time develops calibration data. They know their biases. They know where their confidence tends to be miscalibrated.

That self-knowledge is an edge. It is very rare and almost entirely from deliberate practice.

---

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION (6:00 - 7:30)

Building an analytical edge is a long-term practice, not a quick technique.

Here is how to start:

First, define a specific domain where you want to develop an edge. Being a generalist information consumer is fine, but having one domain where you go deeper than most people is where useful insight tends to come from.

Second, build your research habits in that domain around primary sources. Develop a set of primary sources you track regularly. Know where the data comes from before you read the interpretation.

Third, practice the questioning framework: base rate, key variable, incentive map, probability assignment, revision trigger. Apply it consistently until it is automatic.

Fourth, track predictions. This is where the compound improvement happens. Without tracking, you are practicing without feedback. With tracking, every outcome teaches you something about your own analytical patterns.

Fifth, read across domains. Pick one book per quarter from a field completely outside your main focus. Apply the key frameworks from it to questions in your main domain. See what unusual connections appear.

The edge is not a single moment. It is the accumulation of better habits applied consistently over time.
