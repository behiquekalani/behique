# Batch 47 — Polymarket and Prediction Markets

## Script 1: "What Prediction Markets Are and Why They're More Accurate Than Polls"

**Target length:** 9-11 min
**Hook type:** Conceptual explanation
**CTA:** Newsletter

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:45)

Before the 2024 US election, polls were showing a toss-up. Prediction markets were consistently pricing a Trump win at 60-65%. He won.

That gap is not an accident. It's the result of a structural difference in how polls and prediction markets aggregate information. Understanding why prediction markets are more accurate is useful even if you never place a single trade.

---

### SECTION 1 — What a Prediction Market Actually Is (0:45-2:30)

A prediction market is a mechanism where people bet real money on the probability of a future event.

The price of a contract represents the crowd's collective probability estimate for that event. If a contract pays $1 if an event happens and currently trades at $0.65, the market is saying there's roughly a 65% chance of that event occurring.

Polymarket is the largest prediction market by volume right now. It operates on the Polygon blockchain, which means transactions are settled on-chain and publicly visible. The markets cover elections, geopolitical events, economic indicators, sports, and increasingly, tech and business milestones.

The key thing to understand: the prices are set by real people putting real money behind their beliefs. Unlike a poll, where someone can say whatever they want at no cost, a prediction market forces skin in the game.

---

### SECTION 2 — Why Markets Beat Polls (2:30-5:00)

Polls have two major structural problems.

First: response bias. People tell pollsters what they think the pollster wants to hear, or what they're comfortable saying publicly, not necessarily what they actually believe. This is called social desirability bias and it's well-documented.

Second: equal weighting. A poll counts one vote from someone who has thought about the question for thirty seconds the same as a vote from someone who has studied the issue for months. There's no mechanism to weight by knowledge or conviction.

Prediction markets solve both.

On social desirability: when you're putting money at risk, you tend to bet what you actually believe, not what sounds good. The financial incentive cuts through the social pressure.

On equal weighting: markets are self-weighting. If you know something others don't, you can take a larger position and move the price. The price continuously updates to reflect the aggregate of everyone's conviction, not just their opinion.

This is called the Efficient Markets Hypothesis applied to information aggregation. It's imperfect — markets can be wrong, they can be manipulated on thin volume, they can reflect consensus errors — but on average and over time, they outperform polls on calibration.

---

### SECTION 3 — Where Markets Get It Wrong (5:00-7:00)

I want to be balanced here because prediction markets have real failure modes.

Thin markets. When volume is low, a few large traders can move prices significantly. A market with $50,000 in volume is much easier to manipulate than one with $5 million. I check volume before I trust a price.

Novel events. For events that have never happened before, markets have no historical base rate to anchor to. The early COVID markets were all over the place because no one had a reference point. Markets calibrate better on events with precedent.

Consensus errors. If everyone has access to the same misleading information, the market will reflect that misinformation accurately. The market aggregates information — it doesn't fact-check it.

Liquidity and resolution risk. On Polymarket specifically, you need to verify how a market resolves, who decides the outcome, and whether there's a clear resolution criteria. I've seen markets where the resolution criteria were ambiguous enough to cause disputes.

---

### SECTION 4 — How to Use This Information (7:00-9:00)

You don't need to trade to get value from prediction markets.

The most direct use is as a calibration tool. If you're making a decision that depends on a future event — whether to launch a product before an election, whether to hedge a business bet on a regulatory outcome — prediction market prices give you a better probability estimate than most news sources.

Reading a prediction market as a decision-making input is free. You're just reading a price.

The second use is as a signal for where informed opinion diverges from mainstream narrative. When a market price and a poll diverge significantly, that gap is information. It tells you that the people putting money on the line see something different from what the polls are showing.

---

### OUTRO (9:00-10:30)

Prediction markets are an underused information source. Whether or not you ever trade, checking Polymarket before a major decision that depends on uncertain future events is worth two minutes of your time.

In the next video I'll walk through how I personally research a market before taking a position. Not a recommendation. Just process.

## Script 2: "How I Research Polymarket Before Placing a Position"

**Target length:** 10-12 min
**Hook type:** Process walkthrough
**Disclaimer:** Not financial advice.
**CTA:** Research framework guide

---

### DISCLAIMER CARD

This video is for educational purposes only. Nothing here is financial advice. Prediction markets carry real financial risk. Only trade with money you can afford to lose completely.

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:45)

I don't place money on a Polymarket position the same day I discover the market. I have a research process. It's not complicated, but it's deliberate.

I'm going to walk you through exactly what I look at, in order, before I decide whether to take a position. This is my process. It's not a recommendation for what you should do.

---

### SECTION 1 — Start With the Resolution Criteria (0:45-2:30)

Before I look at the price or think about the underlying event, I read the resolution criteria in full.

This is the document that defines what happens at the end of the market. Specifically: who decides the outcome, what data sources are used, what counts as a "yes" versus a "no," and what happens in edge cases.

The number of positions I've decided not to take because the resolution criteria were ambiguous or unfavorable is significant. If the resolution logic gives the resolver too much discretion, the market is no longer just about predicting the event — it's also about predicting how the resolver will interpret an edge case.

I skip markets where I can't clearly define in one sentence what would make this resolve yes.

---

### SECTION 2 — Look at the Volume and Liquidity (2:30-4:30)

Volume tells me how many real dollars have been committed to this market. A market with $10,000 in total volume is much easier to move, and much more vulnerable to thin-air pricing, than one with $1 million.

Liquidity tells me what the spread looks like. If the best bid is 60 cents and the best ask is 68 cents, I'm immediately down 8% if I need to exit quickly. That's a meaningful cost on short-duration trades.

My rule of thumb: I don't place positions in markets with less than $100,000 in volume unless the event is clearly defined and the time horizon is short. Below that threshold the price is too easy to distort.

---

### SECTION 3 — Research the Underlying Event (4:30-7:00)

Now I actually research the event itself.

For political or regulatory events, I read primary sources — the actual legislation, the court filing, the official announcement — not news summaries. News summaries carry editorial interpretation. The primary source is the ground truth.

For economic indicators, I look at the historical pattern of the indicator, any revision history, and whether there are leading indicators that reliably predict it. For example, if the market is on CPI, I look at PPI and commodities data from the prior weeks because those feed into CPI.

For geopolitical events, I try to find non-US English language sources. Anglophone media has a geographic and political framing that sometimes misses how events look from inside the country or region in question.

I'm trying to find one piece of information that the current market price doesn't seem to be reflecting. If I don't find it, I don't trade. The price is probably already right.

---

### SECTION 4 — Check Expert Opinion and Track Records (7:00-9:00)

I look for people who have made verified predictions on similar events in the past and have a documented record.

For elections, there are forecasters with long public track records. For economic indicators, there are economists who publish predictions with timestamps. For specific domains, there are subject matter experts who post publicly.

I'm not looking for people to tell me what to think. I'm looking for the best available information, and sometimes that information is concentrated in one person's analysis.

The key word is "verified." Anyone can say "I predicted X" after the fact. I'm looking for dated public predictions with a track record I can audit.

---

### SECTION 5 — Size and Position (9:00-10:30)

After all of that, I decide whether there's a genuine edge — whether my research has given me a reason to believe the market price is mispriced.

If I find an edge, I size conservatively. I never put more than a defined percentage of my total prediction market allocation into a single market. I treat this capital as high-risk.

I also set a clear exit condition before I enter. Either the event resolves, or a specific piece of information comes out that would change my thesis. If that information comes out, I exit regardless of P&L.

---

### OUTRO (10:30-12:00)

This process takes anywhere from 30 minutes to a few hours depending on the complexity of the event. If I can't spend that time on research, I don't place the position.

## Script 3: "The Information Edge: How to Find Data That Market Prices Don't Reflect"

**Target length:** 10-12 min
**Hook type:** Analytical framework
**Disclaimer:** Not financial advice.
**CTA:** Newsletter

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:45)

Prediction markets are only as accurate as the information that's been fed into them. That means if you can find credible information that most market participants haven't seen or haven't weighted correctly, you have an edge.

Finding that edge is a research skill. I'm going to break down where I look and how I evaluate whether information is actually being underweighted.

---

### SECTION 1 — What "Information Edge" Means (0:45-2:30)

An information edge doesn't mean you know something illegal or non-public. It means you've done more careful research than the average market participant, or you've found a data source that isn't widely cited in the mainstream coverage.

Markets aggregate the information available to the people trading them. Most people trading on Polymarket read the same news sources, follow the same analysts, and make the same assumptions. If you can read something they haven't read, or interpret something they've misread, the price may not reflect it yet.

This is not a guaranteed edge. Markets are often right. My research has led me to wrong conclusions. The goal is to be better calibrated on average, not to win every trade.

---

### SECTION 2 — Primary Sources Over Secondary (2:30-5:00)

The single most reliable edge I've found is reading primary sources instead of summaries.

When a court decision comes out, I read the actual ruling, not the CNN or Fox summary. The summaries carry editorial framing. The ruling has the actual legal language that determines what happens next.

When a central bank publishes meeting minutes, I read the full minutes, not the CNBC analysis. The full text often contains nuances about dissenting voices or conditional language that the summary strips out.

When a company files with the SEC, I read the 8-K or 10-Q directly. Corporate disclosures have specific legal implications. A press release is marketing. The filing is the document.

This sounds obvious. Most people don't do it because it takes more time. That time difference is the edge.

---

### SECTION 3 — Non-English Sources (5:00-7:00)

For events involving other countries, English-language media covers the story through an Anglophone lens. That lens has blind spots.

For a political event in a Latin American country, I read the major Spanish-language newspapers from that country, not Reuters or AP. The domestic framing of a political situation is often completely different from the international framing, and the domestic framing is more predictive of domestic outcomes.

For events in Europe, I use Google Translate and read the major papers in the relevant language. The translation quality is good enough to extract the key information.

The information asymmetry here is significant. Most English-speaking Polymarket traders are not reading Spanish, French, or Portuguese sources. If the domestic coverage is telling a materially different story from the international coverage, that gap often reflects a mispricing.

---

### SECTION 4 — Leading Indicators and Correlated Data (7:00-9:30)

For markets on economic indicators, the most reliable edge comes from understanding the inputs to the indicator before the indicator is released.

CPI is downstream of PPI, housing costs, and energy prices. If you track those inputs in the weeks before a CPI release, you can often predict the direction of the print more accurately than the market consensus.

Unemployment claims are a leading indicator for the monthly jobs report. If weekly claims have been trending one direction for several weeks, the monthly number is likely to follow.

This isn't guaranteed — the composition of the index, seasonal adjustments, and methodology changes can disrupt the relationship. But understanding the causal chain behind an indicator is more valuable than reading the news about it.

---

### SECTION 5 — Recognizing When You Don't Have an Edge (9:30-11:00)

This section is as important as the rest.

Most of the time, I do not have an edge. I read through a market, do the research, and conclude that the price looks right given what I know. That's the outcome the majority of the time.

The mistake is trading anyway because you feel like you should be doing something. That's how you lose capital on bad reasoning.

The discipline of the framework is saying: "I don't see anything the market has missed. I'm not trading this." And then actually not trading it.

---

### OUTRO (11:00-12:00)

I track which of my research conclusions were correct and which were wrong. Not to punish myself for errors, but to calibrate my own process over time. The cases where I thought I had an edge and was wrong are usually more instructive than the wins.

## Script 4: "Mistakes I Made on Prediction Markets and What I Learned"

**Target length:** 9-11 min
**Hook type:** Honest personal account
**Disclaimer:** Not financial advice.
**CTA:** Research framework

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:45)

I've made money on prediction markets and I've lost money on prediction markets. The losses taught me more.

I'm going to walk through four specific mistakes I made, what I was thinking when I made them, and what I changed afterward. None of this is investment advice. It's just the honest account of what went wrong.

---

### SECTION 1 — Trading Without Understanding the Resolution Criteria (0:45-2:30)

My first notable loss.

I saw a market on a political outcome I felt confident about. The price was 70 cents. I thought it should be closer to 85. I put money on it.

The event happened exactly as I predicted. I was right about the outcome. The market resolved at zero.

The reason: the resolution criteria specified a particular official declaration that never happened, even though the underlying event clearly occurred. The market was not on whether the event happened. It was on whether a specific official body made a specific statement. I didn't read that carefully enough.

I lost the position on a technicality that was plainly written in the resolution criteria that I chose not to read carefully.

The fix: I now read resolution criteria before I look at the price. Not after. Before.

---

### SECTION 2 — Overconcentration (2:30-4:30)

Second mistake: I put too much of my prediction market allocation into a single event.

The event had high volume, clear resolution criteria, and I had done genuine research. I was confident. So I sized up significantly beyond what my usual position limit would allow.

I was wrong about the event. I took a meaningful loss.

The lesson wasn't that my research was bad — my research process was sound. The lesson was that even sound research doesn't produce certainty. Prediction markets are probabilistic. A 75% confidence on a position still means a 25% chance of being wrong. If I size up dramatically on a 75% position, I'm just increasing the cost of being in that 25%.

I went back to fixed position sizing regardless of conviction level.

---

### SECTION 3 — Emotional Re-entry (4:30-6:30)

Third mistake: after a loss, I re-entered the same market at a worse price to "get it back."

This is a classic trading psychology error with a specific name: loss aversion-driven revenge trading. You're not making a research decision at that point. You're making an emotional one.

I re-entered a market I had just lost on, not because I found new information that changed my view, but because I felt like the market owed me a correction. The market doesn't owe anyone anything.

I lost the second position too.

The rule I made for myself: I do not re-enter a market within 48 hours of closing a losing position, and only if I can articulate a new piece of information that wasn't available when I placed the original trade.

---

### SECTION 4 — Thin Market Pricing (6:30-8:30)

Fourth mistake: trusting the price on a low-volume market.

The market had clear resolution criteria and covered an event I knew well. The price was 55 cents. My research suggested it should be closer to 80. I entered.

What I didn't account for: the volume was under $30,000. The price was 55 cents not because the crowd had analyzed it carefully, but because almost no one had traded it. A small amount of capital could move the price significantly in either direction.

After I entered, the price moved down to 40 cents — not because new information came out, but because someone else was exiting a position and there wasn't enough liquidity to absorb it without price impact.

The event resolved correctly for my position, so I eventually made money. But I held through significant unrealized loss because of a liquidity issue I should have noticed before entering.

Volume minimum: I now have one, and I check it before I look at anything else.

---

### OUTRO (8:30-10:30)

Four mistakes, four clear lessons. Read the resolution criteria. Size consistently. Don't trade on emotion. Check volume before you trust a price.

## Script 5: "Using Probabilistic Thinking in Your Business (Not Just Betting Markets)"

**Target length:** 8-10 min
**Hook type:** Framework transfer
**CTA:** Solopreneur OS or newsletter

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:45)

The most useful thing I've gotten from studying prediction markets has nothing to do with trading.

It's a way of thinking about uncertainty that I now apply to every business decision I make. How to estimate probabilities. How to update beliefs when new information arrives. How to make decisions without pretending you know what you don't know.

This video is about that framework, not about trading.

---

### SECTION 1 — The Problem With How Most People Handle Uncertainty (0:45-2:30)

Most people handle uncertainty in one of two ways: they ignore it entirely and assume the plan will work, or they get paralyzed by it and delay making a decision.

Both of these are bad.

Ignoring uncertainty means you don't build contingencies. When the inevitable variance happens, you have no plan B. Paralysis means you're treating "I don't know exactly what will happen" as a reason not to act, which is irrational because you'll almost never know exactly what will happen.

The better approach: put a number on it.

Not a precise number. A rough probability. "I think there's about a 60% chance this product sells 50 units in the first month." That number forces you to be explicit about your confidence level and gives you something to update when reality gives you data.

---

### SECTION 2 — How to Build a Probability Estimate (2:30-5:00)

When I'm evaluating a product idea, a content format, or a business bet, I go through four questions.

First: what's the base rate? How often does this type of thing work for someone in my position? If 1 in 5 Gumroad products in this category reaches 100 sales in the first 90 days, my prior is 20%.

Second: what do I know that's specific to this situation? Maybe I have an existing audience in this niche. Maybe I've validated the concept with direct feedback. Maybe the competition is thin. Each of these adjusts the number up or down.

Third: what would make me wrong? What's the most likely failure mode? If I can't articulate it clearly, I probably don't understand the situation well enough yet.

Fourth: if this fails, what's the cost? Some bets have low downside even if the probability is uncertain. Others have catastrophic downside. The probability estimate only matters in the context of the cost of being wrong.

---

### SECTION 3 — Updating Beliefs (5:00-7:00)

The hardest part of probabilistic thinking is not making the initial estimate. It's updating it when you get new information.

Most people anchor to their initial belief and explain away disconfirming data. The evidence that a product isn't working is rationalized as "it just needs more time." The evidence that a strategy is working is overstated.

Bayesian updating is the formal name for the right approach. When new evidence comes in, you revise your probability estimate based on how likely that evidence would be if your hypothesis were true versus false.

Informally: when something bad happens, honestly ask "does this change my estimate, and by how much?" Don't dismiss it. Don't catastrophize. Just update the number.

---

### SECTION 4 — Applied to Real Decisions (7:00-8:30)

Here are three decisions where I explicitly used this framework.

Whether to add a new product category: I estimated a 40% chance the audience would engage based on base rates for similar expansions. I ran a smaller test first — one product, minimal investment — to get data before committing fully.

Whether to pursue a new distribution channel: I estimated a 25% chance it would generate meaningful revenue in 90 days. Given the low cost of testing it, a 25% chance at a meaningful upside was worth one focused month.

Whether to continue a product that wasn't selling: After 60 days with no sales, I updated my estimate of this product's viability downward significantly. Not because 60 days is definitive, but because the absence of any traction in a category where my other products were moving was informative data.

---

### OUTRO (8:30-9:30)

The goal isn't to be certain. It's to be honest about how uncertain you are and to make decisions that reflect that honestly.
