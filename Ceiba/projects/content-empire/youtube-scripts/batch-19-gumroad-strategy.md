## Script 1: "I Listed 25 Digital Products on Gumroad. Here Is What Actually Sold."

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

I made a spreadsheet tracking every digital product I listed on Gumroad. Prices, traffic sources, conversion rates, what sold and when. I listed 25 products over about six weeks. Some made money the first week. Some made nothing despite real traffic. A few surprised me completely. I want to walk you through the data honestly, because the pattern it revealed was nothing like what the YouTube gurus told me to expect.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

Most advice about Gumroad tells you to build the best possible product and the sales will follow. Make it high quality, make it valuable, charge what it's worth. That advice is not wrong, but it's incomplete. I listed products that were genuinely useful, priced fairly, in categories with clear demand. Some of them made zero sales in the first month. Others sold within hours.

The difference was not quality. Quality is the floor, not the ceiling. The difference was how well the product matched what a specific person was already looking for, and how quickly they could understand that on the page.

**[THE HONEST DATA — 2:00 to 4:30]**

Here is what I found.

The products that sold fastest were the ones with the most specific titles. "Budget Calculator for Freelancers with Irregular Income" outperformed "Budget Spreadsheet Template" even though they were nearly identical products. Specificity signals that you understand who you're selling to. Vague titles make people wonder if it's for them.

The price point that converted best was $9.99. Not free, not $4.99, not $14.99. The $9.99 range hit something specific: cheap enough to be an impulse buy, expensive enough to feel like a real product. People will spend $9.99 on something they scroll past once if the title is right.

Free products got more downloads but fewer email subscribers than I expected. The free tier is a lead magnet, but only if your follow-up system is in place. Without a follow-up sequence, a free download is just a one-time interaction.

The products in the $14.99 to $19.99 range needed more traffic to convert. At those prices, people read the description carefully. The products I wrote detailed, specific, honest descriptions for sold. The ones with short descriptions didn't. Price and description depth are directly correlated in buyer behavior.

**[WHAT ACTUALLY DROVE SALES — 4:30 to 6:00]**

Traffic source mattered more than any other variable. Products that I mentioned directly in videos, in Instagram posts, or in a newsletter converted. Products I just listed and hoped people would find through search mostly sat.

The Gumroad marketplace does not send meaningful traffic unless you're already popular on the platform. Do not list a product and wait for Gumroad to sell it for you. It won't.

The products tied to a story sold better than the products tied to a feature list. When I wrote "I built this because I couldn't find anything that tracked variable income properly" in the description, it converted. When I wrote "This spreadsheet has 12 formulas and tracks 7 categories," it didn't move.

**[CALL TO ACTION — 6:00 to 7:00]**

The single best thing you can do when you're starting on Gumroad is not to perfect your products. It is to understand the difference between a product people find and a product people buy. Those are two different problems, and this video is about the second one.

## Script 2: "The $4.99 Product Strategy That Beats $99 Courses Every Time"

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

There's a creator I watched build a $10,000 month on Gumroad selling nothing above $9.99. No course. No coaching. No high-ticket offer. Just small, specific, immediately useful products. I thought he was leaving money on the table. Then I studied his numbers. He was outselling course creators who charged twenty times more per product.

This video is about why that happens and how to copy it.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

Everyone in the digital product space eventually tries to build a course. It makes sense on paper. More content, more value, higher price. A $99 course versus a $9.99 PDF seems like the obvious move. But the math doesn't work the way most people think.

A $99 course needs a warm audience. It needs trust built over months. It needs a sales page, testimonials, a launch, maybe ads. The barrier to buying $99 is real. People want to know you, want proof it works, and want to believe they'll actually finish it (they won't, but they need to believe they will).

A $4.99 product has none of those barriers. Someone scrolls past it on their phone, it answers the exact question they have right now, they tap buy. No research, no hesitation. The decision takes ten seconds.

**[THE MATH — 2:00 to 3:30]**

Here is where low-ticket pricing flips the whole game.

To make $1,000 with a $99 course, you need about 10 buyers. To make $1,000 with a $9.99 product, you need 100 buyers. Sounds like more work, right? But here is what the math misses.

Converting 10 people at $99 requires a warm audience, a real launch, and usually paid traffic. Converting 100 people at $9.99 requires good Gumroad SEO, one Instagram post, and a specific enough title that the right people find it.

The volume product builds you a buyer list faster. A hundred people who paid you $9.99 is more valuable than ten people who paid you $99, because those hundred people are more likely to buy from you again. The lifetime value math reverses.

**[THE STRATEGY — 3:30 to 5:30]**

Here is how to build a low-ticket product catalog that compounds.

Start with a specific answer to a specific question. Not "how to do email marketing" but "how to write a cold email when you have no social proof." The more specific the problem, the less competition and the higher the conversion rate.

Price at $4.99 to $9.99. This range maximizes impulse purchases. If the product is genuinely useful, you'll get reviews without asking. Good reviews on Gumroad compound into more sales over time.

Release products in batches, not one at a time. One product gets buried. Five products in the same niche create a catalog effect. Buyers who liked the first one look at the others. Your profile starts to look like a destination, not an accident.

Use your low-ticket products as auditions. The products that sell well tell you what the audience wants at a higher level. Once you know which topics convert, you can build a deeper product at $19.99 with confidence that the demand is real.

**[REAL EXAMPLE — 5:30 to 6:30]**

I listed an AI Prompt Engineering Cheat Sheet at $9.99. It's 25 copy-paste prompt templates, organized by category, with a before/after gallery and a one-page reference guide. Not a course. Not a video. A reference tool you keep open while you work.

That product took about three hours to build. It sells because it solves a specific frustration: people know AI is useful but they keep getting generic outputs. The product answers the exact question those people have at the exact moment they have it. The price is low enough that they don't overthink it.

## Script 3: "How to Write a Gumroad Description That Converts (With Real Examples)"

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

I rewrote one product description three times before I got it right. The first version had good traffic and almost no sales. The third version converted at four times the rate on the same amount of traffic. Same product. Same price. Same audience. Different words.

This video breaks down what I changed and why it worked.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

Most people write Gumroad descriptions the way they'd write a product spec. Features, formats, page counts, what's included. That information matters, but it's not what makes someone buy.

The problem is that your buyer is not in rational mode when they find your product. They're scrolling. Something caught their eye. Now they have three seconds to decide if they care enough to read further. If the first two sentences of your description sound like a brochure, they leave.

The second problem is that creators write about the product instead of writing about the buyer. "This guide covers 8 chapters on AI security" tells me nothing about myself. "You're probably using AI tools without knowing how exposed you are. This guide fixes that in a weekend" puts me in the story.

**[THE FRAMEWORK — 2:00 to 4:30]**

Here is a structure that works for almost any digital product description.

First sentence: name the pain or the frustration. Not "this guide covers X." Something like: "Most people using AI tools have no idea how much data they're leaking every day." That sentence makes the right reader stop.

Second paragraph: show that you understand the situation. Tell them what they're probably doing right now and why it's not working. This is where you earn credibility, not by listing your credentials but by accurately describing their experience.

Third paragraph: introduce the product as the answer, not as a collection of features. "This guide turns that fear into a clear action plan." The product is a bridge from their current state to the state they want. Write it that way.

Fourth section: the feature list. Now you earn the right to list features because the buyer already cares. Format this as a bulleted list. Keep each point short. One line per item.

Fifth section: who it's for. Be specific. "Freelancers who use ChatGPT daily but have never thought about what data they're sending" is better than "anyone interested in AI security." Specific targeting makes the right buyer feel seen and the wrong buyer self-select out.

Sixth section: FAQ. Anticipate the three questions that would stop someone from buying. Answer them honestly. Including objections builds trust. Hiding them destroys it.

**[BEFORE AND AFTER — 4:30 to 6:30]**

Here is the before version of one of my descriptions:

"AI Security Guide. 8 chapters covering prompt injection, data security, API key management, and agent permission frameworks. PDF format, 40 pages. Works with all major AI platforms."

Here is the after version:

"The fear is real. Prompt injection. Data leaks. Unauthorized API spending. Agents doing things you never asked them to do. Every week there's a new headline about AI security failures, and most people using AI tools have no idea how exposed they are. Their API keys sit in plaintext. Their prompts contain sensitive business data. Their agents run with zero guardrails. This guide turns that fear into confidence."

Same product. The after version converts because it puts you inside the reader's head before it asks you to buy anything.

## Script 4: "Why Your Gumroad Page Gets Traffic but No Sales (and How to Fix It)"

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

Someone I know had 800 visits to their Gumroad page last month. They made three sales. That's a 0.4% conversion rate on a $7 product. The problem was not the product. The problem was not the price. I read their page in sixty seconds and found four specific things that were killing the conversion. This video covers every one of them.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

Traffic without sales is a specific problem. It means the audience exists, they found you, and they left without buying. That gap between visiting and buying is almost always caused by one of a small number of fixable mistakes.

Most creators diagnose this wrong. They lower the price (wrong), they add more content to the product (wrong), or they try to get more traffic hoping the math improves (wrong). None of those fix the real issue. The real issue is on the page itself.

**[THE 4 KILLERS — 2:00 to 5:30]**

Killer one: a headline that describes the product instead of the outcome. "Social Media Content Templates" describes the product. "Post Every Day Without Running Out of Ideas" describes what the buyer gets. When someone arrives at your page, they're not thinking about what the product is. They're thinking about what their life looks like after buying it. Your headline needs to speak to the after, not the what.

Killer two: a description that leads with features. If the first thing someone reads is a bullet list of what's included, you've lost them. They don't know if they care about the product yet. Lead with the problem. Make them feel understood before you sell them anything.

Killer three: no social proof at any price point. At $7, buyers still want to know it's not garbage. A single review, a quote from one satisfied buyer, or even a specific "built by someone who actually uses this" story builds enough trust to push someone over the edge. No social proof makes the page feel like a stranger asking for money.

Killer four: a call to action that's unclear or buried. Gumroad has a buy button, but your description needs to end with a reason to click it right now. Not "buy if you want." Something like: "If you've been spending two hours a week manually creating content, this template pack will give that time back starting today." Specific, immediate, relevant.

**[THE FIX — 5:30 to 6:30]**

Audit your page with these four questions. Does the headline describe the outcome? Does the description lead with the problem? Does the page include any real social proof? Does it end with a specific reason to buy now?

Fix the ones that are broken. You do not need to rewrite everything. Usually one or two of these is the main leak. Fix those, keep the traffic the same, and watch the conversion rate change.

## Script 5: "Building a Digital Product Catalog: From Zero to 25 Products in 6 Weeks"

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

Week one I had zero products. Week six I had twenty-five. Not twenty-five half-finished ideas. Twenty-five actual products listed on Gumroad, with descriptions, covers, and prices. I want to tell you exactly how I did that without burning out, and more importantly, which ones I'd skip if I were starting over.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

Most creators spend three months building one product. They overthink it, polish it endlessly, price it, list it, and then discover that building one product teaches you almost nothing about what the market actually wants. You've spent your entire learning budget on one bet.

The other problem is perfectionism disguised as quality. "I want to make sure this is really good" is often "I'm scared of putting something out and having people not buy it." A product you never ship helps no one and earns nothing.

The approach I took was the opposite. Ship something every two to three days. Learn fast. Remove what doesn't sell. Double down on what does.

**[THE SYSTEM — 2:00 to 4:30]**

Here is the system I used.

Idea generation: two sessions per week, thirty minutes each. I asked one question: what do I know that took me more than five hours to learn, that I could explain to someone else in under an hour? Every answer is a potential product. I kept the list in a Notion database with a column for effort (low, medium, high) and a column for demand (confirmed, guessed).

Product creation: I set a two-hour cap per product for the first batch. Not because quality doesn't matter, but because speed of learning matters more than polish at volume zero. A product that ships in two hours and gets three sales tells you more than a product that takes a week and gets zero.

Format selection: I matched the format to the time cap. Spreadsheets: two hours or less. PDF guides: two to three hours with a clear outline. Cheat sheets: ninety minutes. Templates: one hour per template. Video courses were out of scope for this batch.

Design: I used Canva for all covers. One template, one layout, consistent brand colors. Every cover took fifteen minutes. Not because design doesn't matter, but because good-enough covers do not lose sales. Bad descriptions do.

Listing: I listed every product on Monday morning. One batch per week. Five products at a time. Each listing took about thirty minutes including the description.

**[WHAT I LEARNED — 4:30 to 6:30]**

The products in the personal productivity and business tools category outsold everything else by a wide margin. People buy things that solve a daily friction. The more specifically the product title named the friction, the better it converted.

The products I was most proud of creatively (the ones where I spent the most time and thought) were not the ones that sold best. The products that were purely utilitarian, a spreadsheet, a checklist, a set of templates, sold immediately and consistently.

The twenty-fifth product taught me something I could not have learned building one product over three months. Building fast creates compounding feedback. You start to see the pattern.

If I were doing it over, I would skip the broader, more conceptual guides in the first batch. Start with tools. Things people open every day. Templates. Calculators. Checklists. You can build the conceptual content later once you have trust and an audience.
