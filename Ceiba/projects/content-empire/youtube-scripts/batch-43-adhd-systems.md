## Script 1: How I Manage ADHD as an Engineering Student and Entrepreneur

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I was diagnosed with ADHD and I'm currently doing a computer engineering degree while building a business. I'm not going to pretend I have it figured out. But I have a system that works most of the time, and it's nothing like the productivity advice you'll find in generic content. Here's what actually works for me.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I want to be honest about what ADHD management actually looks like from the inside. Not the cleaned-up version where I explain a system that sounds simple and polished. The actual version where I describe what fails, what I've replaced it with, and why.

The reason I'm making this video is that most productivity content is built for neurotypical people who just need a reminder to focus. When you have ADHD, the problem is not that you forgot to focus. The problem is that the brain's internal regulation system doesn't reliably engage with things based on importance. It engages based on interest, urgency, novelty, and challenge. Any system that ignores that will fail.

**Section 1 -- What I stopped doing (1:30-3:30):**
I stopped using a traditional to-do list.

A traditional to-do list is an undifferentiated pile of obligations. When everything is on the list, nothing has priority that the brain cares about. I would look at my list, feel overwhelmed, and close the app. Zero productivity.

I also stopped trying to work in the morning because I read that successful people are morning people. I'm not a morning person. My focus peaks in the afternoon and early evening. Fighting my own biology to feel disciplined was costing me two to three hours of actual output per day.

I stopped trying to maintain a perfect system. Every productivity influencer has a system with seventeen steps and a weekly review and a daily shutdown ritual. I tried three of them. I consistently failed the weekly review by week two. The failure made me feel worse than having no system at all.

What I replaced these with: a short daily focus block, one clear objective per day, and a capture tool that doesn't require decisions.

**Section 2 -- The one objective per day rule (3:30-5:30):**
Every day has one primary objective. One thing that, if I do nothing else, counts as a productive day.

Not a task list. One thing.

This is counterintuitive. Shouldn't I be doing more. The logic behind it is that with ADHD, I often have more ideas and intentions than I can execute. The bottleneck is not generating action items. It's completing them. By committing to one objective per day, I increase the probability of completion from roughly 30 percent (when I had six objectives) to roughly 80 percent.

The objective has to be specific enough that I know when it's done. "Work on the product" is not an objective. "Write the first two sections of the pricing guide" is an objective. The specificity is doing most of the work.

I write this the night before. When I wake up, the decision is already made. I don't have to negotiate with my brain about what to start. That negotiation process is where a lot of ADHD productive time gets lost.

**Section 3 -- The capture system (5:30-7:30):**
ADHD brains generate ideas constantly. At inappropriate times. During class, during sleep, during conversations. If I don't capture an idea immediately, it's gone within minutes.

My capture system is a Telegram bot called BehiqueBot that I built myself. I send it voice notes or text whenever an idea hits. It transcribes the voice note, classifies the idea by category, and stores it. No organization required on my end. The bot handles that.

Why a bot and not a notes app. Because the notes app requires me to open it, navigate to the right folder, and type. That's three decisions between having the idea and capturing it. Three decisions is too many when the idea is competing with whatever else is happening. A Telegram message is one action. Send.

The ideas get reviewed in a weekly session, not immediately. The capture is not a commitment to act. It's a commitment to not lose. That distinction matters because it removes the anxiety of "I need to do something about this" that would otherwise attach to every captured idea.

**Section 4 -- Managing the engineering degree (7:30-9:30):**
School and building a business compete for the same attention resource. I've had to be honest about which one gets priority at different times in the semester.

During mid-terms and finals: school takes priority. The business gets maintenance level effort, not growth effort. Accepting this has reduced my guilt about not doing enough significantly.

During regular weeks: I treat my two most engaging courses as automatic focus material (ADHD brains hyper-focus on interesting things) and the less engaging courses as the ones that need deliberate scheduling. I put those in my calendar with specific study blocks, not open-ended "study time."

For assignments, I use a single rule: start any assignment the day it's assigned. Not to finish it. Just to start it. Opening a document and writing the title counts. The goal is to avoid the zero-momentum problem where an assignment exists but has never been touched and the blank page creates extra resistance.

**Section 5 -- What I tell people who are just diagnosed (9:30-11:30):**
Getting the diagnosis was useful mostly because it explained patterns I'd been experiencing since childhood. But the diagnosis itself doesn't change anything. What changes things is building systems that work with your brain instead of against it.

The most important thing I'd tell someone newly diagnosed is to stop trying to adopt someone else's system. Every productivity system you find online was built by someone without ADHD, for people without ADHD. Some principles transfer. Most of the implementation doesn't.

Build your own. Start with the smallest possible unit of structure. One rule, not ten. Test it for two weeks. If it holds, add one more. If it doesn't, change it, not yourself.

Working with a psychologist on this stuff changed things more than any app or framework. Not just for diagnosis but for building strategies that account for how my brain specifically works. That's the investment I'd recommend over any productivity software.

## Script 2: The Only Productivity System That Works With ADHD (Not Against It)

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Every productivity system I've ever tried was built on one assumption: that you can choose to start working on something whenever you decide to. For people with ADHD, that assumption is false. Here's the system I built around how my brain actually works.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I want to explain why standard productivity frameworks fail for ADHD and then walk you through the specific system I'm using now, including the parts that are still not perfect.

The failure point in most systems is not the tools or the tactics. It's that they treat motivation as a decision. "Decide to work. Open the task. Work." With ADHD, that chain breaks at the first link. Motivation is not reliably a decision. It's a neurological response. You can't just decide to care about a report that your brain categorizes as boring regardless of how important it is.

Any system that requires you to muster motivation as a starting condition will fail on ADHD consistently.

**Section 1 -- The four ADHD engagement drivers (1:30-3:30):**
Before I describe the system, you need to understand what actually gets an ADHD brain to engage. There are four things: interest, urgency, novelty, and challenge.

If a task has at least one of these, there's a reasonable chance of getting started. If it has none of them, it's almost impossible regardless of importance.

Interest is obvious. If you care about the topic, you'll do it.

Urgency is the reason ADHD people are often productive under deadlines. The deadline creates an urgency signal that the brain responds to when nothing else worked.

Novelty means the task feels new. The first time you do something, it's easier than the fifth time, because the first time has novelty and the fifth time doesn't.

Challenge is why complex tasks can sometimes be easier to start than simple ones. A simple task like filing a form doesn't engage the brain. A hard problem can trigger a hyper-focus state.

A system that works with ADHD uses these four drivers instead of ignoring them.

**Section 2 -- How I structure my day around the drivers (3:30-6:00):**
I don't schedule tasks by time of day. I schedule tasks by driver.

High-interest tasks go in the first slot when my brain is freshest because they don't need as much energy to start. I just start them.

High-urgency tasks go in dedicated blocks with manufactured deadlines. I set a timer for 45 minutes and tell myself the task has to be in a submittable state when the timer goes off, even if it's not perfect. The timer creates urgency. The "submittable state" rule creates a concrete finish line.

Novel tasks go in my lowest energy slot because novelty carries its own energy. The newness of the task compensates for lower baseline focus.

Challenge tasks are the ones I'm most likely to hyper-focus on, so I put them in the middle of the day when I have enough cognitive energy to sustain the focus state and enough time to let it run.

This restructures my workday from "what should I do at 9am" to "what type of task fits this brain state at this moment."

**Section 3 -- The weekly planning structure (6:00-8:00):**
Every Sunday I do a ten-minute planning session. Not a two-hour weekly review. Ten minutes.

I ask three questions. What is the one thing that, if done, would make this week successful. What are the three things that have to happen for external commitments (school deadlines, client deliverables). What is one thing I want to make progress on even if I can't finish it.

That's the plan. One priority. Three obligations. One progress item.

I write these on a piece of paper, not in an app. The physical paper stays on my desk. I see it every day. The low friction of glancing at a piece of paper beats opening an app by enough margin that I actually check the plan daily.

The three obligations get added to my calendar as time blocks with specific durations. Not "work on assignment" but "write the intro section of the networks lab report, 45 minutes." Specific, bounded, single-task.

**Section 4 -- What to do when the system fails (8:00-9:30):**
The system fails. I want to be honest about this. There are days when even the best-designed system doesn't work because the underlying state isn't right. Low sleep, high stress, a bad day in any area of life, all of these override the system.

When that happens, I use a two-step recovery.

Step one: identify the minimum. What is the absolute smallest version of productivity that counts as something. For me this is usually ten minutes on the one priority task, written the night before so I don't have to decide under low-function conditions. Ten minutes is almost always achievable. Doing ten minutes often triggers a longer session. But even if it doesn't, the day has something.

Step two: do not punish the failure. Cataloging what went wrong and making yourself feel bad about it reduces the probability that tomorrow will go better. The failure is information. "I was at low sleep and high stress and couldn't execute." Note that and move on.

The self-punishment spiral is a productivity killer specific to ADHD. The brain is already harder to regulate. Adding negative emotional load makes regulation harder.

## Script 3: Why ADHD Is Actually an Advantage in Business

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I'm going to make an argument that ADHD is a competitive advantage in business, but I want to do it without the toxic positivity version of this conversation. No "your ADHD is a superpower." Real specific advantages with real specific contexts where they apply.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
There's a version of "ADHD is an advantage" content that's just reassurance. I don't find that useful. If the only thing I take from a video is "you're special, not broken," I've learned nothing I can actually use.

What I want to do instead is identify the specific characteristics of ADHD that create real advantages in specific business contexts, and also be honest about where the same characteristics create problems. Because the same trait that helps in one context will hurt you in another.

**Section 1 -- Hyper-focus as a competitive asset (1:30-3:30):**
People with ADHD don't have a focus deficit. They have irregular focus. When the brain locks onto something interesting, the focus is often more intense and sustained than what most people experience. This is called hyper-focus.

In a business context, hyper-focus is an asset for anything that rewards deep output in a short period of time. Building a new tool, writing a long-form guide, designing a system, debugging a problem. I've produced more output in a four-hour hyper-focus session than I'd produce in two days of conventional work.

The trap is trying to schedule hyper-focus. You can't. What you can do is create the conditions that make it more likely: remove interruptions, work on something novel or challenging, start at a time of day when your baseline focus is higher.

The competitive advantage is that most people work in short interrupted bursts because that's what their environment allows. When I hit a hyper-focus state, I'm producing at a rate that isn't comparable. That asymmetry is real.

**Section 2 -- Pattern recognition and trend spotting (3:30-5:30):**
ADHD brains process information differently. There's more lateral thinking, more connection-drawing between unrelated ideas, and more sensitivity to patterns.

In product research, this shows up as an ability to see a trend forming before it becomes obvious. I notice when the same type of question appears in multiple different communities. I notice when a product shows up in an unrelated context in a way that signals broader cultural traction.

This isn't a mystical ability. It's a processing style. The ADHD brain isn't filtering out as much peripheral information as a neurotypical brain might. That peripheral information is sometimes exactly where trends start.

For content creation, this same trait means I can find angles on common topics that feel fresh. Not because I'm more creative, but because I'm connecting more dots. A video about pricing a digital product draws from what I've seen in eBay listings, what I've read in Reddit threads, what I've tested personally. The connection is the content.

**Section 3 -- Tolerance for iteration and failure (5:30-7:30):**
ADHD is associated with impulsivity, which is usually framed as a problem. In business, controlled impulsivity is an execution advantage.

The fear of failure that prevents most people from shipping their first product, running their first ad, posting their first piece of content, is reduced when your brain naturally moves past problems and onto the next thing faster. I don't sit with a failure as long as I might otherwise. The brain is already onto the next experiment.

This creates a faster iteration cycle. More experiments per unit of time means more data, more learning, and faster convergence on what works.

The caveat: this only holds if you're capturing what you learn. ADHD working memory is weaker, meaning lessons can be lost as fast as they're gained. Documented learning from fast iteration is the asset. Undocumented fast iteration is just chaos.

**Section 4 -- Where ADHD becomes a liability in business (7:30-9:30):**
I said I was going to be honest. Here are the specific ways ADHD creates real problems in business contexts.

Consistency. ADHD makes it hard to do the same thing repeatedly without novelty. Posting content on a consistent schedule. Following up on leads systematically. Doing monthly account reviews. These recurring tasks require systems that remove dependence on motivation, because motivation for repetitive tasks doesn't reliably appear.

Long-term planning. ADHD is present-focused by nature. Three-year goals feel less real than today's problem. Strategic planning requires deliberately working against this tendency.

Paperwork and administration. Anything involving forms, compliance, financial record-keeping. These tasks have no novelty, no urgency until they're overdue, and often no intrinsic interest. They're the category where I've made the most costly mistakes.

Understanding these specific liabilities means I can build around them. Systems, accountability partners, and automations in exactly these areas.

**Section 5 -- The actual advantage framing (9:30-11:00):**
The advantage isn't ADHD itself. The advantage is understanding your own processing style deeply enough to build a work environment that uses the strengths and protects against the weaknesses.

That's not a uniquely ADHD thing. Everyone has a processing style. The difference is that people with ADHD have usually failed enough conventional systems that they've been forced to think more deliberately about this question.

That forced self-knowledge is the real edge.

## Script 4: The 3-Tool Stack I Use to Remember Everything

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
My working memory is genuinely unreliable. I'll have an idea in the shower and it will be gone before I'm dressed. I'll leave a meeting with three action items and remember one. This is not a character flaw. It's how ADHD works. Here's the three-tool stack I've built to compensate for it.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I want to give you a specific system, not principles. The tools I'm going to describe are the ones I actually use, why I chose each one over alternatives, and how they connect to each other.

The goal of this stack is not to remember everything with willpower. It's to create a system where anything worth remembering gets captured immediately, organized automatically, and retrieved when I need it without requiring me to remember that it exists. That last part is the hard part. A system that requires you to remember to check it doesn't solve the memory problem.

**Section 1 -- Tool 1: BehiqueBot (Telegram) for capture (1:30-4:00):**
BehiqueBot is a Telegram bot I built. When I have an idea, I send it a text or voice message from my phone. The bot transcribes voice notes using Whisper, classifies the idea by category, and saves it. I never need to open a notes app. I never need to decide where something goes. I just send the message.

The reason I chose to build this instead of using an existing notes app: every notes app I've tried requires too many decisions at capture time. What notebook does this go in. What tags. Is this a task or an idea. Those decisions create friction. Friction kills capture. Uncaptured ideas are lost.

With BehiqueBot, there are no decisions. Send the message. Done. The classification happens automatically. I can review and organize later if I want to, but I don't have to.

The categories are simple: creative, business, technical, personal, knowledge. One of those applies to everything. The bot picks one. If it picks wrong, I correct it in the weekly review. But it picks right about 80 percent of the time based on context.

The key behavior change this enabled: I capture everything now. Things I used to dismiss as "I'll remember that" I now immediately send to the bot. The capture cost is five seconds. The expected value of capturing versus not capturing is significant over time.

**Section 2 -- Tool 2: Obsidian for reference and project notes (4:00-6:30):**
Obsidian is where structured information lives. Not raw captures. Not to-do lists. Project notes, research summaries, frameworks, and reference documents I need to retrieve.

I use Obsidian because it stores files as plain markdown on my local machine. No subscription required to access my own notes. No third-party server that could disappear. The files are mine.

My Obsidian structure has five folders: projects, reference, resources, daily notes, and archive. I don't use tags. Tags require remembering what tags exist. Folders require knowing a broad category, which is easier.

The daily note is the only thing I write in Obsidian daily. It has three sections: the one priority for today, notes from any meetings or calls, and a link to any documents I created or worked on. This gives me a trail. If I need to find something from three weeks ago, I go to the daily note from that date and find the link.

I do not use Obsidian as a to-do list. To-do lists in note apps become archives. Things sit there forever and the list loses meaning. Obsidian is for reference, not task management.

**Section 3 -- Tool 3: physical calendar for commitments (6:30-8:30):**
I use a physical calendar for deadlines and commitments. Not a digital calendar. A paper wall calendar where I write things in pen.

This might seem like a step backwards given I just described a bot and a piece of software. The reason I use paper is that digital calendars exist in an app. An app can be ignored, swiped away, or forgotten. A paper calendar on my wall exists in physical space. I see it every time I sit down at my desk.

The wall calendar is not for tasks. It's for externally visible commitments: school assignment due dates, product launch targets, meetings, appointments. If something has a real-world deadline, it goes on the wall calendar in ink.

The commitment to writing on a physical calendar also creates a small friction that prevents me from overcommitting. Digital calendar events are easy to add and easy to ignore. Writing in pen feels more binding.

**Section 4 -- How the three tools connect (8:30-10:00):**
The tools have specific jobs and they don't overlap.

BehiqueBot captures everything. No organization at capture time.

The weekly review session, which I do Sunday for ten minutes, is when I move things from the bot's captured ideas into Obsidian if they're worth developing. Most ideas stay in the bot archive and never graduate to Obsidian. That's fine. The capture was still worth doing.

The wall calendar gets updated whenever I add a project to Obsidian that has a target completion date. The link between a project note in Obsidian and a date on the wall calendar creates accountability without requiring me to check the project note to know the deadline.

The three tools handle three different problems: capture, organization, and accountability. Using one tool for all three creates a bloated system that fails at all three.

## Script 5: Building a Business With a Non-Linear Brain: What Changed for Me

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I spent about a year trying to build a business the way productivity content told me to. Linear goals. Structured weeks. Monthly milestones. It mostly didn't work. Not because I wasn't trying. Because the model was built for a linear brain and mine isn't. Here's what changed when I stopped fighting that.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
This is less of a how-to video and more of a story. I want to walk through what my approach to building looked like when I was fighting my brain, and what it looks like now that I've mostly stopped.

I'm not claiming I've found the answer. I'm claiming I've found a better fit. There's a difference. The better fit is still hard. It still fails sometimes. But it fails less often and recovers faster.

**Section 1 -- What linear building looked like for me (1:30-3:30):**
When I was operating under conventional productivity advice, my weeks looked something like this. Sunday: review goals, plan the week, assign tasks to days. Monday through Friday: work through the task list. End of week: review, feel bad about what I didn't do, adjust the plan for next week.

The problem was the gap between the plan and what actually happened. Monday would go fine because the plan was fresh and I was motivated. By Wednesday, one thing would run long or something unexpected would come up, and the rest of the week would cascade. By Friday, I'd completed maybe 40 percent of what I planned and spent a significant amount of energy feeling like I was failing.

The failure was real but the cause was wrong. I was failing the plan, not the goal. The plan assumed linear, predictable progress. My progress is not linear or predictable. So I kept having weeks that looked like failures when I was actually making real progress, just not in the order or at the pace I'd projected.

**Section 2 -- The shift: parallel tracks instead of linear sequences (3:30-5:30):**
I stopped thinking about my work as a linear path and started thinking of it as parallel tracks.

I have several projects running at different stages at any given time. eBay research and listing. Digital product development. Content creation. BehiqueBot development. Engineering coursework.

Instead of assigning time blocks to each one and trying to work through them sequentially, I work on whatever has the highest engagement pull at that moment, with one constraint: each track has to make at least minimum progress each week. Minimum progress is defined as a specific, small thing. Not zero, but not significant either.

This works because it matches how my brain actually allocates energy. Some weeks I'm hyper-focused on the product business and barely touch the eBay side. Some weeks the engineering coursework has a challenging problem that absorbs most of my attention. Some weeks I'll batch six pieces of content in two days because I'm in a creative state.

The parallel track model accommodates this naturally. The minimum progress rule prevents any single track from dying while I'm focused elsewhere.

**Section 3 -- What systems actually look like for a non-linear builder (5:30-7:30):**
The systems I've built are designed to be resumable, not continuous. I can pick up any track after a few days away without significant ramp-up time because the system holds state.

What holds state: the daily note in Obsidian. The last thing I did before leaving a project, and the exact next action, is documented. When I come back, I don't have to reconstruct where I was. I just read the last note and start.

The project file for each major initiative has a breadcrumbs section. Not a full status document. Just the last three decisions or actions and what's next. Enough context to resume without enough overhead to maintain.

For the ADHD brain specifically, the hardest part of switching between projects is the ramp-up cost. If ramp-up requires reconstructing context from memory, it can take 20 to 30 minutes per switch. If it requires reading three bullet points, it takes two minutes. The documentation investment pays back on every resume.

**Section 4 -- What working with a psychologist added (7:30-9:30):**
I want to be specific about this because it's the part most content about productivity and ADHD ignores.

The systems I described work better because I was building them with a psychologist who specializes in ADHD. Not because she gave me better systems. Because working through the psychological components of why certain approaches triggered avoidance and others didn't gave me much better data to work with.

The avoidance patterns, the self-punishment cycles, the perfectionism that prevented starting until conditions were perfect. Understanding why those patterns existed made it possible to build around them instead of just trying to override them with willpower.

Willpower is not infinite. Building systems that reduce the demand on willpower is. That shift in framing came directly from the psychological work.

**Section 5 -- What I think matters most looking back (9:30-11:30):**
The thing that changed most significantly was not finding the right tool or the right framework. It was accepting that my building process looks different from what most people describe and that different does not mean worse.

I ship products in batches after creative sprints, not steadily every week. I do focused research in one or two day intensive sessions, not thirty minutes every day. I write content when the ideas are running, sometimes five pieces in a week, sometimes zero for two weeks.

The output, measured over a month or a quarter, is comparable or better to what I would produce trying to maintain a steady rate. But the within-week variance is high. Learning to read that variance instead of fighting it changed what the work actually felt like.

The consistent things in my system are the minimum progress requirements per track, the capture habit, and the weekly ten-minute planning session. Everything else bends to the brain state.
