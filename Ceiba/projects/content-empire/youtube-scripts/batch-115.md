## SCRIPT 1: Twitter Growth in 2026 (Algorithm Deep Dive)

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

Twitter, now X, changed its algorithm significantly in 2025 and most of the growth advice you've read hasn't caught up.

The tactics that drove follower growth in 2022 are now getting accounts suppressed. The accounts growing fastest in 2026 are doing something fundamentally different.

I'm going to show you what actually works now.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

I've been studying high-growth accounts on X for the last year, testing what actually drives reach and follower acquisition in 2026, and the picture is clear enough now to share.

Here's what we're covering today:

- How the X algorithm distributes content in 2026
- The post formats that get the most reach
- The account structure that signals authority to the algorithm
- The engagement patterns that work and the ones that backfire
- Building a Twitter presence that feeds your business, not just your follower count

This is a tactical deep dive. Let's get into it.

---

**[SECTION 1: How the X Algorithm Works in 2026 — 1:30-4:30]**

X's recommendation system changed substantially after the 2023 open-source algorithm release and has continued to evolve. The core signals driving distribution are: engagement rate relative to impressions, bookmarks (heavily weighted), replies (especially from high-follower accounts), and time spent on post (which they measure through link click behavior and scroll pause time).

What changed: follower count matters less than it used to. A new account with 200 followers can go viral if the engagement rate on a post is high enough. This is the democratization element.

What stayed the same: consistency signals. Accounts that post daily accumulate an engagement history that the algorithm uses to decide how much initial distribution to give new posts. An account that's been consistently active for 90 days will get more initial exposure on a new post than an account that posts sporadically.

What changed in 2025-2026: the algorithm now significantly weights "saves to notes" or bookmarks as a proxy for genuine value. Likes are cheap and the algorithm knows it. When someone bookmarks a post, they intend to return to it. That's a strong quality signal.

The practical implication: write posts that people want to save, not just like. How-to content, resource lists, frameworks, and condensed guides get saved. Hot takes and opinions get liked. If your goal is reach, optimize for saves.

---

**[SECTION 2: Post Formats That Get the Most Reach — 4:30-7:30]**

Six post formats consistently outperform in 2026.

**Format 1: The framework post.** A concise, numbered list of principles or steps. Easy to scan, delivers clear value, highly bookmarkable. "The 5-step system I use to script a YouTube video in under an hour" with the actual steps performs reliably.

**Format 2: The pattern interrupt observation.** You notice something counterintuitive about your niche that most people haven't named yet. "Everyone tells you to post daily. The accounts growing fastest in 2026 are posting 3-5 times per week with significantly better content." This format generates replies from people who agree, disagree, or have nuance to add.

**Format 3: The case study thread.** Walk through a real result with specific numbers. Not "I grew a newsletter." Instead: "I grew a newsletter from 0 to 800 subscribers in 60 days with no ad spend. Here's the exact method, step by step." Threads still perform well when the first tweet earns the click to expand.

**Format 4: The honest take.** A direct opinion on something your audience cares about, stated without hedging. The more precisely you name a frustration your audience has, the more it resonates. This is the Dan Koe model. Lead with the truth that most people know but won't say.

**Format 5: The resource drop.** "Here are 7 tools I use to run a one-person content business. All free or under $20/month." Tag each tool. These get shared by the tools themselves sometimes, and they're highly bookmarkable.

**Format 6: The reply strategy.** Replying substantively to high-follower accounts in your niche is a genuine growth lever. A thoughtful reply to a post with 10,000 impressions will be seen by those same people. If your reply is better than the original post, you'll gain followers from it. This is underused.

---

**[SECTION 3: Account Structure That Signals Authority — 7:30-9:30]**

Your profile is a landing page. Most accounts optimize it poorly.

Your bio needs to do four things: say exactly who you help, how you help them, what they get from following you, and include a credibility signal if you have one.

Bad bio: "Building in public. Sharing thoughts on business and life."

Better bio: "I help solopreneurs build digital product businesses. Daily systems and frameworks. Get my free content OS: [link]."

Your pinned post should be your best performing content. Not an introduction post. Not a "welcome to my page" post. Your best framework, your most compelling case study, or your most useful resource list.

The header image should either be your product, your newsletter, or a visual that reinforces what you do. If you can put a URL in the header, do it.

Profile picture should be a clear headshot on a simple background. Not a logo. Not an AI avatar. A real face that people can recognize.

The first impression your profile creates determines whether a curious new visitor follows you or keeps scrolling.

---

**[SECTION 4: Building a Twitter Presence That Feeds Your Business — 9:30-11:30]**

Twitter reach without a business destination is a vanity metric.

Every 7-10 posts, include a post that drives traffic to your newsletter, product, or content. Not every post, or you'll kill your organic reach. But consistently enough that your growing audience has a clear path to going deeper.

The highest-converting Twitter CTA is the link in bio. "More in my newsletter" or "full breakdown linked in bio" converts better than a direct product link in most posts, because it doesn't feel like an ad.

Build a follow-up system for new followers who engage with your best posts. If someone bookmarks a post or replies with a genuine question, that's a signal of high interest. If your DMs are open, some of those people will reach out. Respond to every DM in the first 90 days of building your account. Those conversations turn into your first buyers.

The metric to track: new email subscribers from Twitter per week. Not follower count. Not impressions. Subscribers, because those are people who took an action that moves them toward your business.

---

**[OUTRO — 11:30-12:30]**

Twitter in 2026 rewards accounts that deliver specific, condensed value to a specific audience and do it consistently.

Optimize for bookmarks, not likes. Post formats that teach, not just opinions. Build a profile that converts visitors into followers. Drive traffic to your email list.

## SCRIPT 2: The Email Sequence That Converts YouTube Viewers

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

You get a new email subscriber from YouTube. What happens next?

For most creators, the answer is: not much. The subscriber gets a welcome email, maybe a few random newsletters, and then either stays on the list passively or unsubscribes.

The creators making real money from email have a different answer. They have a sequence designed to move subscribers from curious to converted, and it works while the creator is making content or sleeping.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

An email sequence is not a sales funnel in the manipulative sense. It's a structured introduction to what you know, what you offer, and why it matters.

Done right, it builds trust faster than any other format because it's one-to-one. The subscriber reads it alone. Your voice, their inbox.

Today I'm breaking down the 7-email sequence that converts YouTube viewers into buyers. Every email, what it says, and why it's structured that way.

---

**[SECTION 1: Email 1 — The Welcome and the Promise — 1:30-3:30]**

Email 1 goes out immediately after signup. The subscriber is at peak interest right now. Don't waste it.

This email does four things. First, it delivers whatever you promised on the signup form (the template, the guide, the resource). Second, it sets expectations for what the subscriber will receive. Third, it asks a question that invites a reply. Fourth, it reinforces why following you is worth their time.

The question is the most important element. "What's the one thing you're trying to figure out right now about [your topic]?" When subscribers reply, you learn exactly what they need. When they reply, your deliverability improves because Gmail learns they want your emails.

Keep Email 1 under 300 words. It should feel like a personal note, not a brand communication.

---

**[SECTION 2: Emails 2 and 3 — Building Trust — 3:30-5:30]**

Emails 2 and 3, sent on days 2 and 4, are pure value delivery. No product mention. No CTA except "reply if this resonated."

Email 2 should teach something specific and useful related to the topic they signed up for. A framework, a process, or a mistake to avoid. This is where you demonstrate that the free content is actually valuable.

Email 3 should tell a story. A case study, a personal experience, or a pattern you've observed. Stories build the emotional component of trust that pure information can't. By the end of Email 3, the subscriber should feel like they know how you think.

The goal of these two emails: prove that staying subscribed is worth their time before you ever mention a product.

---

**[SECTION 3: Email 4 — The Soft Introduction — 5:30-7:30]**

Email 4, sent on day 7, introduces your product for the first time. But not as a pitch.

The structure: open with a problem or question your product solves. Spend the first two-thirds of the email going deep on that problem. Make the subscriber feel completely understood. Then, at the end, mention that you built something specifically to solve this.

Something like: "This is exactly why I put together [Product Name]. It takes everything I've covered in these emails and builds it into a system you can implement this week. It's linked here if you want to go deeper."

One sentence. One link. No pressure. The subscriber has received four emails of genuine value. They're in a completely different mental state than a stranger seeing your product for the first time. The conversion rate on this soft introduction is meaningful.

---

**[SECTION 4: Emails 5 and 6 — Value Plus Social Proof — 7:30-9:30]**

Email 5, day 10: more value. Teach another framework or deep-dive on a sub-topic. In the P.S., mention the product briefly again with one line and one link.

Email 6, day 14: social proof email. Share results or feedback from people who've used your product or framework. Real quotes, real outcomes, real context. Don't manufacture this, but if you have even one or two genuine testimonials, this email is powerful.

Close Email 6 with a clearer product CTA. Not aggressive, but direct. "If [outcome from Email 6] is what you're after, [Product Name] is designed for exactly that."

The subscriber has now received six emails of value before a real ask. Most email sequences get this backwards: they pitch in Email 2 and lose 60% of subscribers before building any trust.

---

**[SECTION 5: Email 7 — The Direct Ask — 9:30-11:00]**

Email 7, day 21: the direct ask.

This email is short. Open with a callback to something from earlier in the sequence. Make it personal. Then make the ask clearly.

"I've shared [X, Y, Z] over the past few weeks. If any of it was useful, you might want to take a look at [Product Name]. Here's what it covers and what it costs. Link is below."

Short. Direct. No manufactured urgency. The subscriber who is going to buy at this point has been thinking about it since Email 4. They needed time to make the decision. Email 7 is the invitation.

After Email 7, transition subscribers into your regular newsletter. The sequence is done. The relationship continues.

---

**[OUTRO — 11:00-12:00]**

Seven emails. Three weeks. One systematic conversion process.

The creators who build email sequences earn more per subscriber than any other creator monetization model I've seen. Because they're not relying on the algorithm to bring buyers. They're building the relationship deliberately.

Build the sequence once. Let it run automatically. Every new subscriber goes through the same trust-building process.

## SCRIPT 3: How to Price Your First Online Course

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

Most creators price their first course too low. They think starting low builds buyers. What it actually does is signal that the content isn't worth more, attract the most price-sensitive customers, and make it impossible to be profitable at any reasonable sales volume.

Pricing is not a function of what feels comfortable. It's a function of the outcome you deliver.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

Pricing your first online course is one of the most consequential decisions you'll make in your creator business. Get it wrong and you'll either undercut yourself or price yourself out of the market before you've established trust.

Today I'm covering the specific framework I use to determine price for a digital product or course, the psychology behind price anchoring, and the most common pricing mistakes to avoid.

This applies to your first course and every course you build after.

---

**[SECTION 1: Why Low Prices Backfire — 1:30-3:30]**

The logic behind low pricing is intuitive: lower barrier to entry means more buyers. But the math doesn't work that way in practice.

A $27 course that sells 50 units makes $1,350. A $197 course that sells 20 units makes $3,940. The $197 course requires fewer buyers and generates three times the revenue.

More importantly, buyers at different price points behave differently. A $27 buyer typically doesn't implement the material. They bought on impulse. A $197 buyer made a conscious investment and is motivated to get a return on it.

The people who get results from your course are your testimonials, your case studies, and your social proof. Low-price buyers almost never generate those. High-price buyers do.

This doesn't mean price everything at $197. It means the price should match the depth of the outcome you're delivering, not the comfort level of the creator.

---

**[SECTION 2: The Outcome Framework for Pricing — 3:30-6:30]**

The question that determines price is not "how many hours of content is this?" It's "what is the economic value of the outcome this course delivers?"

If your course teaches someone to write better YouTube scripts and those scripts lead to growing their channel from 500 to 5,000 subscribers in 6 months, what is that worth? If those 5,000 subscribers eventually generate $2,000 per month in product revenue, the outcome is worth thousands.

Your course price should be a fraction of the outcome value. 10-15% is a reasonable range.

If the outcome is worth $2,000 to the buyer, a price between $197 and $297 is rational. That's a 10-15% investment for a 100%+ return.

Structure your pricing argument around this. Not "here's what's in the course." Instead: "here's what's possible when you implement this." The content is the vehicle. The outcome is the product.

---

**[SECTION 3: Price Anchoring and Tier Structure — 6:30-9:00]**

Price anchoring is the practice of presenting a higher-priced option before the option you actually want to sell. Context determines perception of value.

A course at $197 looks expensive in isolation. It looks reasonable next to a $397 version with added coaching calls. Most buyers choose the middle option when three are presented.

For a first course, a simple two-tier structure works well. A base version at $X, and a premium version at $2X that includes a bonus resource, a template pack, or a community access.

The premium tier serves two purposes: it makes the base tier look like a better deal, and it captures the segment of buyers who want everything and will pay for it.

Don't over-engineer the tiers for your first launch. Two is enough. Three is the maximum. More than three creates decision paralysis.

---

**[SECTION 4: The Launch Price Strategy — 9:00-11:00]**

Your launch price and your permanent price should be different.

Launch price is the beta price, typically 30-40% below the intended permanent price. It rewards early buyers and creates a genuine urgency that isn't manufactured: the price will go up, and it does.

Announce the launch price clearly with a specific deadline. "This course is $97 during the beta launch, which runs through [date]. After that it goes to $147." When the deadline comes, raise the price. Don't extend it. Your credibility depends on your deadlines being real.

The beta launch also gives you something invaluable: real buyers going through the material, which gives you feedback to improve the course before it's at full price.

One more thing about launch: don't wait until the course is perfect to launch it. Launch when it's complete, not when it's polished. Perfection takes months. Completion takes weeks. Every month you delay is revenue you don't make and feedback you don't get.

---

**[OUTRO — 11:00-12:00]**

Price based on outcome, not content volume. Use anchoring to make your primary price point feel rational. Launch at beta price, raise it when you said you would.

Your first course launch will not be perfect. It's not supposed to be. It's supposed to be real. Real revenue, real feedback, real proof of concept.
