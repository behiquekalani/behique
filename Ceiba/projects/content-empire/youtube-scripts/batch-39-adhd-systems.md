## Script 1: "The ADHD productivity system that actually works (no willpower required)"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
Every productivity system built for neurotypical brains has one fatal flaw: it assumes you can decide to focus and then just do it. If you have ADHD, that assumption is wrong. Here is a system built on how your brain actually works.

**Why willpower-based systems fail ADHD brains (0:30-2:30)**
Willpower is a finite resource for everyone. For ADHD brains, it depletes faster and refills slower. A system that requires you to override distraction every 10 minutes is not a system. It is an exhausting negotiation with yourself.

The better model is to remove the decision entirely. Design your environment so the path of least resistance leads to the work you want to do.

This is not a motivational reframe. It is a structural design problem.

**The four components (2:30-7:00)**
A system that works for ADHD brains has four parts.

**One: a single next action.**
Not a project. Not a goal. The one physical action that moves something forward right now. "Work on the report" is not an action. "Open the report and write the first sentence" is an action. The ADHD brain needs a ramp, not a runway. Give it a ramp.

**Two: a time container.**
ADHD makes time invisible. You need external structure because internal time sense is unreliable. Set a 25-minute timer before you start. Not because 25 minutes is magic. Because it makes the session finite and finite feels survivable.

**Three: a capture system.**
ADHD brains generate ideas constantly, including during work sessions. Without a capture system, you either chase the idea and lose the work, or suppress the idea and lose both. Use one place, phone notes or a physical notepad, to dump every off-task thought immediately. Do not evaluate it. Just write it down and return to work. You will sort it later.

**Four: a decompression buffer.**
Build transition time between tasks. Five to ten minutes of nothing. Walk, stretch, stare out a window. ADHD brains do not switch contexts cleanly. The buffer is not wasted time. It is the cost of switching.

**What this looks like in practice (7:00-9:30)**
Morning starts with one question: what is the one thing that would make today feel like a win?

Write the answer down. That is your priority. That is what gets the first time block.

Set a timer. Work. When the thought interrupt arrives, write it down and keep going. When the timer ends, take the buffer. Then decide whether to do another round or move to something else.

That is the full system. It is not complicated. The complexity of most productivity systems is itself an ADHD trap. Complexity creates decision points. Decision points drain focus.

**Outro (9:30-11:00)**
The goal is not to become disciplined. The goal is to design a day where discipline is not required.

Remove decisions. Shorten time horizons. Capture everything. Build in transitions. Those four things will do more for your output than any habit tracker or morning routine ever will.

## Script 2: "Task switching is not a bug: how to work with ADHD hyperfocus"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
Everyone talks about ADHD as a focus problem. It is not. It is a focus regulation problem. The same brain that cannot stay on a boring task for 20 minutes can spend 8 hours in hyperfocus on something that catches its interest. The skill is learning how to use that.

**What hyperfocus actually is (0:30-3:00)**
Hyperfocus is not a superpower that just appears. It is a state that your brain enters when a task hits a specific combination of: novelty, personal interest, challenge, and immediate feedback.

Understanding those four conditions lets you engineer hyperfocus instead of waiting for it to show up randomly.

Novelty: new projects, new angles on old problems, new tools.
Personal interest: work that connects to something you actually care about.
Challenge: the task is hard enough to require full attention but not so hard it triggers avoidance.
Immediate feedback: you can see progress in real time.

When a task has all four, hyperfocus happens naturally. When it has none, you fight yourself for every minute.

**The implication for task design (3:00-5:30)**
Most productivity advice says to start with the hard thing. For ADHD brains, that advice ignores the activation problem.

The activation problem: starting a task that has low novelty, low interest, low challenge, and no immediate feedback is neurologically costly. Your brain is not being lazy. It is responding accurately to a task that provides nothing.

The fix is not forcing yourself to start anyway. The fix is changing the task conditions before you sit down.

Add novelty: do it in a different location, use a new tool, set a weird constraint.
Add interest: connect it to something you care about, even artificially.
Add challenge: time yourself, compete against your last session.
Add feedback: break it into units small enough that you finish something every 10 minutes.

**The task-switching reframe (5:30-8:00)**
ADHD brains switch tasks more than neurotypical brains. That is documented. The conventional reading is that this is a failure of focus.

The alternative reading: your brain is running a parallel search for the task that has the right conditions for engagement.

This is not a character flaw. It is an optimization strategy that sometimes misfires.

The practical implication: have multiple legitimate tasks available during a work session. When the engagement drops on one, switch to another. The goal is sustained output, not sustained attention on a single object.

Work moves forward. That is what matters.

**Protecting hyperfocus when it arrives (8:00-10:00)**
When hyperfocus activates, protect it. This is where ADHD brains can produce extraordinary output in short windows.

Tell people you are unavailable. Turn off notifications completely. Close every tab that is not directly relevant. Let the session run as long as it runs.

Do not try to stop a hyperfocus session at a scheduled time unless you have a hard commitment. The session will end on its own. Let it.

After it ends, rest before forcing another task. You will have spent significant energy.

**Outro (10:00-11:00)**
ADHD is not a deficit of attention. It is a different distribution of attention. Understanding that changes how you design your work.

Work with the hyperfocus. Engineer the conditions for it. Stop trying to force consistent low-engagement focus. That is fighting your neurology and you will lose.

## Script 3: "Why time blindness kills ADHD productivity (and the fix)"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
If you have ADHD, you have probably been late, missed deadlines, or looked up from something and found that two hours passed without you noticing. That is not a character flaw. That is time blindness. Here is what it is and how to compensate for it.

**What time blindness actually is (0:30-3:00)**
Neurotypical brains have an internal clock that passively tracks time passage. It is not perfect but it provides continuous background awareness of how much time has passed and how much remains.

ADHD brains frequently lack this passive clock. Time does not feel like it passes. There is now and there is not-now. That is it. Future deadlines exist intellectually but do not feel real until they become now.

This is why "I know I have a deadline at 5pm" does not create urgency at 10am for an ADHD brain. 5pm is not real yet. It is not now.

The conventional advice to "just plan ahead" assumes your brain naturally feels the distance between now and the deadline. Many ADHD brains do not.

**The three systems that compensate (3:00-8:00)**
You cannot fix time blindness by trying harder. You compensate for it with external structure.

**System one: visible timers.**
A countdown timer on your desk or phone screen makes time visible rather than invisible. You can see it shrinking. That is the feedback loop your internal clock is not providing. Use a physical timer or a large digital one on a second screen. Out of sight means out of mind with ADHD. The timer has to be visible.

**System two: time anchors.**
Time anchors are fixed external events that divide your day. Meals, alarms, scheduled calls. The ADHD brain navigates better between two concrete points than through open unstructured time.

Build your day around 3 to 4 hard anchors. Before the 10am call. After lunch. Before the 3pm timer. These are not goals, they are navigation markers.

Work happens in the spaces between anchors. Each space is small enough to feel finite.

**System three: the 10-minute warning.**
Before any transition, set a 10-minute warning alarm. This gives your brain notice that the current state is ending. ADHD brains struggle with abrupt transitions. The warning gives you time to close the mental loop on what you are doing, reducing the friction of switching.

This one change reduces lateness and missed handoffs more than almost anything else.

**The meta-skill: designing for time blindness instead of fighting it (8:00-10:00)**
Stop relying on feeling urgency. Build systems that create urgency externally.

Deadlines that are not visible are not real. Put them on a physical board or a visible digital display. Not buried in a task app you have to open.

Future time does not feel like present time. Break projects into deliverables that are due today, not next week. Work from today's deliverables, not the final deadline.

You are not broken. You are navigating without the instrument most people take for granted. Build the instrument externally.

**Outro (10:00-11:30)**
Time blindness is real and it is not fixed by motivation. It is fixed by design.

Visible timers. Time anchors. Transition warnings. These are tools, not tricks. Use them consistently and the chaos in your schedule reduces significantly.

## Script 4: "The weekly review for ADHD brains: 15 minutes, not 2 hours"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
Every productivity system recommends a weekly review. Most ADHD brains skip it because it sounds like 90 minutes of administrative work that requires sustained focus on something boring. Here is a version that takes 15 minutes and actually works.

**Why the standard weekly review fails for ADHD (0:30-2:30)**
David Allen's GTD weekly review is comprehensive. It is also two hours long, requires reviewing every project in detail, and demands the kind of sustained administrative focus that is exactly what ADHD makes hardest.

The result: most ADHD people do the weekly review once, maybe twice, then stop. The system decays. They feel like they failed.

They did not fail the system. The system failed them.

The 15-minute version is not a shortcut. It is a different design philosophy. Frequent small reviews beat infrequent comprehensive ones for ADHD brains.

**The four questions (2:30-7:00)**
The entire weekly review is four questions. You write the answers down. That is it.

**Question one: What actually happened this week?**
Not what you planned. What happened. Three to five things you actually completed, showed up for, or moved forward. This is not a guilt exercise. It is a record of real output, and for ADHD brains, visible evidence of progress combats the distorted feeling that nothing got done.

**Question two: What did not happen, and does it still matter?**
Not everything that fell through the cracks matters equally. Some things that did not get done this week do not need to get done at all. Clear them. The ones that still matter move to next week.

**Question three: What is the one most important thing for next week?**
One thing. Not five. Not a full project list. The one thing that would make next week feel like a success if it gets done.

This becomes your priority anchor for the entire week.

**Question four: What is one thing I want to protect time for that is not work?**
This question is not optional. ADHD brains are prone to either ignoring personal needs entirely during high-output periods or swinging to avoidance. Naming one non-work thing you will protect keeps the week from collapsing into either extreme.

**The setup (7:00-9:00)**
Do the review on the same day every week. Friday afternoon or Sunday evening work for most people. The consistency matters more than the timing.

Give it exactly 15 minutes. Set a timer. If you finish early, you are done. If you hit 15 minutes and are not done, write down where you are and stop anyway. The constraint prevents the review from becoming a spiral.

**Outro (9:00-10:30)**
The weekly review is worth doing because it gives you the one thing ADHD brains are bad at generating internally: a clear, low-friction entry point into the next week.

You wake up Monday knowing what matters. That is rare and valuable.

15 minutes. Four questions. Same day every week. That is the system.

## Script 5: "Dopamine budgeting: how to make boring tasks feel worth doing"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
The reason ADHD brains avoid boring tasks is not laziness. It is neurochemistry. The brain is not getting enough dopamine from the task to motivate action. You cannot willpower your way out of that. But you can engineer around it.

**The dopamine problem in plain terms (0:30-3:00)**
Dopamine is involved in motivation, reward anticipation, and action initiation. ADHD brains have lower baseline dopamine activity in the prefrontal cortex, which is the region responsible for executive function: planning, starting tasks, sustaining effort, and ignoring distractions.

When a task has no immediate reward signal, the ADHD brain does not generate enough dopamine to initiate it. This is not a choice. It is a chemical state.

The conventional response is to tell people to just start. That advice is not wrong, but it ignores the actual mechanism. Starting a low-dopamine task costs more neurological energy for an ADHD brain than it does for a neurotypical brain.

The better strategy is to increase the dopamine available to the task so starting is less costly.

**Three ways to raise the dopamine on a boring task (3:00-8:00)**
**One: pair it with something enjoyable.**
Music you like, a drink you enjoy, a physical environment you prefer. The pairing creates a small reward signal associated with the task. Over time this builds a conditioned association that makes starting easier.

The rule: the enjoyable thing is only available during the boring task. This protects the association.

**Two: create artificial stakes.**
Timers, challenges, streaks. The ADHD brain responds to competition and pressure even when the stakes are self-imposed. Setting a timer and trying to beat your last session score adds a game layer to an otherwise flat task.

The feedback loop makes the task feel active instead of passive. Active tasks are easier to start and sustain.

**Three: make progress visible.**
ADHD brains get very little internal feedback from invisible progress. Break the task into units small enough that you can check something off every 10 to 15 minutes. Crossing things off a list is a dopamine hit. Use it.

Physical checklists on paper work better than digital for many people because the physical act of crossing something out provides tactile feedback. Use whatever version creates the strongest feedback signal for you.

**The budgeting framing (8:00-10:00)**
Dopamine budgeting means allocating your high-engagement states to high-priority tasks.

Do not burn your best focus on email. Do not blow your hyperfocus window on social media. Your engagement capacity is finite. Treat it like money. Spend it where the return is highest.

This means doing your most important work in your best window, usually morning for most people, and putting low-stakes tasks in your lower-engagement periods.

It also means deliberately not burning your engagement before work starts. Looking at social media for 30 minutes before sitting down to work is spending dopamine before the session begins.

**Outro (10:00-11:00)**
You are not broken because boring tasks feel hard. Your brain is responding accurately to low-reward inputs.

The fix is not to care more. The fix is to engineer more reward into the task structure. Pair it with something you enjoy. Create artificial stakes. Make progress visible. Protect your best focus for your best work.

That is dopamine budgeting. Use it.
