## Script 1: "The 3-Email Launch Sequence That Sells Digital Products"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
Most people spend weeks building the product and about two hours on the launch. Then they wonder why nobody bought. The launch is not an announcement. It is a sequence. And there are three specific emails that do most of the work.

I want to walk you through the sequence I use every time I drop a digital product on Gumroad. This is not theory. I ran this on my AI employee guide, my self-hosted store guide, and the solopreneur OS. Same structure, different products.

---

**Context: Why most launch emails fail (0:30-2:00)**

Most people send one email. It goes something like: "Hey, I built this thing, here is the link." That is not a launch. That is a notification.

The problem is that a single email does not do enough work. It does not remind people who already said they were interested. It does not address the hesitation that will exist no matter how good your product is. And it does not give latecomers a reason to act.

The three-email sequence fixes all of that. Each email has one job. When you understand what that job is, writing them gets a lot easier.

---

**Email 1: The Announcement (2:00-4:30)**

Email 1 goes out on launch day, ideally early morning. Its job is simple: tell people the thing exists and explain what problem it solves.

What this email is not: a wall of features. What it is: one clear statement of what changes for the reader after they buy.

Structure I use:

Subject line: specific, not clever. Something like "My AI employee guide is live (and it is not what you think)" works better than "Big news from Behike." You want the subject to hint at a tension or a contrast.

Body: two to three paragraphs. First paragraph opens with the problem. Not "I am excited to announce" — with the problem the reader has right now. Second paragraph says what the product does about it. Third paragraph is the link with a one-sentence description of what they get.

One image or cover art if you have it. No long testimonial blocks in email 1 — you do not have them yet and faking it is obvious.

P.S. line: restate the link and add a deadline or a context note. "This is launch pricing, it goes up Friday" or "First 50 buyers get X included."

---

**Email 2: The Objection Handler (4:30-7:30)**

Email 2 goes out two days after launch. Its job is to address the hesitation that stopped people from buying email 1.

Most buyers read the first email, thought "interesting," and did nothing. Email 2 is for them.

Structure:

Open with a question that names the hesitation. Something like: "You saw the AI employee guide launch Tuesday. You did not buy. I want to guess why." That kind of open gets read because it is specific.

Then you work through the two or three most common objections for your product type. For digital products around AI tools, the usual ones are: "I am not technical enough to use this," "I already have something like this," and "I am not sure it is worth the price."

Answer each one directly. Not defensively — just honestly. If someone needs a certain baseline skill level to get value, say so. If your product is different from the thing they already have, say how specifically.

Close with the link and deadline reminder. This email does not need to be long. Six short paragraphs is fine. Dense is not better.

---

**Email 3: The Close (7:30-10:30)**

Email 3 goes out on the last day of your launch window. Its job is to create actual urgency and let the people who were waiting make a final decision.

Real urgency means something actually changes. Price goes up, bonus disappears, early access ends. Not fake countdown timers that reset.

Structure:

Subject line references the deadline explicitly. "Closes tonight" or "Price goes up at midnight" in the subject. People who were interested but sitting on it will open this.

Body is short. Two paragraphs max. Restate what they get, restate what changes tonight, give the link. No new information. People do not need to learn more at this stage, they need a reason to act now.

If you have gotten any replies or feedback from buyers in the days since launch, you can add one real quote here. One line, not a paragraph.

---

**What to do between emails (10:30-11:30)**

Do not ghost your list between the three emails. Post on Instagram, put something on your profile link, maybe a story. The email sequence works better when it is reinforced by presence on other channels.

Also: track opens and clicks per email. If email 1 had a 40% open rate but only 2% click rate, your offer statement is weak. If email 3 had a 15% open but a 20% click rate, the urgency was real and it worked. These numbers tell you what to fix for next time.

---

**Closing (11:30-12:00)**

Three emails. One announces, one handles hesitation, one closes. That is the sequence. If your list is small, the numbers will feel small too — but the ratio is what matters. A 3-5% conversion rate on a 200-person list is not bad. That is three to ten buyers from one product with zero ad spend.

## Script 2: "How to Launch a Product With Zero Followers (Real Tactics)"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
I launched my first Gumroad product when my Instagram had 47 followers and my email list had 11 people — most of them friends. I made three sales the first week. Not life-changing, but it proved the thing worked. And it taught me that the distribution problem is real but it is not the same as the audience problem. You can solve distribution without a big audience if you go about it the right way.

This video is about the tactics I used and still use when launching something without a built-in crowd.

---

**Why the "build an audience first" advice is wrong for you right now (0:30-2:00)**

The standard advice is: grow your audience, then launch to them. That makes sense if you are a full-time creator with eighteen months of runway. Most people watching this are not in that position.

The better framing is: launch first, use the launch to start building the audience. The product gives you something concrete to talk about. It gives you social proof to post. It gives you a reason to reach out to people. If you wait until the audience exists, you are waiting forever.

Zero followers is a constraint, not a death sentence. Here is how you work around it.

---

**Tactic 1: Warm outreach before anything goes public (2:00-4:30)**

Before your Gumroad page is live, message ten people you know who fit your target buyer. Not "hey can you buy my thing" — that is the wrong angle. The message is: "I built something and I want to get feedback before I launch it publicly. Would you be willing to take a look?"

Some of them will say yes. Walk them through the product. Ask what confused them, what they liked, what they would change. This serves two purposes: you get real feedback and you prime people to become buyers or amplifiers.

A few of them will buy right there, before you even have a public page. Those early sales are your launch proof. One real testimonial from someone who is not your roommate is worth more than any copywriting trick.

---

**Tactic 2: Community drops (4:30-6:30)**

Find two or three communities where your exact buyer already hangs out. Reddit, Discord, Facebook groups, Telegram channels. Spaces where people are actively asking questions your product answers.

You do not go in and post "buy my product." That gets you banned and it does not work. You go in and answer questions genuinely for a week or two before your launch. You become a recognizable name in that space. Then when you launch, you can share it as a resource, and the context you built makes it land differently.

The pitch in a community is: "I kept getting asked how I do X, so I wrote it up properly. Here it is if it is useful." That framing respects the community's norms and converts at a much higher rate than a cold product drop.

---

**Tactic 3: Cross-promotion with other small creators (6:30-8:30)**

Find people who are roughly at your size and building in adjacent spaces. Not competitors — complements. Someone building for the same audience but focused on a different problem.

The ask is simple: you share their thing to your list (even if small), they share yours. This is not influencer marketing. It is mutual signal boosting between people who are both in early stages.

The pitch: "I have a small but targeted list. I think your [X] would resonate with them. Would you be open to a swap?" Most people at that level will say yes because they are facing the same problem you are.

---

**Tactic 4: Content timed to launch (8:30-10:00)**

If you have any presence at all — even a new Instagram account or a few YouTube videos — post content in the week before launch that directly connects to what you built.

If you are launching a guide about AI tools, post a reel about one specific AI tool that week. If you are launching a finance template, post a video about one budgeting mistake. The content primes the algorithm and your small audience before the product drops.

You are not lying about your size or faking a big launch. You are sequencing your content so that the people who see it are already thinking about the problem your product solves.

---

**What I would do today starting from zero (10:00-11:30)**

If I were starting over right now, here is the order:

First two weeks: pick one product, build it, do the warm outreach to ten people, collect one to two real pieces of feedback.

Week three: post three pieces of content on one platform — Instagram, YouTube, wherever — connected to the product topic. Nothing about the product itself yet.

Week four: launch the Gumroad page, send the email to whatever list I have (even if it is just the people I messaged), post about it in one community where I have been active.

Repeat. The second launch is always better than the first because you have proof the first one worked, even if it was small.

---

**Closing (11:30-12:00)**

Zero followers is a real constraint but it is not a permanent one. Every person who has a large audience started at zero and did something to move off of it. The tactics in this video are what that something looks like in practice. Start with the warm outreach. It is uncomfortable and it is the most effective thing you can do right now.

## Script 3: "My Gumroad Launch Checklist: 27 Things Before You Publish"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
I shipped a product last year with a broken download link. Found out two days after launch from a buyer email. The product itself was fine but the experience of buying it was broken. That was fixable but it cost me reviews and probably some refund requests I never saw coming.

Since then I run a 27-point checklist every time before I publish anything on Gumroad. I am going to walk through the whole list today. Some of these are obvious. A few of them are not.

---

**Why a checklist matters (0:30-1:30)**

You are excited about the product. You have been working on it for weeks. The moment you are ready to publish is exactly when you are most likely to miss something obvious because your brain is already in "launch mode" and not "quality check mode."

The checklist is not a reflection on your competence. It is a system that compensates for the fact that humans are bad at catching their own errors when they are emotionally involved in what they built.

---

**The Product File (1:30-4:30)**

1. The file opens correctly on a device you did not use to build it. (PDF, Figma export, Notion template — open it fresh somewhere else.)
2. All links inside the product work and go to the right places.
3. If it is a PDF, it is not password-locked in a way that blocks the buyer.
4. If it is a video or audio file, the format is a common one — MP4, MP3, not something that needs a plugin to open.
5. File size is reasonable. Gumroad has limits and large files affect download speed. Compress where you can without losing quality.
6. If it is a template, the buyer gets a copy, not editing access to your original.
7. The file name makes sense to a stranger. "Behike-AI-Employee-Guide-v1.pdf" not "final-FINAL-draft3-USE-THIS.pdf."

---

**The Product Page (4:30-7:30)**

8. The title is specific about the outcome, not just the topic.
9. The description opens with a problem, not with "in this guide you will learn."
10. There is at least one image. Your cover art. No default Gumroad placeholder.
11. The price is set intentionally — not just a round number you picked without thinking about positioning.
12. If you are using a discount or launch price, the original price is visible so the discount is meaningful.
13. The "what you get" section lists specifics: page count, number of templates, video length — something concrete.
14. There is a FAQ or a sentence addressing the most common objection for this type of product.
15. The receipt confirmation email is customized. Not the default Gumroad "thanks for your purchase" text. Add one sentence that tells them exactly what to do next with the product.

---

**The Technical Setup (7:30-9:30)**

16. You have tested the checkout flow yourself using a dummy purchase or a discount code.
17. The product is visible in your Gumroad storefront, not just accessible by direct link.
18. If you are embedding it on a landing page, the embed works on mobile.
19. Gumroad email notifications are turned on so you see every sale in real time.
20. You have set up a Gumroad affiliate percentage if you plan to recruit affiliates.
21. Your Stripe or payment account connected to Gumroad is verified and active.
22. The product is listed in the right category so it shows up in Gumroad's discovery tab.

---

**The Pre-Launch Marketing (9:30-11:00)**

23. You have at least one piece of content ready to post the day of launch — a reel, a thread, something.
24. Your email sequence is written and scheduled — at minimum, the announcement email.
25. The link you are sharing in that content goes to the correct Gumroad product page, not your general storefront.
26. If you are posting to a community, you have done enough activity in that community in the past week that you are not going in cold.
27. You have told at least one real person about the launch who will actually respond — a friend, a community connection, someone. Accountability plus a witness to the fact that you shipped.

---

**Closing (11:00-12:00)**

That is the list. Twenty-seven things. Run it the day before you plan to publish. The whole thing takes about thirty minutes and it catches problems that would take hours to fix after launch.

I have a template version of this checklist linked in the description. It is a Notion page you can duplicate. Free, no email required.

## Script 4: "Why Most Product Launches Fail (And the 3 Things That Fix It)"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
Most product launches fail before the product page is even built. The failure is baked in at the planning stage. And the three failure modes are predictable enough that if you know them, you can avoid most of them.

I have built and launched products that sold nothing. I have also had launches that covered rent. The difference was not the quality of the product. It was three specific things I was doing wrong in the early launches and learned to fix.

---

**Failure Mode 1: The product is about what you built, not what they get (0:30-3:30)**

The most common failure. You spend months building something, and when it comes time to describe it, you describe the artifact. "This is a 47-page PDF about AI tools." No. That is what it is. Not what it does.

The buyer does not care about page count. They care about whether their specific problem gets solved. The product has to be framed in terms of outcome, not contents.

The fix is a one-sentence outcome statement written before you build anything. Finish this sentence: "After using this, the buyer will be able to __________ that they could not do before, or could not do as well." If you can not complete that sentence specifically, the product is not ready to launch.

Example from my own catalog: before, I described the solopreneur OS as "a Notion template with systems for managing your business." After the fix: "a Notion template that gives you a daily system for managing your tasks, revenue tracking, and content ideas in one place without building it yourself." Longer, but specific. The specifics are what create the click.

---

**Failure Mode 2: Launching to no one (3:30-6:30)**

The build is done, the page is up, you post once on Instagram and wait. Nothing happens. This feels like the market does not want the product. Usually the market never saw it.

Most solopreneur product launches reach fewer people than they think. Your Instagram post gets seen by maybe 5-10% of your followers organically. Your email open rate might be 25-30% if you are doing well. If your list is 80 people, that is 20 people who saw your launch email. If 3% of them buy, that is less than one purchase.

The fix is not "get more followers before you launch." That is the wrong timeline. The fix is building a pre-launch list. Before the product is public, collect email addresses from people who explicitly said they are interested. A simple landing page with "notify me when this launches" or a DM thread where you ask "would you want to know when I finish this?" gets you a list of warm leads.

A 50-person interested list converts at 10-20% on day one. A cold Instagram audience converts at less than 1%. The difference is intent signal, not size.

---

**Failure Mode 3: No follow-through after launch day (6:30-9:30)**

You launch, you get a few sales, then you go silent. No more emails. No more content about the product. You move on to the next thing. The product slowly disappears.

This is especially common in the first few weeks after a launch when enthusiasm drops and the daily sale notifications stop being exciting.

The fix is a post-launch content calendar that is planned before you launch. Three to five pieces of content per month that connect to the product without being ads for it. Tutorials. Answers to questions the product addresses. Behind-the-scenes of how you built something using the same method.

The product should stay in rotation for at least three months after launch. Most of the sales on any given product do not happen in launch week. They happen slowly over time as people find the content, find the product page, and decide to buy when the timing is right for them.

---

**The root cause underneath all three (9:30-11:00)**

All three failure modes come from the same place: treating the launch as an event instead of a process.

An event is something you do once. A process is something you run continuously. Product-market fit is not determined on launch day. It is determined over weeks of iteration on the framing, the content, and the distribution.

If you launched something and it sold nothing, that does not mean the product failed. It means one iteration of the launch process did not work. The question is which of the three failure modes was the cause.

---

**Closing (11:00-12:00)**

Outcome framing, pre-launch warm list, post-launch follow-through. Those are the three. Pick the one that is most broken in your current approach and fix that one first. Do not try to fix all three at once before your next launch — that is how you end up in a planning loop that never results in shipping.

## Script 5: "Post-Launch: What to Do After Your First Sale"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
The first sale notification hits. It is usually a small number, a few dollars, whatever the price of your first product is. Most people celebrate for about ninety seconds and then freeze. What do you do now?

I have been at that moment several times and the instinct is always to either over-celebrate or immediately wonder why it is not selling more. Both of those reactions take you off the work. Here is what the actual work looks like after that first sale.

---

**In the first 24 hours (0:30-3:00)**

Message the buyer. Not an automated email — an actual message. If they bought through Gumroad, you can see their email. Send them a short note: "Hey, I saw you picked up the guide. If you hit any questions as you go through it, reply here. Happy to help."

Why this matters: it is a surprise in a world where most digital product purchases feel like dropping money into a void. It also opens a conversation that can generate your first real testimonial. If the buyer responds and tells you what they liked, ask them: "Would it be okay if I shared that?" Most of the time they say yes.

Also: screenshot that first sale notification. Not for a generic "made my first sale on Gumroad" post necessarily — but for your own record. It is useful as proof in future content when you talk about building from scratch.

---

**In the first week (3:00-6:00)**

Three things to do in the first week post-launch:

First, send your email follow-up sequence. If you did not set one up before launch, do it now. At minimum: one email three days after launch that thanks buyers and asks one question — "What made you decide to get it?" The answers to that question are your most valuable marketing research. They tell you what your own copy should be saying.

Second, look at your Gumroad analytics. How many people visited the product page versus how many bought? A high visit-to-sale ratio means the offer converted well. A low ratio means people are seeing the page and leaving — your product description or price needs work.

Third, post one follow-up piece of content about the product. Not another announcement. Something that goes one layer deeper — a tutorial, a case study of what you did to build it, one specific thing from inside the product that you can teach in three minutes.

---

**The refund question (6:00-7:30)**

Refunds will happen. On digital products, Gumroad's default policy is thirty days. When you get a refund request, do not take it personally.

What to do: process it quickly, send a one-sentence reply asking what went wrong. You do not have to grovel or apologize in ways that feel fake. A simple "I processed the refund. Can I ask what the issue was?" usually gets a real answer. Sometimes the product was not the right fit. Sometimes it was a technical problem you can fix. Sometimes people request refunds because they forgot they bought something.

The information is useful. The refund itself is not a sign of failure.

---

**Building on the first sale (7:30-10:00)**

The first sale proves one thing: someone, somewhere, decided your product was worth paying for. That is a real signal and it is more than most people who start building ever get.

What to do with that signal:

Keep the product live and visible. Do not archive it because it is not selling dozens of units a day. Let it accumulate over time.

Add it to your content arsenal. Every piece of content you create that connects to that product's topic is a potential soft referral. You are not running ads. You are building context around the product that makes the right person find it when they are ready.

Look at what the buyer's profile tells you. Who actually bought? Not who you thought would buy — who actually bought. That person's demographics and context should inform how you describe the product going forward.

---

**What not to do (10:00-11:00)**

Do not immediately start building product 2 before you have processed what product 1 taught you. The post-launch learning phase is when the most useful information is available. If you skip it and jump straight to building, you repeat the same mistakes.

Do not announce the sale publicly in a way that makes you the center of it. "I made my first sale" content works, but only if it is useful to the viewer — not just a celebration for yourself. The framing that works: "Here is what I did and what I learned, in case you are building the same way."

---

**Closing (11:00-12:00)**

First sale is a start, not a finish line. Message the buyer, study the analytics, post one more piece of content, and ask a real question to the people who were in your launch window. Those four things done well in the week after your first sale set up your second launch better than any strategy doc you could write.
