# YouTube Batch 56: Writing and Copywriting

---

## Script 1: "How to Write a Product Description That Actually Sells"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
I rewrote the description for my AI employee guide four times. The first version sold zero copies in a week. The fourth version started selling three to five a week without changing anything about the product, the price, or the traffic. The only thing that changed was how I described it. That is what product description writing actually is — a hypothesis about what makes a stranger want to exchange money for what you built.

---

**Why most product descriptions fail (0:30-2:00)**

The failure pattern is almost always the same. The description opens with the creator's perspective, not the buyer's.

"I built this product because I kept struggling with X" — that is your story. Useful eventually, but not the opener. The buyer is asking one question from the first sentence: is this for me, and will it fix my problem? If the opener does not answer that, they scroll past.

The second most common failure: feature lists with no context. "50 AI prompts, 3 Notion templates, 12 video walkthroughs." What do those prompts do? What problem does the Notion template solve? A feature without a connected outcome is just noise.

---

**The three parts of a description that converts (2:00-6:00)**

Part 1: The problem sentence.

One sentence, first line, naming the specific problem your buyer has right now. Not a general observation about the world. Their specific situation.

Bad: "AI tools are confusing and there is too much information out there."
Better: "You are spending two hours a day on tasks that AI could handle in ten minutes, but you do not know which tool to use for which task."

The difference is specificity. The second one describes a person's actual day. Someone who recognizes themselves in that sentence reads the next line.

Part 2: The outcome statement.

One to two sentences on what changes for the buyer after using this. Not what the product contains — what the buyer can do, feel, or avoid after using it.

"After working through this guide you will have a repeatable AI workflow for content creation, inbox management, and research — set up in one afternoon."

Time-bound outcomes work well. "One afternoon" creates a believable scope. "Repeatable" addresses the concern that it is a one-time fix.

Part 3: The specifics block.

Now list the features, but with context. Not "50 AI prompts." Instead: "50 prompts organized by task type — content creation, client emails, research, ideation — so you can find the right one in under a minute."

Every bullet point in this block should answer "why does this matter to me."

---

**Formatting for Gumroad specifically (6:00-8:30)**

Gumroad descriptions are read on mobile more often than desktop. Short paragraphs. No blocks of five sentences without a break.

Use bold for the key phrases that a skimmer needs to see. Skimmers do not read every word. They hit the bold phrases and the bullet points and decide from those.

Do not use a wall of emoji. One or two, fine. Decorative bullets take up visual space but they do not communicate anything.

The length sweet spot on Gumroad: 150 to 300 words. Long enough to be specific. Short enough to not feel like homework.

---

**The test (8:30-10:00)**

Before publishing, hand your description to someone who does not know what you built. Ask them to read it and tell you in one sentence what the product does and who it is for. If their answer matches your intent, the description works. If they get it wrong, the writing is the problem — not their intelligence.

This test takes ten minutes and it will catch problems that an hour of revision will not.

---

**Closing (10:00-12:00)**

Product description writing is not copywriting in the traditional advertising sense. It is translation. You are translating what you built into what a stranger needs to hear to decide it is for them. Problem sentence, outcome statement, specific bullet points. Those three elements done well will outperform any fancy sales tactic.

## Script 2: "The Landing Page Formula I Use for Every Product"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
I built my first landing page by copying what I thought a good landing page looked like. Big headline, stock photo, three feature bullets, a buy button. It sold nothing. The second version, built around a formula I reverse-engineered from pages that actually converted, started working on day one. Today I use the same formula for every product I launch. Let me walk you through it.

---

**What a landing page is actually for (0:30-1:30)**

A landing page has one job: take a stranger who arrived with a question and give them enough clarity to make a decision. That is it. It is not a brochure. It is not a portfolio. It is a single-track conversation from "I just landed here" to "yes I am buying this" or "no, not for me."

Both outcomes are acceptable. The worst outcome is confusion — someone landing and not knowing what to do next. That is a page failure.

---

**Section 1: The Headline (1:30-3:30)**

The headline is the first thing read and the thing most people get wrong.

The formula I use: [Specific outcome] + [for specific person] + [without specific frustration].

Example: "A daily Notion system for solopreneurs who want to track their business in one place without building it from scratch."

That headline has a who, a what, and a without. Someone who matches the who reads it and immediately knows this might be for them.

What to avoid: clever or abstract headlines that require context to understand. "Build the life you deserve" is the worst version of this. It says nothing specific and therefore signals nothing.

---

**Section 2: The Problem Block (3:30-5:30)**

After the headline, two to four sentences describing the state the buyer is in right now before they have your product. Be specific about the texture of the problem.

Not: "Many solopreneurs struggle with organization."
Instead: "You have three different apps for tasks, two for notes, and your revenue is tracked in a Google Sheet that is three months out of date."

The second version makes people feel seen. When someone feels seen, they trust the source of that feeling.

---

**Section 3: The Solution Statement (5:30-7:00)**

One paragraph. What the product is, what it does, and why you built it.

The "why you built it" part is often skipped, but it builds credibility. "I built this because I spent four months looking for a system that worked for a one-person business and everything I found was either designed for teams or too complex to maintain." That sentence tells the buyer you understand their exact constraint.

---

**Section 4: What You Get (7:00-8:30)**

Bullet list. Specific. Context attached to each item.

Every bullet should pass this test: could this bullet be interpreted as good or bad depending on the reader? If so, add one phrase that makes it clearly good. "47 pages" is neutral. "47 pages structured so you can implement one section at a time without reading the whole thing first" is a feature.

---

**Section 5: The CTA (8:30-10:00)**

One button, one action. The button text should not say "Buy Now" if you can say something more specific. "Get the guide," "Start your setup," "Download the template." The specificity of the button text signals that you know what the buyer wants.

Put the button in view on both mobile and desktop without scrolling. If someone has to scroll to find the button, you are losing conversions at that step.

---

**Section 6: The Objection Closer (10:00-11:00)**

Below the CTA, address the one thing that will stop a ready buyer. For digital products, it is almost always: "What if I buy and it is not useful to me?"

Your answer: a clear guarantee or a clear scope statement. "If you open it and it is not what I described, email me and I will refund it. No form required." One sentence. It takes fear off the table for people who were 80% ready.

---

**Closing (11:00-12:00)**

Six sections: headline, problem block, solution statement, what you get, CTA, objection closer. Each one has one job. When all six are doing their jobs, the page works as a system, not just a collection of text. Start with the headline because everything else on the page depends on whether the headline earns the read.

## Script 3: "Email Subject Lines That Get Opened: My Analysis of What Works"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
I went back through twelve months of email campaigns and pulled the open rate data on every subject line I sent. The patterns were clear and several of them were things I did not expect. Today I want to share what I found and what I changed as a result.

The list I was sending to is small — under 500 people at the time I pulled this data. But the percentage patterns are what matter, and percentages scale.

---

**What I was measuring (0:30-2:00)**

Open rate only. Not click rate, not conversion rate. Just: what percentage of people who received this email opened it.

I grouped the subject lines into six categories and averaged the open rates per category. The results shifted how I write every email now.

---

**Category 1: Question subject lines (2:00-4:00)**

Questions performed above average — around 38% open rate versus my baseline of 28%. But not all questions were equal.

The question had to be specific enough that the reader could answer it in their head before opening. "Are you wasting time on the wrong tasks?" is okay. "Is your AI workflow actually saving you hours or just shifting the work?" is better because the tension is specific.

Generic questions like "Are you ready for this?" or "Did you see this?" performed the worst in the question category because they give no information and feel like clickbait.

---

**Category 2: Specific number subject lines (4:00-5:30)**

"3 things before you launch" outperformed "things to check before you launch" by about 8 percentage points in side-by-side tests I ran.

The number signals that the email has a defined scope. The reader knows they are getting a countable thing, not an essay. That removes friction.

Even-number lists performed slightly lower than odd-number lists in my data. I do not have a strong explanation for that but I have started defaulting to odd numbers — 3, 5, 7 — as a result.

---

**Category 3: Curiosity gaps (5:30-7:00)**

These are subject lines where the headline creates a tension that the email resolves. "The product that sold backward" or "Why I stopped using the tool everyone recommended."

These performed well — around 42% open rate on average — but they are the hardest to write authentically. A curiosity gap that feels manufactured is obviously a trick and damages trust. The ones that worked were situations where the tension in the subject was real, not invented.

---

**Category 4: Direct and informational (7:00-8:30)**

"My Gumroad launch checklist is live" or "New guide: AI workflows for content creators." No curiosity gap, no number, just a clear statement of what the email contains.

These performed below average on open rate — around 22% — but above average on click rate. The people who opened already knew they wanted it. So the subscriber quality was higher even if the total number was lower.

My takeaway: direct subject lines work well for existing buyers or highly engaged subscribers. For general list sends, they are not the opener.

---

**Category 5: Social proof and reference (8:30-10:00)**

"What worked for the people who launched last month" or "3 people sent me this question this week." These performed mid-range but were consistent. They work because they signal that other people are doing the thing, which is reassuring.

The mistake in this category: vague social proof. "Everyone is using this tool" is nothing. "Three readers asked me about this in the same week" is specific and real.

---

**What I changed (10:00-11:30)**

I stopped writing subject lines last. Subject lines are now written first, before the email body, because they set the frame for everything else. If I can not write a good subject line for the email I am about to write, I question whether the email is worth sending.

I also started tracking open rate per subject line category in a simple spreadsheet. Twelve months in, my average open rate went from 28% to 35%. The content of the emails did not change significantly. The subject lines did.

---

**Closing (11:30-12:00)**

Questions, numbers, curiosity gaps, direct statements, social proof. Test against your own list. Your readers might respond differently than mine. But the framework for thinking about what a subject line has to do — reduce friction, signal value, earn the open — is consistent regardless of audience.

## Script 4: "The Voice Bible: How to Write AI Content That Sounds Like You"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
Every creator who starts using AI to write content hits the same wall around week two. The content comes out technically correct, well-structured, and completely hollow. It sounds like a press release from someone who has never had an opinion. It sounds like AI.

The fix is not to stop using AI. The fix is to build a voice bible before you prompt anything. I want to walk you through how I built mine and how I use it every time I generate content.

---

**What a voice bible is (0:30-2:00)**

A voice bible is a reference document that captures how you write. Not what you write about — how you write. Sentence length, tone, banned words, structural patterns, the specific things you say that a generic writer would not.

The document has two uses: you reference it when you are writing manually to stay consistent, and you paste sections of it into AI prompts so that the output sounds like you instead of like a productivity blog from 2018.

Most creators skip this and then wonder why their AI-generated content feels lifeless. The AI does not know how you talk. You have to tell it.

---

**Building the voice bible: Step 1 — Pull your real writing (2:00-4:30)**

Go find five to ten pieces of writing you did without any AI help. Old emails, old Instagram captions, old notes you wrote to yourself. Pick ones where you can hear your voice clearly when you read them back.

Paste them all into one document. Now read through them looking for patterns. What is the average sentence length? Do you tend toward short punchy sentences or longer ones that build? Do you open paragraphs with observations or questions? Do you use "I" often or do you write more generally?

Write down the patterns you notice. These are not rules you chose — they are the natural grammar of how you already write.

---

**Building the voice bible: Step 2 — Name what you do and do not do (4:30-7:00)**

From the patterns you found, create two lists.

List 1: things you do. "Short sentences when making a sharp point." "Open with a specific situation, not a general claim." "Use real numbers when referencing results." "Write as if talking to one person."

List 2: things you do not do. This is the banned list. Every writer has words and phrases they would never naturally say. In my voice bible, the banned list includes: game-changing, unlock your potential, deep dive, leverage, streamline, revolutionary, comprehensive, master anything. These are words that appear in generic AI output constantly. They do not appear in how I actually write.

The banned list is not arbitrary. It comes from reading your natural writing and noticing what is absent. If you would not say it in a voice note to a friend, it probably does not belong in your content.

---

**Building the voice bible: Step 3 — Write example sentences (7:00-9:00)**

For every tonal characteristic you identified, write one example sentence that shows it rather than describes it.

"Tone: direct, not preachy" is a description. The example: "I spent three months not publishing anything because I was waiting until the product was perfect. That was a mistake." That sentence shows the tone.

These examples are what you give the AI when you are prompting. "Write in this style, here are examples:" followed by four or five of your real sentences. This alone will dramatically shift the output quality.

---

**How to use the voice bible when prompting (9:00-11:00)**

At the top of any AI writing prompt, include three things from your voice bible:

1. Two to three example sentences from your real writing.
2. Three items from your "I do" list.
3. Three items from your "I do not" list, specifically the banned words.

Then give the actual content prompt. The AI is now constrained to write in a defined lane rather than defaulting to its generic output patterns.

After you get the draft, do one read-through and flag any sentence that you would not have written. Rewrite those sentences manually. This takes less time than writing the whole thing from scratch but ensures the output sounds like you.

---

**Closing (11:00-12:00)**

The voice bible is a one-time build that you refine over time. Spend two hours on it once and your AI-assisted content will sound fundamentally different. The document lives in Notion or a text file. Mine is three pages. That is enough. Start by pulling your real writing and reading it with attention to pattern. Everything else follows from that.

## Script 5: "Headline Writing for Solopreneurs: Specific vs. Vague"

**Hook (0:00-0:30)**
I will show you two headlines for the same piece of content, and I want you to notice which one makes you want to read it.

Version A: "How to Make More Money Online as a Solopreneur"
Version B: "How I Added $800 to Monthly Revenue by Selling One PDF on Gumroad"

The second one wins every time. Not because it is longer or cleverly structured. Because it is specific. Today I want to break down exactly why specificity works, what vagueness costs you, and a formula for making any headline more specific without making it longer.

---

**Why vague headlines feel safe but do not work (0:30-2:30)**

Vague headlines feel safe because they try to appeal to everyone. "How to grow your business" could apply to any business owner. "Tips for success" could apply to any person. The logic is: broad appeal means more potential readers.

The reality is the opposite. When a headline could apply to anyone, it applies to no one specifically. The reader's brain does not feel addressed. It moves on.

The specific headline does the opposite. It signals: "I am writing for a person in this exact situation." Someone who is in that situation reads it and thinks "that is me." Someone who is not in that situation skips it, and that is fine. The click rate from the right person is more valuable than a lot of skims from the wrong ones.

---

**Four types of vagueness and how to fix each (2:30-8:00)**

Type 1: Vague subject. "How to use AI tools better."
Fix: name the specific tool or specific task. "How I use Claude to write product descriptions in twenty minutes instead of two hours."

Type 2: Vague outcome. "Improve your writing."
Fix: name a specific measurable change. "Cut your draft time in half by fixing this one structural habit."

Type 3: Vague time. "Eventually" or "over time."
Fix: name a unit of time even if it is approximate. "In one afternoon" or "in three months" or "in your first week."

Type 4: Vague audience. "For creators" or "for entrepreneurs."
Fix: be more specific about who the creator or entrepreneur is. "For creators who are also full-time students." "For solopreneurs running a one-person business without a team."

The fix in every case is the same move: zoom in. Trade breadth for specificity.

---

**The specificity formula (8:00-10:00)**

There is a reliable way to make any headline more specific without making it awkward. The formula is:

[Specific action] + [specific context] + [specific constraint or outcome]

Applied: "How I Launched My First Digital Product on Gumroad in One Week With No Existing Audience"

That headline has an action (launched), a context (first digital product on Gumroad), and a constraint (one week, no audience). Three specifics in one sentence. Someone in that exact position reads it and clicks.

The formula does not require all three elements every time. Two out of three is usually enough to make a headline work. But the process of applying the formula forces you to ask what the specific situation is, and that question usually finds the answer.

---

**When specificity can be too narrow (10:00-11:00)**

There is a real version of over-specificity. "How I sold exactly 11 copies of my third PDF on Gumroad on a Tuesday" is specific but the specifics do not help anyone apply the lesson. The details are not useful.

The test: are the specific details in the headline the ones that signal relevance to your reader? Or are they just details that happen to be true? If the specifics tell the reader "I am talking about someone like you," they belong. If they just make the headline longer without adding that signal, cut them.

---

**Closing (11:00-12:00)**

Every headline you write before you think about specificity is a first draft. Read it back and ask: could this apply to anyone? If yes, zoom in. Name a specific tool, a specific time, a specific situation. The headline that gets clicked is the one that makes the right reader feel directly addressed. Write for that person first.
