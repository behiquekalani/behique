## SCRIPT 1: How to Write Better YouTube Scripts (Structure and Hooks That Actually Work)

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

The difference between a video that holds attention and a video that people click off after 90 seconds is almost never the topic. It's the structure.

Most creators write scripts the way they write essays: introduction, body, conclusion. That's the wrong format. YouTube viewers are not essay readers. They're impatient, skeptical, and constantly asking "why should I keep watching."

I'm going to show you the scripting framework that keeps viewers watching and converts them into subscribers.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

I've written hundreds of scripts for educational content and built systems for other creators to use. The patterns I'm covering today are not theory. They're what consistently works.

Here's what we're covering:

- The anatomy of a hook that earns the next 10 minutes
- The three-act structure adapted for YouTube
- How to write transitions that hold attention
- The CTA structure that actually converts
- Common scripting mistakes and how to fix them

Whether you script word-for-word or use an outline, this framework applies.

---

**[SECTION 1: The Anatomy of a Hook — 1:30-4:30]**

The hook is the first 30 seconds. Everything depends on it.

A hook does one job: it earns the next 30 seconds. Not the next 10 minutes. Just the next 30 seconds.

Effective hooks open a loop that the viewer needs to close. They surface a tension between where the viewer is and where they want to be. They challenge a belief the viewer holds that might be wrong.

Here are four hook structures that consistently work.

**Structure 1: The counter-intuitive claim.** Make a claim that contradicts common advice. "Most creators are getting YouTube SEO completely backwards." The viewer wants to know what the right way is. They keep watching.

**Structure 2: The before/after gap.** Open with a concrete result. "Six months ago I had 400 subscribers. Here's the exact system that changed that." Specificity and implicit promise of a system.

**Structure 3: The problem naming.** Precisely identify a frustration your viewer has. The more specific, the better. "You spend three hours scripting a video, upload it, and it gets 47 views. Here's what's wrong." If it's accurate, the viewer feels seen and stays.

**Structure 4: The credibility bridge.** Lead with something you know that your viewer doesn't, and hint at what they'll know by the end. "I've analyzed 200 YouTube channels that failed in the first year. There are three patterns that show up in almost all of them."

What kills hooks: starting with your name and a welcome, asking viewers to "sit back and enjoy," vague claims without specifics, and overly long intros before you get to the value.

Write your hook last. After you've scripted the full video, you'll know exactly what the most compelling angle is.

---

**[SECTION 2: The Three-Act Structure for YouTube — 4:30-7:30]**

YouTube scripts have a specific three-act structure that differs from traditional storytelling.

**Act 1: The Promise.** This is the first 90 seconds to 2 minutes. You establish what the video delivers, why the viewer should care, and what they'll be able to do or know by the end. The promise needs to be specific. "By the end of this video you'll have a scripting framework you can use for your next video" is better than "today we're talking about scripting."

**Act 2: The Delivery.** This is 80 percent of your video. Three to five sections, each one advancing the viewer's understanding or capability. Each section should open with the promise of what the section covers and close with a one-sentence transition that connects it to the next.

Think of each section as a mini-video within the video. Open with the point, explain with evidence or example, close with the implication. Repeat.

**Act 3: The Close.** The last 90 seconds to 2 minutes. Summarize the three biggest takeaways, bridge to the next action (subscribe, click the link, watch the next video), and close with a CTA that is specific and low-friction.

The most common structural mistake is burying the most valuable content in the middle of Act 2. Put your best insight early. If your video has a genuinely useful framework, introduce it at minute 4 or 5, not minute 9.

---

**[SECTION 3: Writing Transitions That Hold Attention — 7:30-9:30]**

Transitions are where most scripts lose the viewer. A bad transition sounds like: "Now let's move on to our next point." A good transition creates curiosity about the next section before you enter it.

Effective transition structure: close the current section with a complete thought, then plant a seed for the next one. "This is why the hook matters so much. But the hook only works if the structure underneath it can hold the viewer. That's what we're covering next."

The viewer now expects something specific from the next section. They're primed to receive it. Compare this to "Now let's talk about structure." The second version gives them nothing to anticipate.

Another technique: use tension bridges. End a section with a question or unresolved tension that the next section answers. This is why people watch entire documentaries. Every chapter ends with a question the next chapter answers.

---

**[SECTION 4: The CTA That Converts — 9:30-11:30]**

Your call to action fails when it's generic. "Like and subscribe" stopped working years ago. Viewers have tuned it out.

Effective CTAs are specific and tied to the content of the video. "If you want the script template I use for every video, it's in the Behike newsletter, link in the description." That CTA is specific, offers tangible value, and connects directly to what the viewer just watched.

Layer your CTAs. A soft mention mid-video, a clear CTA at the end, and a specific link in the description. Each CTA should offer something distinct so you're not just repeating the same ask three times.

For newsletter signups: lead with what they get, not the action. "I send one tactical content framework every Sunday" is better than "sign up for my newsletter."

For products: lead with the outcome. "It takes most creators 3 months to develop a scripting system that works. This template compresses that into a weekend." Then the link.

---

**[OUTRO — 11:30-12:30]**

Better scripts produce better videos. Better videos hold attention longer. Longer watch time signals the algorithm that your content is worth recommending.

But more importantly, better scripts mean clearer thinking. The process of scripting forces you to understand your own ideas well enough to teach them. That makes everything else better.

Write your hook last. Structure in three acts. Transition with tension. CTA with specificity.

## SCRIPT 2: The YouTube SEO Playbook

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

YouTube SEO in 2026 is not about keyword stuffing your title and description. The creators who understand how YouTube's recommendation engine actually works are building channels that grow while they sleep.

The ones relying on outdated SEO tactics are wondering why their perfectly researched videos get no views.

This is the current playbook.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

YouTube is the second largest search engine in the world. But it operates on a different logic than Google. YouTube doesn't just match search terms. It predicts viewer satisfaction.

Understanding that distinction changes everything about how you approach SEO.

Today I'm covering:

- How YouTube's recommendation algorithm actually works in 2026
- Keyword research for YouTube (the right method)
- Title, thumbnail, and description optimization
- The watch time and engagement signals that matter most
- How to structure a video for maximum algorithmic performance

This is the full playbook.

---

**[SECTION 1: How YouTube's Algorithm Works — 1:30-4:00]**

YouTube's primary goal is to keep users on the platform. Its recommendation algorithm optimizes for one thing: the probability that a viewer, after watching your video, continues watching more YouTube.

The signals it uses to determine this: click-through rate, watch time percentage, post-video behavior (do they watch another video or leave?), satisfaction surveys, and engagement signals (likes, comments, shares, saves).

This is different from Google SEO, which optimizes for relevance to a query. YouTube optimizes for a satisfied viewer who keeps watching.

The implication is that a well-packaged video on a competitive topic can outperform a technically perfect SEO video if it has higher viewer satisfaction. Package and substance both matter.

For search-driven content (how-to videos, tutorials), keyword alignment still matters because you need to appear in search results. For discovery-driven content (opinion pieces, case studies), the packaging matters more than the keywords.

Know which type of video you're making before you start optimizing.

---

**[SECTION 2: Keyword Research for YouTube — 4:00-6:30]**

YouTube keyword research starts with intent, not volume.

The best YouTube keywords have three characteristics: they have consistent search volume, the existing results don't fully satisfy the intent, and you can create something better than what's currently ranking.

Tools for YouTube keyword research: YouTube's own autocomplete (type a keyword and see what it suggests), VidIQ or TubeBuddy for volume and competition data, and Google Trends to see if interest is growing or declining.

But the most important keyword research tool is watching your audience. Look at the comments on your top performing videos. Look at what questions come up repeatedly. Look at what the people in your niche are searching on Reddit and Quora.

Real questions from real people beat keyword tool data every time. Someone asking "why does my YouTube channel have good content but no views" is telling you exactly what they need. Build a video that answers that precisely.

Long-tail keywords are underrated. A video on "how to grow a YouTube channel to 1000 subscribers in 90 days with no budget" will rank faster and convert better than a video on "how to grow on YouTube" even if the second one gets more overall searches.

---

**[SECTION 3: Title, Thumbnail, and Description — 6:30-9:00]**

The title and thumbnail work together. They need to convey a single, specific promise that creates enough curiosity to earn the click.

For titles: lead with the benefit or the tension. Include the primary keyword. Keep it under 60 characters. "How I grew to 10K subscribers" is weaker than "The 3-Video Strategy That Got Me to 10K Subscribers." Specificity always beats generality.

For thumbnails: the face-and-text combination still performs because it creates human connection. Your facial expression should convey the emotion of the video. Curiosity, surprise, or confidence. The text on the thumbnail should complement but not repeat the title.

For descriptions: the first 150 characters are visible without clicking "show more." That space should contain your primary keyword and your newsletter/product CTA. The rest of the description should include a timestamp breakdown, secondary keywords, relevant links, and social links.

Chapters (timestamps) are both a UX feature and an SEO tool. YouTube indexes chapter titles and surfaces them in search results. Use chapters in every video longer than 5 minutes.

---

**[SECTION 4: Watch Time and Engagement Signals — 9:00-11:30]**

Watch time percentage is the most important algorithmic signal. A video that holds 70% of its viewers for the full runtime will get recommended more than a video that holds 40%.

The first 30 seconds are critical. If viewers are clicking off in the first 30 seconds, your hook is failing. Check your YouTube Analytics audience retention graph. The point where the graph drops sharply is where your script is losing people.

Engagement signals (likes, comments, shares) matter, but they're secondary to watch time. A video with 10,000 views and 500 comments is performing well. A video with 10,000 views and 5 comments with 35% watch time is not.

To improve engagement without begging for it: ask a specific question at the end of the video. Not "let me know what you think in the comments." Instead: "What's the biggest thing stopping you from publishing consistently right now? Tell me below, I read every one."

Specific questions get specific answers. Specific answers are comments that signal engagement to the algorithm.

---

**[OUTRO — 11:30-12:30]**

YouTube SEO is not a trick. It's a system for building content that satisfies viewers and signals that satisfaction to the algorithm.

Nail the keyword research. Build the title and thumbnail together. Optimize for watch time in your structure. Ask for specific engagement.

Do this consistently and the algorithm starts working for you instead of against you.

## SCRIPT 3: Building a YouTube Channel Around One Topic for 12 Months

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:30]**

Most YouTube channels fail for a reason that has nothing to do with content quality or consistency.

They fail because the creator never committed to a topic long enough to become the obvious authority.

They pivot. They chase trends. They make the "something for everyone" mistake and end up being nothing to anyone.

The 12-month one-topic YouTube strategy is how you build something that actually compounds.

---

**[INTRO — 0:30-1:30]**

I'm going to lay out the full 12-month playbook for building a YouTube channel around one specific topic. What to do in months 1-3, months 4-6, months 7-9, and months 10-12.

By the end of this you'll have a clear framework for what to build, when to build it, and how to know if it's working.

Here's the thesis: when you go deep on one topic for 12 months, two things happen. You become genuinely knowledgeable in a way that shows on camera. And YouTube's algorithm learns exactly who to recommend your videos to.

Both of those take time. That's why the 12-month commitment is not optional. It's structural.

---

**[SECTION 1: Choosing the Right Topic — 1:30-4:00]**

The right topic for a 12-month YouTube build has three characteristics.

First: you have genuine interest or experience in it. You're going to make 50-100 videos on this topic. If you're manufacturing enthusiasm from day one, you won't last.

Second: there's a paying audience. Interest doesn't matter if no one in the topic spends money. Validate by checking if there are products being sold in the niche, if there are other channels monetizing, and if there's advertising spend (which indicates commercial intent).

Third: it's specific enough to build authority. "Business" is not a topic. "Building a one-person content business" is a topic. "YouTube SEO for solopreneurs" is an even more specific topic. The more specific your topic, the faster you become the authority.

A useful test: can you name 50 video ideas for this topic right now? If you struggle to get to 20, the topic might be too narrow. If you can easily name 100, you've found a topic with enough depth.

---

**[SECTION 2: Months 1-3 (Foundation Phase) — 4:00-6:30]**

The first three months are not about growth. They're about finding your format and learning the production process.

Publish one video per week. Every video should be on the same topic cluster. Every video should have a clear structure: hook, three to five sections, CTA.

In this phase, don't worry about thumbnails being perfect, SEO being optimized, or subscriber count. Those come later. Right now you're learning what your audience responds to and what your process looks like.

Watch your analytics obsessively for one thing: which videos have the highest watch time percentage. Not views. Watch time percentage. This tells you what your audience wants more of.

By month 3 you should have 12 videos published, a clear sense of your best-performing format, and a list of 30+ video ideas for the next quarter.

---

**[SECTION 3: Months 4-6 (Optimization Phase) — 6:30-9:00]**

Months 4-6 are when you start applying what you learned.

Take the two or three video formats that had the highest watch time and double down on those. Stop making video types that consistently underperform. This is not quitting. This is optimization.

Start applying SEO intentionally. Use keyword research to find topics with search volume that fit your niche. Build a content calendar that mixes search-driven content (how-to videos on high-volume keywords) with authority-building content (your opinions, case studies, unique frameworks).

Launch your email list if you haven't already. Add a CTA in every video. The goal by month 6 is 250-500 email subscribers. This is your owned audience separate from YouTube.

By month 6 you should start seeing organic search traffic to your older videos. This is the compounding effect beginning to work. Every video you publish now benefits from the authority the channel has been building.

---

**[SECTION 4: Months 7-9 (Monetization Phase) — 9:00-11:00]**

By month 7, you have 26-30 published videos, a growing email list, and a clear sense of what your audience needs.

This is when you launch your first product. It should be the direct answer to the most common question your audience has asked in the first six months.

The product doesn't need to be large. A $27 guide or template that solves one specific problem is better than spending three months building a $297 course. Test demand with something small before committing to something large.

Add the product CTA to every new video. Update the descriptions of your top performing older videos to include the product link.

Continue publishing weekly. The channel is now a three-layer system: it grows your audience, builds your email list, and drives product sales.

---

**[SECTION 5: Months 10-12 (Scale Phase) — 11:00-13:00]**

By month 10 you're close to or past 1,000 subscribers. Your SEO is working. Your product has made its first sales. The email list is growing.

The scale phase is about deepening what's already working, not expanding to new platforms. Add a second product. Build a more comprehensive email sequence. Start creating content series (a multi-part series on one theme performs well and encourages viewers to watch multiple videos).

At month 12, review the full year. Which videos drove the most subscribers? Which drove the most product sales? Which topics had the highest watch time? Use this to plan year two.

Year two of a focused one-topic channel is almost always dramatically better than year one. The algorithm knows who you're for. Your audience trusts you. Your content is better because you've made 50-100 videos on this topic.

The 12-month commitment is not about gritting your teeth through a hard year. It's about building the compound interest curve. The first year is the slow part. Everything after is the payoff.

---

**[OUTRO — 13:00-14:00]**

Pick one topic. Build in four phases. Don't quit at month 4 when growth feels slow, because that's exactly when the algorithm is starting to learn who to send to you.

Most channels that fail do so between month 3 and month 6. They mistake the slow-growth compounding phase for failure. They're not failing. They're investing.

Stay the course.
