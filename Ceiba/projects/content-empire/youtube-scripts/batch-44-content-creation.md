## Script 1: The Exact Instagram Strategy That Got Me My First 1,000 Followers

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I hit 1,000 followers on Instagram in 87 days. Not from going viral. Not from paid ads. From a specific posting strategy I built by looking at what actually drove follows, not just likes.

In this video I'm going to break that strategy down exactly. The posting frequency, the content types, the specific things I changed at day 30 that made the second half of that journey twice as fast as the first.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I'm Kalani. I'm a computer engineering student in Puerto Rico and I build digital products and automation tools. I started publishing on Instagram because I needed to build an audience before I had products to sell.

What I found is that most advice about Instagram growth is useless because it's either too generic or it describes what worked for someone with 50,000 followers, not someone starting from zero.

So I ran my own experiment. Here's what I found.

**Section 1 -- The Starting Point (1:30-3:00):**
Week one, I had 12 followers. All people I knew personally. Zero strangers.

I made two mistakes immediately that I see everyone make:

First, I posted whatever I felt like posting. No theme, no consistency. Tech stuff, then a photo of food, then an opinion about something random. The algorithm and potential followers had no idea what I was about.

Second, I was optimizing for likes instead of follows. A like is passive. A follow is a decision. The content that gets the most likes is often not the content that gets the most follows.

The fix was defining one specific topic I'd post about for at least 90 days. Not "AI and tech and business and life." Just: building a digital business as a student using AI tools.

That's specific enough that someone who cares about that topic will follow immediately, and broad enough that I had plenty to post about.

**Section 2 -- The Posting Cadence (3:00-5:00):**
I posted 4 times per week. Here's the breakdown:

Monday: Carousels. Educational content. 5 to 8 slides. These got the most saves, and saves are the highest-value engagement signal on Instagram.

Wednesday: A short personal story. Something real that happened while I was building. Not inspirational fluff. Actual events with a specific lesson.

Friday: A Reel. Under 60 seconds. Either a tip or a hot take. These got the most reach to new accounts.

Sunday: A single image. A quote or a one-line observation. Low effort, maintains posting consistency without burning out.

The key insight is that different content types serve different purposes. Carousels build trust. Personal stories build connection. Reels build reach. You need all three.

**Section 3 -- The Change at Day 30 (5:00-7:00):**
At day 30 I had around 180 followers. Growth was linear and slow.

I looked at which posts had driven actual follows, not just likes. Turns out 70% of my new followers came from two carousels and one Reel. Everything else generated engagement but not follows.

So I doubled down on what worked. More carousels on specific how-to topics. More Reels with a clear, specific hook.

I also started paying attention to the first line of every caption. Instagram only shows two lines before the "more" button. Those two lines determine whether someone reads the rest or scrolls.

I rewrote all my draft captions to put the most interesting or specific detail in the first sentence. Not a vague teaser. An actual statement that makes someone think "I want to know more about that."

**Section 4 -- What Did Not Work (7:00-9:00):**
Follow-for-follow does not work. I tried it for three days. The followers you get don't engage and they inflate your metrics in a way that hurts you later.

Posting every day burned me out in week two. Four times a week is the maximum I can sustain with a university schedule. Consistency over time beats frequency in bursts.

Hashtags alone don't drive growth. I use 10 to 15 per post but they've never been the primary driver of new followers. Good content shared by real people drives more discovery than hashtag traffic.

Motivational content. I tested it. It gets likes from passive scrollers. It does not get follows from people who want to build something. Know who you're trying to attract.

**CTA (9:00-9:30):**
If you're starting from zero right now, the one thing I'd focus on first is this: pick one specific topic and post about it four times a week for 30 days. Then look at which posts drove actual follows and do more of those.

Subscribe if you want to see me document the rest of this build. I post every week about what's working and what isn't.

## Script 2: Why I Publish in English and Spanish (And Why You Should Too)

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I write every piece of content twice. Once in English, once in Spanish. It takes more time, but it gives me access to two completely separate audiences that barely overlap.

In this video I'm going to explain why I made this decision, how I actually manage the workflow without it taking over my week, and whether it's worth it for you specifically.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
Most creators pick one language and stick to it. That's a reasonable default. But if you're bilingual and you're building a personal brand around business, tech, or solopreneurship, you're leaving a significant audience untapped.

I'm Kalani, I'm from Puerto Rico, and I've been building a bilingual content operation for several months now. Here's what I've learned.

**Section 1 -- The Market Opportunity (1:30-3:30):**
The English-language creator economy is massive and crowded. There are millions of people making content about building businesses online.

The Spanish-language digital business space is growing fast and has far less competition at the level of quality, specific, educational content. The audience is there. The hunger for this content is real. But most of the people who could serve that market are either posting in English because that's where the money seems to be, or they're posting generic inspiration content in Spanish that doesn't actually teach anything.

If you're bilingual and you post quality educational content in both languages, you're in a much less crowded space for the Spanish content while still competing in English.

This is not a theory. I see it in the numbers. My Spanish posts get proportionally more saves and shares than my English posts, which suggests the demand exceeds the supply.

**Section 2 -- How the Workflow Actually Works (3:30-6:00):**
I don't write everything twice from scratch. That would take too long.

My workflow:

Step 1: Write the original content in the language that feels most natural for that specific topic. For technical or AI topics, I usually start in English. For personal stories about being a student in Puerto Rico, I start in Spanish.

Step 2: Use Claude to translate, with a specific instruction: not to translate literally, but to adapt for the target audience. English and Spanish audiences have different expectations, different cultural references, different levels of familiarity with certain terms.

Step 3: Edit the translation myself. This is non-negotiable. The AI translation is a starting draft, not a finished product. I go through it and fix anything that doesn't sound natural or that loses something in translation.

The whole process adds maybe 20 minutes per piece of content. Not double the work.

**Section 3 -- What to Translate and What to Skip (6:00-8:00):**
Not everything is worth translating.

I translate carousels, longer captions, and YouTube scripts. These are high-effort pieces that deserve maximum distribution.

I don't always translate quick Reels or short posts. If something is culturally specific to one audience, forcing it into the other language often makes it worse.

The filter I use: if the value of the content is in the information, translate it. If the value is in the specific cultural context, keep it in the original language.

**Section 4 -- The Audience Building Benefit (8:00-10:00):**
Something that took me a while to notice is that the Spanish-language audience is more likely to share with their network. There's a community dynamic in Latin American digital business communities that I don't see as much in English.

When a piece of Spanish content lands well, it circulates through Telegram groups, WhatsApp threads, and friend networks in a way that English content doesn't always replicate.

That's not universal, but it's worth knowing if you're deciding whether to invest the time in bilingual content.

The other benefit is positioning. I'm one of a small number of people making this specific type of content for both markets. That's a defensible position that takes time for others to replicate.

**CTA (10:00-10:30):**
If you're bilingual and you're building a content brand, experiment with this for 30 days. Pick your three best performing pieces, translate them, publish them for the other audience, and measure the results.

If you want to see how I structure the bilingual workflow in more detail, I've got a post linked below that walks through the exact process.

## Script 3: The Content Schedule I Actually Follow Without Burnout

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I've tried every content schedule I've read about. Daily posting, batching content, spontaneous posting when inspiration hits. Most of them failed within two weeks.

What I actually follow is something I built based on what I can sustain with a full university course load, not what sounds good in theory. In this video I'll show you exactly what that looks like.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I'm Kalani. I'm a computer engineering student in Puerto Rico building a one-person digital business. I create content in English and Spanish, across Instagram, YouTube, and Twitter.

I'm not a full-time creator. I'm a student with ADHD who has to fit content creation into the margins of a life that's already full. So everything I've built is optimized for sustainability, not for maximum output.

**Section 1 -- Why Most Content Schedules Fail (1:30-3:30):**
The most common reason people burn out on content creation is that they set a schedule based on what they want to produce, not what they can actually produce consistently.

There's also a second problem: schedules built around inspiration. "I'll post when I have something good to say." That sounds reasonable. In practice, it means you post in bursts when you're energized and disappear for weeks when you're not.

The audience you're trying to build doesn't know your schedule. But they do notice when you disappear. Inconsistency signals that you might disappear permanently.

The third problem is not batching. Writing one post, publishing it, then writing the next post is the most cognitively expensive way to create content. You're paying a context-switching cost every single time.

**Section 2 -- My Actual Weekly Schedule (3:30-6:30):**
Here's what I do.

Sunday afternoon, 1 to 2 hours: Content planning. I look at the ideas I've captured during the week in my Telegram bot, pick the best 4 or 5, and decide what format each one works best in. I also write the hooks and outlines for each piece. Not the full content, just the skeleton.

Friday evening, 1 to 2 hours: Content writing. I take the skeletons from Sunday and write the full captions, carousel copy, or script drafts. This is when the actual writing happens.

Saturday morning, 30 to 45 minutes: Review and scheduling. I read everything I wrote Friday. I edit whatever sounds off. I schedule posts in Buffer for the following week.

That's it. Three sessions. About 3 to 4 hours total per week.

During the week, when ideas come up, I record a voice note or send a message to my Telegram bot. I don't try to develop ideas in the moment. I capture them and process them on Sunday.

**Section 3 -- The Batching System (6:30-8:30):**
The Sunday planning session is the most important part.

When I sit down to plan, I'm not trying to be creative. I'm making decisions. What format. What topic. What angle. What the hook is.

When I sit down to write on Friday, I'm not making decisions. I'm executing. The skeleton is already there. I just fill it in.

This separation between deciding and executing is the main thing that prevents creative burnout. You're not trying to think and write simultaneously.

For YouTube, the schedule is different. I write one script per week, usually on Saturday, using the same skeleton approach. I record when I have two or three scripts queued, so I'm not recording every week.

**Section 4 -- What to Do When the Schedule Breaks (8:30-10:00):**
During exam weeks, I don't create new content. I rely on posts I've batched in advance.

My target is to always have one week of content scheduled ahead. That buffer means one bad week doesn't create a gap in publishing.

When I fall behind, the rule is: publish something, even if it's shorter or simpler than what I normally post. A short, direct post on a topic you know well is better than silence while you wait to write something "worth it."

The schedule doesn't have to be perfect. It has to be consistent enough that your audience knows you're still there.

**CTA (10:00-10:30):**
Build a schedule based on the time you actually have, not the time you wish you had. If you can do 3 hours a week, build for 3 hours. That's enough to publish consistently across two platforms.

## Script 4: How to Repurpose One Idea Into 10 Pieces of Content

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I had one idea last month: that most people use AI tools as substitutes instead of collaborators, and that's why their output feels generic. From that one idea I produced a YouTube script, two carousels, three Instagram captions, two Twitter threads, and an email to my list.

That's not magic. It's a system. In this video I'll show you the exact system.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I'm Kalani. I build digital products and create content about solopreneurship, AI tools, and building online businesses as a student. I learned about content repurposing from Dan Koe's One Person Business framework, which I've adapted for how I work.

The core idea is that ideas are the scarce resource, not content. Once you have a solid idea, you should be able to extract maximum value from it across multiple formats and platforms.

**Section 1 -- The OPB Content Waterfall (1:30-4:00):**
The framework I use is called a content waterfall.

It starts with a long-form piece at the top: either a YouTube video, a newsletter essay, or a long Twitter thread. This is the full version of the idea. Everything below flows from this.

From the long-form piece you extract:
- The main argument becomes a carousel
- A specific data point or story from the piece becomes a short Instagram caption
- The hook becomes a Reel
- Each major section can become a separate short post
- The conclusion or lesson can become a quote image

The key is that you're not rewriting the idea. You're reformatting different parts of it for different contexts.

A carousel is not a summary of the YouTube video. It's the most visual, step-by-step part of the argument, extracted and formatted for people who won't watch the video.

A Reel is not a short version of the carousel. It's the hook, the most surprising or counterintuitive part, formatted to stop a scroll.

Each format serves a different type of consumer.

**Section 2 -- The Actual Process (4:00-7:00):**
Start with the longest format you're going to produce. For me, that's usually a YouTube script or a newsletter.

Write it fully. Don't think about repurposing yet.

After you've written it, go back through and identify:
- The strongest hook (first 2 sentences that would stop someone scrolling)
- The single best insight (the thing someone would screenshot and share)
- The clearest step-by-step section (easiest to turn into a carousel)
- The most personal story or specific moment (best for an Instagram caption)

Those four extractions give you most of your content for the week.

Then use Claude to help you format each extraction for its target platform. Not to rewrite the content. To reformat it: shorter for Twitter, more visual cues for carousels, first-person present tense for Instagram stories.

**Section 3 -- The Platforms and What Works Where (7:00-9:00):**
Instagram carousels: educational, step-by-step, save-worthy. The person reading this has 30 seconds and wants clear, actionable information.

Instagram captions under an image: personal, specific, story-based. This is where the human moment goes.

Twitter threads: arguments and lists. Start with the strongest claim and support it with evidence or examples.

YouTube: the full picture. Context, nuance, the parts that don't compress into short formats.

Email: the most direct version. No algorithm, just the reader and the content. This is where I put the most honest version of what I think.

Each platform has a different contract with the audience. The same information presented with the right format for each platform performs better than the same post copy-pasted everywhere.

**Section 4 -- Common Mistakes (9:00-10:30):**
Posting the same thing on every platform. Each platform has a different culture and a different audience expectation. Copy-paste distribution feels lazy and usually performs poorly.

Starting with the short format and trying to expand. It's much harder to expand a tweet into a YouTube script than to compress a script into a tweet. Always start long.

Repurposing before the original piece is solid. If the underlying idea is weak, the repurposed versions will also be weak. Invest in the core idea first.

**CTA (10:30-11:00):**
This week, take one idea you've already explored once, in any format, and build five pieces from it using this waterfall. See how much content you can extract from something you've already done the thinking on.

If you want a template for mapping your content waterfall, it's linked in the description.

## Script 5: Why Most Solopreneur Content Fails (And What Works Instead)

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I've been studying content from solopreneurs who are growing fast and comparing it to content from people who've been posting for a year with no traction. The difference is not production quality. It's not posting frequency. It's something more specific that I see people get wrong over and over.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I'm Kalani, I build and publish content about AI tools and digital business from Puerto Rico. I've been doing this for several months and I've made most of the mistakes I'm about to describe.

The goal of this video is not to make you feel bad about what you're currently doing. It's to give you a clear diagnostic for what might be holding your content back.

**Section 1 -- The Generic Trap (1:30-4:00):**
Most solopreneur content fails because it's generic.

What does generic mean exactly? It means the content could have been written by anyone. It doesn't reference a specific situation, a specific decision, a specific failure or success. It describes a concept without grounding it in reality.

"AI can help you save time in your business." That's generic. Everyone agrees. No one feels anything.

"I used Claude to cut my content writing time from 3 hours to 45 minutes, and here's the exact prompt structure I use." That's specific. Someone reading that either relates to the problem or is curious about the solution.

The fix is specificity. Every piece of content should contain at least one detail that couldn't have come from anyone but you. A number, a decision, a specific tool, a moment in time.

Specificity is also how you build trust. Generic content says "I know about this topic." Specific content says "I have actually done this thing."

**Section 2 -- The Wrong Audience Problem (4:00-6:00):**
A lot of solopreneur content is written for other solopreneurs who are at the same stage, or worse, for people who are already more advanced.

But if you're building an audience to eventually sell something, you need to be speaking to people who have the problem you solve, not to people who've already solved it.

Ask yourself: is this content for someone who's already doing what I do, or for someone who wants to start?

Content for people who are already doing it looks like meta-commentary on content creation, complexity for the sake of complexity, insider terms without explanation.

Content for people who want to start looks like clear first steps, honest accounts of what it actually looks like at the beginning, and practical tools and processes without assuming prior knowledge.

Neither is wrong. But they're different audiences with different needs. Pick one and stay consistent.

**Section 3 -- Performing Without Communicating (6:00-8:30):**
Some solopreneur content performs on surface metrics, likes, comments like "fire" or "this," but doesn't actually communicate anything.

This is the motivational content trap. "Bet on yourself." "You're one idea away." "Start before you're ready."

These posts are easy to like and easy to forget. They don't create a reason to follow. They don't demonstrate expertise. They don't solve a problem.

The test I use: after reading this, does the person know something they didn't know before, or could do something they couldn't do before?

If the answer is no, the content is entertainment at best and noise at worst.

That doesn't mean you can't have a perspective or an opinion. You can and should. But anchor it to something real. A story. A decision. A result. Not just a declaration.

**Section 4 -- What Actually Works (8:30-10:30):**
The content that consistently builds audiences for solopreneurs has a few things in common.

It documents real decisions and real results. Not just "here's what you should do" but "here's what I did and here's what happened."

It teaches something specific and actionable in every post. Not a theory. A thing you can do.

It has a consistent point of view. The creator's perspective is visible. You know what they care about, what they don't like, what they're trying to build.

It shows up consistently over time. Not perfectly. But regularly enough that the audience knows you're still there.

If you look at your content and it's missing any of these four things, that's the diagnostic. Start there.

**CTA (10:30-11:00):**
Go look at your last 10 posts and run them through these four criteria. Be honest. You'll know immediately what to fix.

Subscribe for more of this. I post every week about what I'm building and what I'm learning along the way.

**Approx runtime:** 10-11 min
