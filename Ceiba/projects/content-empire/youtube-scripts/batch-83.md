## SCRIPT 1: "The Honest AI Tools Tier List for Solopreneurs in 2025"

**Format:** Talking head / screen share
**Target length:** 10-12 minutes
**Hook type:** Contrarian ranking, cuts through hype

---

[HOOK]

I have used over 30 AI tools in the last two years trying to build a real one-person business. Most of them were a waste of time. Some of them genuinely changed how I work. Today I am ranking them honestly, not based on what the YouTubers are being paid to promote, but based on actual output they produced for me.

---

[INTRO]

This is not a sponsored video. I am going to tell you which tools actually saved me hours and which ones gave me busy work disguised as productivity. If you are building something on your own and you want to know where to put your time and money, this is the video.

---

[SECTION 1: THE S TIER — TOOLS THAT CHANGED MY ACTUAL OUTPUT]

Let's start at the top.

S tier means this tool permanently changed how I work. Not "I use it sometimes." I cannot imagine operating without it.

**Claude / GPT-4o for writing and thinking.** This is obvious but it needs to be said correctly. Most people use LLMs badly. They ask them to think for them. The correct use is: you do the thinking, the LLM accelerates the production of output from that thinking. When I sit down with a clear idea and a specific prompt, I can produce a first draft of a newsletter, a product outline, or a content brief in twenty minutes instead of two hours. That is real time savings.

The key I learned: the more specific your input, the closer the output is to what you need. I keep a prompt library for every recurring task. Newsletter drafts, thread rewrites, product descriptions, email sequences. Each prompt is built around my specific style and format requirements. Building that library took a few weeks. Now it saves me hours every week.

**Whisper for voice transcription.** I did not expect this one to be in S tier. But capturing ideas via voice and having them transcribed instantly changed how much of my thinking actually makes it into written form. I was losing maybe 40 percent of my best ideas because I was not near a keyboard when they happened. Voice note to text removes that barrier.

---

[SECTION 2: THE A TIER — STRONG TOOLS WITH A LEARNING CURVE]

A tier means: genuinely useful, but it took time to figure out how to use them well.

**n8n for automation.** This is where you connect everything else. n8n lets you build workflows that move data and trigger actions between your tools without writing much code. The learning curve is real. The first few weeks I built workflows that broke constantly. But once I understood the logic, I could automate things like: new YouTube video publishes, trigger a process that extracts the transcript, summarizes it, and drafts a newsletter issue. That workflow alone saves me two to three hours per video.

The barrier: you need to think in systems to use it well. If you have never thought about your business as a series of inputs and outputs that can be mapped, n8n will feel overwhelming. Start with one simple automation. Learn that one. Then build.

**Runway / CapCut AI for video editing.** I am not a video editor. I have always avoided video content because the editing felt like it required skills I did not have. AI-assisted editing changed that. Auto-captions, auto-cut on silence, basic B-roll suggestions. These do not make you a professional editor but they make the barrier to shipping video content much lower.

---

[SECTION 3: THE B TIER — USEFUL BUT NOT ESSENTIAL]

B tier: works as advertised, but I do not find myself reaching for it daily.

**Midjourney for images.** Genuinely good at what it does. Produces professional-looking product covers and thumbnails. The issue is that the prompting to get consistent results for a specific brand aesthetic takes significant time investment. Once you have your style prompts locked in, it is fast and reliable. Before that, it is a time sink.

**Perplexity for research.** Faster than Google for research tasks where I need sources. I use it for competitive research and fact-checking. Not for writing. Not for ideation. Just for finding and citing real information quickly.

---

[SECTION 4: THE C AND D TIER — OVERHYPED AND UNDERPERFORMING]

C tier: I tried it, it did not save me time in any measurable way.

Most "all-in-one AI business tools" land here. They promise to replace your entire stack. In practice they do each function less well than a dedicated tool, and you end up doing more cleanup work than if you had just used the right tool for each job.

D tier: tools that created more work than they saved. The main category here is AI social media managers that claim to generate and schedule all your content. The output quality is low enough that editing each piece to a publishable standard takes almost as long as writing it from scratch, and the posts do not sound like you, which defeats the purpose of building a personal brand.

---

[SECTION 5: THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND THE TIER LIST]

Here is what separates S and A tier tools from everything below them.

The tools that actually work well for solopreneurs have two properties: they accelerate a step you already do well, and they reduce friction at a specific point in your workflow.

The tools that disappoint are trying to replace a step entirely. Replace your thinking, replace your voice, replace your judgment. That does not work at the level of output quality that builds a real audience and a real business.

Use AI to go faster. Not to go somewhere different.

## SCRIPT 2: "I Built a Digital Product Business While Studying Engineering. Here's the Real Report."

**Format:** Talking head / narrative
**Target length:** 11-13 minutes
**Hook type:** Personal report with real numbers

---

[HOOK]

I am a computer engineering student in Puerto Rico. I started building a digital product business six months ago with zero audience, zero sales history, and no money to put into ads. I want to give you the actual report, with real numbers, real mistakes, and what I would do differently.

---

[INTRO]

Most "building from zero" videos skip the boring middle part. The part where nothing is happening and you are starting to wonder if the whole thing was a mistake. I am not going to skip that. This is the honest version.

---

[SECTION 1: THE STARTING CONDITIONS]

Here is what I had at the beginning.

Zero followers on any platform. Zero revenue from anything I had built. A folder with 22 digital products I had created over the previous year that had never been listed anywhere. I kept telling myself they were not ready.

One of the most honest things I can tell you about the early stage: "not ready" is almost always "afraid." The products were functional. They solved real problems. I had not listed them because listing them meant finding out whether they would sell, and not knowing felt safer than knowing.

The shift that got things moving was not a mindset breakthrough. It was a practical decision. I set a rule: one product listed per week, regardless of how I felt about it. Execution on a schedule, not on inspiration.

---

[SECTION 2: MONTHS ONE AND TWO — MOSTLY SETUP WITH ONE REAL LESSON]

Month one was almost entirely setup work. Gumroad account, product covers, landing pages, figuring out which platform I was going to lead with for content. I spent probably sixty percent of my time on things that did not produce output.

The lesson: infrastructure work feels important because it is real, visible progress that does not require testing whether your ideas are good. It is a form of avoidance disguised as productivity. The test is simple: is this work getting something in front of a potential buyer? If not, it is infrastructure work and it can be minimized.

Month two: first sales. Three products, eleven total sales, $87 in revenue. Small number. But real proof that strangers would exchange money for something I made. That proof changed how I thought about the whole operation.

---

[SECTION 3: MONTHS THREE AND FOUR — WHERE THINGS GOT HARD]

Month three was the hardest. Revenue did not grow meaningfully. I was posting consistently but the audience growth was slow. I started questioning the content strategy, the product selection, everything.

What I learned during that period: the answer to slow growth is almost never "change the strategy." It is almost always "improve the execution of the current strategy." I was posting consistently but the content was not sharp enough. The topics were too broad. The perspective was not specific enough.

The adjustment I made: I stopped trying to make content that would appeal to the widest possible audience and started making content that would be specifically useful to one type of person. A computer engineering student building a business. A person with ADHD trying to manage a content operation. Someone building their first digital product with no audience and no budget.

When I got that specific, engagement improved. Not dramatically. But measurably.

---

[SECTION 4: MONTHS FIVE AND SIX — WHAT STARTED TO COMPOUND]

By month five, the content compound effect became visible. Older posts were still being found and followed. The email list was growing from the free lead magnet. The products I had listed early were accumulating reviews that reduced friction for new buyers.

Revenue month six: significantly above month one. The rate of growth was directly correlated with the consistency of the previous months, not with any specific breakthrough moment.

The thing nobody tells you about compounding: it feels like nothing is working for a long time, and then it starts to feel like everything is working at once. The middle period is the test. Most people quit in the middle period.

---

[SECTION 5: WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY]

If I started today with the knowledge I have now, three things I would change.

First: list the first product in week one. Not month two. One product, even imperfect, with a sales page and a price. Everything else is research until money changes hands.

Second: pick the content platform where my target buyer already lives and commit to it exclusively for the first 90 days. I split attention between platforms early and grew slowly everywhere instead of quickly on one.

Third: start collecting emails from day one. The platform distributes your content. The email list is yours. I built a list too late. The list converts better than any other channel I have.

## SCRIPT 3: "The One-Person Content System That Replaced My Need for a Team"

**Format:** Tutorial / walkthrough
**Target length:** 12-14 minutes
**Hook type:** System reveal, practical framework

---

[HOOK]

Running a content operation across four platforms as one person used to feel impossible. I tried hiring freelancers. I tried posting less. Neither worked. Then I built a system that lets me produce a week of content across every platform in about four hours. I am going to walk you through exactly how it works.

---

[INTRO]

This is not a video about working harder. It is about designing a workflow so that each piece of work you create gets used five to eight times instead of once. The principle is called the content waterfall. I want to show you how I built mine and what it produces.

---

[SECTION 1: THE CORE PRINCIPLE — ONE SOURCE, MANY OUTPUTS]

Everything in the system starts from one piece of long-form content per week. That is the source material. Everything else is derived from it.

The long-form source can be a YouTube video, a podcast episode, a newsletter, or a long written post. For me, the YouTube video is the source. I record one video per week. That video contains everything: the framework, the examples, the nuance, the full argument.

From that one video, here is what I extract:
- Full written transcript (auto-generated with Whisper)
- Newsletter issue (the core argument, summarized and restructured)
- Five to seven Twitter threads (each major point from the video becomes one thread)
- Three Instagram carousels (visual versions of the frameworks and lists)
- Two LinkedIn posts (professional framing of the same content)
- Short-form clips for Reels and YouTube Shorts (punchy moments pulled from the video)

One video. Fourteen pieces of content. That is the waterfall.

---

[SECTION 2: THE PRODUCTION WORKFLOW — STEP BY STEP]

Let me walk through the actual workflow day by day.

Monday: Record the video. No editing. Raw recording into a folder.

Tuesday: Whisper transcribes the video automatically via n8n. The transcript is cleaned and fed into a Claude prompt that generates the newsletter draft, the thread outlines, and the carousel text. I review and edit each output. This takes about 90 minutes.

Wednesday: I edit the video. This is the only part of the workflow I have not automated. I use CapCut for basic editing, auto-captions, and the short-form clips. About two hours.

Thursday: Video goes live. Newsletter goes out. Threads are loaded into a scheduling tool and queued for the next seven days. Carousels are scheduled.

Friday: The week's content is live and scheduled. I spend Friday reviewing what performed and noting adjustments for the following week's video topic.

Total time: approximately four hours of active work beyond the recording itself.

---

[SECTION 3: THE TOOLS AND HOW THEY CONNECT]

The tools in this workflow are not expensive or complicated.

Whisper handles transcription. It is either via an API integration in n8n or through a lightweight app that runs locally. Fully automated once the video file is in the right folder.

Claude handles reformatting. The transcript goes in with a detailed prompt. The newsletter draft, thread outlines, and carousel copy come out. I edit each output for voice and accuracy. The editing is faster than writing from scratch.

n8n handles the connections. When a video file appears in a specific folder, the n8n workflow triggers. The transcription runs. The transcript moves to the LLM. The outputs go to the right document. This is one workflow with about fifteen nodes. It took a full day to build. It saves me two hours every single week.

A scheduling tool handles distribution. Every output is loaded, reviewed, and scheduled once per week. No daily posting decisions.

---

[SECTION 4: THE QUALITY CONTROL STEP MOST PEOPLE SKIP]

The step that makes this system produce good content instead of mediocre content: the editing layer.

Every AI-generated output is a draft, not a final product. I read every newsletter before it sends. I read every thread before it schedules. I change things that do not sound like me. I add the specific observations and examples that only I have from my actual experience.

This step takes about thirty minutes per output type. It is not optional. The difference between AI content that builds an audience and AI content that blends into the background is whether a human with a specific perspective touched it after generation.

Do not let the automation remove the human layer. The human layer is the product.

---

[SECTION 5: WHAT THIS SYSTEM ACTUALLY PRODUCES OVER TIME]

After six months of running this system, the compounding effects are visible.

The YouTube channel has a library of searchable videos that continue to send new subscribers. The newsletter has an archive that I link to in new issues, building authority over time. The Twitter archive means new followers can scroll back and see consistent, substantive content.

Long-form content has a longer half-life than short-form. A well-made YouTube video gets recommended for months. A Twitter thread from six months ago still gets retweets. The investment in the source material compounds in a way that individual short-form posts do not.
