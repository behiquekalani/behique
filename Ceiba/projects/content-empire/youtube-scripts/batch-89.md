## Script 1: How to Start a Digital Business With Zero Followers (The Honest Timeline)

**Target length:** 1,500 words (~10 min)

---

### Hook (0:00-0:30)
The question I get asked most often is: how do I start a digital business when I have no audience? And the honest answer is that the question itself is slightly wrong. You don't need an audience to start. You need an audience to scale. Those are different problems with different timelines and different solutions.

I started with zero followers on every platform. I have products that sell consistently. I'm going to walk you through the actual timeline of what that looks like, what the early stages feel like, and what actually moves you from zero to your first consistent revenue.

---

### Intro (0:30-1:30)
This video is not going to tell you about the overnight success story or the viral moment that changed everything. There was no viral moment. What there was is a slow accumulation of the right decisions repeated consistently over about eight months.

I run Behike. It's a one-person digital business focused on AI tools, productivity systems, and digital products for people building independent businesses. Everything I'm about to share is from that experience, not from theory.

If you're starting from zero right now, this is the video I wish I had watched at the beginning.

---

### Section 1: The Zero-Audience Problem Is Actually Three Different Problems (1:30-4:00)
When people say they have no audience, they usually mean one of three different things. And the solution to each is different.

The first problem is no followers on any platform. You have no distribution channel. Nobody sees what you publish.

The second problem is followers but no trust. You have some following but they don't engage, don't buy, and don't share your content. The numbers exist but the relationship doesn't.

The third problem is trust but no offer. You have an audience that likes you but nothing to sell them. This is actually the best position to be in, but a surprising number of people get stuck here.

If your problem is the first one, which is the most common starting point, the solution is not to post more content on your own channel and wait for it to compound. The compound effect takes time that you might not have emotionally or financially. The faster path is to go where your audience already exists.

---

### Section 2: Going Where Your Audience Already Exists (4:00-7:00)
Every niche has existing communities: Reddit forums, Facebook groups, Discord servers, YouTube comment sections, LinkedIn groups, niche forums. These communities have concentrated populations of people who already care about the topic you're building in.

The strategy is not to promote your product in these spaces. Most of them have rules against that and even where they don't, it doesn't work. The strategy is to become genuinely useful. Answer questions thoroughly. Share knowledge that would normally be behind a paywall. Demonstrate expertise through the quality of your contributions.

This does three things. First, it builds your reputation in the community faster than any amount of content on your own platform would. Second, it teaches you what your potential buyers actually struggle with, which is the most valuable market research you can do. Third, it creates a warm path to your own platform: people who find your answers useful look at your profile, follow your account, and become your first genuine audience.

This is not a hack. It takes real effort and real expertise. But if you have the expertise, this is the fastest path from zero to first audience without paying for ads.

---

### Section 3: The First Ninety Days Without Followers (7:00-9:00)
The first thirty days should be entirely focused on understanding the market. No product. No promotion. Read everything in your niche. Join every relevant community. Find the ten most common questions that people ask and the ten problems that people consistently struggle to solve.

The second thirty days should be focused on contributing. Start answering questions. Start publishing content on your own platform, but low stakes: short posts, observations, things you've learned. The goal is not to go viral. The goal is to develop the habit of publishing and to start building a record of your thinking.

The third thirty days should be focused on validating. Take the problem you've identified from your market research and validate it with real people before building anything. Post about the problem and see if it resonates. Ask your small audience directly what they'd want to learn. Pre-sell before you produce.

By the end of ninety days, you have market intelligence, the beginning of an audience relationship, and a validated product idea. That's not revenue yet, but it's a foundation that revenue can actually be built on.

---

### Outro with CTA (9:00-10:00)
The honest timeline for going from zero to consistent revenue in a digital business is between six and twelve months of consistent execution. Not six to twelve months of occasional effort.

That timeline is not discouraging if you understand what's happening at each stage. The first three months are the hardest because you're building without visible results. The compound effect doesn't show up until month four or five. By month twelve, the business can look completely different from where it started.

## Script 2: You Already Have Knowledge Worth Selling. Here's How to Turn It Into a Product.

**Target length:** 1,400 words (~9 min)

---

### Hook (0:00-0:30)
Most people who should be selling their knowledge aren't. Not because they don't have anything worth selling, but because they've convinced themselves that what they know is either too obvious or too niche to be worth packaging.

Both of those beliefs are wrong, and I can demonstrate why in the next ten minutes.

---

### Intro (0:30-1:30)
The knowledge economy runs on one observation: what is obvious to you is not obvious to someone who hasn't spent years doing what you've done.

You don't need to be the world's leading expert on a topic to sell knowledge. You need to be meaningfully ahead of the person who has the problem you solve. That gap is the value. And for most people, that gap is larger than they realize.

I'm going to walk you through the process of identifying what you know that's worth selling, how to package it, and how to price it in a way that reflects the actual value you deliver.

---

### Section 1: The Knowledge Audit (1:30-4:00)
Most people can't articulate what they know in a way that connects to market value. The knowledge audit is the exercise that changes that.

Take thirty minutes and answer these four questions in writing.

What can you do that takes most people much longer to figure out? This is not about what you're the best at globally. It's about where you have a significant head start compared to a beginner.

What do people ask you for help with? Not what you think you're good at, but what other people consistently come to you for. That external signal is more accurate than your own self-assessment.

What have you figured out by failing at it first? Practical knowledge from lived experience carries more value than academic knowledge precisely because it includes the mistakes and the recovery.

What system or process do you use that produces results others struggle to get consistently? A repeatable system is more valuable than a one-time achievement.

Your answers to those four questions are your raw material. You're not looking for a single answer. You're looking for the intersection between what you know deeply, what others need, and what you can explain clearly.

---

### Section 2: Packaging Knowledge Into a Product (4:00-7:00)
Once you've identified the knowledge, the packaging question is which format matches the type of knowledge and the type of buyer.

There are three formats that work well for one-person digital businesses without large teams or production infrastructure.

The written guide or ebook works best for knowledge that can be explained sequentially. A step-by-step process, a framework, a collection of principles with examples. The advantage is low production cost and high margin. The disadvantage is that it requires the buyer to be willing to read, which is a smaller percentage of the market than video.

The template or toolkit works best for procedural knowledge. Things that people need to do repeatedly and that can be given a structure. A spreadsheet template, a Notion system, a prompt library, a set of swipe files. The advantage is that the value is immediately apparent and the format is inherently practical.

The course or workshop works best for knowledge that requires demonstration and sequencing. The advantage is higher price point. The disadvantage is higher production cost and a longer sales cycle.

For most people starting with zero audience, the written guide or template is the right starting point. Low cost to produce, fast to market, easy to update.

---

### Section 3: Pricing Your Knowledge Correctly (7:00-8:30)
The most common pricing mistake for knowledge products is pricing based on how long it took to create rather than what result it produces for the buyer.

The correct question is: what is the value of the outcome this product helps the buyer achieve?

If your guide helps someone set up an automation that saves them five hours per week, and their time is worth thirty dollars per hour, that's six hundred dollars per month in value. A guide priced at forty-seven dollars is a rational purchase for a buyer in that situation.

The calculation doesn't work in every niche the same way. Some outcomes are harder to quantify. But the principle holds: the price ceiling is determined by the value of the outcome, not the hours of effort that went into creating the product.

Start at a price that feels slightly high. You can always discount. It's much harder to raise a price that buyers have anchored to.

---

### Outro with CTA (8:30-9:30)
The knowledge you've accumulated has market value. The question is whether you're going to let it sit as something only you benefit from, or turn it into something other people can benefit from too.

The path from knowledge to product is not complicated. It requires clarity about what you know, a decision about format, and a commitment to finishing something imperfect rather than perfecting something unfinished.

## Script 3: n8n Automation for Solopreneurs: The Workflows That Save Me 10+ Hours Per Week

**Target length:** 1,800 words (~12 min)

---

### Hook (0:00-0:30)
I spend about two hours per week on tasks that most people spend ten to fifteen hours on. Not because I work faster, but because I built automations that handle the repetitive parts while I focus on the work that actually requires my attention.

The tool is n8n. It's free if you self-host it. And the workflows I'm about to show you don't require any coding background to understand or implement.

---

### Intro (0:30-1:30)
n8n is a workflow automation tool. If you've heard of Zapier or Make, n8n does the same thing, with one critical difference: you can run it on your own server at a fraction of the cost.

I host mine on Railway for about five dollars per month. That's my entire automation infrastructure cost. For context, Zapier charges sixty-five dollars per month for the same volume of tasks.

I'm going to walk you through the five workflows that have had the biggest impact on my business. I'll explain what each one does, why I built it, and approximately how long it would have taken me to do that work manually.

---

### Section 1: The Idea Capture Workflow (1:30-4:00)
The problem this solves: I had ideas constantly and captured maybe forty percent of them because the friction of writing them down manually was too high.

The workflow: I send a voice message to a Telegram bot. The bot receives the audio file and sends it to OpenAI's Whisper API for transcription. The transcription comes back as text. n8n takes that text, adds a timestamp and a category based on keywords, and saves it to my notes database.

Time this replaces: about fifteen minutes per day of manually typing or transcribing ideas. That's about ninety minutes per week.

What changed: my idea capture rate went from around forty percent to nearly one hundred percent. The quality of the ideas I work with is higher because I'm capturing things in the moment rather than trying to reconstruct them later.

How to build this: the n8n workflow has four nodes. A Telegram trigger, an HTTP request node calling the Whisper API, a function node for parsing and categorizing, and a database write node. Total setup time is about two hours if you're doing it for the first time.

---

### Section 2: The Content Distribution Workflow (4:00-6:30)
The problem this solves: I was manually posting the same content to multiple platforms, reformatting each time, which took thirty to forty-five minutes per post.

The workflow: I write the post once in a specific format in Notion. When I change the status field to "ready to publish," a webhook triggers n8n. n8n reads the content, formats it appropriately for each platform, and schedules posts to Twitter, LinkedIn, and sends me a formatted version for Instagram via Telegram. I still manually post to Instagram because the image-based format requires visual decisions, but the caption and hashtags are already formatted.

Time this replaces: about forty-five minutes per day across all platforms. That's five hours per week.

What changed: the consistency of my posting schedule improved significantly because the friction of distribution dropped. When distribution is easy, you're more likely to follow through.

---

### Section 3: The Newsletter Compilation Workflow (6:30-8:30)
The problem this solves: compiling the weekly newsletter used to take about two hours because I was manually going through bookmarks, notes, and content to find what I wanted to share.

The workflow: throughout the week, when I read something worth sharing, I send it to a dedicated Telegram bot with a short note. n8n saves it to a structured database with the URL, my note, and the date. On Saturday morning, n8n compiles everything from that week into a formatted draft in my email tool, organized by category: tools, ideas, resources.

Time this replaces: about ninety minutes per week of compilation work. The writing is still mine, but the research aggregation is automatic.

---

### Section 4: The Product Sale Notification and Fulfillment Workflow (8:30-10:30)
The problem this solves: when someone bought a product, I was manually monitoring Gumroad and manually sending follow-up emails. That's fine for one sale per day. It breaks down when you have multiple sales.

The workflow: Gumroad sends a webhook to n8n on each sale. n8n logs the sale to a spreadsheet with buyer email, product, price, and date. It then triggers a welcome email sequence in my email tool. For digital products, Gumroad handles delivery automatically, so the workflow is really about the data and the follow-up sequence rather than fulfillment itself.

Time this replaces: about fifteen minutes per sale for manual logging and email. At ten sales per week, that's two and a half hours.

---

### Section 5: The Weekly Review Workflow (10:30-11:30)
The problem this solves: my weekly review required pulling numbers from five different places manually before I could actually analyze them.

The workflow: every Sunday morning, n8n pulls data from Gumroad (sales), from my email tool (open rates, list growth), from my social accounts via their APIs (follower growth, engagement), and from my notes database (tasks completed). It compiles everything into a formatted report and sends it to my Telegram. By the time I sit down for my review, all the data is already in one place.

Time this replaces: about thirty to forty-five minutes of data gathering per week.

---

### Outro with CTA (11:30-12:00)
Those five workflows together save me about ten hours per week. That's time I redirect to content creation, product development, and learning.

The total investment was about fifteen hours to build all five workflows, spread over about three weeks. That's already paid off in the first month.
