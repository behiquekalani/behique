## Script 1: The eBay Dropshipping Research Method Nobody Talks About

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Everyone tells you to use Zik Analytics or Terapeak. Those tools are fine. But the method that actually found my best-performing products costs nothing and takes 40 minutes. Reddit plus eBay completed listings. I'll show you exactly how it works.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
There's a problem with most product research tools for eBay dropshipping. They show you what's selling now, which means by the time you list it, twenty other dropshippers are already competing on the same item at the same price. You're always chasing.

What I want instead is to find products that are about to trend, or products in low-competition niches where buyers exist but sellers are sparse. That's what this method finds. It's slower than a dashboard. It's also more reliable.

**Section 1 -- Starting with Reddit (1:30-3:30):**
Reddit is where buyers talk before they become buyers. Specifically, I look at product subreddits, hobby subreddits, and "what should I buy" style threads.

The search I run is: a subreddit related to a product category, sorted by top posts of the past month, filtered for posts asking where to buy something or recommending a product. I write down every product name that appears more than twice.

Then I cross-reference with a secondary subreddit. If the same product is being discussed in two separate communities with different demographics, that's a signal. It means demand is broad enough to support sales volume.

I'm specifically looking for products that have online discussion but no obvious dominant retailer. If every post links to Amazon, that's not my market. If people are linking to random Etsy stores, specialty shops, or saying "I can never find this anywhere," that's exactly where I want to be.

**Section 2 -- Validating with eBay completed listings (3:30-5:30):**
eBay completed listings are free and they show you what actually sold versus what just got listed. That distinction is everything.

I take the product names from my Reddit research and search them on eBay. Then I filter for completed listings. I look at three numbers: sell-through rate (how many sold versus how many were listed), average sale price, and how recently they sold.

A product I'll actually list has to meet three criteria. At least 50 percent sell-through rate in the past 30 days. Average sale price of $20 or more. At least five recent sales, meaning within the last two weeks.

If a product shows 80 percent sell-through but only four total sales, the market might be too small. If it shows 20 percent sell-through but 200 listings, there's too much competition for a new dropshipper without a feedback score.

The sweet spot is a product with 60 to 80 percent sell-through, 15 to 40 total listings, and consistent sales across multiple sellers rather than one dominant seller.

**Section 3 -- Supplier check on AliExpress (5:30-7:00):**
Once a product passes the eBay test, I check AliExpress.

I search the product name and sort by orders. I look at the top three suppliers. I check: does the price allow for at least 30 percent margin after eBay fees (roughly 13 percent) and PayPal or payment processing fees (roughly 3 percent). That means I need to buy it for about 55 percent or less of my eBay sale price.

I also check the supplier's shipping time to the US. Anything over 15 business days is a risk. I prefer suppliers with 8 to 12 business day delivery and at least 4.5 star ratings with a minimum of 100 orders on that specific product variant.

If the product passes margin and shipping checks, it's on my test list.

**Section 4 -- The test listing process (7:00-9:00):**
I don't scale before testing. I list one item at the price the completed listings data supports. Not lower to try to win sales. At market rate.

I run the listing for 14 days. If it sells at least once in 14 days, I consider it validated and add it to my active inventory. If it gets views but no sales, I adjust the title or price. If it gets no views, the SEO is wrong and I need to rebuild the listing from scratch using better keyword data.

This 14-day single-listing test has saved me from scaling products that looked good on paper but didn't move in practice. The research narrows the field. The test confirms the winner.

**Section 5 -- What to do with a validated product (9:00-11:00):**
Once a product sells, I run the Reddit research again but focused specifically on variations of that product. Color variants, size variants, adjacent products that people who bought the original also want.

A single winning product becomes a product family. A product family in the same niche starts building category authority on my eBay store, which helps the algorithm surface my listings more.

I document every winning product in a spreadsheet: product name, supplier link, eBay listing link, current sell-through rate, margin, and notes on performance. I review this spreadsheet weekly and cut products that drop below 40 percent sell-through.

This whole cycle -- Reddit signal, eBay validation, supplier check, test listing, scale -- takes about 40 minutes per product in research phase and 15 minutes to create the listing. For the return on that time, it's one of the highest leverage activities in the eBay dropshipping process.

## Script 2: How to Write an eBay Listing That Actually Sells

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Most eBay dropshipping advice focuses on finding products. The listing itself gets maybe two minutes of attention. That's a mistake. Two listings for the same product at the same price can have radically different conversion rates based entirely on how the listing is written. Here's how I write mine.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I want to walk through the anatomy of a high-converting eBay listing. Not theory. The exact process I follow every time, from title to description to photo selection.

The goal of a listing is not to describe a product. It is to rank in search and then convert the click into a sale. Those are two separate jobs. The title handles the first. The photos and description handle the second. Most people conflate them and do both badly.

**Section 1 -- Title research (1:30-3:30):**
The title is 80 characters of keyword real estate. Every word needs to earn its place.

I start by searching my product on eBay and sorting by best match. I look at the top five sold listings. I'm not copying their titles. I'm extracting their keyword choices. What specific words do they use. What modifiers. What specs.

Then I run a secondary search using eBay's cassini search terms. A simple way to do this is to type your product into the eBay search bar without hitting enter and read the autocomplete suggestions. Each one is a search term real buyers are using. I pick the two or three highest-volume ones that are relevant and make sure they're in my title.

A strong title structure for most products is: [brand or material] + [product name] + [key spec] + [use case or variant]. No punctuation. No words like "nice," "great," or "wow." Those eat character space without adding search value.

Example of a weak title: "Vintage Style Leather Journal Nice Gift"
Example of a stronger title: "Leather Journal Handmade Vintage A5 Writing Notebook Diary Travel Sketchbook"

The second title has more search coverage, more buyer intent keywords, and no filler.

**Section 2 -- Photos (3:30-5:30):**
I use the supplier's photos for the main listing image. Not because they're the best photos. Because they're the product on a clean white background, which is what eBay's algorithm favors for the primary image slot and what buyers expect when they're comparison shopping.

For additional images I look for photos that show scale, context, and any included accessories. A phone case next to a phone. A bag next to a person. A notebook open to show the paper quality. These photos answer the questions buyers have before they need to ask them.

Images I never use: low resolution photos, photos with watermarks, photos that show the AliExpress branding or supplier name. Any of those can get a listing flagged or suppress conversion.

If the supplier photos are genuinely poor quality, I order the product myself, photograph it, and use those. That also lets me write a description from first-hand experience, which reads completely differently.

**Section 3 -- Description structure (5:30-8:00):**
eBay descriptions are not read the way people read articles. They're scanned. Your description needs to deliver value in the first three seconds of a scan.

I start with the three most important specs or features as a short bullet list. No intro paragraph. Bullets immediately.

Then I write two to three sentences of plain explanation: what the product is, who it's for, what problem it solves. Not marketing copy. Plain description.

Then a second set of bullets covering: dimensions or specs, materials, what's included, shipping time estimate, and my return policy.

I end with a brief note that reads like a human wrote it. "I personally review every supplier I use. If you have any questions before purchasing, message me and I'll respond within a few hours." This signals to the buyer that a real person is on the other end of this listing.

The description should never use all caps, should never include irrelevant keywords (keyword stuffing), and should never make claims the product can't back up.

**Section 4 -- Pricing and free shipping (8:00-9:30):**
I always offer free shipping. I build the shipping cost into the item price. Buyers consistently prefer a $19.99 item with free shipping over a $15.99 item with $4 shipping even though the math is identical. This is buyer psychology. Don't fight it.

For price, I start at the median of the completed listings data from my research. Not the lowest. Median. If I'm new to a niche and have limited feedback, I might price five to ten percent below median initially to get the first sales and build my feedback score. Once I have 20 to 30 feedback at 100 percent, I bring the price back to median.

I revisit pricing every two weeks. If a product starts selling faster, the market is telling me I can raise the price. If it slows down, I check whether competition increased or whether the price shifted.

## Script 3: My First 90 Days Selling on eBay: Real Numbers

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I want to give you the actual numbers from my first 90 days on eBay. Not a highlight reel. The actual trajectory, including the months where nothing worked and the specific change that turned it around.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
Most "results" content either shows the good and skips the bad, or shows the bad to seem relatable and then reveals a dramatic turnaround. I'm not doing either. I'm going to give you month-by-month numbers with the specific reasons behind each result.

I started selling on eBay as a product validation pipeline. My thesis was: test products on eBay first. If they sell there, they're worth building a Shopify page for. That thesis is still intact, but the first 90 days tested it more than I expected.

**Section 1 -- Month 1: Getting started and making all the mistakes (1:30-4:00):**
Month 1 revenue: $0. That's not a dramatic opener. That's the reality.

I spent the first three weeks of month one doing research instead of listing. I read every dropshipping guide I could find. I watched hours of YouTube content. I built a spreadsheet. I did everything except put a product on eBay.

Week four I listed six products. All of them were chosen based on what I thought would sell, not based on what the completed listings data showed. I priced them below the market because I was afraid to compete at full price. None of them sold in that month.

The lesson I took from month one: the research to execution ratio was completely wrong. I spent 80 percent of my time researching and 20 percent doing. It should have been the opposite.

**Section 2 -- Month 2: First sales and first problems (4:00-6:30):**
Month 2 revenue: $67.

I changed my approach. I spent one week doing focused product research using completed listings data, picked four products, listed them all in one day, and left them alone for two weeks.

Three of the four didn't sell in that period. One sold twice. The one that sold was a small household tool I'd found through Reddit. The Reddit signal was right. The other three products I'd chosen based on just the eBay data with no community signal backing them up.

Month two also introduced my first problem: a buyer dispute. One of my sales had a supplier who shipped slower than expected and the buyer opened an item not received case. I resolved it, refunded the buyer, and absorbed the loss. That was a $14 cost and a useful education. I immediately updated my supplier criteria to require faster shipping commitments.

**Section 3 -- Month 3: The system starts working (6:30-9:00):**
Month 3 revenue: $312.

I applied the Reddit-plus-completed-listings method consistently for the whole month. I listed fourteen products total, nine of which came directly from products discussed in Reddit communities. Eight of the nine Reddit-sourced products sold at least once. One of the non-Reddit products sold once.

The ratio held. Community signal plus completed listings data was more reliable than completed listings data alone.

I also raised my prices in month three. I had been pricing conservatively out of habit. I went back through all my active listings and repriced them at the median of recent completed sales. Three of my listings sold faster after the price increase, which confirmed what I'd read about low prices signaling low quality.

**Section 4 -- What the numbers mean (9:00-11:00):**
$379 total over 90 days. Not financial independence. But enough to confirm the model works.

More importantly, I had a replicable process by the end of month three. Research, validate, list, monitor, cut or scale. That process is the actual asset. The $379 is just proof it runs.

The products that sold consistently in month three I moved to a tracking list for potential Shopify expansion. That's the pipeline working as designed.

My current focus is increasing the number of listings that pass the research bar and reducing the time per listing from about 30 minutes to 15. The bottleneck right now is listing creation speed, not product research quality.

If you're in your first 30 days on eBay and wondering if you're doing it right, drop your question in the comments. I check them regularly. And the product research spreadsheet I mentioned is linked below.

## Script 4: Why Most eBay Dropshippers Fail in Month 2

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Month one on eBay feels like progress even when nothing is selling. You're learning the platform, setting up accounts, doing research. Month two is where the real filter happens. Most people who start eBay dropshipping quit in month two. I want to explain the specific reasons why.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I've talked to people who tried eBay dropshipping and quit. I've also read through a lot of post-mortems in dropshipping communities. The reasons people fail in month two are not random. They're predictable. And most of them have nothing to do with the products they chose.

This video is about the actual failure points. Not generic advice. Specific patterns I've seen and experienced.

**Section 1 -- The research addiction problem (1:30-3:30):**
The most common failure mode is continuing to research instead of listing.

Month one gives you permission to research. You're new. You don't know the platform. Research feels productive. By month two, that permission should be expiring. You should be listing products and learning from actual data.

The people who fail in month two often do so by extending their month-one research habits. They find a tool they're not fully using yet. They decide they need to understand the algorithm better before listing. They keep waiting for the perfect product before they start.

This is not laziness. It's anxiety managing itself as preparation. The problem is that preparation without execution generates no feedback. No feedback means no learning. No learning means no improvement.

The fix is a listing quota. Pick a number. My early goal was two new listings per week. Not a research goal. A listing goal. If you meet the listing goal, the research is sufficient. If you can't meet the listing goal because you feel unprepared, that's the signal to reduce research time and increase action time.

**Section 2 -- Scaling too fast after one win (3:30-5:30):**
The opposite failure: you get a sale in month one or early month two. You get excited. You immediately list fifteen products in the same category using the same supplier.

Then the supplier delays shipping on one order. You get one bad review. Your account metrics drop. Now all fifteen listings are ranking lower because of one supplier problem.

This is not a theoretical risk. It happened to me in a smaller way and I've watched it sink other sellers completely.

The rule I follow is never scale a supplier until you've processed at least five orders through them without issues. One or two successful orders doesn't tell you how they perform under volume. Five does.

When I expand into a new product category, I use at most two suppliers for the first ten listings. Once one of them has a clean track record across at least five orders, I'll increase the number of products from that supplier.

**Section 3 -- Ignoring account metrics (5:30-7:30):**
eBay measures five things that directly affect your listing visibility: transaction defect rate, late shipment rate, cases closed without seller resolution, tracking upload rate, and late tracking upload rate.

Month two is when new sellers start getting defects because they're handling real volume for the first time. A buyer dispute in month one was a learning experience. Three buyer disputes in month two without a process for handling them can push your account into "below standard" status, which suppresses every listing you have.

The fix is having a clear process before something goes wrong. Know exactly what you'll do when a buyer says the item hasn't arrived. Know how quickly you'll respond to messages. Know your refund threshold. Most of these situations are resolved cleanly when you have a default response ready and you act within 24 hours.

Defects on eBay are survivable if you catch them early. An account in "below standard" status for more than a few months is much harder to recover.

**Section 4 -- Wrong expectations for month two revenue (7:30-9:30):**
Month two revenue expectations is probably the most common psychological failure point.

Sellers who read about people making $3,000 per month on eBay and then make $47 in month two feel like they're failing. They're not failing. They're in month two.

The actual trajectory for a methodical eBay dropshipper starting from zero looks something like this. Month one: 0 to 1 sales. Month two: 5 to 20 sales. Month three: 15 to 50 sales. The slope starts small and steepens once you have feedback, understand your niche, and have a proven product list.

If you compare your month-two performance to someone's month-twelve performance, you will always feel like quitting. Compare yourself to your month-one performance. If you went from zero sales to any sales, you learned something real.

**Section 5 -- The one thing that actually predicts month three success (9:30-11:00):**
Did you build a system in month two or did you just sell a few things.

A system means: you have a documented product research process. You have a listing template. You have a supplier vetting checklist. You have a metric review cadence.

Sellers who treat month two like an experiment but document what they learn become month-three sellers. Sellers who treat month two like a grind through random products and quit when it's hard are the ones this video is about.

## Script 5: From eBay to Digital Products: How I Made the Transition

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
eBay and digital products seem like two completely separate businesses. For me they're connected. The eBay research that finds physical products also tells me what information people need to buy them. That's how the first digital products got built. Here's the full picture.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I want to explain how I ended up with both an eBay operation and a digital product store running at the same time, and why I think that path makes more sense for most people than starting with digital products from scratch.

The short version is this. eBay gave me a feedback loop that digital products alone can't give you when you're starting with no audience. Physical product sales tell you what people actually buy. That data informs what guides and frameworks they'd pay to learn.

**Section 1 -- What eBay taught me about buyers (1:30-3:30):**
When someone buys a product on eBay, they're making a fairly specific decision. They searched for something, compared a few options, and chose mine. That decision path tells me more about buyer psychology than almost any survey or market research exercise could.

Three things I learned from eBay that directly shaped my digital products.

First: buyers are very specific about what they want. They're not searching for "kitchen tools." They're searching for "silicone spatula heat resistant 600 degrees." The specificity tells you exactly what attribute matters to them. A digital product that addresses that specific concern will always beat one that addresses the topic generally.

Second: price sensitivity is real but not universal. Some products sold consistently at the market rate and never at lower prices. Some products I couldn't move until I dropped the price by 15 percent. That taught me that pricing elasticity varies by product category and by buyer type. The same principle applies to digital products.

Third: the best sellers were products where the buyer had a clear before-state. They needed something specific because they were doing something specific. "I need a wide-mouth jar opener for my arthritic hands" is a very different buyer than "I need a jar opener." The more specific the problem, the more committed the buyer.

**Section 2 -- Finding the knowledge gap (3:30-5:30):**
The transition from selling products to selling information about products started with a specific observation. People kept messaging me on eBay asking questions that weren't about my listing. They were asking where else they could find similar items. Whether a specific brand was worth buying. How to tell if a product was genuine versus a knockoff.

Those questions are a knowledge gap. Someone needs information that a product listing can't provide. That's a digital product.

My first two digital guides came directly from eBay buyer messages. One was a buying guide for a product category I was selling in. The other was a sourcing guide explaining how I found products like the ones I was selling. Both sold immediately to people who were already in the market.

The key insight is that your customer doesn't change between your physical product business and your digital product business if you build the digital products for the same buyer. The trust you build as a physical product seller transfers directly. The Gumroad store and the eBay store serve the same type of person.

**Section 3 -- Building the value ladder (5:30-7:30):**
A value ladder is the sequence of things a customer can buy from you at increasing price points.

My value ladder looks like this. The bottom rung is a free lead magnet. A checklist or resource I give away to build an email list. Above that is a $7 to $15 entry guide. Above that is a $27 to $47 framework or template bundle. Above that is a $97 or higher done-for-you resource or consulting offer.

The eBay operation is not explicitly on this value ladder, but it funds the time I spend building the digital product side while the digital product side is still in early revenue stages. They're parallel, not competing.

The transition from eBay to digital wasn't a switch. It was an addition. I didn't stop selling on eBay when I started building guides. I used the eBay operation to validate demand and fund the slower build.

**Section 4 -- Why starting with physical products first is underrated (7:30-9:30):**
Most digital product advice assumes you already have an audience or an established niche. The reality for most people starting at zero is that they don't have either.

Starting with eBay dropshipping gives you something digital-product-first approaches rarely offer: actual buyers before you have an audience. You're serving real buyers from day one. You're learning what they value, what language they use, what problems they have. All of that becomes the foundation for content and products.

The other thing eBay gives you is a tolerance for the uncomfortable parts of building a business. Handling disputes, dealing with slow sales, iterating on listings that don't convert. Those are the same skills you need to iterate on digital products that don't sell. The feedback loop is just faster on eBay because you're buying and selling physical items with real purchase intent.

**Section 5 -- What I'd do differently (9:30-11:00):**
I'd start building the email list sooner. The eBay buyers can't become digital product customers unless there's a connection between the two stores. I didn't build that bridge until I had 30 digital products. If I'd built it at 5, the compounding would have started earlier.

I'd also have published the first digital product at the two-month mark on eBay, not the four-month mark. By month two I had enough niche knowledge to produce something useful. I waited for a level of expertise that was probably higher than what my buyers actually needed.

The path is eBay first for market signal, digital products second for scalable revenue, content third for distribution. In that order. That's the stack I'm building toward.
