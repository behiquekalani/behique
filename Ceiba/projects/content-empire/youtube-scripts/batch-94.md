## Script 1: How to Use Claude for Business (The Practical Guide Nobody Made)

**Target length:** 1,400-1,600 words | 10-12 min

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:30)

Everyone is talking about AI for business. Most of what they're teaching is how to use ChatGPT to write emails slightly faster.

That's not what I want to talk about.

Claude is a different tool for a different kind of work. It reasons. It holds complex context across a long conversation. It doesn't just generate output, it helps you think through problems.

I've been using it to run significant parts of my solo business. Today I'm going to show you the specific workflows that actually save time and create leverage.

---

### INTRO

Before we get into the specific use cases, I want to address the most common mistake people make with AI tools.

They use them as a replacement for thinking instead of an amplifier of thinking.

If you have a vague idea and you ask an AI to flesh it out, you get vague output that sounds confident. Garbage in, garbage out. The tool is only as useful as the quality of what you bring to it.

Claude is most powerful when you come to it with a clear problem, real context, and specific constraints. That's when it becomes a genuine thinking partner rather than a text generator.

---

### SECTION 1: Research and Synthesis

The use case that saves me the most time is synthesis research.

Instead of reading 10 articles about a topic and trying to hold all of it in my head, I paste the relevant sources directly into Claude and ask it to synthesize the key insights, find the contradictions, and surface what's most relevant to my specific situation.

The prompt structure that works: "Here is [X amount of content] on [topic]. My situation is [specific context]. I need to understand [specific question]. Synthesize this for me and flag what's most important."

What you get back is a compressed, contextualized understanding of the material. What would take two hours of reading and note-taking happens in 15 minutes.

---

### SECTION 2: Thinking Through Decisions

This is where Claude is genuinely different from other AI tools.

When I'm facing a business decision that has real stakes, I use Claude as a structured thinking partner.

The process: I explain the situation in full. I state the options I'm considering. I give the constraints (budget, time, what I can actually execute). Then I ask Claude to steelman each option, find the weaknesses I haven't considered, and tell me what it would need to know to give a stronger recommendation.

The key is that I'm not asking it to make the decision. I'm using it to find the blind spots in my own thinking. The decision is still mine. But it's better-informed because I've pressure-tested it against a system that doesn't have my emotional investment in any particular outcome.

---

### SECTION 3: Content Creation at Scale

For a solo operator, content creation is usually the bottleneck. You know what you want to say. Getting it out in the right format for every platform is where time disappears.

Here's the workflow I use.

I record a voice memo of my raw thinking on a topic. Maybe 5-10 minutes of unfiltered ideas. I get the transcript. I paste it into Claude with the prompt: "This is my raw thinking on [topic]. My voice is direct, specific, and builds arguments step by step. I don't use hype. Turn this into [format: Twitter thread / newsletter / YouTube script outline]. Keep my voice, don't inflate the language."

The output isn't perfect. It never is. But it's 70% of the way there in 3 minutes instead of starting from a blank page.

The editing is faster than the writing. That's the leverage.

---

### SECTION 4: Customer Research and Positioning

Most solopreneurs underinvest in understanding their buyer. They assume they know the problem because they lived it themselves.

Claude can help you build a more rigorous understanding of your customer.

The approach: paste in 20-30 customer reviews from competitors' products in your space (from Gumroad, Amazon, Reddit, wherever). Ask Claude to analyze the language patterns, identify the core frustrations, find the gap between what's promised and what's delivered, and extract the exact phrases buyers use to describe the problem.

This is market research that would cost thousands of dollars from a consultant. You can do it in an afternoon.

The output feeds directly into your product positioning, your landing page copy, and your content strategy.

---

### SECTION 5: Operations and Systems

The final category is using Claude to build and document your own operating systems.

Every time you do a process more than three times, it should be documented. Claude makes this fast.

Do the task. Then describe what you just did to Claude in plain language. Ask it to turn your description into a clear, step-by-step SOP that someone else could follow. Then store that SOP somewhere you can find it.

Over time, you're building a library of documented processes for everything in your business. When you want to delegate, hire, or automate, the documentation is already there.

## Script 2: Building a Newsletter from Zero (The Real Guide)

**Target length:** 1,300-1,500 words | 10-11 min

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:30)

Everyone says "build your email list." Almost nobody tells you the actual mechanics of how to do it from zero, what to write, how to get the first 500 subscribers, and when it starts to matter for revenue.

I'm going to cover all of that today. No theory. Just the structure that works.

---

### INTRO

The email newsletter is the most underrated asset a solo operator can build.

Here's why. Social media platforms are rented land. Your Instagram, your Twitter, your TikTok, all of that can be taken away or algorithmically suppressed at any time. You don't own the relationship.

Your email list is different. Those are people who gave you their address and said "yes, send me things." You own that relationship. It's the floor underneath your entire digital business.

The newsletter is how you build it.

---

### SECTION 1: Choosing the Platform

The platform decision matters less than people make it.

Beehiiv is the best option right now for most people starting out. Clean interface, good deliverability, built-in growth tools like referral programs, and the free tier is genuinely usable.

ConvertKit (now Kit) is the other strong option. Better automation tools, better if you plan to sell products directly through the platform.

Substack is good if you want built-in discovery from their network. The trade-off is you have less control over the product experience.

My recommendation: Beehiiv for most new starters. Get on a platform today. The tool doesn't matter until you have subscribers.

---

### SECTION 2: What to Write

The most common newsletter mistake is trying to write a general interest newsletter. A little bit of tech news, a little bit of productivity, a little bit of business.

Newsletters that grow have a specific promise for a specific person.

The formula: "Every [frequency], I send [specific type of content] for [specific person] who wants [specific outcome]."

Example: "Every Tuesday, I send one actionable framework for solopreneurs who want to replace their salary with digital products."

That sentence is the core of your newsletter. Every issue should deliver on that promise. When the reader gets your email, they should immediately know why they subscribed and what they're about to get.

---

### SECTION 3: Getting the First 500 Subscribers

The first 500 subscribers are the hardest. Here's what actually works.

The lead magnet. Create one specific, useful thing and give it away for free in exchange for an email address. Not a generic guide. Something specific and immediately useful. A template, a checklist, a curated list of tools. The more specific, the better the conversion rate.

Promote it everywhere, consistently. Every piece of content you create should point toward the newsletter or the lead magnet. Not occasionally. Every time.

Personal outreach. In the first 100 subscribers, reach out directly to people in your network who fit the audience description. Tell them what the newsletter is about. Ask if they want in. This is uncomfortable and it works.

Cross-promotion. Find other newsletters in adjacent spaces and propose swaps. They mention you to their list, you mention them to yours. This scales once you have at least 200-300 subscribers to offer in return.

---

### SECTION 4: The Issue Structure

Consistency in format is underrated. When readers know exactly what they're getting, they're more likely to open every time.

A simple structure that works:

Opening paragraph: one insight, observation, or question related to the newsletter's core theme. Sets the tone.

Main section: the core content. One idea, explored with depth. Not a listicle of 10 things. One thing, done well.

Resource or tool: one specific recommendation relevant to the reader. A tool, a book, a resource you've actually used.

Short CTA: one action. Subscribe to a related thing, reply to this email, check out the product. One, not five.

This structure takes roughly 45-60 minutes to produce once you're practiced. It's sustainable.

---

### SECTION 5: When It Starts to Matter for Revenue

The newsletter becomes a revenue asset between 500 and 1,000 subscribers, but only if those subscribers are the right people.

1,000 engaged subscribers in a specific niche is worth more than 10,000 unengaged subscribers from a giveaway.

The revenue paths for a newsletter:

Product sales. Direct links to your own digital products in every relevant issue. This compounds over time as the list grows.

Affiliate promotions. Recommending tools and products for a commission. Only for things you actually use. One bad recommendation destroys trust that took months to build.

Sponsorships. Once you're past 2,000 engaged subscribers, some brands will pay for newsletter placements. This is not the primary path for a solo operator, but it's there.

---

Start with the platform today. Write the first issue this week. Send it to whoever will read it. The list grows from there.

## Script 3: The Digital Product Pricing Masterclass

**Target length:** 1,400-1,600 words | 11-12 min

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:30)

The single question I get asked most often about digital products is "how do I price it?"

Most people underprice. They're afraid of rejection, afraid the product isn't good enough, afraid that someone will say the price is too high. So they price it at $9 or $17 and wonder why they're working hard for almost nothing.

Pricing is not about what you think is fair. It's about value anchoring, psychology, and market positioning. Let me show you how it actually works.

---

### INTRO

Pricing is a leverage point that most solopreneurs ignore. They spend weeks building a product and 20 minutes setting a price.

The price you choose communicates something to the buyer before they read a single word of your sales page. Too low and the product feels cheap. Too high without the right framing and it creates friction.

The goal is to find the price that maximizes both conversion rate and revenue per sale. Those two things are in tension. Today I'm going to show you how to navigate that tension.

---

### SECTION 1: The Price Anchoring Framework

Most buyers don't know what a digital product should cost. They have no reference point.

Your job is to give them one.

This is called anchoring. The first price they see becomes the reference point for everything that comes after.

How to use this: before you show your price, show the alternatives. If hiring someone to do what your product teaches would cost $500, say that. If the information exists in a $200 course scattered across 40 hours of video, say that. Then position your product as the concentrated, faster version at a fraction of the cost.

You've now created a reference point that makes your actual price feel like a bargain, regardless of what the number is.

---

### SECTION 2: Price Points by Product Type

Here are the price ranges that convert, organized by product type.

Templates and single-use assets: $9-$27. Low friction purchase. Buyer doesn't need to think hard.

Focused guides and PDFs (under 50 pages): $17-$47. Should solve one specific, urgent problem.

Comprehensive guides or toolkits (multi-component): $37-$97. The perceived value of quantity matters here. Bundle related assets together.

Systems and frameworks with implementation depth: $97-$197. Buyer is paying for clarity, not just information.

Community access or live elements added: $197-$497. The price of human access justifies a significant jump.

The key is that your product has to deliver the full value implied by its price point. Overpriced products get refunds and destroy your reputation. Price at or slightly below what you can confidently deliver.

---

### SECTION 3: The Price Testing Method

You should not set a price and leave it forever. Prices should be tested like any other variable.

Here's the method.

Launch at a price 20-30% lower than what you think the product is worth. Call it a "founding price" or a "launch price." This creates urgency and rewards early buyers.

After the first 20-30 sales, raise the price by $10-$20. Watch the conversion rate. If sales continue at a similar pace, raise again.

Keep raising until you find the price where conversion rate drops meaningfully. Then stay one step below that.

Most products can support a higher price than their creator thinks. The only way to find out is to test.

---

### SECTION 4: Bundling for Higher AOV

Average order value (AOV) is the real revenue lever, not just your base price.

Bundles let you increase AOV without increasing your traffic. The buyer was already going to purchase. You're just giving them a reason to spend more in the same transaction.

How to build bundles effectively:

The anchor bundle: two products that logically belong together. If you sell a Notion template for managing freelance clients and a guide to pricing freelance work, those two products are a natural bundle. Price the bundle at 70-80% of the combined individual prices.

The premium bundle: add a "done for you" element or a template that's faster to use than the guide alone. Price this significantly higher.

The lite version: a stripped-down version at a lower price. This is for buyers who need a lower entry point. Some will upgrade.

---

### SECTION 5: Psychological Levers That Move Buyers

Three pricing psychology principles that actually work.

The power of 7. Prices ending in 7 consistently outperform round numbers in digital product markets. $27 converts better than $25 or $30. $47 better than $50. This is well-documented.

Scarcity. If you're doing a launch, a real deadline or a real quantity limit creates urgency. "First 50 buyers at this price" works only if it's real. Fake scarcity destroys trust permanently.

Comparison to an alternative. Not just "compared to a course." Compare to the cost of the problem continuing. If a bad pricing strategy costs a freelancer $500/month in lost revenue, then a $47 guide that fixes it is a 10x return in month one. Make that math explicit.

---

### SECTION 6: The Refund Policy Question

Whether to offer refunds is a real question with a real answer.

Offer them. A 30-day money-back guarantee on a digital product consistently increases conversion rate by more than the percentage of people who actually request refunds.

The guarantee removes the risk for the buyer. Most people who buy with the intention to refund never get around to it. And the buyers who do refund would have charged back anyway.

Set clear terms. "30-day satisfaction guarantee, no questions asked" is the standard that converts. Make it visible on the sales page.

---

Pricing is not guessing. It's a system of anchoring, testing, and positioning that you can learn and apply methodically.

Start higher than you're comfortable with. Test downward if needed. Most people need to test upward.
