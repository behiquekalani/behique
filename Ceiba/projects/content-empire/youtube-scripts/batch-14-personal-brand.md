---

### INTRO

I was diagnosed with ADHD a few years ago and I have been working with a psychologist on managing it. Not just the focus stuff. The emotional regulation, the avoidance patterns, the part where you have 40 browser tabs open and you are procrastinating on the thing you actually care about most.

Running a business solo with ADHD is a specific combination of hard. On one hand, the hyperfocus you get when you are locked into something is a real advantage. I have shipped products in a single evening that took me hours of focused work. On the other hand, the same brain that does that will spend two weeks unable to do one 20-minute task because something about it triggers avoidance.

I want to walk through what my actual setup looks like. Not the aspirational version. The real one.

---

### Section 1: Why Standard Productivity Advice Fails

Most productivity content is built around a specific assumption: you have working memory and the ability to choose what to focus on.

ADHD breaks both of those things.

The Pomodoro technique, for example. The theory is solid. 25 minutes on, 5 minutes off. But if you have ADHD, the 5-minute break is a real risk. That is when you pick up your phone. That is when you see something that captures your attention and 45 minutes disappear.

Same with to-do lists. A flat list of tasks assumes you can pick the most important one and start. For a lot of ADHD brains, a long list creates paralysis. You see 12 things and you start none of them.

The things that actually work for me operate on different principles. They are about reducing the number of decisions I need to make before starting something. Every decision point between me and the work is a risk of not starting.

---

### Section 2: The Capture System

The single highest-leverage thing I have built is a capture system.

I built a Telegram bot, I call it BehiqueBot, that I can send any idea to at any time. Voice message, text, doesn't matter. It transcribes, categorizes, and stores it. The reason this helps with ADHD specifically is it removes the cognitive pressure of holding things in your head.

One of the things my psychologist explained is that ADHD brains often resist starting tasks because they are also trying to hold onto every other pending idea at the same time. The background load is high. When you have a reliable place to put things, the background load drops.

I do not need to remember the shower idea. I sent it to the bot. I do not need to remember the product concept I had at 11pm. It is logged. Now I can focus on the one thing in front of me.

This is the principle: capture everything immediately, so your brain stops trying to hold it.

---

### Section 3: The Single-Task Rule

I have a strict rule for myself when I am in a work session. One task. That is it.

Not "work on the business." That is not a task. That is a category. A task is "write the first 300 words of the product description for the AI guide." Specific. Completable in one sitting.

I got this from my psychologist's framework. She calls it minimizing cognitive load. The more abstract the task, the more energy your brain spends figuring out what to actually do before you do anything. For ADHD this overhead is not minor. It is the reason you sit at your desk for an hour and feel like you did nothing.

I now write my tasks like a software spec. Clear input, clear output, clear definition of done.

---

### Section 4: Accountability Structures That Are Not Fake

Most accountability advice for entrepreneurs is either "find an accountability partner" or "post publicly." Both are fine in theory. Neither works for me in practice.

Accountability partners require coordination. Finding time to meet, updating someone, depending on their schedule. For ADHD brains, adding another coordination layer is usually a net negative.

Public accountability has a different problem. It triggers performance mode. I start thinking about how things look rather than what is actually happening.

What works for me is private, low-friction check-ins. I have a system built around my psychologist's framework that runs check-ins on my own. The accountability is to myself and to a record, not to an audience.

The key principle she gave me: ask the right question, not "did you do the thing" but "what got in the way of the thing." That second question is actually useful information. You can act on it.

---

### Section 5: The Avoidance Pattern

I want to name this directly because it is the thing that has cost me the most time.

Avoidance with ADHD is not laziness. It is a specific feeling, usually triggered by a task that carries some kind of emotional weight. Uncertainty about whether you will do it well. A task that involves rejection risk. Something that feels ambiguous.

When I catch myself reopening the same task file and closing it without doing anything, that is the signal. Something about that task is triggering avoidance.

The protocol I use now: write down what feels hard about the task. Not what the task is. What about it feels hard. Getting it out of my head and into words usually makes it smaller. Then I reduce the scope. If the task is "write the blog post," I change it to "write the first sentence of the blog post." Not the whole thing. One sentence.

It almost always unfreezes the loop.

---

### INTRO

Claude is my primary AI tool. I use it for everything from building products to writing content to thinking through problems at midnight when there is nobody else to think with.

But there is a big difference between typing a question into Claude and having a working relationship with it. The difference is the quality and structure of how you ask.

I want to walk through seven specific patterns I use constantly. These are not clever tricks. They are repeatable structures that make the output reliably better. I will give you the actual phrasing I use and the reason it works.

---

### Section 1: The Briefing Command

The first command I use almost every session is what I call the briefing.

The prompt is: "You are my thinking partner. Here is everything relevant about what I am working on right now: [context dump]. Your job is to ask me clarifying questions until you have enough to help me make a real decision."

The reason this works is that most AI interactions fail at the context stage. You ask a question with incomplete information, you get a generic answer, and you are not actually further along. The briefing command forces a back-and-forth before any real work happens.

I use this at the start of any session where I need to make a decision, not just get information.

---

### Section 2: The Devil's Advocate Command

The prompt is: "I have a plan. I am going to describe it to you. Your job is to tell me every way this could fail, every assumption I am making that might be wrong, and every variable I have not thought through. Be specific."

This one is uncomfortable to use. That is the point.

I built several products that I thought were solid before I started actually questioning the assumptions. The devil's advocate command surfaces problems before they become real ones. It is not about pessimism. It is about building things that actually work.

---

### Section 3: The Scope Reducer

This is one I use specifically for ADHD management, but it works for anyone who tends to overplan.

The prompt is: "I want to build [thing]. I have been thinking about it for a while and it keeps getting bigger in my head. Help me find the version of this that I could build and ship in 48 hours. What is the smallest thing that would still prove the concept?"

Most builders overbuild the first version. This command is the forcing function for MVPs. It is also the command I use when I have been avoiding a project because it feels too large.

---

### Section 4: The Thumbnail and Title Generator

For content I use this one before I write anything.

"I am going to describe a video topic to you. Before I write the script, I need a title under 60 characters that is honest, specific, and would make someone who builds things on the internet stop scrolling. Then give me three thumbnail concepts with text overlays. Do not use hype words."

The reason the last sentence matters is that Claude will default to clickbait phrasing if you do not constrain it. Adding "do not use hype words" shifts the output toward the style I actually want.

---

### Section 5: The Product Listing Command

"I built a digital product. Here is what it is: [description]. Write me a Gumroad listing in the voice of someone who has used this product and found it genuinely useful. No buzzwords. Thesis first. Short paragraphs. Specific about what it solves."

This one I use for every product I list. The "thesis first" instruction matters a lot. It forces the output to lead with the core value, not with a setup that buries the point.

---

### Section 6: The Code Partner Command

I am not a great programmer yet. I know enough to work with code but not enough to write everything from scratch.

The prompt I use: "I need to build [thing]. I am going to describe what I want it to do. Write the first working version, then walk me through it line by line so I understand what it does. Prioritize simplicity over cleverness."

The "walk me through it line by line" part is what turns AI-generated code into learning, not dependency. If I cannot explain what the code does after Claude walks me through it, I ask again until I can.

---

### Section 7: The End-of-Day Summary

The last one is for when I finish a work session.

"Here is everything I worked on today: [list]. Write me a one-paragraph summary of what actually got done, what is still open, and what the single most important next action is. Be brutally honest about whether I made real progress or just stayed busy."

This one keeps me honest. The difference between a productive day and a busy day is real and it is easy to confuse them when you work alone. Having something that will actually tell you the difference is worth more than a 10-step productivity system.

Zero feels like something. I want to describe it accurately. And then I want to walk through what I actually think caused it and what I am changing.

---

### Section 1: What Zero Actually Feels Like

It does not feel like failure, exactly. It feels like a test of whether you believe the premise.

You built the thing. You genuinely think it is useful. And nobody bought it. That gap between "I think this is valuable" and "someone agreed enough to pay for it" is where most first-time builders stop.

The temptation is to conclude the product is bad. That might be true. But it is almost never the full story in month one. More often it is a distribution problem. The product exists in a folder that nobody knows about yet.

But knowing that intellectually and feeling it at 11pm when you have been working for three hours and the sales dashboard still says zero, those are different things.

---

### Section 2: The Real Reason It Was Zero

I am going to be honest about this.

Most of the products were not listed publicly for most of the month. I built them. I did not list them. That is not a distribution problem. That is an execution gap.

I know what caused it. The listing felt final in a way that building did not. Building is low-stakes. You are making something that nobody sees yet. Listing is different. Listing means the judgment can happen.

This is a specific pattern my psychologist and I have talked about. The avoidance is not about laziness. It is about the emotional weight of the moment when something stops being private.

Naming it is useful because now I know what to watch for in month two.

---

### Section 3: What the Data Did Tell Me

Even with zero sales, month one generated useful data.

What content got the most engagement tells you what people care about. Not what they will pay for, but what they will stop for. That is the first signal in a longer chain.

The topics that performed in month one: anything about building something concrete, anything honest about what is not working, anything specific about tools with real examples.

What did not perform: generic tips, aspirational content without specifics, anything that felt like advice rather than documentation.

That is a clear signal. The format that works for this audience is "here is what is actually happening" not "here is what you should do." So I am adjusting the content mix.

---

### Section 4: The Three Changes for Month Two

Change one: products listed on day one. Not when they feel ready. Not when the description feels perfect. Day one of the month, everything is live. This removes the window where I can convince myself to wait.

Change two: traffic before sales. I was thinking about conversion when I should have been thinking about awareness. The funnel does not matter if nobody is entering it. Month two is about getting traffic to the Gumroad page first.

Change three: document more aggressively. The "building in public" format only works if you actually publish the updates. I wrote a lot in month one that I did not post. That changes.

---

### Section 5: What I Think Month Two Looks Like

I am not going to predict revenue because I do not know. But I have a process-based target.

Post every day. Have one product on every major platform, not just Gumroad. Get 1,000 people to see the product page.

Revenue is an outcome. Those three things are inputs I control.

The reason I am documenting this publicly is that I think the gap between "building the thing" and "shipping the thing" is where most people get stuck and where almost nobody is honest about being stuck. If this video helps someone close that gap faster than I did, the zero month was worth documenting.
