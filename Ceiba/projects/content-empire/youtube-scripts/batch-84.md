## SCRIPT 1: "How to Build and Launch Your First Digital Product in 30 Days"

**Format:** Tutorial / roadmap
**Target length:** 11-13 minutes
**Hook type:** Clear promise, step-by-step structure

---

[HOOK]

Thirty days from now you could have a digital product live, listed, and accepting payments. Most people spend that same thirty days thinking about what they would make if they had the time. The difference between those two outcomes is not talent or experience. It is having a clear process and following it.

---

[INTRO]

I am going to give you the exact 30-day process I used for my first three products. Not the optimized version. The version that actually worked when I had no audience, no experience, and no idea whether anyone would buy.

---

[SECTION 1: WEEK ONE — VALIDATION BEFORE CREATION]

Week one does not involve building anything. It involves proving that what you want to build is something people will pay for.

The validation process has three steps.

Step one: identify the problem. What is a specific problem that a specific type of person has that you understand well? The narrower, the better. Not "people struggle with productivity." That is too broad. "Freelancers lose track of client work when managing five or more clients simultaneously" is specific enough to validate.

Step two: write the sales page before the product exists. Describe the problem, the transformation the product creates, what is included, and the price. This forces you to be clear about what you are actually selling. If you cannot write a clear sales page, you do not have a clear product idea yet.

Step three: put the sales page or the concept in front of real people. Post it in relevant online communities. Share it with people who match the target profile. Watch for genuine purchase intent, not encouragement from friends.

If five or more people ask how to buy or express real interest: proceed to week two. If the response is silence or only vague encouragement: adjust the framing or the problem before building.

---

[SECTION 2: WEEK TWO — BUILDING THE CORE]

Week two is the build. The goal is a minimum viable product, not a perfect product.

The question that keeps you on track: what is the minimum set of content that creates the transformation I promised on the sales page? Everything beyond that is version two.

For most digital information products, the structure is: introduction (context and credibility), problem diagnosis (why the reader has this problem), core framework (the solution), implementation guide (how to apply it), and a completion section (what the buyer should do now).

That structure does not require 200 pages. Twenty to thirty pages of dense, useful content outperforms a bloated 100-page product that dilutes the core value. Every page should earn its place.

Formatting matters more than most creators admit. Clear headers, short paragraphs, callout boxes for key points, and action steps at the end of each section. These are not design choices. They are usability choices. A product that is easy to use gets used. A product that gets used generates reviews.

---

[SECTION 3: WEEK THREE — PACKAGING AND LISTING]

Week three: package the product and list it.

Cover design is not optional. The cover is the product's first impression in a marketplace and on a sales page. It signals the level of care that went into what is inside. A well-designed cover for a digital product takes two to three hours using a template-based tool or one hour if you use an AI image generator with a clear brief.

The listing on Gumroad, Payhip, or whatever platform you choose should include: a clear headline (the transformation, not the title), a problem statement that the target buyer will recognize immediately, a description of what is included and why each piece matters, and social proof if you have it or a credibility statement if you do not.

Price it at a point that feels slightly uncomfortable. The discomfort usually means you have actually priced it based on value rather than on fear.

---

[SECTION 4: WEEK FOUR — FIRST DISTRIBUTION PUSH]

Week four: get it in front of buyers.

The distribution strategy for a first product with no audience focuses on communities, not platforms. Reddit communities, Discord servers, Facebook groups, and online forums where the target buyer already spends time.

The approach is not to spam links. It is to engage authentically in the community, contribute genuine value, and mention the product only where it is directly relevant to a conversation that is already happening. This takes longer than running ads. It also builds trust that converts better.

Email any relevant newsletter writers or content creators in the niche. Not with a pitch for a promotional partnership. With a brief note about the product and an offer of a free copy in exchange for honest feedback. Two or three genuine mentions from respected voices in a niche outperform most early paid promotion strategies.

---

[SECTION 5: AFTER LAUNCH — WHAT THE FIRST 30 DAYS ACTUALLY SHOW YOU]

The first 30 days after launch are a learning period, not a revenue period.

What to track: conversion rate (visitors to buyers), which distribution sources sent paying customers, which part of the product generates the most questions, what buyers say in feedback.

Every piece of that data shapes version two of the product, the next product, and the content strategy going forward. The first product is as much a research instrument as it is a revenue source.

The goal after 30 days: at least five to ten paying customers, a conversion rate above two percent, and enough buyer feedback to identify the one thing you would change about the product. That is a successful first product, even if the revenue number is small.

## SCRIPT 2: "Why Your Content is Not Converting to Sales (And What's Actually Wrong)"

**Format:** Diagnostic / talking head
**Target length:** 10-12 minutes
**Hook type:** Direct problem diagnosis, reframes common misconceptions

---

[HOOK]

You are posting consistently. Your engagement is decent. But your conversion to actual product sales is near zero. That is a specific problem with a specific diagnosis. It is almost never about the content quality. Let me show you what is actually wrong.

---

[INTRO]

Conversion from content to product sales is one of the most misunderstood problems in the creator and solopreneur space. Most people assume low conversion means bad content or a bad product. Usually it means a broken or absent bridge between the two.

---

[SECTION 1: THE MISSING BRIDGE — WHY GOOD CONTENT DOES NOT AUTOMATICALLY SELL]

Content and sales are different functions. Content's job is to demonstrate that you understand the problem and that you have a perspective on it. Sales' job is to make a clear offer to a person who is aware of the problem and looking for a solution.

Most creators conflate these. They think if the content is good enough, the sale will follow naturally. It rarely does. The bridge between "this content is useful" and "I should buy this person's product" requires explicit construction.

The bridge is built from three things: awareness that a paid product exists, clarity about what problem the product solves, and social proof or credibility that reduces the buyer's risk.

If your content is high quality but you mention your product rarely, awkwardly, or without context, the bridge is broken. Buyers are not going to investigate on their own. You have to build the path.

---

[SECTION 2: THE AUDIENCE MATCH PROBLEM]

The second most common conversion issue: the audience your content attracts is not the audience that would buy your product.

This is painful to diagnose because it means your content strategy is working, just not for the right person. An account about general productivity will attract general productivity readers. If your product is specifically for freelancers managing client work, the general productivity audience will consume the content and never buy.

The fix is specificity at the content level. Your free content needs to attract the exact person you built the product for. That means the topics, the language, and the examples in your content should match the specific context of your buyer.

General content builds large audiences. Specific content builds buying audiences. You can have a smaller, highly specific audience and significantly better conversion than a large, diffuse one.

---

[SECTION 3: THE TRUST GAP]

Even with the right audience and a visible product, there is often a trust gap. The buyer is not yet convinced that you are the right person to solve this problem.

Trust is built through demonstration, not declaration. Saying you are an expert does not build trust. Showing the work, documenting the process, and sharing real results builds trust.

The content formats that build trust most effectively: detailed case studies with real numbers, before-and-after transformations with specific examples, behind-the-scenes process content, and responses to the most common objections your buyer has.

Compare these to content formats that get engagement but rarely build trust: hot takes, motivational posts, and relatable struggle content. These are fine for growth. They do not move buyers.

---

[SECTION 4: THE WEAK CALL TO ACTION]

The most mechanical conversion problem: the call to action is weak, absent, or awkward.

Most creators fall into one of two failure modes. They never mention the product (product-shy), or they mention it constantly in a way that feels like an ad (spam-mode). Neither works.

The effective call to action is specific and contextual. After a piece of content about a specific problem, the call to action connects that problem to the product that solves it. Not "check out my store." "If this is a problem you are dealing with, the Behike Method has a specific section on this with a step-by-step workflow. Link below."

That is a bridge, not a pitch. It tells the buyer why the product is relevant to what they just read, not just that it exists.

---

[SECTION 5: THE CONVERSION AUDIT — HOW TO FIX IT]

Here is a practical audit to run on your current setup.

Open your last ten pieces of content. For each one, ask: does this clearly attract the specific person who would buy my product? Does it mention the product in a contextual, natural way? Does it build trust through demonstration rather than just positioning?

Then look at your product page. Does it clearly state the transformation? Does it address the objections your buyer has before they form? Is there evidence that it works?

The conversion problem is almost always traceable to a specific gap in this chain. Find the gap. Fix that one thing first.

## SCRIPT 3: "The Solopreneur's Guide to Building Income Streams That Don't Require You"

**Format:** Framework / talking head
**Target length:** 12-14 minutes
**Hook type:** Addresses a core desire with a concrete framework

---

[HOOK]

The version of a solo business that most people actually want is not one where they work more hours. It is one where the hours they do work produce income that continues to come in after those hours are done. That is not a fantasy. It is an engineering problem. Here is how to build it.

---

[INTRO]

This is a video about designing a business where the work you do today produces income tomorrow, next week, and next year. Not passive income in the "lie on a beach" sense. That framing is misleading. Active income that runs on systems you built and maintain, not on your constant presence.

---

[SECTION 1: THE SPECTRUM FROM FULLY ACTIVE TO FULLY PASSIVE]

Most income sources exist on a spectrum.

At the fully active end: trading time for money at a fixed rate. Consulting, freelancing, client work. Every dollar requires a specific hour. The ceiling is the number of hours you have.

At the other end: truly passive income. Returns on invested capital, royalties on created works. These require no ongoing time. They also require significant upfront capital or years of prior work to establish.

Most solopreneurs should target the middle of that spectrum. Semi-passive income: income that requires upfront work to build, some ongoing maintenance, but not a proportional exchange of time for revenue.

Digital products are the most accessible semi-passive income source for a person starting from scratch with skills and knowledge but not capital.

---

[SECTION 2: THE ANATOMY OF A SEMI-PASSIVE DIGITAL PRODUCT INCOME]

Here is how the semi-passive model works in practice.

You create a product once. It takes significant time to build well. The first sale might come weeks or months after you build it.

The sales process is automated. Someone lands on the product page, they read the description, they pay, they receive the product. You are not involved in any individual sale.

The marketing is content-driven. You post content that attracts the right buyer. Content goes out on a schedule. The best content continues to attract buyers weeks and months after you posted it.

The maintenance is periodic. You update the product when it needs updating. You improve the sales page when conversion drops. You add new content when you have something worth adding. This might take two to four hours per month per product.

That is the model. Build it once. Market it with content. Let the system run most of it.

---

[SECTION 3: LAYERING INCOME STREAMS OVER TIME]

The mistake is trying to build multiple income streams simultaneously. The correct approach is sequential.

Layer one: one digital product on one platform with one traffic source. Get this to consistent revenue before adding anything. Consistent means the same revenue two months in a row without extraordinary effort.

Layer two: a second product aimed at the same audience or a second traffic source for the same product. At this point you understand your buyer well enough to either serve them in a different way or reach them through a different channel.

Layer three: an email list as an owned distribution channel. By the time you are at layer two, you should be building an email list. The list is the asset that makes every new product launch more predictable than the last.

Layer four: higher-ticket options. A workshop, a group coaching cohort, a premium version of your existing product. These require more of your time but at a much higher per-hour rate than one-to-one services.

Each layer is built on the foundation of the previous one. Skipping layers is how people end up with scattered revenue and no clear business model.

---

[SECTION 4: THE MAINTENANCE SYSTEM — KEEPING IT RUNNING WITHOUT RUNNING YOU]

The thing most people do not plan for: what does ongoing maintenance of a semi-passive income stream actually require?

For a digital product business, the maintenance tasks are: responding to buyer questions, updating products periodically, creating new content that sends traffic to the products, reviewing and improving the sales pages.

Most of these tasks can be batched. I handle buyer questions once per day in a 20-minute window. Product updates happen quarterly or when something becomes inaccurate. Content creation is on a fixed weekly schedule. Sales page reviews happen monthly.

Batching is the operational practice that keeps maintenance from becoming a constant drain. If you respond to every buyer question in real time throughout the day, you will spend four hours on something that could be handled in twenty minutes.

---

[SECTION 5: THE REALISTIC TIMELINE]

I want to be honest about the timeline because most content about this topic implies it happens faster than it does.

Month one to three: building the first product, getting the first sales, learning what works in your specific niche and platform. Revenue is low. Learning is high.

Month three to six: first consistent revenue. The content is finding its audience. The product is generating reviews. The conversion rate is improving as you refine the sales page.

Month six to twelve: first meaningful compound effects. Older content is still sending buyers. The email list is growing and converting. You understand your buyer well enough to build the second product.

Year two: the second or third product benefits from everything you built in year one. The traffic channels are established. The list is warm. Revenue starts to look like what most people imagine when they think "passive income."

This is not a quick path. It is a consistent path with a real destination.
