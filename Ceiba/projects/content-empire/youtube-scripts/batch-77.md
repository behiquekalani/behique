### Script 1 — The Solopreneur Tech Stack 2026

**Thumbnail concept:** Grid of app icons with a clear hierarchy. Bold text: "My exact stack." Clean dark background with icon grid.

**Hook (0-30s):**
I run a one-person business. I create content, sell digital products, manage email, handle client work, and stay on top of finances. I use 11 tools to do all of that. In this video I am going to walk through my entire stack, explain what each tool actually does in my workflow, and tell you which ones I would prioritize if I was starting from scratch today.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
There is no shortage of content about productivity tools. Most of it is tool reviews, not workflow walkthroughs. This video is different. I am not reviewing features. I am showing you how a real solopreneur workflow operates across the full business stack: content creation, product delivery, email, communication, finances, and automation. Whether you are building your stack from scratch or wondering if your current one is bloated, this should give you a useful reference point.

**Section 1 — The Core Stack by Category (2-4min):**
I organize my stack into five categories: capture, create, sell, communicate, and automate.

Capture is everything that helps me collect ideas, save information, and intake tasks. I use Notion for structured capture, Telegram for quick notes to myself, and a physical notebook for daily priorities.

Create is the tools I use to actually produce things. My writing goes into Notion and sometimes Obsidian for longer-form work. Videos are recorded in a basic setup and edited in CapCut. Design work happens in Figma for structural things and Canva for fast social assets.

Sell covers the product and transaction infrastructure. Gumroad handles digital product delivery and payment processing. ConvertKit manages my email list and automations. A simple landing page built in HTML handles most product pages.

Communicate means email, Telegram for community, and occasionally a scheduling tool for calls.

Automate is n8n, which handles the workflows that connect everything else. Repurposing content, scheduling distribution, monitoring, and notifications.

That is the full stack: Notion, Telegram, Obsidian, CapCut, Figma, Canva, Gumroad, ConvertKit, n8n. Nine tools that cover everything.

**Section 2 — Deep Dive: The Three Tools I Use Most (4-7min):**
The three tools I use most hours per day are Notion, n8n, and Gumroad.

Notion is my operating system. It holds my second brain, my content calendar, my project management, my customer research notes, my product roadmap, and my weekly review. It is the thing I have open almost every working hour. The key to making Notion useful is discipline about what goes in it. Not everything. Only things you will actually need to find again.

n8n is the automation layer that saves me the most time. The automation that delivers the most value is the one that takes a finished YouTube script in a Notion database and automatically drafts LinkedIn posts, Instagram captions, and a newsletter section based on the same content. I write once and the repurposing happens automatically. This single workflow saves me three to four hours a week.

The second n8n workflow that matters most is the one that monitors product sales and sends me a daily summary with revenue, new customers, and refunds. I do not need to log into Gumroad constantly. The information comes to me.

Gumroad is the simplest possible way to sell digital products. No complex setup. No subscriptions required. The fees are acceptable, the UX is clean, and it handles VAT compliance for international sales automatically. For a solopreneur who wants to ship products and get paid without building custom infrastructure, Gumroad is the right choice at the beginning.

**Section 3 — What I Would Do Differently If Starting Over (7-10min):**
If I was starting from scratch today, I would build the stack in this order.

Week one: set up Notion and start using it for everything. Notes, tasks, ideas, reference material. Get in the habit of putting information somewhere searchable before worrying about structure.

Week two: set up an email list. ConvertKit free tier, one lead magnet, one landing page. Start collecting emails from day one even if you have no product yet.

Month one: ship a first product on Gumroad. Something small. A template, a checklist, a short guide. The goal is to complete the loop from creation to sale so you understand the mechanics.

Month two: add CapCut and start creating short-form video. One video per week, minimum. Short-form video is still the fastest way to build audience from zero in 2026.

Month three: add n8n and start automating the most repetitive parts of your workflow. Start simple. One workflow that solves one specific friction point.

The tools I would cut if I had to drop half my stack: Figma (Canva does enough for most needs), Obsidian (Notion can absorb that function), and the scheduling tool (direct booking links do the same job).

The tools I would never cut: Notion, ConvertKit, Gumroad, n8n. Those four are the foundation of the whole business.

### Script 2 — The Tools I Use Every Day to Run My Business

**Thumbnail concept:** Desktop screenshot. Multiple apps visible. Text overlay: "My daily driver apps." Authentic feel, not staged.

**Hook (0-30s):**
People ask me constantly what tools I use. I used to hesitate to answer because I thought my stack was too basic to be interesting. Then I realized that is the point. The tools I use every day are not the flashiest ones. They are the ones that create the least friction between having an idea and getting it done. Here they are.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This is not a comparison review. I am not going to tell you which tool is best in each category. I am going to tell you which specific tools I open before 9 AM every day, which ones I rely on for the highest-stakes parts of my work, and why I have stayed with them instead of constantly switching. By the end you will have a clear picture of a real working stack, not a curated list of tools I was paid to mention.

**Section 1 — Morning Tools (The First Three I Open) (2-4min):**
The first thing I open every morning is my Notion dashboard. It has my daily task list, my current project status, and a summary of the week's priorities. I built it once and now it takes me about two minutes to review. It answers the question: what matters today.

The second thing I open is my email client. I process email once in the morning and once in the late afternoon. I do not leave it open all day. The key habit here is decision on first touch: either respond, archive, or create a task. Nothing stays in inbox after I have seen it.

The third thing is my analytics overview. Sales on Gumroad, email stats in ConvertKit, and a quick look at content performance. This takes five minutes and gives me the data signal I need to know if anything requires attention that day.

Those three tools tell me where I am, what I need to do, and whether the business is moving. Everything else is in service of executing on what those three surfaces reveal.

**Section 2 — The Creative Tools (4-7min):**
For writing, everything goes into Notion first. Quick capture, longer drafts, final copy. I used to fragment my writing across five apps and constantly lost work or context. Notion as the single writing home fixed that. The trade-off is that Notion is not the best writing environment, but the advantage of everything in one place outweighs that.

For video, I use CapCut. I know there are more powerful tools. Premiere, DaVinci, Final Cut. For the style of content I make, CapCut handles everything without requiring a learning curve tax every time I open it. Speed of iteration matters more than maximum capability when you are a one-person operation.

For design, Canva handles 80 percent of my needs. Thumbnails, social graphics, slide decks, product covers. The 20 percent that Canva cannot do well, I do in Figma. But if I had to cut one, I would cut Figma.

For AI-assisted work, I use Claude. Draft generation, research synthesis, code when needed, editing. I treat it as a thinking partner rather than a ghostwriter. The work still needs to sound like me. Claude gets me there faster.

**Section 3 — The Infrastructure Tools (7-10min):**
These are the tools that run in the background and keep the business functioning without requiring daily attention.

ConvertKit manages my email list. Sequences run automatically. New subscribers get the welcome sequence. Product buyers get the post-purchase sequence. I check it once a week to review metrics and update copy when something is not performing.

Gumroad handles all product transactions. The dashboard is simple. Revenue, sales, refunds. The payout schedule works. The fee structure is clear. I have not had a reason to move to a different platform.

n8n is the automation engine. I run it on a dedicated machine. It connects everything and handles the workflows that would otherwise require me to manually transfer information between tools. The biggest one: new email subscriber triggers a sequence of events in Notion that tracks the subscriber through the funnel stages. This gives me a live view of how people are moving through the business.

For finances, a simple spreadsheet tracks income and expenses. I have not needed dedicated accounting software yet. When the business gets more complex, that will change.

### Script 3 — What I Would Cut From My Stack If I Had to Simplify

**Thumbnail concept:** List of 15 apps. Half crossed out. Bold text: "The 7 that actually matter." Red X marks on cut apps.

**Hook (0-30s):**
I have tested over 40 tools in the past two years. Some I paid for monthly for longer than I should have. Most got cut not because they were bad tools, but because they added complexity without adding proportionate value. Here is the honest audit of what I cut, what I kept, and the principle I now use to decide whether any new tool earns a place in my stack.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Tool bloat is a real problem for solopreneurs. Every new tool requires learning time, switching cost, and a mental overhead of managing it. Most tools promise to save time and many do, but they often save 30 minutes a week while costing 20 minutes of context-switching overhead. The math does not always add up. This video is the honest version of what happens when you actually audit your stack against the results it produces.

**Section 1 — The Tools I Cut and Why (2-4min):**
Notion AI. I had it for three months. It was convenient when I was already in Notion, but I was using Claude for everything it could do and doing it better. Duplicate function. Cut.

Zapier. I was paying for Zapier while also running n8n. The overlap was nearly complete. n8n handles more complex logic and runs on my own infrastructure at lower cost. Cut Zapier, kept n8n.

Notion Calendar integration. The idea of syncing my calendar with Notion seemed useful. In practice I never looked at it. My calendar app does one thing and does it well. Integration for its own sake adds complexity, not clarity.

Buffer for social scheduling. I tried three social scheduling tools over 18 months. I eventually cut all of them because by the time I was done setting up scheduled posts, I could have just posted them. For my current volume of content, direct posting is faster.

Jasper, then Writesonic, then several other AI writing tools. I went through a phase of testing AI writing assistants. All of them got replaced by a single Claude workflow that fits into how I actually think and write.

The pattern: I cut tools that duplicated something else in my stack, or tools where the workflow around using them cost more than the tool itself saved.

**Section 2 — The Principle I Now Use Before Adding Any Tool (4-7min):**
The principle is the friction test. I ask one question before adding any new tool: does this reduce friction on something I do every day, or does it create a new workflow I have to manage?

Friction reduction sounds obvious, but most new tools actually fail this test. They promise to make something easier while adding a new set of decisions: when to use the tool, how to integrate it, how to handle edge cases, what to do when it breaks.

The tool earns its place only if the friction it removes is greater than the friction it introduces. And the friction it removes has to be on something I do frequently enough for the time savings to compound.

The second question I ask: can I accomplish this with a tool I already have, even if it is slightly less elegant? Often the answer is yes. A Notion template can replace a dedicated habit tracker. A simple spreadsheet can replace a dedicated financial dashboard. The marginal gain from specialization usually does not justify the cost and complexity of a separate tool.

The third question: what does this tool look like in six months? Some tools are genuinely sticky. Once you build your workflow around them and store data in them, leaving is painful. I think harder about those tools before adopting them, because cutting them later is expensive.

**Section 3 — The Non-Negotiable Seven (7-10min):**
After two years of testing and cutting, here are the seven tools I would keep if I had to cut everything else.

Notion. Operating system for the business. Nothing replaces it.

Claude. Thinking partner, writer, coder, researcher. The Swiss Army knife of the AI layer.

ConvertKit. Email is too important to route through anything less reliable. It earns its keep every month.

Gumroad. Product delivery and payment. Simple, reliable, handles compliance.

n8n. Automation layer. The more complex my workflow gets, the more valuable this becomes.

CapCut. Video creation. Does 80 percent of what I need in 20 percent of the time of more complex tools.

A plain text editor. Drafts, ideas, quick notes. Sometimes the lowest-tech option is the right one.

Everything else is additive. Useful in some situations, replaceable in others. These seven are load-bearing.

If a tool cannot pass all three, consider cutting it on your next billing cycle.
