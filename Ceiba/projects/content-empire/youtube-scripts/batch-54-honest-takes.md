## Script 1: "Most AI Courses Are Scams. Here's How to Tell the Difference."

**Target length:** 10-12 min
**Hook type:** Strong claim with immediate credibility backing
**CTA:** Comment with courses you've bought, subscribe

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:50)

I'm not saying all AI courses are scams. I'm saying most of the ones that spend heavily on ads and promise specific income outcomes are not built to teach you anything. They're built to convert you.

There's a difference between a course that teaches and a course that sells. Here's how to tell them apart before you hand over your money.

---

### WHAT A SCAM COURSE LOOKS LIKE (0:50-4:00)

I want to be specific because vague warnings don't help anyone.

**The income claim in the thumbnail.** "I made $47,000 in 30 days with this AI side hustle." The income claim is the biggest red flag. Not because the number is always false — sometimes it's technically real. But because the framing implies you'll get the same result, which is almost never true. Real education doesn't need to promise you an income to get you interested.

**The mystery of the actual content.** Watch three videos from the creator before buying their course. Are the free videos vague hype or specific practical teaching? If the free content is vague and promises that the course goes deeper, be skeptical. Good course creators give away a lot and still sell you the structured version. Bad ones withhold everything to manufacture curiosity.

**The price is tied to "what it's worth" instead of what it cost to make.** "$497 for what normally costs $4,997." This is pure anchoring. The original number is made up. There's no $4,997 version. The price is whatever maximizes conversion from their ad audience.

**No clear skill outcome.** A good course answers the question "what will I be able to do when I finish this?" specifically. "You'll be able to build an n8n workflow that automates your client onboarding process" is specific. "You'll master AI automation" is not.

**Testimonials with no verifiable identity.** Screenshots of income with faces blurred or names hidden. Testimonials that all use the same phrasing and were clearly written by the same person. No LinkedIn profiles, no real businesses you can look up. This is fabricated social proof.

---

### WHAT A LEGITIMATE COURSE LOOKS LIKE (4:00-6:30)

Specific, testable skills. You should be able to describe the skill you're buying before you buy it.

A sample of the actual teaching style, not a highlight reel. Many good courses have a free first module. Watch it. Is the instructor clear? Do they go deep or stay surface level? Can you tell they've actually done the thing they're teaching?

Credentials that are verifiable and relevant. Not "I made $1 million with AI" — tell me what you've actually built. Real builders have things you can look at. GitHub repos, live products, client case studies with real companies, published work.

A realistic expectation of time and effort required. If the course promises you'll build something in a weekend with no prior knowledge, ask yourself: is this skill really that simple, or is the complexity being hidden to sell the course?

Price that matches scope. A 2-hour course doesn't cost $997. A 40-hour curriculum with ongoing support and community might be worth $500-1,500 depending on the demand for that skill. The price should make sense relative to what you're getting.

---

### THE MIDDLE GROUND (6:30-8:30)

Not everything is clearly scam or clearly legitimate. There's a lot of middle ground.

Some creators who hype aggressively still produce decent content. The marketing is misleading but the course itself has real value. You should still be cautious because the mismatch between the promise and the reality will frustrate you even if the content is technically okay.

Some well-priced courses with humble marketing are just mediocre. Good intentions, poor execution. The instructor knows the topic but can't teach.

The middle ground question to ask: if you removed all the income claims and testimonials, would you still want to buy this course based purely on what it teaches and who's teaching it? If yes, it's probably worth considering. If the answer is "I don't actually know what it teaches," walk away.

---

### WHAT TO DO INSTEAD (8:30-10:00)

Free resources first. For most AI skills I've learned, YouTube tutorials, official documentation, and Discord communities taught me more per hour than paid courses. Not always, but often.

Paid communities over paid courses. Some of the best learning I've gotten is from being in a community where people are building the same things I am. A $30/month community where practitioners share real work is worth more than a $300 course in some cases.

Books and documentation. Underused. Boring-sounding. Often the most accurate.

If you do buy a course: check for a money-back guarantee and use it if the course doesn't deliver. Legitimate creators offer this and don't fight refund requests. The ones who make refunds difficult are telling you something.

---

### RECAP + CTA (10:00-11:30)

Red flags: income claims tied to outcomes, vague content promises, fabricated testimonials, no verifiable instructor credentials.

Green flags: specific skill outcomes, sample of real teaching, verifiable instructor work, realistic expectations.

What's the worst course you've bought? Comment below. I want to hear what the pattern is for people actually in the market.

## Script 2: "I Tried Selling on Every Platform. Here's What Actually Worked."

**Target length:** 10-12 min
**Hook type:** Personal experiment recap
**CTA:** Subscribe, link to platform comparison guide

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:50)

Over the past year I put products on six different platforms. Different setups, different audiences, different fee structures. Some were a waste of time. Some surprised me.

This is what actually happened, not what the platform marketing pages say.

---

### THE PLATFORMS I TESTED (0:50-1:30)

Gumroad, Etsy, Amazon KDP, Hotmart, my own website (Shopify), and eBay for physical products.

Not all at the same scale. Some I invested real time into. Some I tried once and moved on. I'll be specific about what I actually tested rather than give you a one-size-fits-all ranking.

---

### GUMROAD (1:30-3:30)

This is where most of my digital product sales happen and where I've spent the most time.

What's good: incredibly simple to set up, handles everything from checkout to file delivery. The fee structure is clear — 10% on free plan, flat fee per month for higher tiers. You can have a product live in 20 minutes.

What's real but people don't say: Gumroad gives you almost no built-in discovery. You're not going to get organic traffic from the Gumroad marketplace the way Etsy gives you organic traffic. You bring your own audience. If you have no audience, Gumroad is just a checkout tool.

It's a great checkout tool. Use it for that. Don't expect it to be your marketing channel.

**Verdict:** Best for existing audiences. Useless as a discovery platform.

---

### ETSY (3:30-5:00)

I tested this with digital planners and printable templates. Two products. Three months.

What's real: Etsy has a built-in audience looking for these specific things. Discovery is better than Gumroad for products that fit Etsy categories. I got sales from complete strangers who found the products through Etsy search.

The limitation: the market is specific. AI automation guides don't sell on Etsy. Planners, templates, worksheets, design assets — yes. Anything that sounds like a business course — no. The Etsy audience is not the same as the creator economy audience.

Also: fees are real. Listing fees, transaction fees, offsite ads fees. Read the fee structure before pricing your products.

**Verdict:** Real discovery but only for the right product categories.

---

### AMAZON KDP (5:00-6:30)

I put one ebook on KDP. A short practical guide.

Discovery exists and it's real. People do buy random books on Amazon from authors they've never heard of. This is different from Gumroad.

The tradeoffs: you're in Amazon's ecosystem on Amazon's terms. The royalty rates (35% or 70% depending on pricing and region) are lower than Gumroad's effective rate for most sales. You can't collect buyer emails. You can't run your own promotions. Amazon owns the customer relationship.

For building a business, the email list matters more than the sale. KDP gives you the sale but not the relationship. You're building Amazon's customer base, not yours.

**Verdict:** Good for brand visibility and discovery, not good for relationship-building.

---

### HOTMART (6:30-7:30)

I set up a mirror of my main products here for the LATAM market specifically.

The local payment methods support is real and it matters. Sales I would have lost on Gumroad from buyers who couldn't pay with a US credit card, I recovered on Hotmart.

The platform is more complex to set up than Gumroad. The affiliate system is robust and creators in the Brazilian market use it heavily.

If you're targeting LATAM, you need a presence here. If you're purely targeting US/EU buyers, it's not worth the extra setup.

**Verdict:** Essential for LATAM, unnecessary for US-only.

---

### SHOPIFY (7:30-9:00)

My Shopify store exists. It has a logo and a theme and products. It has not generated significant organic sales.

This is entirely because Shopify is a store builder, not an audience builder. I knew this going in. The plan was to use Shopify as the destination for eBay tested products. That pipeline hasn't fully activated yet.

Shopify is the right choice for physical products and building a branded storefront you control. It's not a discovery platform. You build the traffic; Shopify is just the store.

**Verdict:** Right tool for the right use case. Not a shortcut to sales.

---

### EBAY (9:00-10:00)

I sold physical products on eBay as part of the resell testing pipeline. Specifically testing whether products I found would sell before listing them anywhere else.

eBay has real discovery for physical products. Buyers search eBay constantly. The fees are higher than most digital platforms but the audience is already there.

The reality of eBay for a solo operator: it's operational. Photographing products, writing listings, managing shipping, handling returns, responding to questions. It works but it's time-heavy compared to digital products where delivery is automatic.

**Verdict:** Real platform with real buyers. Time-intensive. Worth it for physical product testing.

---

### RECAP + CTA (10:00-11:30)

No platform does everything. Gumroad is the simplest checkout for existing audiences. Etsy has discovery for the right product types. KDP has scale but you lose the customer relationship. Hotmart is necessary for LATAM. Shopify is a branded storefront, not a traffic source. eBay works for physical products if you can manage the operations.

Match your platform to your current situation: audience size, product type, and which market you're targeting.

## Script 3: "The Business Advice That Hurt Me (And What I Do Instead)"

**Target length:** 9-11 min
**Hook type:** Counterintuitive personal lesson
**CTA:** Comment with advice that didn't work for you

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:50)

Not all popular business advice is bad. But some of it is context-specific advice presented as universal truth, and following it without understanding the context can slow you down significantly.

Here are three pieces of advice that hurt me and what I replaced them with.

---

### "PICK ONE THING AND FOCUS" (0:50-3:30)

This is the most popular solopreneur advice and I followed it religiously for about four months.

I picked one product. One platform. One type of content. I executed with focus. And I made almost no progress.

Here's the problem with "pick one thing" advice: it assumes the constraint is attention. Often the constraint is not attention — it's information. You don't know which of the three things you're considering will actually work until you try them. Focusing on the wrong thing with perfect discipline doesn't help.

What I do instead: I run small experiments in parallel on things that don't have dependencies. I can write a thread and record a video in the same week. I can have two products live at the same time. I can test two different offers simultaneously.

I still have a primary focus. I know what the most important thing is for each week. But I don't artificially limit myself from running low-effort tests on other bets.

The "pick one thing" advice is right when you have enough information to know which one thing is actually worth all your focus. Most people don't have that information yet and they're just guessing which thing to focus on.

---

### "BUILD YOUR AUDIENCE BEFORE YOUR PRODUCT" (3:30-6:00)

The logic: build an audience of people who trust you, then sell them something. If you have the audience, the product will sell.

I spent time building content with no clear product direction, no lead magnet, no email list, just growing a following. Then when I built the first product, I had an audience but no clear connection between the audience and the product.

The problem: audiences don't automatically buy. Audiences trust you for the thing they followed you for. If they followed you for entertaining content and you launch a practical how-to product, the conversion is lower than you'd expect.

What I do instead: I build the audience and the offer simultaneously. Every piece of content has a destination — a lead magnet or a product. The audience grows alongside a clear business model, not before it.

This also forces you to be specific about who you're trying to reach. When you're building an offer at the same time as content, you get clearer on your audience faster because you're already asking "would this person buy?"

---

### "KEEP YOUR PRICES LOW TO GET EARLY BUYERS" (6:00-8:30)

This one hurt me financially.

I priced my first products very low because I thought the barrier to entry would help me get buyers and testimonials. And it did — I got buyers. But low prices trained those buyers to expect low prices from me. When I raised prices, a portion of them didn't follow.

Low prices also communicate something about the quality of the product, even when the quality is genuinely good. A $9 product and a $49 product are perceived differently regardless of what's inside.

What I do instead: I price at what the market will bear for the value delivered. I offer a specific first-time buyer discount, not a permanent low price. And I'm clear about what justifies the price — specific outcomes, specific scope, specific credentials.

I'd rather have 20 buyers at $29 than 200 buyers at $2.99. The math is obvious. But more importantly, the higher-priced buyers are usually more engaged, more likely to apply what they bought, and more likely to come back.

---

### WHAT ACTUALLY HELPS (8:30-9:30)

Instead of universal advice, I pay attention to advice that's specific to my situation.

"What's the fastest way to validate that this product will sell?" is a more useful question than "should I focus on one thing?"

"Who specifically is going to buy this and why?" beats "build an audience first."

"What price makes this product believable and accessible?" beats "start low."

Context matters more than the advice itself. Ask where the advice came from, what their situation was, and whether your situation resembles it.

---

### RECAP + CTA (9:30-11:00)

Three things that didn't work for me: focusing on one thing before knowing which one was worth it, building audience without a simultaneous business model, and pricing low to acquire early buyers.

What I do instead: parallel small experiments, offer and audience built together, price at value with selective discounts.

What advice hurt you? Drop it in the comments. Genuinely curious.

## Script 4: "Why 'Passive Income' Is a Misleading Framing (And What to Say Instead)"

**Target length:** 8-10 min
**Hook type:** Language deconstruction
**CTA:** Subscribe, value ladder video

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:45)

"Passive income" is the most successfully marketed phrase in the creator economy and it's one of the most misleading.

Not because passive revenue doesn't exist — it does. But because the framing creates a false expectation about how you get there and what maintaining it actually looks like.

Here's the honest version.

---

### WHAT THE PHRASE IMPLIES (0:45-2:30)

"Passive income" implies you do work once and money flows forever without you doing anything. The dream version: you build a course in January and spend the rest of the year on a beach while the money comes in.

This framing has sold millions of courses and generated enormous engagement on social media. It's appealing because financial freedom is genuinely desirable. The desire behind the phrase is real.

The problem is the word "passive" — it obscures the ongoing inputs required to maintain revenue, the upfront work to build the asset, and the infrastructure that has to be in place for any of it to work.

When you follow the passive income path expecting to do work once and collect money forever, you're going to be disappointed by how much ongoing work is actually required.

---

### WHAT IT ACTUALLY LOOKS LIKE (2:30-5:00)

Digital products are probably the closest thing to genuinely passive that most solo operators can build. You build a product, someone buys it, the platform delivers it automatically. No active work per transaction.

But here's what you still need to do to keep that revenue coming in:

Publish content consistently to drive traffic to your products. Stop publishing, traffic drops, sales drop. This is not optional.

Update products when they become outdated. A guide to AI tools from 2023 that still mentions tools that no longer exist or features that have changed is going to get refund requests and bad reviews. Products require maintenance.

Build and maintain your email list. The email sequence that converts subscribers to buyers has to be written, updated, and maintained. When something breaks, you fix it.

Customer support. People have questions. Some products generate more questions than others. This takes real time.

The honest frame is not "passive" — it's "asynchronous" or "leveraged." You do the work at a different time than when you collect the revenue. The work still happened. The ongoing maintenance still happens. The word passive papers over all of it.

---

### WHY THE FRAMING MATTERS (5:00-6:30)

When you approach building a digital product business with "passive income" as your mental model, you make different decisions than if you approach it with "I'm building a leveraged system that still requires ongoing input."

Passive income thinkers: build a product, wait for money, get frustrated when it doesn't come, move to the next thing. Repeat.

Leveraged system thinkers: build the product as part of a system — content feeds the funnel, the funnel feeds the list, the list converts to buyers. Each part requires maintenance. The system produces revenue consistently.

The first framing generates a lot of abandoned projects. The second framing generates a real business.

---

### WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD (6:30-8:00)

When I talk about this kind of income, I say "scalable revenue" or "product revenue" or "asynchronous sales." These aren't as catchy. They don't generate as much engagement. But they're accurate and they set correct expectations.

"Scalable" because the revenue can grow without a linear increase in time spent — unlike service work where more revenue always means more hours.

"Asynchronous" because the timing between your work and your revenue is decoupled. You're not trading a specific hour of work for a specific payment.

"Product revenue" because it's revenue from a product, not from doing nothing. The product required work to build and requires work to maintain.

None of these are as marketable as "passive income." That's fine. The goal is not to maximize the marketability of how you describe your business model. The goal is to build something that actually works.

---

### RECAP + CTA (8:00-9:30)

Passive income is a misleading framing. The underlying thing is real: scalable, asynchronous revenue that grows without linearly more hours. But calling it passive sets expectations that don't match the reality of building and maintaining it.

Build with the correct mental model — ongoing system, not one-time setup — and you'll make better decisions about where to put your time.

## Script 5: "Dropshipping Reality Check: What Nobody Tells You About Month 2"

**Target length:** 10-12 min
**Hook type:** Time-specific framing
**CTA:** Subscribe, eBay research video link

---

### HOOK (0:00-0:50)

Month 1 of dropshipping gets covered endlessly. Set up your store, find a supplier, run your first ad, get your first sale. Content creators can make that story sound easy.

Month 2 is where reality arrives. The systems break down. The supplier has issues. The product you thought was a winner stops selling. The math stops working.

This is what month 2 actually looks like and why most people quit right before it gets better.

---

### WHAT MONTH 1 ACTUALLY IS (0:50-2:30)

Month 1 is the setup and testing phase. You're learning the platform, learning the product research process, learning how to write listings, learning how to handle customer questions.

If you get any sales in month 1, that's a signal, not a result. A signal that the product has demand and your listing is at least good enough to convert. It's not proof you've built a business.

The dopamine of first sales is real and it's useful — it keeps you going. But it creates a false sense of momentum. You haven't figured out the hard parts yet. You've just passed the tutorial level.

---

### WHAT HAPPENS IN MONTH 2 (2:30-6:00)

**The supplier problem.** Your product was in stock when you started. In month 2, inventory fluctuations are real. Your supplier runs out, ships late, ships wrong. You have buyers expecting delivery and a supplier who can't deliver. How you handle this is the first real operational test.

**The competition finds your niche.** If your product is good and your listing was successful, other sellers notice. Month 2 you start seeing lower-priced competition on the same or similar products. Your sales slow. Your margin gets squeezed. Do you lower your price and compress your margin further? Find a way to differentiate? Move to a different product?

**The ad math stops working.** If you were running paid ads, month 2 is when the audience gets saturated or your cost per acquisition creeps up. The first two weeks of ads often perform better than the next two because you're reaching the warmest audience segment first. Month 2 you're spending the same or more to reach colder buyers.

**Customer service gets real.** The first few weeks might go smoothly. By month 2, you have enough orders that you start seeing the full distribution. Returns, questions, complaints, damaged packages, wrong items. Each one requires your time and attention. How you handle these determines your feedback score and your ability to keep selling.

**The motivation dip.** This is the most predictable one and the least discussed. The novelty of month 1 is gone. You're doing operational work that feels like a job. Revenue might be flat or declining. The path from here is not obvious.

---

### WHAT SEPARATES THE PEOPLE WHO MAKE IT (6:00-8:30)

Three things.

First: they treat month 2 as the data collection phase, not the failure phase. Every problem in month 2 is information. The supplier issue tells you to qualify suppliers better or build redundancy. The competition issue tells you about market dynamics. The ad performance tells you about audience size and saturation. If you treat these as failures and quit, you lose the data before you can use it.

Second: they have a product research system, not just a product. One winning product is not a business. If you only have one product and it stops working, you're done. The people who make it have a consistent process for finding the next product. The eBay and product research methodology I've built is specifically designed for this — testing multiple products in parallel rather than betting everything on one.

Third: they separate the question "is this product working?" from "is this business working?" A single product dying in month 2 is normal. It's not a sign that the whole model is broken. The business question is whether your system for finding and testing products is working. If it is, one dying product is a data point, not a disaster.

---

### THE HONEST MONTH 2 TO 3 TRANSITION (8:30-10:00)

Month 2 is the hardest. Month 3, if you've been building the right things, starts to look different.

By month 3 you have: a supplier relationship that's been stress-tested, a product catalog that's been filtered by real sales data, a customer service process that's somewhat systematized, and actual knowledge of the market you're selling in.

The people who started alongside you and quit in month 2 are now your competitive advantage. You have information they gave up on acquiring.

This is not a promise that month 3 will be profitable. It's a statement that the curve of learning and iteration finally starts working in your favor around that point — if you're paying attention to what month 2 was trying to teach you.

---

### RECAP + CTA (10:00-11:30)

Month 2 is where supplier issues, competition pressure, ad performance decline, customer service volume, and motivation dip all arrive at the same time. This is not unusual. It's predictable.

The response: treat it as a data collection phase. Build a product research system, not just a product. Separate product failure from business failure.
