### Script 1 — The One-Person Business Model Explained (How to Build a $10K/Month Solo Operation)

**Thumbnail concept:** Single figure standing in front of a revenue dashboard showing $10,000. No team. No office. Just one person and a laptop. Bold text: "ONE PERSON. $10K/MO."

There is a business model that does not require employees, investors, an office, or a product that takes six months to build. It generates real income, scales without hiring, and you can start it this week. It is called the one-person business, and most people have no idea how it actually works. I am going to break the whole model down in this video.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
I am building a one-person business right now. Digital products, content, and automation services, all run by me, from my laptop, in Puerto Rico. I have studied how solo operators like Dan Koe, Justin Welsh, and hundreds of smaller creators have built this, and the model is more systematic than it looks from the outside. **Section 1 — What the One-Person Business Actually Is (2-4min):**
A one-person business is not a freelance gig. Freelance means you trade time for money directly. A one-person business creates systems that generate income whether you are working or not. The structure has four layers. Layer one is a content engine. You publish content consistently on one or two platforms that attracts the right people to you. This content does not sell directly. It builds trust and authority over time. Layer two is a digital product. Something you create once and sell repeatedly with no delivery cost. A guide, a template, a course, a tool. Layer three is a service. High-ticket, limited slots. You use your knowledge to help specific clients get results. This is your highest income per hour. Layer four is community or recurring. A newsletter, a membership, or a cohort program where people pay monthly to stay connected to you. When all four layers are running, you have predictable income from multiple directions. Most people try to skip to layer four without building layer one. That is why they fail.

**Section 2 — The Economics That Make This Work (4-7min):**
Here is why the math works better than a traditional job. A digital product priced at $27 sold to 370 people a month equals $10,000. You create it once. You market it through your content. Every piece of content you publish becomes a permanent salesperson working for you. A service at $500 per client with 10 clients a month equals $5,000. That is 10 hours of actual delivery if each client takes one hour. A newsletter with 5,000 subscribers and one $97 product offer per month generates $2,000 to $4,000 per issue if your list is engaged. These numbers are not theoretical. They are what people running this model actually see. The key insight is that your content is your distribution. You do not need ads. You do not need a sales team. You need to be genuinely useful and consistent in public, and the audience builds the pipeline for you.

**Section 3 — The First 30 Days (7-10min):**
Day one through seven: pick one platform and one topic. Not five platforms. Not three topics. One of each. The platform should be where your target customer is. The topic should be the intersection of what you know, what people want to learn, and what you would buy a product about. Days eight through fourteen: create your first digital product. It does not need to be a course. A well-organized PDF guide at $17 to $27 that solves one specific problem is enough. Use Gumroad to sell it. Days fifteen through twenty-one: post five times on your chosen platform about the problem your product solves. Do not pitch the product yet. Just be useful. Days twenty-two through thirty: make your first offer. One post or video where you mention the product directly, explain who it is for, and link to it. Track what happens. You will not make $10,000 in month one. But you will have built the foundation that every month from now runs on.

### Script 2 — How to Price Digital Products (The Framework That Doubled My Conversion Rate)

**Thumbnail concept:** Two versions of the same product listing. One at $7 with a red "WRONG" stamp. One at $47 with a green checkmark and "SOLD OUT" badge. Bold text: "PRICING KILLS YOUR SALES"

**Hook (0-30s):**
I used to price my digital products as low as possible because I thought that would make them sell faster. It did the opposite. When I raised prices, sales went up. Not because of demand, but because of perception. Pricing is not math. It is psychology. Here is the framework I use now.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Pricing is one of the most misunderstood parts of selling digital products. Most creators underprice because they are afraid of rejection. They think a lower price removes the barrier. What it actually does is remove the perceived value. Someone looking at a $5 guide thinks it is a quick read that will not change anything. Someone looking at a $47 guide thinks it contains real information worth acting on. The content can be identical. The price shapes the expectation. This video breaks down the pricing framework I use, the psychological principles behind it, and the exact price points I tested across different product categories.

**Section 1 — The Psychology of Price Points (2-4min):**
There are three zones in digital product pricing. Under $20: impulse buy territory. People buy these without much thought. Conversion rates are high but revenue per sale is low. You need volume to make money here. The sweet spot is $17 to $19, not $10, because $10 feels arbitrary and $20 crosses a psychological threshold. $20 to $99: the decision zone. Buyers think about this briefly. They read the description more carefully. They want to know specifically what they get. Conversion rates drop but not dramatically. Revenue per sale goes up significantly. This is where most digital products belong. $100 and above: high consideration. These are courses, coaching programs, templates with ongoing value. Buyers take time. They look for social proof. They want to feel confident in the investment. Conversion rates are low but one sale can equal 10 sales of a lower-priced product. Most people put their products in the first zone because it feels safe. They are leaving significant revenue on the table.

**Section 2 — How to Find Your Right Price (4-7min):**
The right price is the one that attracts buyers who act on the product. Underpriced products get purchased and ignored. Properly priced products get purchased and used. Here is the three-step process. Step one: look at what similar products sell for on Gumroad, Etsy, and competitor sites. Do not copy, but understand the market expectation. Step two: estimate the value your product delivers. If your guide saves someone five hours of research, and their time is worth $20 per hour, the product is worth $100 to them. Price at a fraction of that value, around $27 to $47. Step three: test. Start at the higher end of your range. If you sell zero in the first 30 days and you have real traffic, drop the price. If you sell consistently, test raising it. Most creators never test. They set a price and leave it forever, even when it is wrong.

**Section 3 — Bundling and Anchoring to Increase Revenue (7-10min):**
Two techniques that work reliably. Bundling: combine two or three related products and price the bundle at 20 to 30 percent less than buying them separately. The bundle feels like a deal. The buyer gets more, you make more per transaction. Anchoring: show a higher price before showing your actual price. "Normally $97. Today $47." Even if it was always $47, the anchor makes it feel like a discount. This is not deceptive if the value is real. It is helping the buyer make a decision. I also use a three-tier structure on some products: basic at $17, standard at $37, premium at $67. Most buyers choose the middle option. Price your most important product at the middle tier.

### Script 3 — Why Most Creators Stay Broke (The Real Reason It Is Not About Followers)

**Thumbnail concept:** Creator with 50,000 followers label and "$0 REVENUE" badge. Another creator with "2,000 followers" label and "$8K/MO" badge. Bold text: "FOLLOWERS LIED TO YOU"

**Hook (0-30s):**
There are creators with 100,000 followers making less than $500 a month. There are other creators with 2,000 followers making $8,000 a month. The difference is not the follower count. It is not the niche. It is not even the content quality. It is one thing most creators completely ignore. I am going to tell you exactly what it is.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
I spent a long time believing the model was: get followers, then make money. It is backwards. The creators who make real money built the money infrastructure first. The audience came after. Or the audience came at the same time as the infrastructure. But the infrastructure had to exist. In this video I am going to break down the five mistakes that keep creators broke, and what to do instead. This applies whether you have zero followers or 100,000.

**Section 1 — Mistake 1: Content Without a Destination (2-4min):**
Most creators post content with no place to send people. No product. No email list. No service. They are building an audience that has nowhere to go when they are ready to buy. Every piece of content should point somewhere. Not aggressively. Not in every post. But somewhere. The destination can be: a free lead magnet that captures emails, a digital product that solves a specific problem, a service you offer, or a community you run. If you have been posting for three months and you still do not have a way to convert a follower into a buyer, you are doing marketing without a business. Fix the destination first. Then build the audience.

**Section 2 — Mistake 2: The Monetization Menu Problem (4-7min):**
The second mistake is trying to monetize in too many ways at once. Sponsorships. Affiliate links. A course. A membership. Consulting. When you spread across all of these, you go deep on none of them. The creators making real money have one primary income source that they optimize relentlessly. Justin Welsh makes most of his money from two courses. Dan Koe from a writing product and a high-ticket program. Ali Abdaal from one book and courses. Pick one. Run it hard for six months before adding a second. The other monetization channels can exist, but they should be passive. Do not run active campaigns for five things simultaneously. You will do all of them poorly.

**Section 3 — Mistakes 3, 4, and 5 (7-10min):**
Mistake three: not building an email list. Social media platforms do not own your audience. You do not own your audience either until you have their email address. When Instagram changes the algorithm or your account gets restricted, you lose distribution overnight. An email list is yours. Start building it before you think you need it. Any lead magnet will do. A free PDF, a checklist, a mini course. Mistake four: pricing for scale instead of for value. I covered this in the last video. The short version: stop underpricing because you are afraid. Price for the value you deliver. Mistake five: waiting to be ready. The creators who make money today started before they were ready. Their first product was imperfect. Their first content was rough. They shipped it anyway and improved in public. Waiting for perfect is waiting forever. Ship the minimum version this week.
