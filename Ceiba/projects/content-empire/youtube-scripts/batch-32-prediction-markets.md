## Script 32-1: "What are prediction markets and why they're more accurate than polls"

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**
[B-roll: poll graphic showing 60/40 split, then Polymarket showing 78/22 split on same event]
Polls ask people what they think. Prediction markets ask people to put money on what they think. The difference in accuracy between those two methods is not small.

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**
[B-roll: Polymarket interface, market list, resolution prices]
I am Kalani. I study computer engineering and I build AI tools for solo businesses. I also trade prediction markets. This video is about what prediction markets are, how they work, and why the aggregate signal from real money is more informative than any survey or pundit.

This is educational content, not financial advice. Never trade money you cannot afford to lose.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45 | The fundamental difference between polls and markets]**
[B-roll: survey form vs. trading interface side by side]
Timestamp: 0:45

A poll asks: what do you believe will happen? There is no cost to answering wrong. People overestimate their confidence, project their preferences onto outcomes, and give strategic answers to influence the poll itself.

A prediction market asks: are you willing to stake money on this outcome? The cost of being wrong is real. That cost filters the noise. Only people with genuine conviction and informed beliefs tend to hold large positions.

When being wrong costs you money, you become more careful about being wrong. That is the entire logic of prediction markets.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:00 | How prediction markets set prices]**
[B-roll: contract price moving from 30 cents to 75 cents as news breaks]
Timestamp: 2:00

Each outcome in a prediction market is a contract. If you buy a contract for $0.60 that pays $1.00 if the event occurs, you are betting on a 60% probability. If you think the real probability is 80%, buying at $0.60 is positive expected value.

Prices move as traders buy and sell. New information enters the market. Prices adjust. The market is constantly aggregating the beliefs of everyone participating, weighted by how much money they are willing to stake.

This is the wisdom of crowds mechanism, but with skin in the game. Random opinions are worth nothing. Paid-for opinions are worth something.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 3:30 | Historical accuracy of prediction markets vs. polls]**
[B-roll: election night graphics, market prices vs. poll averages side by side]
Timestamp: 3:30

The research consistently shows prediction markets outperform polls. In political events specifically, market prices tend to be better calibrated than polling averages, especially for low-probability events.

The 2016 and 2024 U.S. elections are the most cited cases. Polls showed much tighter races than the market prices implied in some states. The markets, not the polls, had better-calibrated uncertainty.

This does not mean markets are always right. They are wrong regularly. But on average, over many events, the market price is a better estimate of true probability than a survey of stated preferences.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 5:00 | What prediction markets are actually used for]**
[B-roll: Polymarket markets list: elections, sports, crypto, economic data]
Timestamp: 5:00

Prediction markets exist for political events, sports outcomes, cryptocurrency prices, Federal Reserve decisions, company earnings reports, and even scientific research replication.

Platforms like Polymarket run on blockchain infrastructure. The contracts are settled automatically when outcomes resolve. No counterparty risk, no manual settlement.

The scope of what can be predicted is expanding. Any event with a verifiable outcome and enough public interest can become a prediction market. That scope is part of what makes them interesting as research tools, separate from trading them.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 6:30 | The limitations of prediction markets]**
[B-roll: thin market, one side heavily weighted, low volume warning]
Timestamp: 6:30

Prediction markets are not infallible. Thin markets with low liquidity can be moved by a single large trader whose position distorts the price signal. Low-volume markets are less informative than high-volume markets.

Markets also reflect existing public information. They do not predict unpredictable events. A market on a geopolitical event that nobody has information about will cluster around 50/50 because nobody knows more than chance suggests.

The market is as good as the information feeding into it. Where there is genuine uncertainty and no asymmetric information, the market price converges to fair value very fast.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 8:00 | How to read a prediction market as a research signal]**
[B-roll: person reading Polymarket, taking notes, comparing to news]
Timestamp: 8:00

Even if you never trade, prediction markets are useful as a research signal. Before a major policy announcement or election result, check the market price. It reflects what the most informed and financially committed participants believe.

When the market price diverges sharply from media consensus, that divergence is informative. Either the market knows something the media is not pricing in, or the market is wrong and there is an opportunity.

Reading markets is a skill separate from trading them. Calibrating your own beliefs against market prices makes you a better analyst.

---

**[CONCLUSION — 9:30]**
[B-roll: Polymarket dashboard, multiple markets open]
Prediction markets are not a gimmick. They are an information aggregation mechanism with a track record of outperforming most alternatives.

Understanding how they work, what they measure, and where they fail is useful regardless of whether you ever place a trade. The skill of reading market probabilities is applicable everywhere you need to think clearly about uncertain outcomes.

## Script 32-2: "Polymarket: how to read the odds and what the market is actually saying"

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**
[B-roll: Polymarket interface loading, market list scrolling]
Polymarket prices are not predictions. They are probability estimates built from real money. Reading them correctly requires knowing what the price actually represents.

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**
[B-roll: specific Polymarket contract open, price hovering at 65 cents]
Polymarket is the largest prediction market platform by volume. Billions of dollars have been traded on it across political, economic, and crypto events. The prices it generates are referenced by journalists, analysts, and researchers as real probability signals.

This video is a practical breakdown of how to read Polymarket. What the prices mean, how to interpret the order book, and what the market is actually communicating.

This is educational content, not financial advice. Never trade money you cannot afford to lose.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45 | The basic price mechanic]**
[B-roll: contract showing YES at $0.67 and NO at $0.33]
Timestamp: 0:45

A Polymarket contract has two sides: YES and NO. The prices always sum to approximately $1.00. If YES is at $0.67, that is a 67% implied probability. NO at $0.33 is a 33% implied probability.

When you buy YES at $0.67, you are paying 67 cents for a contract that pays $1.00 if the event happens. Your profit if correct: 33 cents per contract. Your loss if wrong: 67 cents per contract.

The price is not a prediction from Polymarket. It is the current equilibrium of all buy and sell orders in the market.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:00 | Reading price movement as information]**
[B-roll: price chart of a market moving from 50 to 80 over 48 hours]
Timestamp: 2:00

Price moves in a prediction market carry meaning. When a market moves from 50% to 80% in 24 hours, something happened. Either new information was released, a large well-informed trader entered a position, or the situation on the ground changed in a visible way.

Watching how markets move in response to news is more informative than reading the news alone. The market tells you how the most financially committed participants are interpreting events.

A market that barely moves on major news is telling you: the market already priced this in. That non-reaction is itself informative.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 3:30 | The order book and what it reveals]**
[B-roll: order book screen, stacked bids and asks]
Timestamp: 3:30

The order book shows pending buy and sell orders at various prices. Deep liquidity on both sides means the market has genuine two-sided participation. Thin liquidity means the price can be moved easily and should be weighted less.

When you see a large wall of sell orders at a specific price, some participant believes the market is unlikely to go higher than that. When you see a large buy order, someone is defending a floor.

You do not have to trade to learn from the order book. It is a map of conviction levels at various price points.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 5:00 | Common misreadings of Polymarket prices]**
[B-roll: pundit on TV citing Polymarket incorrectly, then correction graphic]
Timestamp: 5:00

The most common misreading: treating a 70% market as a prediction that the event will happen. It is not. It is saying the market assigns 70% probability. Thirty percent of the time, the 70% favorite does not happen.

The second misreading: assuming a market drop means something bad happened. A drop from 70% to 50% means new information reduced certainty. It does not mean the outcome is now unlikely. Both sides are still possible.

The third misreading: trusting a thin market. A market with $10,000 total volume is not the same signal as a market with $10 million. Volume matters.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 6:30 | How to use Polymarket for research (not just trading)]**
[B-roll: research notes, Polymarket tab open alongside news articles]
Timestamp: 6:30

Even if you never place a trade, Polymarket is a useful calibration tool. Before a major event, note the market price. After it resolves, note whether the market was well-calibrated.

Over time, you build an intuition for what 60% really feels like compared to 90%. Most people have miscalibrated confidence on uncertain events. Tracking prediction markets teaches you what probability actually means in practice.

For business decisions, the same logic applies. What is the actual probability that this strategy works? Not "I think it will work" but what would you bet at what odds.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 8:00 | Limitations and failure cases]**
[B-roll: market that moved sharply then reversed, post-mortem chart]
Timestamp: 8:00

Polymarket has resolution risk. If the resolution criteria are ambiguous, disputes happen. The platform has resolution processes but they are not always fast or obvious.

Manipulation risk exists on thin markets. A single trader with enough capital can move a low-volume market to an extreme and then exit when natural correction brings it back.

The platform is also not available in all jurisdictions. U.S. citizens face legal restrictions on using prediction markets. Know your local laws before engaging.

---

**[CONCLUSION — 9:30]**
[B-roll: Polymarket dashboard, multiple markets visible]
Polymarket is a research tool that happens to also be a trading platform. Reading it well requires understanding what prices represent, how liquidity affects signal quality, and where the mechanism breaks down.

Most people who dismiss prediction markets have not looked at them carefully. Most people who obsess over them do not understand their limitations. The useful position is in between.

## Script 32-3: "The edge in prediction markets: why most retail traders lose and how to avoid it"

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**
[B-roll: P&L chart going negative over time, then positive]
Most people who trade prediction markets lose money over time. Not because the markets are rigged. Because they have no edge. Here is what edge actually means and how to find it.

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**
[B-roll: trading interface, position ledger, mix of green and red]
In every market, for every dollar someone profits, someone else loses a dollar. This is zero-sum. The question is always: why would you be on the winning side of that trade?

If you cannot answer that question clearly, you do not have an edge. And without an edge, trading is a slow donation to people who do.

This is educational content, not financial advice. Never trade money you cannot afford to lose.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45 | What edge means in a prediction market context]**
[B-roll: probability estimate written on paper vs. market price on screen]
Timestamp: 0:45

Edge is having a better probability estimate than the current market price. If the market says 40% and you have genuine reason to believe the true probability is 65%, that gap is your edge.

Edge is not intuition. It is not a gut feeling or a political preference. It is a structured, defensible reason why your estimate of probability differs from the market's and why your estimate is more accurate.

Most retail traders do not have edge. They have opinions. Opinions without information advantage are worth less than the market price.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:00 | The main sources of edge]**
[B-roll: news sources, data feeds, domain expertise annotations]
Timestamp: 2:00

Information edge: you have access to or can process information the market has not fully priced in. This is the hardest edge to maintain. Markets are fast at processing public information.

Domain expertise edge: you know an industry, a political landscape, or a scientific field well enough to evaluate events that generic market participants cannot. A doctor trading on FDA drug approval markets has domain expertise that a generalist trader does not.

Analytical edge: you process publicly available information more accurately than the average market participant. Better probability calibration, better base rate thinking, better Bayesian updating.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 3:30 | Why retail traders lose]**
[B-roll: overconfident position sizing, all-in on one market]
Timestamp: 3:30

The primary reason retail traders lose is overconfidence in weak signal. They read the same news as everyone else, form the same opinion as everyone else, and bet as if their opinion is contrarian. It is not.

The second reason: emotion-driven position sizing. Conviction feels like certainty. Certainty produces oversized positions. When those positions lose, the loss is catastrophic relative to the account size.

The third reason: no exit criteria. Retail traders often know when to enter but have no logic for when to exit. A position that is losing needs pre-defined criteria for cutting it, not hope.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 5:00 | Building a simple edge assessment framework]**
[B-roll: checklist written in Notion, questions about each trade]
Timestamp: 5:00

Before any position, answer three questions. One: why is the market price where it is. Who is on the other side and why are they right. Two: what do I know or believe that is different and why is my source of that belief reliable. Three: what would have to be true for the market to be correct and am I confident enough in my alternative that I would stake real money on the difference.

If you cannot answer all three, do not enter the position. The discipline to skip trades with no clear answer is where most of the long-term P&L comes from.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 6:30 | Bankroll management as an edge multiplier]**
[B-roll: Kelly Criterion formula, position size calculator]
Timestamp: 6:30

Even a trader with genuine edge can lose everything through poor position sizing. Betting 50% of your account on a single 70% market is not rational even if your estimate is accurate.

The Kelly Criterion provides a mathematical framework for optimal bet sizing given your estimated edge. Most serious traders bet a fraction of Kelly. Half Kelly or quarter Kelly is common.

Bankroll management does not give you edge. But it prevents edge from being destroyed by variance. The trader who survives long enough to let their edge compound is the trader who wins.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 8:00 | Markets where retail can compete]**
[B-roll: niche market categories on Polymarket: science, local events, crypto]
Timestamp: 8:00

Retail traders have the best chance in markets that institutional capital ignores. Low-volume, niche markets where domain expertise matters more than speed.

Large political markets like U.S. elections have billions in volume. Professional traders, sophisticated algorithms, and well-funded research teams dominate. The retail information advantage is near zero.

A niche market on a specific scientific study replication might have $50,000 in total volume and mostly uninformed participants. A researcher in that field has genuine edge there that they do not have in a $100M election market.

---

**[CONCLUSION — 9:30]**
[B-roll: disciplined trading setup, checklist visible, modest position sizes]
Edge is earned, not assumed. Most traders enter markets assuming their opinion is worth more than the market price. It usually is not.

The traders who survive and profit are the ones who ask the hard question before every trade: why would I win this. And who stop trading when they cannot answer it.

## Script 32-4: "How I research a prediction market position (my actual process)"

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**
[B-roll: research notes, browser tabs open, Polymarket contract visible]
Most traders look at a market, feel a pull toward one side, and enter. My process is slower and more systematic. Here is exactly what I do before touching a position.

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**
[B-roll: research workflow: news tabs, base rate data, position sizing calculator]
This is a walkthrough of my actual research process for a prediction market position. I will use a real example structure, anonymized, to show each step.

This is educational content, not financial advice. Never trade money you cannot afford to lose. I am a CS student sharing a process, not a licensed analyst recommending trades.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45 | Step 1: Read the resolution criteria before anything else]**
[B-roll: contract resolution page, careful reading of exact terms]
Timestamp: 0:45

Before looking at the price, read exactly how the market resolves. Many bad trades happen because a trader assumes a resolution criteria and bets on it without reading the actual terms.

Markets resolve on specific, narrow criteria. A market titled "will X happen in Q2" might resolve based on a specific data source, a specific announcement, or a specific court filing. If the outcome you predict is real but does not match the resolution criteria, you lose.

I read the resolution criteria twice. Then I ask: what is the exact scenario where this resolves YES and what is the exact scenario where it resolves NO.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:00 | Step 2: Establish the base rate]**
[B-roll: historical data search, similar past events, percentage outcomes]
Timestamp: 2:00

Before consulting any current news, I look for historical base rates. How often have similar events resolved the way the market is pricing.

If a market is at 80% and historical base rates for similar events are closer to 55%, the market is pricing something above the historical norm. I want to know why. Either there is new information justifying the premium, or the market is overconfident.

Base rates are not destiny. They are anchors. They tell me where the burden of proof lies.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 3:30 | Step 3: Map the information landscape]**
[B-roll: news source quality matrix, credibility notes]
Timestamp: 3:30

I list every piece of publicly available information that is relevant to the outcome. Not just the information that supports my initial lean. All of it.

Then I ask: which of this information has the market already priced in. News from yesterday morning is already in the price. A report published an hour ago might not be.

The market processes new information fast. The edge from news is very short-lived. The edge from domain knowledge or better probability calibration lasts longer.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 5:00 | Step 4: Estimate the probability independently]**
[B-roll: probability written down on paper before looking at market price]
Timestamp: 5:00

After reviewing base rates and available information, I write down my probability estimate before looking at the current market price. This is critical.

If I look at the market price first, I anchor to it. My estimate will be biased toward whatever the market shows. By writing my estimate first, I can compare it against the market without contamination.

If my estimate is 55% and the market is at 55%, there is no bet. The market and I agree. If my estimate is 75% and the market is at 45%, that gap warrants further investigation.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 6:30 | Step 5: Stress-test the position]**
[B-roll: devil's advocate notes, opposite position being written out]
Timestamp: 6:30

Before entering, I spend ten minutes building the strongest possible case for the opposite side. If I am leaning YES, I try to convince myself NO is the right bet.

This is not to change my mind automatically. It is to identify what I might be missing. If the best case for the opposite side requires information I do not have or reveals an assumption I made that might be wrong, I pause.

Positions that survive stress-testing are the ones I trust most.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 8:00 | Step 6: Size the position and set exit criteria before entry]**
[B-roll: position size calculation, stop-loss and exit note written down]
Timestamp: 8:00

Position size is set before entry, not after. I use a fraction of Kelly based on the size of the gap between my estimate and the market price.

More important: I write down the conditions under which I would exit the position before the market resolves. What new information would cause me to close early. What price movement would signal that the market knows something I do not.

Traders who set exit criteria before entry make decisions with a clear head. Traders who make exit decisions mid-position make them with emotion.

---

**[CONCLUSION — 9:30]**
[B-roll: research notes compiled, clean position entry logged]
The research process is slower than most traders want it to be. That is the point. The trades that go wrong usually went wrong before entry. Poor resolution criteria reading, no base rate anchoring, no stress-testing.

A slower entry process filters out the weak trades. What remains is a smaller number of positions with clearer justification and better expected value.

## Script 32-5: "Prediction markets vs stock markets: key differences for a retail trader"

**[HOOK — 0:00-0:15]**
[B-roll: S&P 500 chart vs. Polymarket contract, side by side]
Most traders apply stock market intuitions to prediction markets and lose. The mechanics are different enough that your existing mental models will hurt you here.

**[INTRO — 0:15-0:45]**
[B-roll: two trading interfaces open, comparison layout]
I trade both. They are different animals. The skills that help you in one do not transfer cleanly to the other. Understanding the structural differences is the first step to operating in both without making systematic errors.

This is educational content, not financial advice. Never trade money you cannot afford to lose.

---

**[SECTION 1 — 0:45 | Binary vs. continuous outcomes]**
[B-roll: stock price chart vs. prediction market resolving to $1.00 or $0.00]
Timestamp: 0:45

Stock prices are continuous. A stock can go from $100 to $150 to $80 over any time period. Your profit or loss is the spread between your entry and exit prices.

Prediction market contracts are binary. Either the event happens and the contract pays $1.00, or it does not and it pays $0.00. The only variance is in the probability estimate during the life of the contract.

This changes everything about how you think about position management. In stocks, you can hold a losing position and still exit above zero. In a prediction market, if you are wrong, you lose 100% of the contract cost.

---

**[SECTION 2 — 2:00 | Time horizon and resolution]**
[B-roll: stock chart over years, prediction market countdown timer]
Timestamp: 2:00

Stocks can be held indefinitely. A stock thesis can play out over years. Time is on the side of a good long-term thesis in equities.

Prediction markets have defined end dates. The market resolves when the event happens or when the deadline passes. You cannot wait out a bad entry. If the event does not happen by the resolution date, you lose.

This makes time management critical in prediction markets in a way it is not in stocks. A correct long-term view is worth nothing if the resolution date does not accommodate it.

---

**[SECTION 3 — 3:30 | Liquidity profiles]**
[B-roll: stock order book deep vs. prediction market thin book]
Timestamp: 3:30

The most liquid stock markets have trillions in daily volume. The most liquid prediction markets have millions. Most prediction markets have thousands.

Low liquidity means your position size is limited before you start moving the market price against yourself. It also means the price signal is less reliable.

In stocks, you can trust that Apple at $180 is a real price because millions of transactions support it. In a thin prediction market, the price might reflect only a few trades and can be manipulated by a single participant.

Adjust your trust in the signal accordingly.

---

**[SECTION 4 — 5:00 | Information processing speed]**
[B-roll: algorithmic trading vs. human reading news]
Timestamp: 5:00

Stock markets have algorithmic traders processing news in milliseconds. Any public information is priced in before a human can react. The information advantage for retail in liquid stock markets is effectively zero in the short term.

Prediction markets move slower. Not because the participants are less informed, but because the participant base is smaller and less capitalized. A major news event can take minutes or hours to fully price into a prediction market.

That lag is one of the few places retail can find a real-time information edge in prediction markets that does not exist in stock markets.

---

**[SECTION 5 — 6:30 | What does and does not transfer from stocks]**
[B-roll: checklist, skills column and prediction markets applicability column]
Timestamp: 6:30

What transfers: probability thinking, position sizing discipline, reading macro signals, not trading on emotion, setting exit criteria before entry.

What does not transfer: technical analysis of price charts. Prediction market prices move based on event probability, not on chart patterns. RSI, MACD, moving averages: none of that applies here. The price goes to $1.00 or $0.00 at resolution. There is no multi-year price trajectory to analyze.

What also does not transfer: holding through volatility. In stocks, a temporary dip can be ignored. In prediction markets, a significant move against you often reflects real new information about the resolution probability. You have to update, not hold blindly.

---

**[SECTION 6 — 8:00 | Regulatory and jurisdictional differences]**
[B-roll: legal disclaimer graphic, map showing jurisdiction restrictions]
Timestamp: 8:00

This is critical. Prediction markets face different regulatory regimes than stock markets in most jurisdictions.

In the United States, binary options are heavily regulated and most prediction market platforms are not accessible to U.S. residents due to CFTC rules. Polymarket, the largest platform, operates on blockchain and has restricted U.S. access.

Stock markets are heavily regulated but accessible to retail investors in most major economies with basic account setup.

Know your jurisdiction and the legal status of prediction market trading before putting any money into any platform.

---

**[CONCLUSION — 9:30]**
[B-roll: both interfaces side by side, trader's notes comparing them]
Prediction markets and stock markets share a surface structure: buy low, sell high, manage risk. The underlying mechanics are different enough that you need separate mental models for each.

The trader who understands both and knows which skills are transferable and which are not will make fewer systematic errors moving between them.
