## Script 1: How to Build a Digital Product in 24 Hours

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Most people spend six months thinking about their first digital product. I built one in 24 hours. Not because I'm fast. Because I stopped treating it like a big thing. Here's exactly what I did, step by step, no theory.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I'm going to walk you through a real 24-hour build. The product I'm talking about is a PDF guide. Simple format, low friction, fast to produce. I know that sounds underwhelming. But a PDF that solves a specific problem will outsell a course that covers everything. I've tested this. The people who make real money with digital products are not the ones with the most content. They're the ones who got something out the door and started learning what actually sold.

This is the process I used to go from idea to published product in one day.

**Section 1 -- Finding the problem (1:30-3:30):**
I started with one question: what do people in my space keep asking about? Not what I want to teach. What people keep asking.

I went to Reddit. Searched for communities around the topic I was working in. Looked for threads with high upvote counts and lots of comments. Not the top posts. The posts where people were clearly frustrated or confused. That frustration is the signal. If someone writes a three-paragraph post asking how to do something, there are a thousand people who searched for that and found nothing.

I wrote down five specific questions I saw repeated in different forms. Then I picked one. The narrower the better. A guide called "How to price your first digital product" will always outsell "The complete guide to digital products." Every time.

Once you have the specific question, you have your product. The product is the answer.

**Section 2 -- Building the outline in two hours (3:30-5:30):**
I opened a blank document and wrote every answer I knew to that question. Not organized. Just everything. Stream of consciousness. This took about 30 minutes.

Then I looked at what I had and found the natural order. What do you need to know first before the next thing makes sense. That sequence becomes your table of contents.

For a PDF guide targeting 8-12 pages, I shoot for five to seven sections. Each section answers one sub-question. If a section is trying to answer two things, I split it.

I do not use AI to write the content. I use it to check my outline. I paste my outline and ask: what's missing that someone would need before they can use this. That usually surfaces one or two things I skipped because they were obvious to me but not to someone just starting.

**Section 3 -- Writing the draft (5:30-7:30):**
One sitting. No editing while writing. The goal is a complete draft, not a polished one.

I write like I'm explaining to one specific person. Not a general audience. One person I know who would need this. Every sentence has to pass this test: would this person need to read this, or are they already past it. If they're past it, I cut it.

My average PDF guide runs 2,500 to 3,500 words. That's eight to twelve pages at a readable font size with some visual breathing room. That's enough to deliver real value without padding.

I write the draft in under four hours. The key is not stopping. If you get stuck on a section, write a placeholder and keep moving. You can fill it in later. A complete messy draft is more useful than a perfect introduction.

**Section 4 -- Formatting and publishing (7:30-9:30):**
I use a Canva template. I'm not a designer. I don't need to be. The job of the design is to make the content readable, not to look impressive.

Black and white with one accent color. Clear headers. Enough white space that it doesn't feel dense. No stock photos. Maybe one or two simple diagrams if the concept benefits from visual explanation.

Export as PDF. That's the product file.

For publishing I use Gumroad. Account setup takes fifteen minutes if you don't have one. Upload the file, write the product description, set the price, hit publish. Done.

The description is not a sales page. It's a clear explanation of what the person will know after reading it that they don't know now. Specific. No fluff.

**Section 5 -- What actually matters (9:30-11:00):**
You will not get this perfect on the first try. That's not the point. The point is to build something that can be improved based on real feedback.

The 24-hour constraint is not about rushing. It's about removing the permission structure you've been waiting for. You don't need more research. You don't need a bigger audience. You need to ship something and see what happens.

My first product sold three copies in the first week. That was enough signal to keep going. The second product sold better because I understood my audience a little more. By product five, I had a system.

## Script 2: Why Gumroad Is Still the Best Platform for First-Time Creators

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
There are twelve platforms you could use to sell digital products. I've tried six of them. I keep coming back to Gumroad. Not because it's perfect. Because for where most people are starting, it removes more friction than anything else.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
Every few months someone writes a thread about how Gumroad is dying, or overpriced, or how you should use Lemon Squeezy or Stan or Podia instead. Some of those critiques are fair. But they're usually written by people who already have an audience and are optimizing for things that don't matter when you're just starting.

I want to walk you through exactly why I chose Gumroad for my first products, what the tradeoffs are, and when it actually makes sense to switch to something else. I'm going to be specific. No vague comparisons.

**Section 1 -- What Gumroad actually costs you (1:30-3:30):**
Gumroad charges ten percent of every sale on the free plan. That's the number people complain about.

Here's what that means in practice. If you sell a ten-dollar product, they take a dollar. If you sell a fifty-dollar product, they take five dollars. That's real money, and I'm not going to pretend it isn't.

But the alternative is paying a monthly fee before you've made a single sale. Podia starts at $33 per month. Kajabi starts at $149 per month. If you're selling zero products, you're paying that every month anyway. The Gumroad model means you pay nothing until you make money. For someone validating their first product, that's a better deal regardless of the percentage.

The Gumroad Pro plan is $10 per month and drops the transaction fee to zero. Once you're making consistent sales, that math changes. But I didn't start there.

**Section 2 -- Setup time is not a trivial thing (3:30-5:30):**
I've helped people try to set up Podia and Kajabi. Those platforms have powerful features. They also have enough settings and configurations that you can spend a week setting up your store before you publish a single product.

Gumroad setup is under an hour. Create account, add payment info, upload your file, write your description, publish. The interface is basic. That's not a criticism. That's the feature.

When you're starting, every hour you spend on platform configuration is an hour you're not building the product or writing the content that drives traffic. Complexity is the enemy at the beginning. You can add complexity once you have something that sells.

**Section 3 -- The discovery factor (5:30-7:30):**
Gumroad has a built-in marketplace. It's not huge. But it exists. Some creators get meaningful traffic from Gumroad discovery, especially in certain niches. You're not going to build your business on Gumroad discovery, but it's a real channel that other platforms don't have.

The other thing Gumroad does well is embeds. You can embed a Gumroad product card anywhere. On a blog, in a newsletter, on a landing page. The transaction still happens through Gumroad but the experience looks native to your site. That matters for conversion.

**Section 4 -- Where Gumroad falls short (7:30-9:30):**
Let me be honest about the gaps.

Gumroad's email tools are minimal. If you want automated sequences, segmentation, or any real email marketing functionality, you'll need a separate tool. ConvertKit or Beehiiv. Gumroad can collect emails and send simple broadcasts. That's it.

The checkout page customization is limited. You can add a cover image and write a description. You can't build a real sales page inside Gumroad. For products under thirty dollars, that probably doesn't matter much. For higher ticket products, you'll want to drive traffic to a standalone landing page and then link to Gumroad for checkout.

Course delivery is also not Gumroad's strong point. If you're building a structured course with multiple modules, there are better tools. Gumroad can deliver video content, but the experience is not as clean as a dedicated course platform.

**Section 5 -- When to switch (9:30-11:00):**
Once you're making consistent monthly revenue, the ten percent fee adds up fast. At that point, switching to a flat-fee platform makes financial sense.

Once you're building structured courses with multiple modules, a dedicated course platform gives you a better student experience.

Once you need serious email marketing built into your product delivery, you'll want something more integrated.

But those are later problems. If you haven't sold your first product yet, none of that matters. Get something published and get your first ten sales. Then optimize.

## Script 3: How I Priced 34 Products: My Exact Framework

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Pricing is the decision most creators get wrong because they're trying to be fair instead of being strategic. I've priced 34 products across three categories and I've had to rethink my approach multiple times. Here's the framework I actually use.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I want to talk about pricing in a way that's actually useful, which means being specific about numbers. Not "price based on value" and leaving it there. I'm going to walk through the three tiers I use, the psychology behind each price point, how I've adjusted prices after launch, and what I've learned from pricing mistakes.

This is not theory. These are the actual prices on my actual products and the reasoning behind them.

**Section 1 -- The three tier structure (1:30-3:30):**
Everything I sell falls into one of three tiers.

Tier one is the entry tier. Seven to fifteen dollars. The job of this tier is not to make significant revenue. The job is to convert cold traffic into buyers. Someone who buys a seven-dollar guide from you is fifteen times more likely to buy something more expensive from you later. That's the data. The entry tier is a trust purchase.

At this price point, the product has to be good. Not padded good. Actually useful good. If the seven-dollar product is a ten-page guide that solves one specific problem clearly, that's a strong tier-one product. If it's fifty pages of loosely organized information, it doesn't matter what you charge for it.

Tier two is the core product tier. Twenty-seven to forty-seven dollars. This is where most of your revenue will come from if you have a functional audience or any kind of content strategy pointing at your store. The product at this tier needs to be more complete. Not longer necessarily, but more complete. It should take the person from a defined starting point to a defined outcome.

Tier three is the premium tier. Ninety-seven to two hundred dollars. At this price point, the product needs to include a stronger outcome guarantee or a significant time savings. Most of my tier-three products include templates, frameworks, or done-for-you components. Not just information. Usable assets.

**Section 2 -- How I pick the price within a tier (3:30-5:30):**
Within each tier there's still a range. Here's how I narrow it down.

I look at what similar products sell for in the same niche. Not to copy the price. To understand what buyers in this space consider normal. If everyone is charging forty-seven dollars for a similar product, charging twenty-seven might actually hurt you because buyers assume something is missing.

I think about the buyer's cost of the problem. If the problem costs someone $500 per month in wasted time, a $47 solution is easy math. If the problem is minor inconvenience, the same $47 feels expensive. Price relative to the cost of the problem, not the cost of the product.

I consider format. A template is worth more than the same information written out as a guide. A checklist with a video walkthrough is worth more than the checklist alone. Format affects perceived value even when the underlying information is similar.

**Section 3 -- Pricing mistakes I made (5:30-7:30):**
I underpriced my first six products because I was uncomfortable charging for something I'd made. That's an ego problem dressed up as a pricing decision. I thought lower prices would make them easier to sell. They didn't. They made buyers wonder what was wrong with them.

I also made the mistake of anchoring all my prices on what I would personally pay for something. That's irrelevant. You are not your customer. I wouldn't pay $97 for a Shopify product research template because I know how to build one. My customer doesn't. That's the entire reason they're buying it.

The third mistake was not testing price adjustments. I set a price at launch and left it there even when something wasn't selling. A product that doesn't sell at $27 might sell well at $17 or at $47. The price is a variable, not a commitment.

**Section 4 -- Post-launch pricing adjustments (7:30-9:30):**
I give every product 30 days at its launch price before I consider changing anything. One month is enough data to see if the price is the problem or if the traffic or the product itself is the problem.

If a product gets clicks but no purchases, the price might be the issue. Or the product description. Or the cover image. I change one variable at a time.

If a product gets purchases but a lot of refund requests, the price is probably fine but the product isn't delivering what the description promised.

I've raised prices on three products after seeing strong early sales. If something sells well at $27, it can probably sell at $37. I raise the price and monitor for a few weeks. If conversion holds, the new price stays.

**Section 5 -- Bundling and tiering (9:30-11:30):**
Once you have multiple products, bundling is one of the most reliable ways to increase average order value.

I group related products into bundles priced at roughly 60 to 70 percent of what they would cost individually. So three $17 products that would cost $51 together become a $37 bundle. The buyer feels like they're getting a deal. You increase the revenue per transaction without creating any new product.

Tiered pricing within a single product also works. A base version at $27. A version with templates at $47. A version with templates plus a bonus video at $67. You'll find that about 30 to 40 percent of buyers choose the middle tier, and 15 to 20 percent choose the top tier. This is consistent across products.

## Script 4: The Content That Actually Sells Digital Products

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
Most content about selling digital products tells you to "add value" and "build trust." That's true and it's useless. I want to show you the specific post types and content formats that have actually moved sales for me. Specific. With examples.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
I've been posting content for Behike for a while now and I've tracked what drives product clicks versus what drives follows versus what drives nothing. They are not the same type of content. Most people mix them all up or just post whatever feels right and wonder why they have followers but no buyers.

There are five content types that actually convert to product sales. I'm going to go through each one, explain why it works, and give you a real example of what it looks like.

**Section 1 -- The problem + cost post (1:30-3:30):**
This is the single most reliable content type for selling digital products.

The structure is simple. You name a specific problem your audience has, then you make the cost of that problem concrete. Not abstract. Concrete. Numbers, time lost, money wasted, specific pain.

Example. "Most eBay sellers waste three hours per week writing listings that don't rank. That's twelve hours per month. That's 144 hours per year on a task that could take 20 minutes with the right process."

Then you present the solution. Which can be your product or can just be a tip. The product mention at the end is optional. What matters is that the person reading it thinks "I have this exact problem."

This type of post works because it does the psychological work of justifying the purchase before the person even clicks the link. They already feel the cost. The product is the obvious next step.

**Section 2 -- The before and after post (3:30-5:30):**
Before and after is not just for fitness. It works for any transformation that involves a system or skill change.

My version of this for digital products is not "I made zero dollars, now I make X." That's too vague and people don't believe it. My version is process-level before and after.

"Before: I spent four hours picking a product to test on eBay. I'd look at trending lists, search on Alibaba, check the price, list it, and usually get no sales. After: I spend 45 minutes. I check completed listings for sell-through rate, verify the supplier is on AliExpress, confirm the margin is above 30%, and list. That's the system."

The specific numbers are what make this believable. Four hours versus 45 minutes. No vague claims. This type of content positions you as someone who has actually solved the problem, which is the core credential for selling a product about it.

**Section 3 -- The mistake breakdown post (5:30-7:30):**
People stop scrolling when they see someone admit a mistake. It's a pattern interrupt.

The structure: name the mistake, explain why it's so common, show what the correct approach looks like.

This works for digital product sales because it positions you as someone who has done the wrong thing and figured out the right thing. That's more credible than someone who just tells you what to do.

For me, this looks like: "I priced my first product at $9 because I thought it would sell faster. It didn't. Lower prices don't reduce sales resistance. They increase doubt. Here's what I do now."

The lesson is the product. The product is the extended version of the lesson.

**Section 4 -- The result without the hype post (7:30-9:30):**
Share real results with real context. Not income screenshots with no explanation. Not vague "I made money" content.

Real example: "I sold 14 copies of a $27 guide in the first week. It came from two Instagram posts, one Reddit comment where I genuinely answered a question, and one DM from someone who asked for help. No ads. No email list. The distribution is what I'm building."

Why this works: it shows the process, not just the outcome. People can follow the process. They can't replicate an outcome. When someone reads a result with context, they're already mentally modeling how they could do the same thing. That mental model is what gets them to click on your product.

Keep these posts honest. If you had a bad week, you can say that too. Consistency of honesty builds more trust than consistency of wins.

**Section 5 -- The specific tip with no pitch (9:30-11:00):**
Some of your best sales come from content that doesn't mention your product at all.

A specific, immediately usable tip that comes from the same expertise your product is built on. If someone saves that post or shares it, they're signaling to the algorithm and to themselves that they trust your information.

The key word is specific. "Write better headlines" is not a tip. "Add a number and a timeframe to your headline. Not 'How to price your product' but 'How to price your first product in under 10 minutes.' Specificity increases clicks by 30-40% in my testing." That's a tip.

These posts don't need a product link. They build the credibility that makes every other post work.

## Script 5: Building a Digital Product Business at 20 with No Audience

**Hook (0:00-0:30):**
I'm 20 years old, I'm a computer engineering student in Puerto Rico, and I've built 34 digital products with no existing audience, no startup capital, and no mentor. I want to be honest about what that actually looks like.

**Intro (0:30-1:30):**
This is not a success story video. It's an in-progress story video. I'm not going to tell you I've figured it out. I'm going to tell you what I've learned so far and what I'd do differently if I started over six months ago.

I think a lot of content about building online businesses is made by people who are already successful and are telling the story backwards. They've forgotten what it actually felt like to start with nothing. I haven't forgotten because I'm still in it.

Here's what building this actually looks like from the inside.

**Section 1 -- Why I started here specifically (1:30-3:30):**
I chose digital products over ecommerce, freelancing, and content creation as my primary focus for a specific reason. Digital products are the only model where you do the work once and it can sell repeatedly with no additional time from me.

I'm a student. I have maybe three to four hours of focused work per day that I can dedicate to building something. Freelancing would eat all of that and then some, and I'd be trading time for money with a ceiling. Ecommerce requires constant attention to inventory, suppliers, customer service. Digital products let me build in concentrated sprints and then let the asset work while I'm in class.

That logic held up. But the part I didn't fully understand was that building the product is maybe 20 percent of the job. The other 80 percent is distribution.

**Section 2 -- What no audience actually means (3:30-5:30):**
When I say no audience, I mean I started with zero followers on any platform, no email list, no existing community, and no one who knew what Behike was.

The first month was humbling. I published three products. They got maybe 40 combined page views. Two sales total.

The thing about having no audience is that you have no feedback loop. You don't know if the product is bad, if the description is bad, if the price is wrong, or if the right people just haven't seen it yet. All of those look identical from zero.

What I learned is that you have to build the distribution channel before the product becomes meaningful. Not a huge following. A small, specific, actual group of people who have the problem your product solves. I found mine through Reddit, through commenting on other creators' posts, and through being genuinely useful in online communities before I ever mentioned I had something to sell.

**Section 3 -- How I built 34 products without burning out (5:30-7:30):**
I have ADHD. I'm not going to skip that fact because it's directly relevant to how I build.

The way I avoid burning out is by batching creation. I don't work on one product until it's perfect before starting the next. I do idea generation in one session. Outlining in another. Writing in another. Design in another. That separation keeps any single step from feeling like a grind.

I also keep products small by design. My average product is 8 to 15 pages. Not because I can't write more. Because a focused 12-page guide that solves one specific problem will always sell better than an unfocused 80-page guide that covers a topic broadly. Small products are also faster to build and faster to validate.

The 34 products happened over about four months. Some weeks I built three. Some weeks I built zero. The total matters more than the pace.

**Section 4 -- What actually got the first ten sales (7:30-9:30):**
Not social media posting. Not SEO. Not ads.

Genuine participation in communities where my customers already were.

I found three Reddit communities related to my product topics. I spent two weeks answering questions, being useful, not mentioning my products at all. Then when it was appropriate, I mentioned in a comment that I had a guide on the exact topic someone was asking about.

That got me my first six sales. Six people who bought because I had already demonstrated I knew what I was talking about.

The other four came from a single Instagram post that got shared by someone with a larger following. Not a viral post. A specific, useful post that one person with 8,000 followers found valuable enough to repost.

Neither of those things was a strategy I designed. They were accidents that taught me what actually works. Useful content in the right communities and specificity in every post.

**Section 5 -- What I'm still figuring out (9:30-11:30):**
I don't have email marketing figured out. I have a list but I barely use it. That's a gap I'm actively working on.

I'm also still figuring out how to build content consistently while managing school, building products, and running an eBay side operation at the same time. Most days one of those things gets less attention than it should.

And I'm still learning when to stop iterating on something and just publish it. I spent two weeks on a product that I could have published in three days. The extra time didn't make it twice as good. It made it slightly better and cost me two weeks.

I'm telling you the gaps because I think the most useful thing I can offer at this stage is not a success blueprint. It's an honest account of what it looks like to build something real while being young, broke, and still in school.
