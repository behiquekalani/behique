**Video 1: Building Habits That Actually Stick When You Have ADHD**
**Channel:** Lifestyle
**Hook (0-30s):** The reason most habit advice does not work for people with ADHD is that it was written for brains that experience time linearly and have a consistent relationship with motivation. ADHD brains do not. Here is what actually works.
**Intro (30s-2min):** ADHD is not a lack of willpower. It is a difference in how the brain responds to dopamine and how it processes time and urgency. When you understand that, habit-building stops being about discipline and starts being about design. You design an environment and a system that makes the habit happen without relying on motivation, which for ADHD brains is unpredictable at best. This video is practical. No theory lecture. Just the approaches that actually work.
**Main Content:**

**The Core Problem: Motivation Is Not Reliable**
Neurotypical habit advice assumes you can remember to do the habit, feel motivated to do it, and then do it consistently over time. ADHD disrupts all three. The fix is to remove memory from the equation, reduce the activation cost of starting, and make the habit itself inherently rewarding rather than relying on future rewards.

**Approach 1: Habit Stacking on High-Interest Activities**
If you reliably do one thing every day (make coffee, open your laptop, sit in a specific chair), that activity becomes your anchor. Attach the new habit immediately before or after it. "After I make coffee, I write three sentences in my journal." The anchor behavior carries the habit through without requiring a decision. The key: the anchor must already be automatic, and the stacked habit must take less than two minutes to start.

**Approach 2: Reduce Friction to Almost Zero**
The gap between thinking about doing something and actually doing it is where ADHD habits die. Reduce the gap. Put the thing you need to do directly in your path, literally. Want to take supplements? Put them on top of your keyboard. Want to read more? Physical book on your pillow. Want to work out? Shoes by the door. The goal is zero setup time.

**Approach 3: Time Blocking with Flexibility**
Fixed schedules break down for ADHD brains because unexpected stimulation or hyperfocus derails them, and then the entire day feels like a failure. Instead: use time blocks as intentions, not appointments. "Morning block is for deep work. Afternoon block is for communication." You still decide what within the block, but the structure reduces decision fatigue without punishing you for being ADHD.

**Approach 4: External Accountability Over Internal Willpower**
Tell someone about the habit. Check in publicly. Use a Telegram accountability group, a friend who asks you every morning if you did the thing, an app that shows a streak. External accountability is not a crutch. For ADHD brains, it is a prosthetic. There is no shame in using the tools that work for your brain.

**Video 2: The Student Founder Playbook — Running a Business While Finishing School**
**Channel:** Lifestyle
**Hook (0-30s):** I am running a business while finishing my computer engineering degree. Here is what I have learned about not failing both simultaneously.
**Intro (30s-2min):** The two biggest lies about being a student founder are that you need to drop out to succeed and that you can fully do both without tradeoffs. Neither is true. You do not have to drop out. But you do have to be honest about the tradeoffs you are making and intentional about how you structure your time. This video is for the person trying to build something real while still showing up to lectures and finishing assignments.
**Main Content:**

**Accept That You Cannot Do Both at Full Capacity**
The person who graduates with a 4.0 GPA while also building a six-figure business is the exception, not the rule. Most student founders who succeed are average-to-good students, not top of their class. That is a real tradeoff and it is okay. What matters is that your grades do not get low enough to create actual consequences (academic probation, lost scholarships, failing core courses). Define your floor and protect it.

**The Non-Negotiable Block**
Pick one time block per day that is always for the business. Not "when I have free time." A fixed block. For most students this is 7am to 9am before classes, or 9pm to 11pm after. Two hours per day is 14 hours per week. That is enough to run an early-stage digital product or service business. Use that block for output, not planning. Thinking happens in the margins. Building happens in the block.

**Use School Resources, Not Just Time**
You have access to resources most full-time entrepreneurs pay for. Computing clusters, research databases, professors who are subject matter experts, a built-in network of potential customers, users, and collaborators in your cohort. Use your coursework where it overlaps with your business. Building an automation tool? Your software engineering class is relevant. Selling digital products? Your marketing elective is research.

**The Portfolio Mindset**
Document what you are building, publicly. A student who ships products, writes about what they are learning, and shows their process is not just building a business. They are building a portfolio that will outperform any GPA when they decide whether to continue building post-graduation or enter the job market. The documentation is compounding asset creation.

**Energy Management by Semester**
Not all semesters are equal. Heavy course loads demand adjusted expectations for the business. Lighter semesters are sprint opportunities. Plan your product launches and major business initiatives around your academic calendar. This is not making excuses. It is intelligent resource allocation.

**Video 3: Energy Management Over Time Management — The Reframe That Changed Everything**
**Channel:** Lifestyle
**Hook (0-30s):** Time management assumes all hours are equal. They are not. The hour before you have rested is not the same as the hour right after your best sleep. Stop scheduling your most important work at the wrong time and start scheduling it when your brain is actually capable of doing it.
**Intro (30s-2min):** You have 24 hours in a day. So does every other person alive. Time is not the bottleneck. Energy is. Specifically, cognitive energy, the mental bandwidth available for difficult, creative, or high-stakes work. Most productivity systems are built around time management. This video is about energy management, which is the underlying principle that makes time management actually work.
**Main Content:**

**The Energy Curve**
Most people have two to four hours of peak cognitive performance per day. For the majority, this window falls within the first three to four hours after fully waking. During this window, your prefrontal cortex (the decision-making, creative problem-solving part of your brain) is most active. Whatever work requires your highest cognitive output needs to happen here. Not emails, not admin, not meetings. Your hardest, most important task.

**Protecting Peak Hours**
Once you know your peak window, protect it like a non-negotiable appointment. No meetings before noon if you are a morning peak person. No low-energy tasks (scrolling, responding to messages, administrative work) in your best hours. This single constraint, ruthlessly applied, produces more output than any time management system.

**The Three Energy States**
High energy: creative work, deep focus, strategy. Medium energy: communication, reviewing work others have done, learning new material. Low energy: admin tasks, routine processes, passive consumption. Match the task to the state. Never do creative work in a low-energy state and then wonder why it was not good.

**Recovery Is Not Optional**
Sleep is the biggest lever. A well-rested brain in five hours of work outperforms a tired brain in twelve. Exercise and daylight are the second and third levers. Not because they are morally virtuous but because they directly modulate the neurochemistry that determines how much cognitive energy you have available. This is not lifestyle advice. It is system design.

**The Weekly Energy Audit**
Once per week, spend ten minutes reviewing which days felt productive and which did not. Identify what was different. Was it sleep quality? What you ate? What time you started? Whether you moved your body that day? Over a month, patterns emerge. Those patterns are the data you need to optimize your schedule.

**Video 4: Minimalism for Productivity — What to Cut and What to Keep**
**Channel:** Lifestyle
**Hook (0-30s):** Minimalism is not about owning the fewest possible possessions or living in a white apartment with no furniture. It is about intentionally removing everything that is not earning its place in your life and your environment. Here is how that applies directly to your productivity.
**Intro (30s-2min):** Every object in your environment that is not actively serving your current goals is competing for cognitive bandwidth. Every tab open in your browser, every app on your phone, every project you said you would get to eventually, all of it creates low-grade mental noise. Minimalism for productivity is the practice of reducing that noise systematically so the things that matter can move faster. This video is practical. You can apply it today.
**Main Content:**

**Digital Minimalism First**
Start with your devices because that is where most of your cognitive overhead lives. Audit every app on your phone. Delete anything you have not intentionally opened in the last 30 days. Remove all notifications except calls and direct messages from people you actually know. Move social media apps off your home screen, not delete them, just make them require intention to access. This single change reduces mindless distraction more than any willpower exercise.

**The Desktop and Tab Rule**
Your computer desktop should have zero loose files. Your browser should never have more tabs open than you can act on in the current session. If you cannot close a tab because you might need it later, save it to a read-later tool like Readwise or a notes app and close it. The visual noise of 30 open tabs consumes attention even when you are not looking at them.

**Project Minimalism**
Most productivity problems are really project overload problems. If you have ten active projects and can only meaningfully advance two or three, the other seven are not projects. They are anxiety. Cut your active project list to three maximum. Everything else goes into a someday/maybe list that you review quarterly. This is not giving up. It is honest prioritization.

**Tool Minimalism**
Every additional tool you adopt requires maintenance, learning, and switching cost. The creator who uses one note-taking app, one task manager, and one communication tool will outperform the creator who uses twelve half-adopted apps in a complicated system. Ask every tool: what would I lose if I removed this? If the answer is "not much," remove it.

**Physical Space and Work Quality**
Clear surface, clear mind is not a myth. Research on environmental psychology confirms that visual complexity in the workspace increases cognitive load. Your desk does not need to be sterile, but it should have only what you are working on today. At the end of each session, clear it. The act of clearing is also a signal to your brain that the session is closed.

**Video 5: Creating Work-Life Structure as a Solopreneur**
**Channel:** Lifestyle
**Hook (0-30s):** When you work for yourself, every hour is technically available. No one is telling you when to start or stop. That sounds like freedom. After about three months, it starts to feel like the opposite. Here is how to build structure that protects both your work and your life.
**Intro (30s-2min):** Structure is not the enemy of creativity or flexibility. Structure is what makes creativity sustainable. Without it, solopreneur life turns into a blur where you are always partially working and never fully present anywhere. Your creative output suffers, your relationships suffer, and your sense of progress suffers because everything bleeds into everything else. This video gives you a framework for building structure that actually holds.
**Main Content:**

**The Start Ritual**
Your brain needs a signal that work has started. Without a commute or an office, you have to create that signal deliberately. It can be simple: make your coffee, sit at your dedicated workspace (even if it is just one chair or one desk), open your task manager, review your top three priorities for the day. The ritual itself matters less than its consistency. Do the same thing every day to start work and your brain will learn to shift into focus mode on cue.

**The Stop Ritual**
This is more important and more often skipped. Define a time when work ends. Then actually stop. Not stop-ish. Close the laptop, put the phone face down, do something that is categorically not work. If you cannot stop, you do not have freedom. You have a prison with a nicer view. The stop ritual might be a short walk, a specific piece of music, cooking dinner. Anything that is clearly not work.

**The Weekly Review**
Once per week, probably Sunday or Monday morning, do a ten-minute review. What got done last week? What did not get done? What is on the agenda this week and is it realistic given your actual energy and schedule? This is not a planning session. It is a reality calibration session. It prevents you from perpetually planning while never shipping.

**Batching by Task Type**
Context switching is expensive. Every time you shift from deep work to communication to administrative tasks and back, you lose 15 to 20 minutes of focus recovery time. Solve this by batching. All emails and messages in one block. All creative or deep work in another. All administrative tasks in another. You do not respond to messages during your deep work block. They wait.

**Building in Non-Negotiable Rest**
One day per week where you do not open your work tools. Not a planning day, not a "light work" day. Off. The science on this is clear: sustained cognitive performance requires periodic full rest. The founders and creators who burn out do so because they removed rest from the schedule when they felt behind. Rest is not a reward for finishing. It is part of the system.
