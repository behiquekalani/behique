## Script 1: "From idea to Gumroad in 4 hours: the FL Studio AI guide build"

**Format:** Behind-the-scenes / case study
**Length:** ~9 min
**Hook type:** Speed claim, then reality check

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:30)

Four hours from blank document to live Gumroad listing.

That's not a pitch. That's the actual timeline for the FL Studio AI guide. I'm going to show you exactly what happened in those four hours, every decision, every shortcut, and where the time actually went.

---

### SECTION 1: Why this product, why now (0:30 - 2:00)

The FL Studio AI guide wasn't on my product roadmap.

I was doing product research with my competitor monitor when I noticed three different creators had guides about music production software. All of them were general. None of them were about using AI inside the production workflow specifically.

That gap is the product.

The opportunity is specific: someone who already uses FL Studio wants to know how AI fits into their existing workflow, not how to start from scratch. That's a narrow audience. Narrow audiences buy faster because the product is clearly for them.

The decision to build it was made in about five minutes. The research supported it. The question was how fast I could ship it.

---

### SECTION 2: The build timeline (2:00 - 6:00)

Hour one: outline.

I opened a blank document and wrote the table of contents first. Ten sections, each with a single sentence describing the content. This is the minimum viable structure. If I can't write a one-sentence description of a section, I don't understand it well enough to write it.

For the FL Studio guide, the sections were: current AI tools in the DAW ecosystem, how to use AI for sample generation, MIDI generation with AI, vocal processing with AI tools, mixing assistance, mastering suggestions, prompt writing for music-specific AI, workflow integration, real examples with timestamps, and a resource list.

That took forty minutes.

Hour two: first draft.

I wrote every section at whatever quality came out. No editing. No stopping to research mid-sentence. If I didn't know something, I wrote a placeholder and kept moving. The goal was a complete draft, not a good one.

Complete drafts are more valuable than polished fragments. You can edit a complete draft. You cannot ship a perfect fragment.

That took sixty minutes.

Hour three: revision and cuts.

I read through the draft and cut anything I didn't personally know to be true. Anything sourced from vague memory got removed. The guide shrank by about twenty percent. That's fine. A tighter guide is a more trustworthy guide.

Then I formatted it. Headers, numbered lists, one insight per paragraph. The reading experience matters. A wall of text is a product that feels like homework.

That took fifty minutes.

Hour four: PDF, cover, listing.

I exported to PDF. I opened the product cover template and generated a cover using the existing system. I wrote the Gumroad listing: title, short description, long description, price. The listing copy took twenty minutes because I already knew the product. I had just written it.

I uploaded everything, set the price at $24.99, and hit publish.

---

### SECTION 3: What made it possible (6:00 - 8:00)

Two systems that didn't exist six months ago.

The product cover template. I have a Figma file with a cover template that takes about ten minutes to fill in. Before that, every cover was a one-hour design session. The template reduced that to ten minutes.

The listing formula. Every Gumroad listing follows the same structure: one-line hook, problem, what's in it, who it's for, what they'll be able to do after. When you have a formula, writing the listing is filling in blanks. It's not starting from zero.

The four-hour build is only possible because of infrastructure built in prior sessions. The first product took three days. The fifteenth took four hours. The work was in building the system, not just the product.

---

### CLOSE (8:00 - 9:00)

The guide is at behike.shop. The Gumroad listing is linked in the description.

If you want the listing formula I use for every product, it's in the description as a free download.

## Script 2: "The product that made me rethink my entire pricing strategy"

**Format:** Case study / decision breakdown
**Length:** ~8 min
**Hook type:** Specific revelation

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:30)

I had a $9.99 product outselling my $19.99 product two to one.

That should not have surprised me. It did. And understanding why it happened changed how I price everything now.

---

### SECTION 1: The setup (0:30 - 2:00)

The $9.99 product was the ADHD Finance template. A spreadsheet. Simple. The $19.99 product was the AI Employee Guide. Twenty pages, detailed, more work to produce.

The AI Employee Guide had better reviews. Higher perceived value from everyone I showed it to. It was objectively more useful.

It sold half as much.

The temptation was to lower the price on the guide. That was the wrong instinct. The problem wasn't the price. The problem was that the template had something the guide didn't: an obvious, immediate use case.

---

### SECTION 2: What I figured out (2:00 - 5:00)

Price sensitivity on digital products is not a straight line. There is not a rule that says cheaper always sells better.

The real variable is perceived activation cost. Not price. Activation cost.

A $9.99 spreadsheet template has zero activation cost. You buy it, you open it, you see your numbers. Done. Fifteen minutes later you have something.

A $19.99 guide has high activation cost. You buy it, you open it, you have to read twenty pages, you have to decide what to implement, you have to actually implement it. That's hours of work before you see a result.

The guide is more valuable in the long run. But the template is easier to start. People buy what they can see themselves using right now.

This explains why short courses outsell long courses at similar prices. Why templates outsell strategy guides. Why checklists sell easily and frameworks sell with effort.

The fix isn't to make everything cheap. The fix is to reduce activation cost at every price point.

---

### SECTION 3: What I changed (5:00 - 7:00)

Every product above $14.99 now includes a quick-start component. A checklist, a template, a one-page summary. Something with zero activation cost that gets you a result in fifteen minutes.

The AI Employee Guide now ships with a two-page "first day implementation" checklist. You can ignore the full guide and just do the checklist. If the checklist helps you, you come back for the guide.

Bundling changed too. I stopped bundling by theme and started bundling by journey. A starter bundle, a builder bundle, a complete bundle. Each one has at least one zero-activation-cost item as the entry point.

Sales on the guide went up by about forty percent in the month after I added the checklist. The guide itself didn't change. Only the entry point changed.

---

### CLOSE (7:00 - 8:00)

The ADHD Finance template and the AI Employee Guide are both at behike.shop.

If you're pricing your own products and wondering why the expensive ones aren't selling, think about activation cost before you think about price.

## Script 3: "What happened when I posted consistently for 30 days (real numbers)"

**Format:** Transparency report / case study
**Length:** ~9 min
**Hook type:** Numbers lead, then context

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:30)

Thirty days. Ninety-four Instagram posts across two accounts. 1,847 new followers. Eleven Gumroad sales.

Those numbers are real and they're not what I expected. Here is the actual breakdown.

---

### SECTION 1: The context (0:30 - 2:00)

Two accounts: @behikeai for the business brand and @kalaniandrez for personal content.

I started both on the same day with zero followers. The content strategy was different for each.

@behikeai posted AI tools, digital product tips, and builder content. Three posts a day: a carousel, a reel, and a text post. Fully systematized with pre-written batches.

@kalaniandrez posted more personal content. Project updates, thoughts on building while in school, the occasional behind-the-scenes. One to two posts a day, less structured.

The goal was to test whether the systematic approach or the personal approach grew faster. The answer was not what the algorithm advice online would suggest.

---

### SECTION 2: The numbers (2:00 - 5:30)

Follower growth by week:

Week 1: @behikeai +89, @kalaniandrez +143
Week 2: @behikeai +201, @kalaniandrez +178
Week 3: @behikeai +412, @kalaniandrez +198
Week 4: @behikeai +567, @kalaniandrez +259

Total: @behikeai 1,269, @kalaniandrez 578.

@behikeai won on volume but took longer to start. The personal account had faster early growth because I shared it with people I already knew. The business account started slower and accelerated.

Reach: @behikeai had three posts that each hit above 4,000 accounts. All three were carousels. The reels performed worst on reach, which contradicts the standard advice to prioritize reels.

The eleven Gumroad sales all came from @behikeai. Three came directly from a post that included the product link. Eight came from new followers who found the Gumroad store through the link in bio.

Revenue from the thirty days: $142.87.

---

### SECTION 3: What I learned (5:30 - 8:00)

Carousels are the format. On @behikeai, every post above 2,000 reach was a carousel. Reels averaged 400-600 reach. This is the opposite of what most creators say. My theory: the audience is builders who read. They swipe carousels.

Consistency compounds late. The first two weeks felt like nothing was working. Week three was when things shifted. The account hit a threshold where the algorithm started recommending it to non-followers. This is the part that kills most new accounts. Most people quit in week two.

Personal content builds trust faster, business content builds authority faster. The @kalaniandrez audience is warmer but smaller. They engage more per post. The @behikeai audience is larger but less engaged per post. Both are useful for different reasons.

Eleven sales in thirty days from zero is not viral growth. It is proof that the system works at a small scale and will scale further with a larger audience. At 10x the followers, the projection is 100+ sales per month. That is not a guarantee. It is a hypothesis worth testing.

---

### CLOSE (8:00 - 9:00)

Month two starts tomorrow. I'll post the full numbers again at sixty days.

If you want the content batch template I used to pre-write ninety-four posts, it's in the description.

## Script 4: "How I went from 0 to 25 products: the complete timeline"

**Format:** History / case study
**Length:** ~10 min
**Hook type:** Scale statement, then the real story

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:30)

Twenty-five digital products. Most of them built in the last two months.

The timeline doesn't look like the usual overnight success story. There were dead ends, products I built and abandoned, and a long period where I had the infrastructure but nothing to sell.

Here's the honest version.

---

### SECTION 1: The first product and what went wrong (0:30 - 2:30)

The first product was the Roblox Builder's Guide.

I picked it because I knew Roblox and I knew that parents were looking for ways to teach their kids coding through games. The product made sense. The audience was real.

What went wrong: I built it for an audience I couldn't reach directly.

The people who buy Roblox guides for their kids are parents. My social media following at the time was mostly people my own age interested in tech. The guide sat on Gumroad for two months with two sales.

The lesson: product-audience fit is not just about whether the product is good. It's about whether the people who would buy it can find you.

I didn't abandon the product. I changed the target. Instead of marketing it to parents, I started marketing it to Roblox players who wanted to build games themselves. Slightly different angle, different buyer, better fit for my audience.

---

### SECTION 2: Products 2 through 10 (2:30 - 5:00)

Products two through ten came in a sprint. I had learned the lesson of the Roblox guide and started with audience first.

The question I asked for each product: "Does the person who already follows me want this?"

Products in this batch: the 3D Fashion Design Guide, Budget Template, AI Prompt Cheat Sheet, Solopreneur OS, Behike Method v2, AI Employee Guide, AI News Tracker Guide, Social Media Pipeline Guide, and the Self-Hosted Store Guide.

Pricing ranged from $4.99 to $19.99. The logic was deliberate. I wanted a product at every price point so that anyone who landed on the store could find something at a price they were comfortable with. A $4.99 buyer who gets value becomes a $19.99 buyer later.

The system started clicking at product seven. That was the Behike Method v2. It sold nine copies in the first week. That was the first time I saw that the catalog approach was working.

---

### SECTION 3: Products 11 through 20 (5:00 - 7:30)

This batch was different. I started building products in response to market signals instead of personal interest.

Competitor Radar showed me what other Gumroad creators were listing. When I saw three AI security guides appear in two weeks, I built mine with a different angle: not just concepts, but a practical checklist format. That became the AI Security Guide at $14.99.

The Niche Sniper came from my own frustration with product research. I built a tool to solve my own problem, then wrote a guide about it. That sequence, build for yourself first, then document it, is now the model I use for any technical product.

By product fifteen I had a full cover template, a listing formula, and a PDF export process. Building a new product went from three days to four hours.

---

### SECTION 4: Products 21 through 25 (7:30 - 9:00)

The last five products are where the catalog shifted from utility to experience.

Ceiba Sanctuary is a browser-based wellness app. Focus DJ generates ambient music for different brain states. These aren't guides. They're interactive tools sold as digital products.

The insight behind them: the most memorable thing you can sell is an experience, not information. Information is everywhere. An experience is specific to your product.

These products are priced higher because the experience justifies it. Ceiba Sanctuary is $49.99. Focus DJ is $29.99. Both had higher conversion rates than most of my lower-priced guides.

---

The full catalog is at behike.shop.

If you want the product build system I use, including the cover template, listing formula, and PDF pipeline, that's the Solopreneur OS product. It's in the description.

## Script 5: "The mistake that almost killed the Behike store before it launched"

**Format:** Mistake breakdown / honest case study
**Length:** ~8 min
**Hook type:** Near-failure, then recovery

---

### HOOK (0:00 - 0:30)

I spent six weeks building a store that I almost couldn't launch.

Not because the product wasn't good. Because I built it in the wrong order. I built everything except the thing that would tell me whether any of it mattered.

---

### SECTION 1: What I built first (0:30 - 2:30)

The first thing I built was the technical infrastructure.

A self-hosted Shopify alternative. A custom landing page system with sixty-six individual pages. A particle animation brand identity. Product covers for every listing. A deployment script. A command-line interface for managing the store.

All of this before I had validated a single product.

The logic at the time: get the infrastructure right and the products will follow. That logic is backwards.

Infrastructure is easy to rebuild. Audience is hard to build. I had spent six weeks on things that could be changed in a weekend and zero weeks on the one thing that couldn't be automated: understanding what my audience actually wanted to buy.

---

### SECTION 2: The moment I realized the problem (2:30 - 4:30)

I was two days from the planned launch date. The store looked good. The products were listed. The deployment script worked.

And I had no idea if anyone would buy anything.

I hadn't posted about any of it. I hadn't shared a single product. I hadn't gotten a single person to look at anything and tell me if it was worth their money.

The platform risk was real too. A self-hosted store means you handle the payment processing, the delivery, the dispute resolution. I had built a complex system when Gumroad handles all of that for free.

The near-kill: I was about to launch a technically solid store that required me to manage infrastructure instead of building products, with no audience to sell to, and with no validation that anything was worth buying.

---

### SECTION 3: What I changed (4:30 - 7:00)

Three pivots in one week.

First: Gumroad instead of self-hosted. I listed every product on Gumroad. Payments handled. Delivery handled. I kept the custom store as a discovery layer but the actual transaction happens on Gumroad. That reduced the technical surface I had to maintain by ninety percent.

Second: validation before building. I started posting about products before they were finished. The response rate told me which ones to prioritize. Three posts about the Niche Sniper before launch got forty-seven saves. That's a signal. I finished that one first.

Third: content as the actual product. I realized that the most valuable thing I could do was not build another page on the store but post content that built an audience. Every post is a free sample of the products. The posts that perform best tell me what products to build next.

---

### CLOSE (7:00 - 8:00)

The store is live. The products are on Gumroad. The self-hosted infrastructure still exists but it's a secondary channel, not the main one.

The lesson is not that I wasted six weeks. The infrastructure work was necessary eventually. The lesson is sequence. Audience and validation come before infrastructure. Every time.
