**Section 1 — What Polymarket Is and How It Works (2-4min):**
Polymarket is a decentralized prediction market platform built on the Polygon blockchain. Every market on the platform is a question about a real-world outcome. Examples: "Will the Fed cut rates at the next meeting?" "Will a specific team win the championship?" "Will a specific legislation pass by a certain date?"

Each market has two sides: Yes and No. Each side has a price between $0.01 and $0.99. That price represents the market's implied probability of that outcome. If Yes is priced at $0.60, the market collectively believes there is a 60 percent chance the event happens.

If you buy Yes at $0.60 and the event happens, your position pays out $1.00. That is a profit of $0.40 per share. If the event does not happen, your position goes to $0.00. You lose your $0.60.

The currency used is USDC, a stablecoin pegged to the US dollar. One USDC equals one dollar. So there is no crypto price volatility on top of your market bet. You are betting dollars, just held in a digital wallet.

Polymarket settles markets after the event resolves, typically within 24 hours of the outcome being confirmed. Resolution is handled by UMA, an oracle protocol that verifies real-world outcomes.

**Section 2 — How to Get Started (4-7min):**
Step one: create an account. Go to polymarket.com. You can sign up with an email address or a Google account. Polymarket uses a smart wallet called a Magic Wallet which handles the on-chain complexity for you without needing a separate crypto wallet app.

Step two: deposit funds. Once you have an account, you need USDC in your Polymarket wallet. The easiest method: use the built-in on-ramp. Polymarket supports deposits via debit card or bank transfer through providers like Moonpay or Stripe. You can convert dollars directly to USDC and deposit to your Polymarket wallet in one flow. The minimum is typically $10.

Step three: find a market. Browse the homepage or search by category. Categories include: politics, sports, economics, science, crypto, world events. Sort by volume to find the most active markets. High volume markets tend to have tighter spreads and better liquidity.

Step four: evaluate a market. Look at the current price, the trading volume, and the resolution date. Read the resolution criteria carefully. Every market has specific rules about how it will be settled. A market that seems obvious can still resolve against you if the resolution criteria are written narrowly.

Step five: place a position. Click Yes or No, enter the amount in USDC, and confirm the transaction. The transaction happens on-chain in seconds. You can see your position in your portfolio immediately.

**Section 3 — What Makes a Good Market to Trade (7-10min):**
Not all markets are equal. Here is what to look for and avoid.

Look for: markets with clear, unambiguous resolution criteria. The question should be answerable by a simple fact check, not an interpretation. "Will X happen by Y date" is better than "will X be considered a success."

Look for: markets where you have an information or reasoning edge. You have an edge if you have read more primary sources than the average market participant, or if you understand the base rates of a category better than most people do. Markets involving niche knowledge, local politics, or specialized domains tend to have more mispricing than major political or sports markets where millions of people are actively trading.

Avoid: markets with very low volume. Low volume means wide spreads and difficulty exiting your position if you need to. You may not be able to sell before resolution.

Avoid: high-profile markets where everyone has an opinion. Presidential elections, major sporting events. Those markets tend to be efficiently priced because the volume of participants is enormous. Your edge in those markets is small.

Start with small positions. There is no minimum beyond the platform minimum. Put in $20 to $50 across a few different markets and experience a full resolution cycle before scaling up. The learning happens through experiencing wins, losses, and the psychology of watching a position move.

### Script 2 — Prediction Markets Explained

**Thumbnail concept:** A probability meter going from 0 percent to 100 percent, styled like a gauge. The needle is pointing at 67 percent. Text below: "The crowd is usually right. Here is why."

**Hook (0-30s):**
In 2020, prediction markets had the outcome of the US presidential election closer to correct, earlier, than most major forecasting models. In 2023, prediction markets called several Fed rate decisions more accurately than the Fed's own projections implied. These are not coincidences. There is a structural reason why markets that aggregate the bets of thousands of individual people tend to outperform centralized expert predictions. This video explains that mechanism and why it matters for how you think about information and probability.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Prediction markets are one of the most misunderstood tools in finance and information theory. Most people either dismiss them as gambling or treat them with more certainty than they deserve. The truth is somewhere in the middle: they are an information aggregation mechanism with real strengths and specific limitations. Understanding what they are actually measuring, and where they break down, makes you a much better thinker about probability and uncertainty in general. And if you plan to participate in platforms like Polymarket, understanding the theory is what separates informed participants from gamblers.

**Section 1 — The Theory: Why Markets Aggregate Information (2-4min):**
The core idea behind prediction markets comes from a concept called the wisdom of crowds, formalized by economist Friedrich Hayek in the 1940s. Hayek argued that the price system is fundamentally an information aggregation mechanism. When millions of people act on their individual information through prices, the resulting price reflects information that no single person or institution could have assembled on their own.

Prediction markets apply this logic directly. When thousands of people bet real money on whether an event will happen, the price that emerges from those bets reflects the aggregate information and beliefs of all those participants. Someone who knows something the market does not know can profit by betting against the current price, which pushes the price toward the truth.

This mechanism is called the incentive compatibility of markets. Because participants lose money for being wrong and gain money for being right, there is a financial incentive to be as accurate as possible rather than as confident-sounding as possible. This is fundamentally different from pundit commentary or analyst reports where being wrong has no direct financial consequence.

**Section 2 — Where Prediction Markets Are Most and Least Accurate (4-7min):**
Prediction markets are most accurate in domains where: the event has a clear, verifiable outcome, information is widely distributed across many participants, the resolution timeline is not too far in the future (long-term markets have more uncertainty), and there is sufficient trading volume to represent a real diversity of opinion.

Elections with high volume, binary outcomes, and clear resolution criteria are well-suited for prediction markets. Many documented cases show that platforms like PredictIt and Polymarket have outperformed mainstream forecasting models in election predictions.

Prediction markets are least accurate in domains where: the participant pool is systematically biased, the event depends on private information that is not accessible to traders, the market has low volume and is dominated by a few large positions, or the resolution criteria are ambiguous.

A market about whether a private company will IPO in a given quarter can be less reliable because most participants do not have access to the relevant private information. The price may reflect rumor and speculation rather than real probability.

**Section 3 — How to Read Prediction Market Prices Without Misleading Yourself (7-10min):**
The price in a prediction market is not a prediction. It is a betting price. The distinction matters.

A price of 70 percent on Yes does not mean the market is saying this will happen. It means the market is pricing the event such that betting Yes at $0.70 is a fair bet if the true probability is 70 percent. People participating in the market may have very different internal estimates. Some think it is 90 percent likely. Some think it is 50 percent. The price is the clearing point where buyers and sellers agree to trade.

This means you can be a profitable trader even if you disagree with the market price. If you consistently identify cases where the market is pricing an event at 40 percent but the true probability is 55 percent, you will profit over time even if no individual bet is certain.

The mental model: think in expected value, not outcome certainty. If your estimated probability is higher than the market price, and the discrepancy is large enough to cover fees and your uncertainty about your own estimate, there is positive expected value in taking that position.

### Script 3 — My Polymarket Betting Strategy

**Thumbnail concept:** A spreadsheet visible on screen with columns: Market, My Estimate, Market Price, Gap, Position Size. Text overlay: "This is how I actually bet." Real, no stock photos.

**Hook (0-30s):**

I want to be transparent before we get into this. Prediction markets involve real financial risk. You can lose money. I have lost money on specific positions. The goal is not to win every bet. The goal is to have positive expected value over a large number of bets, which means being right often enough and sizing positions appropriately when you are more confident. This video is about process, not tips. I am not going to tell you what to bet on right now. I am going to show you how I think, so you can develop your own process. Let us get into it.

**Section 1 — Category Selection and Edge Identification (2-4min):**
The first strategic decision is which categories to focus on. I do not trade every category on Polymarket. I focus on: US economic policy, specifically Fed rate decisions and major legislation. Technology industry events. And selective international politics in regions I follow closely.

Why those. Because I spend time reading primary sources in those domains. Fed decisions: I read FOMC minutes and speeches from Fed governors. I understand the framework they use to make decisions. Technology events: I track product releases, regulatory proceedings, and company earnings closely. I have a base of knowledge that goes deeper than the average Polymarket participant, most of whom are betting based on news headlines.

Your categories should be wherever you genuinely have an information advantage. Not where you have a strong opinion. Opinion is not edge. Depth of information and quality of reasoning is edge.

Avoid trading in domains where you are just guessing based on vibes. Even if you win a few times, that is luck, not edge. And luck does not scale.

**Section 2 — The Position Sizing Framework (4-7min):**
I use a simplified version of Kelly Criterion for position sizing. Full Kelly is too aggressive for prediction markets given the uncertainty in my own probability estimates. I use a fraction: about 25 percent of what full Kelly would suggest.

Here is the simplified version. If the market is pricing an event at 40 percent (Yes at $0.40) and my estimate is 60 percent probability, my edge is the difference: 20 percentage points. I would size this as a small to medium position. If my estimate is 80 percent versus a market price of 40 percent, the edge is much larger and I would size the position meaningfully larger.

I cap any single position at a percentage of my total prediction market budget. For me that is currently 20 percent max per market. Diversification across markets matters because any individual position can resolve against you even when your reasoning was correct.

I also separate high-confidence positions from exploratory positions. High-confidence means I have done significant research and the market is clearly mispriced in my estimation. Exploratory means I have an intuition I want to test with a small amount. I track these separately so I know which types of bets are generating my returns.

**Section 3 — Learning From Losses and Updating Your Process (7-10min):**
The most important thing I do after any market resolves against me is a quick post-mortem. Three questions. One: was my reasoning sound but the outcome was genuinely uncertain (bad luck, not bad process). Two: was my reasoning flawed, specifically in what way. Three: did I have the right estimate but sized too aggressively or too conservatively.

Those three questions separate bad outcomes from bad processes. A bad outcome with good process is fine. You will experience those regularly in prediction markets and you have to be psychologically okay with that. A bad process that happens to produce a good outcome is dangerous because it builds false confidence.

The most common error I have made: underweighting tail risks on markets that seemed obvious. Something that looks like 85 percent probability of happening will fail 15 percent of the time. Over 20 bets at 85 percent confidence, you should expect to lose approximately three of them. When I have not mentally prepared for that, a loss at high confidence felt worse than it should have and affected my next few decisions.

The second most common error: trading in categories where I thought I had edge but actually had opinion. I have learned to distinguish between those more carefully over time.
