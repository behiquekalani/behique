## SCRIPT 1: "Why Every Budget App I Tried Failed (And What Finally Worked)"
**Product:** ADHD Budget Guide ($9.99)
**Channel:** Finance faceless
**Format:** Talking head
**Length:** 6-8 minutes
**Hook type:** Personal failure story, ADHD-specific

---

[HOOK . first 15 seconds]

I have tried seven budget apps.

Every single one failed. Not after a month. Usually within a week.

This is what I figured out after the seventh time.

[INTRO CARD: "7 apps. 7 failures. What was actually wrong."]

---

[THE APPS . 90 seconds]

Here is the list. YNAB. Mint. Copilot. Monarch. Honeydue. A spreadsheet I made myself. Another spreadsheet.

Each one felt good on day one. Day one is easy. You are motivated. The system is fresh. Everything gets categorized.

Day four is different.

By day four, I had already skipped logging two purchases. The categories were slightly off. There were transactions I could not remember. The app started showing me a budget gap I did not want to look at.

So I closed it.

I did not delete it. I just stopped opening it. It sat on my phone for three weeks doing nothing. Then I deleted it.

---

[WHY APPS FAIL WITH ADHD . 2 minutes]

Here is the actual reason, and it took me a while to name it clearly.

Budget apps are built for consistency. You log every transaction. You check in daily. You review weekly. The app rewards sustained attention over time.

ADHD does not work that way.

Attention is not consistent. Motivation is not consistent. The habit of opening an app every single day, no matter what, is exactly the kind of routine that breaks down.

When the streak breaks, guilt kicks in. When guilt kicks in, the app becomes something to avoid. When the app becomes something to avoid, the system is dead.

Three specific failure points I noticed:

First: too many categories. YNAB let me create unlimited categories. I created 22. Eating out, coffee, groceries, work lunches, snacks. That is five categories for food alone. Every transaction required a decision. Decisions have a cost. I ran out of decision energy by day three.

Second: daily check-ins. Most apps push you to log transactions same-day. I missed one day, then two, then I was catching up on a week of purchases I could barely remember. Catching up is miserable. I quit rather than catch up.

Third: the numbers were accurate but not actionable. I could see I was overspending on food. The app told me every week. But the app did not tell me what to actually do differently. I just felt bad on a recurring basis.

---

[WHAT CHANGED . 2 minutes]

I stopped trying to find a better app. That was the first change.

Every new app was a new system to learn. Learning a new system activates the same dopamine hit as being productive without actually producing anything. I was shopping for solutions instead of using one.

The thing that actually worked is simpler than any app.

Five categories instead of twenty. One weekly review instead of daily. No streaks, no guilt cycles. A written protocol for the moment I want to buy something I did not plan to buy.

That last part is the piece nobody talks about. ADHD is not primarily a budgeting problem. It is an impulse problem. A budget tells you where your money went. It does not stop you from spending it wrong in the first place.

## SCRIPT 2: "I Audited My Subscriptions. Found $87/Month I Forgot About."
**Product:** Personal Budget Template ($9.99)
**Channel:** Finance faceless
**Format:** Screen recording + talking head
**Length:** 6-7 minutes
**Hook type:** Live audit, surprise reveal

---

[HOOK . first 15 seconds]

Last month I found $87 in subscriptions I forgot I was paying.

Not one big thing. Eight small things I had stopped using and never canceled.

Here is exactly how I found them.

[INTRO CARD: "$87/month. 8 forgotten subscriptions. How to find yours."]

---

[THE PROBLEM . 60 seconds]

Subscriptions are designed to be forgettable.

They charge you on the same day every month. Small amounts. Automatic. No action required from you. The whole model depends on you forgetting.

It works. I forgot eight of them.

Here is the math on why it matters: $87 a month is $1,044 a year. For services I was not using. That is real money.

The fix is a single audit. One hour. You do it once, then put a recurring reminder to do it every six months.

---

[STEP 1: FIND EVERY RECURRING CHARGE . 2 minutes]

[SCREEN: bank account statement, last 90 days]

Open your bank account. Go back 90 days.

You are looking for anything that repeats. Same company, roughly same amount, same day each month.

Do not filter by "subscriptions." Banks do not always categorize correctly. Scroll manually.

I found mine spread across three payment methods: my debit card, one credit card, and PayPal. Check all of them.

Write down every recurring charge. Company name. Amount. Date. That is your list.

Here is what I found when I did this:

Spotify: $10.99. Using it. Keep.
iCloud 200GB: $2.99. Using it. Keep.
Adobe Creative Cloud: $19.99. Barely using it. On the fence.
Notion Plus: $8. Not using it, I switched to a free tier. Cancel.
Some SaaS tool I signed up for in 2024: $12. No idea what it is. Cancel.
An AI writing tool: $19. Used it twice. Cancel.
A gym app that synced with a gym I no longer go to: $7.99. Cancel.
A music licensing service: $5.99. Used it for one video last year. Cancel.

Total canceled: $52.98 per month.

The Adobe subscription I eventually canceled too after a trial with cheaper alternatives. That brought the total to $72.98.

The other $14 came from a service I had on an old email. Found that by checking that inbox for "receipt" or "payment confirmation."

---

[STEP 2: THE RATING PROCESS . 90 seconds]

Once you have the list, rate each subscription on one question.

Did I use this in the last 30 days?

Yes: keep it for now.
No: it goes in the cancel pile.

Do not ask whether you might use it in the future. That question will always produce a reason to keep paying. Ask only about the last 30 days.

For the ones on the fence, set a 30-day trial: use it intentionally for one month or cancel at the end of that month. No extensions.

---

[STEP 3: THE CANCEL PROCESS . 60 seconds]

Cancel immediately. Do not wait.

Most subscriptions require you to navigate to settings, find a "manage subscription" page, and confirm multiple times. They designed it that way.

Set a timer for 20 minutes. Cancel every subscription on your list before the timer runs out. Do not read the retention offers. Do not consider the discounts they offer. Just cancel.

If cancellation requires a phone call, schedule it in your calendar right now. Not tomorrow. Now.

---

[THE TOOL . 60 seconds]

[SCREEN: subscription tracker tab]

Every subscription. Monthly cost. Annual cost calculated automatically. Date added. Last used. Status: keep, cancel, or trial.

The annual cost column is the important one. $9.99 a month looks different when you see $119.88 a year next to it.

The template is $9.99. One-time. Link in the description.

Most people have between $40 and $150 in subscriptions they are not using.

That number is not going to zero. But it should not be invisible.

One hour audit every six months. That is the habit.

## SCRIPT 3: "The 5-Category Budget: How I Went From 47 Categories to 5"
**Product:** ADHD Budget Guide ($9.99)
**Channel:** Finance faceless
**Format:** Talking head
**Length:** 6-7 minutes
**Hook type:** Simplification story, specific numbers

---

[HOOK . first 15 seconds]

At one point I had 47 budget categories.

Forty-seven.

I want to explain why that happened, and why I now use five.

[INTRO CARD: "47 categories to 5. What changed."]

---

[HOW IT GOT TO 47 . 90 seconds]

It started reasonable. Rent. Food. Transport. Savings. Utilities.

Then I added "eating out" as separate from "groceries." Fine. Then I added "coffee" as separate from "eating out." Then "work lunches." Then "snacks." I had five food categories.

Then I added "streaming" and "software" and "apps" as separate from "entertainment." Then "personal care" separate from "health." Then "gifts" separate from "personal."

Each split felt logical at the time. Each one was a small decision that made the budget feel more precise.

But precision is not the goal. Behavior change is the goal.

Forty-seven categories did not change my behavior. They just created 47 places to feel bad about my spending.

---

[THE PROBLEM WITH COMPLEX BUDGETS . 90 seconds]

Here is what too many categories actually does.

Every transaction requires a categorization decision. Is this Amazon purchase "technology" or "household" or "entertainment"? Is this pharmacy trip "health" or "personal care"? Is this dinner with a friend "eating out" or "entertainment"?

Each decision is small. But there are dozens of them per week. For a brain that already struggles with decision fatigue, this is a real cost.

And when the system is too much work, you abandon it. Not dramatically. You just stop doing the weekly review. The categories sit there, unchecked. By month two the budget is theoretical. By month three it does not exist.

Simple systems survive. Complex systems get abandoned.

---

[THE FIVE CATEGORIES . 2 minutes]

These are the five I use now.

Category one: Fixed. Everything that is the same every month. Rent, subscriptions, loan payments, insurance. You know these numbers before the month starts. They do not change.

Category two: Food. Everything you eat or drink. Groceries, restaurants, coffee, delivery. One category. One number.

Category three: Transport. Gas, Uber, public transit, parking, car maintenance. Everything that moves you.

Category four: Lifestyle. Entertainment, clothing, personal care, gifts, anything that is not food or transport. This is the discretionary bucket. It is intentionally broad.

Category five: Savings and Debt. Every dollar going toward building your future or paying down your past.

That is everything. Every single transaction fits into one of those five.

---

[REAL NUMBERS EXAMPLE . 90 seconds]

Let me show you what this looks like with real numbers.

[SCREEN: simple table in Google Sheets]

Income this month: $1,400.

Fixed: $600. Rent $450, subscriptions $87, phone bill $63.
Food: $280. Groceries $190, restaurants $70, coffee $20.
Transport: $120. Gas $80, one Uber $40.
Lifestyle: $150. Went to the movies twice, bought a book, one piece of clothing.
Savings and Debt: $250. $200 to savings, $50 extra on a credit card.

Total: $1,400. Done.

That took me eight minutes to put together. Not because I am fast, but because there are only five decisions to make.

The goal is to see clearly enough to make better decisions.

## SCRIPT 4: "The Impulse Purchase Protocol: How I Stop Myself From Buying Things I Don't Need"
**Product:** ADHD Budget Guide ($9.99)
**Channel:** Finance faceless
**Format:** Talking head
**Length:** 5-6 minutes
**Hook type:** System reveal, behavioral science brief

---

[HOOK . first 15 seconds]

I used to buy things within about 90 seconds of wanting them.

Not big things always. Cheap things. $12, $25, $40 purchases. But they added up.

Here is the system I built to stop it.

[INTRO CARD: "The Impulse Purchase Protocol. How I stopped buying things I don't need."]

---

[THE PROBLEM . 60 seconds]

Impulse spending is an ADHD thing. Not exclusively, but significantly.

The dopamine response to buying something new is fast and strong. The regret comes later, if at all. In the moment of wanting, the brain is not doing a cost-benefit analysis. It is responding to a stimulus.

Budgets do not fix this. A budget tells you what you spent. It does not prevent the spend.

You need something that interrupts the moment.

---

[THE BRIEF NEUROSCIENCE . 60 seconds]

I am not a neuroscientist, and this is not a science video. But one piece of research is worth knowing here.

The wanting and the liking systems in the brain are separate.

Wanting something is a dopamine response. You feel urgency, desire, the pull to acquire. That feeling does not last.

Liking something is different. It is the satisfaction of actually having and using something. It is much more stable.

Impulse purchases are driven by wanting. When you wait and then decide, you are actually asking whether you will like this thing, not whether you want it right now. Those are different questions with different answers.

---

[THE PROTOCOL . 2 minutes]

This is what I do.

Step one: I have a note on my phone called the Cooling List. It is a simple list. Item name, price, date I added it, and one line on why I want it.

When I want to buy something that was not already planned, I add it to the list instead of buying it.

That is the whole interruption. Instead of buying, I write it down.

Step two: 24-hour rule for anything under $50. 72-hour rule for anything over $50. I cannot buy anything on the list until the waiting period is over.

Step three: when the waiting period expires, I look at the item again. I ask one question: do I still want this, or did I just want it in the moment?

Most items disappear on their own. I stop thinking about them. When I review the list and I no longer remember why I added something, that is my answer.

---

[THE RESULTS . 60 seconds]

Three months after I started this, I tracked my impulse spending.

Before the protocol: I was spending roughly $180 a month on unplanned purchases. Small things, mostly. Tools, apps, clothing, random Amazon buys.

After three months of the protocol: that number was $40.

Not zero. I still bought things from the list. But they were things I still wanted after thinking about them. The purchases that disappeared from the list were the ones that were pure impulse.

$140 a month is $1,680 a year. That is what the waiting period is worth.

## SCRIPT 5: "Building an Emergency Fund When You're Bad With Money"
**Product:** Cash Flow Dashboard ($14.99)
**Channel:** Finance faceless
**Format:** Talking head
**Length:** 6-7 minutes
**Hook type:** Direct address, practical setup

---

[HOOK . first 15 seconds]

Every personal finance guide says you need three to six months of expenses saved.

That number is paralyzing if you are bad with money.

Here is a smaller version that actually works.

[INTRO CARD: "Emergency fund. ADHD approach. Smaller goal, real system."]

---

[WHY THE STANDARD ADVICE FAILS . 90 seconds]

Three to six months of expenses. For most people, that is $5,000 to $15,000 or more.

That number is correct as a destination. It is useless as a starting point.

When the goal is far away and abstract, the brain does not engage with it. Especially with ADHD. The reward for saving money is invisible and delayed. The reward for spending money is immediate.

A goal of $10,000 that you make no progress on for three months feels like failure. Failure produces avoidance. Avoidance produces nothing.

You need a goal close enough to feel real.

---

[THE SMALLER GOAL . 90 seconds]

My first emergency fund target was $400.

Not three months of expenses. Not $1,000. Four hundred dollars.

Here is why $400. It is the number that covers most actual emergencies that hit people under 30. Car trouble, urgent medical visit, device failure, unexpected travel. Not everything. But the most common things.

$400 also feels achievable in weeks, not years. A goal you can see getting closer is a goal you will actually pursue.

Once you hit $400, your next target is $1,000. Then three months of your fixed expenses. You are building the habit and the balance at the same time.

---

[THE SEPARATE ACCOUNT . 90 seconds]

The account location matters more than people admit.

Do not keep your emergency fund in the same account you spend from. If it is visible and accessible, it will be spent. For a brain that struggles with impulse control, "accessible savings" is just delayed spending.

Open a separate savings account. Different bank if possible.

The friction is the feature. Having to log into a different app, wait two business days for a transfer, and consciously decide to use emergency funds creates a pause. That pause is where you ask: is this actually an emergency?

The goal is not to make emergencies impossible to fund. It is to make sure you think for one second before you spend money you saved for actual emergencies.

---

[DEFINING EMERGENCY . 60 seconds]

Before you start, write this down somewhere visible.

An emergency is: a necessary and unexpected expense with no other available source of funds.

It is not: a sale that ends tonight. A purchase that would be really useful. A social situation that feels expensive to skip. A thing that would be cheaper if you bought it now.

Those are temptations, not emergencies.

Write your definition. Put it in the description of the savings account if your bank lets you. You want it in front of you at the moment you consider touching the fund.

---

[THE AUTOMATION . 60 seconds]

The best system for building the fund is automated.

Pick an amount you will not notice leaving your account. Start smaller than you think. $10 a week if that is what you can do. $25. Whatever feels slightly uncomfortable but not painful.

Set up an automatic transfer to the separate account, scheduled for the day after you typically get paid.

You do not have to decide each week. The decision is made once. The transfer happens. Over time, the balance builds.

$25 a week is $1,300 a year. Enough to cover a real emergency for most people in their twenties.

---

[THE DASHBOARD . 60 seconds]

I track my savings progress inside the Cash Flow Dashboard.

[SCREEN: Savings tab]

One tab. Current balance, goal, progress percentage, weekly contribution, projected date to hit the goal.

Seeing the projected date helps. When the goal has a real date attached to it, it feels less abstract.

You do not need $10,000 in savings before you start. You need $400. Then $1,000. Then more.

The emergency fund is not about the number. It is about having something between you and the worst-case scenario.

Start small. Automate it. Define what qualifies. Keep the account separate.
