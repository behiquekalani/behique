## SCRIPT 1: "I Tracked Every Dollar for 90 Days. Here Is What I Found."

**TARGET LENGTH:** ~550 words spoken
**HOOK DURATION:** 0:00–0:15

---

### HOOK

Ninety days. Every dollar. No exceptions.

Most people think they know where their money goes. They don't. The number in their head is usually wrong by 20 to 40 percent.

Here is what I actually found.

---

### BODY

**[0:15 – 1:00] The Setup**

This started because I got tired of running out of money and not knowing why. Not broke, just always slightly short. I had income. The math should have worked. It didn't.

So I opened a spreadsheet and started logging every transaction. Not an app. Not a subscription service. A spreadsheet I built myself.

The rule was simple: nothing gets spent without being logged. Food, gas, Spotify, the coffee at the gas station. All of it.

**[1:00 – 2:30] What I Found**

The first surprise was food. Not restaurants. Groceries.

I thought I was spending around $200 a month at the grocery store. The actual number was $340. Same thing happened with transportation. The car itself is paid off, but between gas, parking, and the occasional Uber, the real number was almost double what I estimated.

The category that actually shocked me was subscriptions.

I found seven subscriptions I had either forgotten about or stopped using. Not because I'm careless. Because subscription services are designed to hide from you. They bill monthly, the charge is small enough to scroll past, and there is no moment where you consciously decide to keep paying. You just do. Every month.

Total subscription leak: $94 per month. $1,128 per year.

**[2:30 – 4:00] The One Pattern That Changes Everything**

Here is the thing about seeing your spending on a screen. It removes the emotion from the decision.

Before tracking, money was anxiety. After tracking, money was data.

One specific pattern I saw: my spending spiked on Sundays. Not a lot, but consistently. Looking at the categories, it was always the same thing: food and small impulse purchases. Something about the end of the week, the transition into Monday, triggered a pattern I didn't know I had.

Seeing it didn't fix it automatically. But it meant I could prepare for it. Now Sunday has a cap, a specific number I can spend without touching the week's budget.

That one insight alone changed the pattern within a month.

**[4:00 – 5:00] Why Budget Apps Fail for ADHD Brains**

Most budget apps have the same three problems.

First: they require ongoing maintenance. Miss three days of logging and the data is useless. The habit collapses.

Second: they want a monthly subscription to track your own money. That is the exact backwards incentive. You are paying to see the problem. The problem is the paying.

Third: your data lives on their server. When they shut down or change their pricing model, your history disappears.

A spreadsheet has none of these problems. It runs locally. You own the file. The interface is exactly as complicated as you make it.

**[5:00 – 5:30] Closing**

90 days of tracking taught me one thing. The problem was never income. The problem was visibility.

The spreadsheet I use is linked in the description. It's the exact template I built for this. Set it up once, update it weekly. That's the whole system.

## SCRIPT 2: "The Budget Template That Actually Works When You Have ADHD"

**TARGET LENGTH:** ~560 words spoken
**HOOK DURATION:** 0:00–0:15

---

### HOOK

ADHD and money is a specific kind of problem. Not because people with ADHD are bad with money. Because most budgeting systems were not built for how ADHD brains actually work.

This template was.

---

### BODY

**[0:15 – 1:15] How ADHD Brains Handle Money Differently**

There are four patterns that show up consistently.

Impulsive spending. Not reckless, just fast. A decision that takes a neurotypical person thirty seconds of consideration takes an ADHD brain three. The buffer between impulse and action is shorter. So you spend before you've thought it through.

Subscription amnesia. You sign up for something, you use it twice, and then it disappears from your awareness entirely. But it keeps billing. Every month. For years sometimes.

Feast-or-famine mode. When money comes in, it feels abundant. When it goes out, it feels like crisis. The ADHD brain does not have a reliable internal sense of the middle state. You either feel fine or you feel broke, and neither feeling is necessarily accurate.

Variable attention. Some months you track everything. Some months you forget entirely. A system that requires perfect consistency will fail.

Most budget apps assume none of this. They assume you check in daily, remember your goals, and feel motivated by graphs. That assumption is wrong.

**[1:15 – 3:00] The Design Decisions That Account for ADHD**

The template is built around four specific choices.

One: it works weekly, not monthly. Monthly feels too abstract for ADHD brains. A week is concrete. You can see the beginning and end of it. The weekly view gives you a tight enough horizon that the numbers feel real.

Two: it has a dedicated subscription audit tab. Once a month, you open it, you see every subscription, and you make one decision per row: keep or cancel. That's it. No hunting through bank statements. No surprise charges. The list is always current because you update it once.

Three: it separates fixed costs from variable costs visually. Fixed costs are your floor. Variable costs are where the behavior happens. Seeing them as separate categories changes what you pay attention to.

Four: the weekly column has a color indicator. Green, yellow, red. Not a precise number you have to calculate. Just a signal. ADHD brains respond to visual cues better than to numbers in isolation. The color tells you where you are without requiring you to do mental math.

**[3:00 – 4:30] Budget That Changes Future Decisions**

Most people use a budget to track the past. They look at what they spent last month and feel bad about it. That is not useful.

This template is built to change the next decision, not document the last one.

The weekly view means you're always looking forward. There are four days left in this week. Here is what you have. Do you want to spend $40 on food tonight or do you want to move that $40 to savings on Friday?

That is a real question with a real answer. Not a theory about monthly discipline.

**[4:30 – 5:30] Closing**

The template is in the description. It's a spreadsheet. One-time download, no subscription. Takes about 20 minutes to set up. The subscription audit alone will recover what you paid for it within a month.

Set it up on a Sunday. That's it.

## SCRIPT 3: "Why I Track Cash Flow Separately From Budget"

**TARGET LENGTH:** ~530 words spoken
**HOOK DURATION:** 0:00–0:15

---

### HOOK

Budget and cash flow are not the same thing. Most people use those words interchangeably. That is why their finances feel confusing even when they're "doing everything right."

Here is the actual difference, and why both matter.

---

### BODY

**[0:15 – 1:00] The Confusion**

A budget is a plan. It answers: how do I want to allocate my money this month?

Cash flow is reality. It answers: what money actually came in and went out, and when?

You can have a perfect budget and still run out of money on the 20th of the month. That happens when the budget does not account for timing. You planned to spend $800 on rent, $300 on food, $100 on utilities. Those numbers are correct. But rent hit on the 1st, your paycheck didn't clear until the 5th, and in that window you were technically negative.

That is a cash flow problem, not a budget problem.

**[1:00 – 2:30] What Each Dashboard Actually Answers**

The monthly budget tab answers: am I allocating my money according to my priorities?

It is forward-looking. You fill it in at the start of the month. You update it when something changes. It is a plan, and like all plans, it will be slightly wrong. That is fine. The value is the intention it sets.

The weekly cash flow tab answers: what is the actual movement of real money in real time?

It tracks income by date, not by month. Expenses by date, not by category. The question it answers is not "did I overspend on food" but "do I have money available on Thursday to cover this charge."

These are different questions. They require different tools.

**[2:30 – 4:00] The Practical Setup**

Two tabs. That is the whole system.

Tab one: Monthly Budget. Categories on the left, planned amounts in the middle, actual amounts on the right. Update the actual column weekly. Review at the end of the month. One hour of work per month.

Tab two: Weekly Cash Flow. Seven columns for the days of the week. Income rows at the top. Fixed expense rows in the middle. Variable expense rows at the bottom. The running total on the right tells you your real balance for each day.

The weekly tab takes ten minutes to update. You do it Sunday night or Monday morning. You look at the week ahead, you know what's coming in and what's going out, and you make one or two decisions about where to be careful.

The two tabs talk to each other. The actuals in the monthly budget pull from the weekly cash flow. You update one, the other stays current.

**[4:00 – 5:00] Why This Matters for ADHD**

ADHD brains do not hold financial state in working memory reliably. The question "how much do I have right now" requires you to mentally simulate three different accounts, three different pending charges, and one transfer that hasn't cleared.

That simulation fails. It either gives you the wrong number or it gives you anxiety instead of a number.

The cash flow tab removes the simulation. It shows you the answer. Today. This week. Next Friday.

**[5:00 – 5:30] Closing**

One spreadsheet. Two tabs. Budget and cash flow are both in there.

The template is linked below. Download it once. It runs locally. Your data stays yours.
