### Script 1 — ADHD and Building a Business: What Actually Works

**Thumbnail concept:** Split brain image. Left half: chaos (scattered papers, scattered icons). Right half: clean, organized system with checkmarks. Text: "ADHD DOESN'T HAVE TO STOP YOU."

**Hook (0-30s):**
If you have ADHD and you are trying to build a business, the standard productivity advice is almost useless for you. Systems built for neurotypical focus patterns will break down within a week. What you need is a set of structures that work with how your brain actually operates, not against it. That is what this video is about.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
I have ADHD. I build businesses. The two coexist, but not by accident. It took me longer than I want to admit to figure out that the problem was not my discipline or my motivation. The problem was that I was using the wrong tools.

This video is for anyone with ADHD or attention challenges who is trying to build something consistently over time. Freelancers, solopreneurs, students who are also building. The frameworks here are things I actually use.

I am not going to tell you to meditate more or wake up at 5am. I am going to give you specific systems.

**Section 1 — The Core Problem: Task Initiation and Context Switching (2-4min):**
ADHD does not mean you can not focus. It means you focus differently. The challenge is not sustaining attention, it is starting and switching.

Task initiation is the hardest part. The empty page, the undefined task, the decision about what to do first. ADHD brains often get stuck here not because of laziness but because of executive function challenges that make starting without a clear trigger genuinely difficult.

Context switching is the other side. Once you are in flow, interruptions cost you more than they cost a neurotypical person. Getting back into flow after an interruption can take 30-45 minutes.

The systems that work for ADHD are built around two principles: eliminate decision points and protect flow states.

Eliminate decision points means you plan your work when you are not trying to do your work. You set up the day the evening before. You define the next action on every task, not just the task name. "Write email to client" is bad. "Open Gmail, click compose, write 3 sentences to Marcus about the deliverable" is good.

Protect flow states means you batch similar work, you turn off notifications during work sessions, and you use time blocking that is longer than you think you need.

**Section 2 — The Weekly Structure That Actually Sticks (4-7min):**
Rigid schedules do not work for ADHD. Loose structures do.

The difference is this: a rigid schedule says "write content Monday 9-10am." A loose structure says "content creation happens in the morning, never at night, and always before I check email."

Rules about order and context, not about specific times.

Here is the structure I use.

Every day starts with a three-item list. Not a full task list. Three items. What are the three things that, if I complete them today, make the day a win. Everything else is bonus. This keeps the day from becoming overwhelming the moment something takes longer than expected.

Weekly, I do a brain dump. Sunday evening or Monday morning, I write down everything that is in my head. Projects, worries, ideas, errands. Get it out. Triage it into four buckets: do this week, do later, delegate, delete.

This brain dump prevents the "I'm forgetting something important" anxiety that derails focus.

For the actual work: Pomodoro structure. 25 minutes of focused work, 5 minute break. Repeat. The break is not optional. The timer creates an artificial external deadline that ADHD brains respond to because the urgency is real even if the stakes are small.

**Section 3 — The Tools That Help Without Adding Complexity (7-10min):**
The tool trap for ADHD is real. We are drawn to new productivity apps because novelty activates dopamine. We spend hours setting up systems we use for three days.

The rule is: fewer tools, used consistently, beat more tools used occasionally.

My actual setup:

Notion for the brain dump and project notes. I have one main page with three sections: active projects, this week, and the brain dump inbox. Nothing else.

A physical notebook for daily tasks. The three-item list is handwritten every morning. There is something about writing by hand that makes the commitment feel more real.

A Telegram bot I built that accepts voice notes. When I have an idea while doing something else, I send a voice note and it gets transcribed and categorized automatically. This prevents ideas from disappearing and prevents me from stopping what I am doing to write something down.

These three tools cover 90% of what I need to build consistently with ADHD. I have tried more complex setups. They never last.

### Script 2 — The Second Brain System (How to Actually Build One That Lasts)

**Thumbnail concept:** A human brain connected by wires to a clean digital interface. Text overlay: "YOUR SECOND BRAIN IN 30 MINUTES." Dark background, neon blue accent.

**Hook (0-30s):**
The second brain concept has been talked about so much that it has become meaningless. Most people who say they have a second brain have a messy folder structure they have not opened in two months. I am going to show you what a second brain that actually gets used looks like and how to build one in an afternoon.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
A second brain is just an external system for capturing, organizing, and retrieving information so your actual brain does not have to hold it all.

The problem with most implementations is they are built for the setup, not the retrieval. They look good on day one and become unusable by day 30 because they require too much maintenance to stay organized.

What I am going to show you is a minimal version that is designed for retrieval, not for aesthetics. The goal is to be able to find anything in under 30 seconds.

**Section 1 — The Four Capture Categories (2-4min):**
Everything you capture goes into one of four buckets.

Projects: things you are actively working on that have a clear outcome.
Resources: reference material for topics you care about. Not projects, just knowledge.
Areas: ongoing responsibilities that do not have an end date. Your business. Your health. Your finances.
Archive: everything that is no longer active but might be useful later.

This is the PARA system from Tiago Forte. I am not going to reinvent it. It works.

The key insight is that most second brain systems fail because they are organized by topic, not by actionability. Topic-based organization creates sprawl. Project-based organization creates clarity.

When something lands in your second brain, the first question is: does this relate to an active project. If yes, it goes into that project folder. If no, it goes into resources or archive.

**Section 2 — The Capture Habit (4-7min):**
The second brain only works if you capture consistently. Capture is the habit that makes everything else possible.

You need a frictionless inbox. One place that everything goes before it gets organized. Do not try to organize at capture time. Just capture.

My inbox is a single Notion page called Inbox. Everything goes there: links I want to read, ideas I want to develop, notes from calls, things I need to follow up on.

Once a week, I process the inbox. For each item I decide: project, resource, area, archive, or delete. This takes about 20 minutes.

The frictionless part matters a lot for ADHD specifically. If it takes more than 10 seconds to capture something, you will not do it consistently. The inbox page is pinned in Notion and bookmarked in my browser.

For mobile capture, I use the Telegram bot I mentioned in the ADHD video. Voice notes get transcribed and sent to the inbox automatically. No typing required.

The combination of a voice capture system and a weekly processing session is the core habit loop that keeps the second brain alive.

**Section 3 — Making It Useful: Search and Retrieval (7-10min):**
The point of capturing information is being able to use it later. A second brain that can not be searched efficiently is just a digital attic.

Two retrieval methods that work.

First: tagging. Tag every note with one to three relevant concepts. Keep the tag list short. If you have 200 tags, you have a tagging problem, not a retrieval system. I have about 20 tags total: AI, business, writing, tools, finance, health, and similar.

Second: good titles. Every note title should tell you what is in the note without opening it. "Meeting notes 3/15" is bad. "Marcus call: deliverable timeline and pricing concerns" is good. You should be able to scan a list of note titles and find what you need.

The third retrieval method is full-text search. Notion's search is good enough for most purposes. If you have more than a few thousand notes, Obsidian with its local search is faster.

The benchmark for a working second brain: you should be able to find any piece of information you have intentionally saved in under 30 seconds. If it takes longer, your naming or tagging needs work.

### Script 3 — How to Stop Losing Ideas (The Capture System That Actually Works)

**Thumbnail concept:** A lightbulb disappearing into thin air on the left, then a lightbulb landing safely in a net/inbox on the right. Text: "STOP LOSING YOUR BEST IDEAS."

**Hook (0-30s):**
You have had an idea that was genuinely good, you told yourself you would remember it, and two hours later it was completely gone. That has happened to me more times than I can count. The solution is not a better memory. It is a better capture system. Here is the one I built.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
The problem with most idea capture advice is that it requires you to stop what you are doing to write something down. That breaks flow, adds friction, and means you will only capture ideas when it is convenient, which is rarely when the best ideas show up.

The best ideas come in the shower, during a walk, while you are falling asleep, or in the middle of a different task.

The system I am going to show you captures in all of those contexts with minimal friction. And it ensures the ideas are actually retrievable and actionable later, not just dumped into a black hole folder.

**Section 1 — The Capture Channels (2-4min):**
You need a capture method for three contexts: hands-free, at your computer, and mobile with your hands free but available.

Hands-free context: voice. The fastest way to capture an idea when your hands are busy or you are moving is to speak it out loud. I use a Telegram bot that receives voice messages and transcribes them automatically. You can also use the native voice memo app on your phone as an alternative. The key is that recording takes two seconds.

At your computer: a hotkey-triggered note. I use a quick-entry shortcut in Notion that opens a new note in my inbox without disrupting the current window. Mac users can also use Alfred or Raycast for this. Three seconds from idea to captured.

Mobile: same Telegram bot or a quick-capture app. The goal is one tap to a blank input, not navigating through an app.

You do not need all three. You need at least one that covers your most common idea context. For most people that is mobile voice capture.

**Section 2 — The Inbox Processing Ritual (4-7min):**
Capturing ideas is only useful if you process them.

Without processing, your inbox becomes a graveyard of fragments. You captured the idea but never did anything with it. After a few weeks of this, you stop trusting the system and you stop capturing.

The processing ritual is simple. Once per week, open the inbox. Read each item. For each one, make one of four decisions.

One: delete it. The idea seemed good in the moment but on reflection is not worth pursuing. Most ideas end up here and that is fine.

Two: add it to a project. The idea connects to something you are actively working on. Drop it into that project folder.

Three: save it as a resource. The idea is interesting but not immediately actionable. It goes into a topic-based resource folder.

Four: schedule it. The idea requires a specific action. That action goes into your task manager with a date.

This four-decision framework means every item in your inbox gets a clear fate. Nothing lingers indefinitely.

The ritual takes about 20 minutes per week. I do it on Sunday evenings. After it I have zero items in my inbox and a clear sense of what the week ahead contains.

**Section 3 — Making Ideas Actionable (7-10min):**
The gap between "good idea" and "executed idea" is usually not motivation. It is specificity.

Most captured ideas are too vague to act on. "Write something about pricing" is an idea. "Write a 10-minute YouTube script about how to price digital products using value anchoring" is an actionable task.

When you process your inbox, your job is to sharpen ideas until they are actionable. If an idea can not be sharpened into a specific next action, it probably needs more thought before it gets scheduled.

The sharpening question is: what is the first physical action that would move this idea forward.

For a business idea: "research comparable products on Gumroad for 30 minutes."
For a content idea: "write a 5-bullet outline."
For a technical idea: "open a new file and write the function signature."

The first action is always small. Small enough that there is no reason not to do it when you see it on your task list.

One final thing: review your idea archive every 90 days. Ideas that were not ready three months ago sometimes become relevant when your situation changes. A regular archive review surfaces these.
