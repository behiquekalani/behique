### Script 1 — Polymarket Explained for Beginners: What It Is, How It Works, and How to Profit

**Thumbnail concept:** Polymarket interface screenshot with a hand pointing at a prediction market. Bold overlay text: "BETTING ON FACTS" with a green upward arrow. Clean dark background.

**Hook (0-30s):**
There is a platform where you can put money on real-world outcomes, politics, economics, sports, technology, and get paid out when you are right. It is called Polymarket. It is not gambling in the way most people think. It is a prediction market, and understanding the difference is what separates the people who make money on it from the ones who lose. I am going to break this down completely.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Prediction markets have been around for decades in academic research. The internet made them accessible to everyone. Polymarket is the largest decentralized prediction market right now. It runs on the Polygon blockchain, which means transactions are fast and cheap, and anyone in the world can participate. Here is the core concept: markets on Polymarket represent questions with binary or multiple outcomes. "Will X happen by date Y?" You can buy shares of YES or NO. If you are right, your shares are worth $1 each. If you are wrong, they are worth $0. The price of a share at any moment reflects the crowd's estimate of probability. If YES shares trade at $0.62, the market thinks there is a 62 percent chance the outcome is YES. This video covers how to get started, how to read a market, and the strategy that separates information arbitrage from guessing.

**Section 1 — How Polymarket Actually Works (2-4min):**
You need a wallet to use Polymarket. The easiest path is MetaMask or Coinbase Wallet. You fund it with USDC, which is a stablecoin pegged to the dollar. One USDC equals one dollar. You deposit this into Polymarket and use it to buy shares in markets. Each market has a resolution date and a resolution source, usually a credible news outlet or official announcement. When the market resolves, winning shares pay out $1 each. Losing shares pay out $0. The profit or loss is the difference between what you paid and the payout. If you bought YES shares at $0.40 and the outcome is YES, your $0.40 per share became $1.00 per share. A 150 percent return. If you bought at $0.80 and paid out $1.00, that is a 25 percent return. The key is finding markets where the current price does not accurately reflect the real probability.

**Section 2 — The Information Edge Strategy (4-7min):**
Casual users of prediction markets bet on what they think will happen. Serious users look for markets where the crowd is mispriced. There are three types of mispricings I look for. First: recency bias. After a major event, markets sometimes over-adjust. If a team loses badly, the market might price their next game too low. If a political candidate has one bad news cycle, their overall odds might drop more than warranted. Second: information asymmetry. You follow a specific industry or topic closely. You know things the average market participant does not. A niche software market. A regional political race. A technology timeline. If you have genuine expertise, your edge is real. Third: long odds on near-certain outcomes. Sometimes a market prices something at $0.85 that I believe is $0.95 certain. Buying at $0.85 and collecting $1.00 is a consistent 18 percent return on something I am highly confident about. The risk is that I am wrong about my own confidence level.

**Section 3 — Risk Management for Prediction Markets (7-10min):**
Prediction markets are real money. Treat it seriously. Three rules I follow: one, only deploy capital I can afford to have locked for weeks or months while a market resolves. Two, never put more than 10 to 15 percent of my prediction market budget into a single position, no matter how confident I feel. Three, track every position in a spreadsheet. Record the question, the price paid, the resolution date, the amount, and the outcome. Review this monthly to see where your edge actually exists versus where you are guessing. The biggest mistake I see from beginners is treating Polymarket like sports betting, which is pure gambling based on preference. Polymarket rewards research, not rooting.

### Script 2 — How to Use Claude AI for Real Work (Not Just Demos, Actual Daily Use)

**Thumbnail concept:** Side-by-side comparison. Left: generic ChatGPT demo answer for a simple question. Right: Claude producing a multi-section strategic document. Bold text: "THIS IS WHAT IT ACTUALLY DOES"

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Claude is Anthropic's AI model. If you have only used ChatGPT, Claude will feel different. It is more careful about accuracy, better at following complex instructions, and produces longer, more structured documents without degrading in quality. Those properties make it particularly good for knowledge work. This video covers six specific ways I use Claude in my business daily, with the actual prompt structures I use for each one, not generic advice. If you want to understand how to use AI as a real productivity tool rather than a novelty, this is the video.

**Section 1 — How I Use Claude for Research and Strategy (2-4min):**
Use case one: research synthesis. I paste articles, documents, or long text into Claude and ask it to extract the key insights, identify patterns, or compare multiple sources. The prompt I use: "Here are three articles on [topic]. Extract the main claims from each, identify where they agree, where they conflict, and what is not addressed by any of them." Claude produces a structured analysis in seconds. Use case two: strategic planning. I describe a business problem in detail, including constraints and context, and ask Claude to outline possible approaches with tradeoffs. I do not ask "what should I do." I ask "here are three approaches I am considering, what are the second-order effects of each." The difference in output quality between those two prompts is significant. The first gets generic advice. The second gets analysis tailored to my specific situation.

**Section 2 — How I Use Claude for Writing and Content (4-7min):**
Use case three: first drafts. I give Claude the key points I want to make, the audience I am writing for, and the tone I want. It produces a draft. I then edit that draft, which takes 20 to 30 percent of the time it would take to write from scratch. The key is giving Claude the thinking before asking for the writing. If you just say "write a blog post about AI tools," you get generic content. If you say "here are five specific observations I have made from using these tools in my business, write a post that uses these as the backbone," you get something that sounds like you. Use case four: editing and feedback. Paste any piece of writing and ask: "What is the weakest section of this and why. What would make the argument stronger. Is there anything here that contradicts itself." Claude is a rigorous editor if you prompt it to be critical rather than complimentary.

**Section 3 — Advanced Uses That Most People Miss (7-10min):**
Use case five: prompt refinement. If you are building AI workflows or using other AI tools, Claude is excellent at helping you write better prompts for those tools. Describe the output you want and the tool you are using. Ask Claude to write a prompt that would produce that output. Test it. Refine with Claude's help. This compounds over time. Use case six: devil's advocate. Before making a business decision, describe it to Claude and ask: "What is the strongest argument against this decision. What am I not seeing. What would have to be true for this to fail." This is not about finding reasons not to act. It is about testing your reasoning before you commit resources. The goal in all of these use cases is the same: Claude is a thinking partner, not a replacement for your thinking. Your judgment, your context, and your specific expertise are still the most valuable inputs. Claude amplifies them.

### Script 3 — How to Build a Telegram Bot With No Code (Using n8n and a Free Account)

**Thumbnail concept:** Telegram app on a phone receiving a message from a bot. The phone screen shows a menu with bot commands. Behind it, an n8n workflow canvas. Bold text: "FREE. NO CODE. LIVE IN 1 HOUR."

**Hook (0-30s):**
In the next hour you can have a working Telegram bot that responds to messages, sends scheduled updates, and connects to any service you already use. Zero code. Zero paid subscriptions if you self-host. I am going to walk you through the exact setup step by step.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Telegram bots are one of the most underused tools in the creator and solopreneur toolkit. You can use them to deliver lead magnets automatically, send daily briefings to yourself or your audience, build an accountability system, notify yourself of important events, and much more. The reason most people do not build them is the assumption that it requires coding. It does not. n8n handles all of the logic through a visual workflow. Telegram's API does all the messaging. You are just connecting the two. This video covers: creating a bot on Telegram, setting it up in n8n, building a basic message-response workflow, and three real use cases you can build in under an afternoon.

**Section 1 — Creating Your Telegram Bot (2-4min):**
Open Telegram and search for BotFather. This is Telegram's official bot creation service. Send the command /newbot. BotFather will ask for a name and a username. The name is what users see. The username must end in "bot" and is the handle people use to find it. After you create it, BotFather gives you a token. This is a long string of characters that looks like numbers and letters. Copy it and save it somewhere safe. This token is how any external service, including n8n, authenticates with your bot. The bot is now live on Telegram but it does not do anything yet. You can search for it by username and start a conversation. You will see the messages you send to it logged in your n8n workflow once we set that up. One important step: send the /start command to your new bot so the chat is initialized. This creates the chat ID that n8n uses to send messages back to you.

**Section 2 — Connecting Telegram to n8n (4-7min):**
Open n8n. Create a new workflow. Add a Telegram Trigger node. This listens for incoming messages to your bot. Paste your bot token into the credential field. Set the trigger to "Message" so it fires on every new text message. Now add a second node after the trigger. Start with a simple Respond to Webhook or Telegram Send Message node. For the Respond to Webhook approach: when a message comes in, send a reply back. The reply node needs your bot token and the chat ID. In n8n you get the chat ID dynamically from the trigger output using an expression. Set the message text to something static first, like "Received your message." Save the workflow, activate it, send a message to your bot on Telegram, and watch the reply come back. That is the loop working. Now you can replace the static text with anything: data from an API, content from a Google Sheet, an AI-generated response from the OpenAI node.

**Section 3 — Three Real Use Cases to Build Now (7-10min):**
Use case one: personal daily briefing. Build a workflow that triggers on a schedule, every morning at 7am. It pulls your top three tasks from a Google Sheet or Notion, formats them into a message, and sends them to your Telegram. You wake up to your own priorities delivered to you. Use case two: lead magnet delivery. Publish your bot username publicly. When someone sends a message to the bot, n8n detects it, adds their Telegram username to a Google Sheet, and sends them a link to your free resource. No email required. Instant delivery. Use case three: sale notifications. Connect your Gumroad webhook to n8n. Every time someone buys a product, n8n sends you a Telegram message with the product name, the buyer's country, and the amount. You get a real-time ping every time money comes in.
