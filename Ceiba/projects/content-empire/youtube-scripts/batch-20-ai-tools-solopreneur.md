## Script 1: "The 7 AI Tools I Actually Use Every Day (No Hype)"

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

I have a simple rule for AI tools. If I haven't used it in seven days, it's off the list. I don't care how impressive the demo was. I don't care if everyone on Twitter is talking about it. If it's not in my actual workflow, it doesn't exist for me. By that filter, I'm currently using exactly seven tools. Let me tell you what they are and why they're still on the list.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

The AI tools conversation is dominated by hype cycles. Every week there's a new tool that is supposedly going to replace five other tools, remove the need for humans, and make your business run itself. Most of those tools are demos built by engineers who've never run a business. The features are impressive in a controlled environment and useless in a real one.

The other problem is that people treat AI tools as collections instead of selections. They sign up for everything, use nothing consistently, and end up paying for twelve subscriptions that overlap completely. Having more tools is not the same as having a better workflow.

**[THE 7 TOOLS — 2:00 to 6:30]**

Tool one: Claude. This is my primary thinking tool. Not for shortcuts. For hard problems that benefit from another perspective. When I'm stuck on a pricing decision, a product idea, or a piece of writing that isn't working, I use Claude to pressure-test my thinking. The quality of what you get out depends entirely on the quality of what you put in.

Tool two: n8n. My automation backbone. Every workflow that saves me time lives here. Email sequences, data collection, content triggers, API connections. n8n runs in the background doing the repetitive work so I don't have to. If you want to understand what n8n does, I have a full video on it linked in the description.

Tool three: Notion AI. I keep everything in Notion. My tasks, my project notes, my product catalog, my content calendar. Having AI built directly into the tool where my data already lives means I can ask questions about my own work without copy-pasting context into a separate chat window.

Tool four: Whisper (via a transcription tool). Every voice note I record gets transcribed automatically. I think faster by talking than by typing. Whisper lets me capture ideas in their raw form without losing anything to the gap between thought and keyboard.

Tool five: Perplexity for research. When I need factual information, pricing data, market context, or anything that requires current information from real sources, Perplexity is faster and more accurate than a standard search engine for how I work. I don't use it for creative work. I use it for fact-finding.

Tool six: Canva AI for design. Specifically the AI image generation and the Magic Write feature. I'm not a designer. I don't have hours to spend on graphics. Canva AI lets me produce product covers, social media graphics, and presentation assets that look professional without a design degree.

Tool seven: ElevenLabs for voice. Occasionally I need voiceover for a video or a demonstration. ElevenLabs produces quality audio fast. Not every day, but often enough that it stays on the list.

**[THE PATTERN — 6:30 to 7:00]**

These seven tools share one thing. Each one does one job well. None of them try to be everything. I chose them by asking: what is the thing I spend time on that doesn't require me specifically to do it? Then I found the simplest tool that handled that thing. Simplest is faster to learn, cheaper to run, and easier to replace when something better comes along.

## Script 2: "How I Use Claude to Write Content That Sounds Like Me"

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

Three months ago a friend read something I posted and said, "did you write this or did AI write this?" That was the wrong answer. I had used Claude to help draft it and the seams were showing. I spent about two weeks after that figuring out how to use AI as a writing tool without losing my voice in the process. What I landed on changed how I work with it completely.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

Most people use AI for writing the same way. They open the chat window, type "write me a post about X," and get back something competent and completely generic. It sounds like every other AI-generated post. Correct grammar, reasonable structure, no personality. It reads like it was written by someone who has never had an opinion in their life.

The problem is not the AI. The problem is that you gave it nothing to work with. You gave it a topic. You didn't give it your perspective, your vocabulary, your specific way of approaching problems, your specific reader. So it defaulted to average.

Average is the worst possible output for content. Content that sounds average makes you invisible. The whole point of creating content is to build a recognizable voice.

**[THE FRAMEWORK — 2:00 to 4:00]**

Here is how I changed my approach.

I stopped asking Claude to write from scratch. I start with my own rough draft. It doesn't have to be good. It just has to be mine. A paragraph, a few sentences, a scattered list of points. Whatever I actually think about the topic, in my actual words.

Then I hand that to Claude with a specific instruction. Not "improve this." Something like: "Keep my voice and structure. Cut the filler. Make each sentence land. Don't add ideas that aren't already here." That instruction does three things. It protects my perspective, it removes the generic additions AI loves to insert, and it keeps the output focused on editing rather than inventing.

The second change: I built a style reference. One document that contains examples of writing I'm proud of, descriptions of how I like sentences to be structured, and a list of words I actually use versus words I don't. I paste the relevant section of that document into context before any writing request. Claude cannot sound like you without knowing what you sound like.

**[THE RESULT — 4:00 to 5:30]**

The output is still AI-assisted. But it no longer reads that way. The perspective is mine because I wrote the raw version. The structure is tighter because Claude edited it. The voice survives because I gave Claude something to work with instead of a blank canvas.

The fastest way to test this is to take something you wrote, paste it in, and ask Claude to "make this punchier without changing a single idea." Compare the before and after. That's using AI as an editor, not a ghostwriter. The distinction matters.

**[WHAT TO AVOID — 5:30 to 6:30]**

Don't ask AI to add examples, stories, or analogies unless you wrote them first. AI-generated stories are detectable. They're too smooth and too convenient. Write the story yourself in rough form and let AI clean it up.

Don't accept the first output. AI writing tends to be most generic on the first pass. Push back. Tell it what's not working. Iterate. The third or fourth pass is usually when it starts to sound like something worth publishing.

## Script 3: "AI Automation for People Who Are Not Programmers"

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

I'm a computer engineering student. When I tell people I use automation in my business, they assume that means I wrote code. Mostly I didn't. The majority of what I have running right now was built without a single line of code, using tools that anyone with patience and a few hours can figure out. This video is for the person who wants the time savings of automation without the two-year detour into Python.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

The automation content online has a severe developer bias. Tutorials that seem to be for beginners include code snippets in the third step and act like that's normal. Listicles of "easy automation tools" include developer APIs with no onboarding context. If you're a non-technical person trying to automate parts of your business, you're constantly hitting walls that were never meant to be there.

The real problem is that most automation tasks do not require code. Connecting your form to a spreadsheet? No code. Sending an email when someone buys? No code. Pulling data from one tool and putting it in another? No code. But the tutorials make everything look complicated because tutorials are usually written by people who find the technical parts interesting.

**[THE NO-CODE PATH — 2:00 to 4:30]**

Here is where to start.

Start with Zapier or n8n. For complete beginners, Zapier has a more guided onboarding. For people who want zero cost and more flexibility, n8n self-hosted is better. Both work with a drag-and-drop interface. You do not type commands. You click nodes and fill in fields.

Your first automation should be the most boring possible thing. Something you do manually every week that follows the exact same steps every time. For most solopreneurs that's something like: "when a form gets submitted, add it to this spreadsheet." Build that first. Get it working. Then build the next one.

Learn the vocabulary. Trigger, action, filter. Trigger: what starts the workflow. Action: what the workflow does. Filter: a condition that decides whether the workflow runs or not. That is 90% of automation logic. Once you understand those three concepts, you can build almost anything without code.

Use ChatGPT or Claude as your documentation. Instead of reading a wall of technical documentation, describe what you want to do and ask for a step-by-step guide for the specific tool you're using. "I want to connect my Tally form to Notion using n8n. Walk me through it step by step." You'll get a usable answer in thirty seconds.

**[REAL EXAMPLE WITHOUT CODE — 4:30 to 6:00]**

Here is a workflow I built in forty-five minutes with no code.

Goal: whenever I save a link to my Notion "Read Later" database, automatically add it to a weekly digest document that gets shared to my Telegram every Sunday.

Tools: Notion, n8n, Telegram.

Steps in n8n: Notion trigger node watches the "Read Later" database for new entries. A Notion read node pulls the title and URL of each new entry. A Telegram node sends a formatted message to my personal channel. A second trigger on a weekly schedule pulls all that week's saved links and sends a compiled digest.

No code. All visual. All built by connecting pre-made nodes in n8n. The whole thing took under an hour including learning where to find the Notion API key.

## Script 4: "I Replaced My Morning Routine with an AI Briefing. Here Is What Happened."

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

My old morning routine was forty-five minutes of checking things. Email, calendar, task list, Instagram analytics, Gumroad sales, news. Forty-five minutes of gathering context I needed before I could start working. Now I spend five minutes reading one message. The rest of the forty minutes I spend working. This is what I built and what it actually changed.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

The modern morning routine for anyone running a business involves touching five to ten different apps before the day starts. Each app demands your attention for a little longer than it should. You open email to check one thing and end up reading three. You open Instagram to check reach and end up scrolling for twelve minutes.

The information gathering is not the problem. You need that context to make good decisions for the day. The problem is the interface. Each app is designed to keep you in it. None of them are designed to give you the exact information you need and then release you.

**[THE SOLUTION — 2:00 to 3:30]**

The briefing I built pulls from four sources every morning at 6:45am. My top three tasks from Notion, my calendar events for the day from Google Calendar, my Gumroad sales from the previous 24 hours, and one curated news item from a specific RSS feed I set up.

n8n collects all of that, formats it into a short structured message, and sends it to Telegram. I wake up, I open Telegram, I have a morning briefing. It looks like this: "Today: 3 tasks. First: [task]. Calendar: 2 events. Next one at 10am. Sales: $47 yesterday. News: [one headline relevant to my niche]."

That message takes fifteen seconds to read. I know exactly what I'm working on, when I need to stop working to be somewhere, whether the business made money yesterday, and one thing happening in my space I should know about.

**[WHAT ACTUALLY CHANGED — 3:30 to 5:30]**

The first week I thought it was a novelty. By the second week it had changed how I start every day.

The biggest change is not the time saved. It's the quality of the start. When your first decision of the day is based on a clean summary of actual information instead of a scattered session of tab-switching, your brain is in a different mode. Calmer. More focused. Less reactive.

I also stopped making reactive decisions based on incomplete context. Before, I would open Gumroad, see no sales, and feel a vague anxiety that would color the first two hours of my day. Now I see the number in context. One day of low sales is data. It doesn't trigger anything.

The briefing also surfaced a pattern I had missed. Three weeks in, I noticed my highest-sales days were always followed by Instagram posts the day before. I hadn't connected those two things manually. Seeing them side by side in the same briefing every day made the pattern obvious.

**[HOW TO BUILD IT — 5:30 to 6:30]**

The short version: n8n schedule trigger at whatever time you want, connected to Notion, Google Calendar, Gumroad API, and an RSS reader. One JavaScript node to format the output. One Telegram node to send it. If you can follow the n8n tutorial in my previous video, you can build this in about two hours.

The longer version, including the exact node configurations and API setup, is in my "How I Built an AI Employee" guide.

## Script 5: "The Honest Guide to AI Tools for Solopreneurs in 2026"

**[HOOK — 0:00 to 0:30]**

I want to tell you two things about AI tools that most channels in this space will not tell you. The first one might save you a hundred dollars a month. The second one might save you three months of distraction.

**[PROBLEM — 0:30 to 2:00]**

The AI tools space has a specific kind of content problem. Everything is a review, and every review has an affiliate link. The person telling you that tool X is essential to running your business is often getting paid when you sign up for tool X. They're not lying exactly, but their incentives are not aligned with yours.

The second problem is that the content is optimized for beginners. Every week there's a new video that's basically "you've never heard of AI, here are five tools." If you've been paying attention for more than six months, you've seen that video forty times. You need different information. You need someone to tell you which tools are worth keeping after the first month, which ones overlap too much to justify paying for both, and which ones are genuinely hard to replace.

**[WHAT'S ACTUALLY WORTH IT — 2:00 to 5:30]**

Let me go category by category.

For writing and thinking, you need one serious language model. Claude or ChatGPT. Not both. They overlap almost completely for most use cases. Pick one, learn it deeply, and ignore the weekly "which is better" discourse. The answer changes every three months and matters less than how well you've learned to use the one you picked.

For automation, you need n8n or Make. If you're just starting, Make has a slightly gentler learning curve. If you're serious about automation and care about cost at scale, n8n is better long-term. You do not need both. You do not need Zapier unless you're using an obscure app that only Zapier supports.

For research, Perplexity has become genuinely useful for factual queries with citations. It does not replace deep research but it replaces a lot of casual Googling. The free tier is enough for most solopreneurs.

For image and design, Canva AI handles 90% of what a one-person business needs. If you need custom realistic images, Midjourney is better. But for social content, product covers, and presentation graphics, Canva is enough and most people don't need Midjourney.

For voice, ElevenLabs has no serious competition in the quality range for the price point. If you need voiceover, that's the tool.

For video, CapCut handles short-form editing well for most people. If you're doing faceless video with AI generation, Kling and Veo are the current leaders. The space is moving fast and the tools from six months ago are already obsolete. Don't overbuy here.

**[WHAT TO WATCH FOR — 5:30 to 6:30]**

Watch for tool overlap. Before you add a new AI tool, ask yourself: does this do something that none of my existing tools do? If you already have Claude and you're considering ChatGPT because someone said it's better at a specific thing, spend two hours testing that claim before you pay for a second subscription.

Watch for launch hype versus six-month behavior. A tool that does something impressive in a demo in January often looks very different in June. Follow the builders and operators in your niche who talk about what they're still using, not what just launched.

Watch for pricing model changes. Several major AI tools have quietly moved from flat-rate to usage-based pricing over the past year. Know what you're paying for and what triggers overages. Surprises on AI bills are more common than people admit.
