**Main Content:**

**Where AI Fits in Beat-Making (And Where It Doesn't)**
There are three phases of making a beat: ideation (what am I making), construction (actually building it), and finishing (mixing, arrangement). AI is useful in phase one. It's mostly useless in phase two and three.

In the ideation phase, you're trying to answer: what tempo, what key, what emotional tone, what reference sound. AI tools can accelerate this. In construction, you need your hands on the controls. Anything that puts AI between you and the piano roll slows you down.

Keep that separation clear and you'll stop wasting time with tools that aren't built for your phase of the process.

**AI Tool 1: Reference Finding with Audiomack / Suno for Mood**
Before I open FL Studio, I spend five to ten minutes finding a mood reference. I used to scroll through playlists for thirty minutes. Now I'll prompt Suno or a reference playlist tool with a specific description: "dark melodic trap, minor key, around 140 BPM, heavy 808, sparse hi-hat pattern." I'm not using the output as my beat — I'm using it as a direction signal.

The output from AI generators is usually not good enough to publish. But it's good enough to tell you "yes, this vibe, build toward this." That saves fifteen minutes of aimless searching at the start of a session.

**AI Tool 2: Chord and Melody Ideas with ChatGPT or Claude**
This sounds odd but it works. I describe the emotion I'm going for — "melancholy but not slow, something that feels like late night driving" — and ask for chord progression suggestions in a specific key. It's not replacing my musical knowledge. It's giving me a starting point to react to.

I take the suggestion, try it on the piano roll, change two or three chords, and now I have a progression that's mine but wasn't born out of a blank page. Blank pages are the enemy of fast production.

**The FL Studio Setup That Maximizes Speed**
Once I have a direction, I use a template. My FL Studio template has: kick channel, bass/808 channel, hi-hat channel, melody channel, sample channel, and FX bus — all pre-routed and pre-named. The first beat I made with this template took an hour. Now I sit down and I'm playing notes within two minutes.

Templates are underused by beginners. Every minute you spend routing and naming is a minute you're not making music. Build your template once, use it forever.

**The 20-Minute Beat Flow**
Minutes 0-3: Open template, set tempo and key based on reference.
Minutes 3-8: Build the drum pattern. Kick and snare first. Hi-hats last. Keep it simple — you can layer later.
Minutes 8-14: Bass and 808. One note per kick hit to start. Pitch to the key.
Minutes 14-18: Melody. Four bars. Three to four notes. Simple is better than complex at this stage.
Minutes 18-20: Listen back. Is it moving? Does something make me want to nod? If yes, save and keep building. If no, restart the melody only — don't throw the drums and bass.

This flow doesn't always produce a finished beat in twenty minutes. It always produces something worth continuing.

**Where AI Gets in the Way**
Auto-mastering plugins with AI: they're a crutch that teaches your ears nothing. AI mixing suggestions: useful for learning references, bad for your own sound development. AI that writes full tracks: the output has no identity. If you're building a catalog and a style, you need to make the decisions.

Use AI to start faster. Never use it to finish instead of you.

**Main Content:**

**Why Standard Workflow Advice Doesn't Work**
Most music production advice assumes you can sustain focus for an hour or two and methodically work through a track from arrangement to final mix. ADHD brains don't do that. The energy spikes hard, produces good ideas, then crashes. Forcing yourself to stay past the crash produces bad decisions, over-layering, and projects you hate by the end.

The solution isn't discipline. It's designing a workflow where you capture the good part and stop before the bad part.

**The Rule: Capture Windows, Not Marathon Sessions**
A capture window is a defined, time-boxed session with a single deliverable. Not "finish a beat." Not "work on music." Instead: "Spend 25 minutes building the drum pattern. Stop when the timer goes off."

The Pomodoro structure works well here — 25 minutes on, 5 minutes off. But the specific deliverable matters more than the timer. Name what you're capturing before you start. "Today's capture: bass line for the project I started Tuesday."

When the window is over, save, close, walk away. Don't stay for "just five more minutes." That's when the bad decisions happen.

**The Three-Session Rule**
I don't try to finish a beat in one session anymore. I use three sessions with clear handoffs.

Session 1: Foundation. Drums, bass, root melody. Four bars, no more. Save and close.
Session 2: Development. Open what you built, extend to sixteen bars, add a variation. Fix nothing from session 1 yet.
Session 3: Finishing. Mix, arrange, add top layers, export.

This breaks the compulsive loop where you keep rebuilding the same eight bars because you can't move forward. Forward motion is built into the structure.

**Managing Distractions Inside FL Studio**
Turn off the project browser during production. You don't need to see 200 samples while you're in the piano roll — that's a context switch waiting to happen. I keep one folder visible: the samples I pre-selected before the session started.

Pre-select samples before the session, not during it. Browsing samples while trying to produce is the biggest ADHD trap in FL Studio. Spend ten minutes before your capture window picking five samples you might use. Then close the browser.

**The Incomplete Project Protocol**
Most unfinished projects die because there's no clear re-entry point. You leave a session with a vague "I need to do more on this" and next time you open it, you don't know where to start, so you start over.

After every session, write one sentence in the project name or a sticky note: "Next: build the B-section melody, 4 bars, minor key, same tempo." When you come back, you have an instruction. You don't have to think to start — just follow the instruction.

**Medication and Timing (Real Talk)**
If you use medication for ADHD, there's an optimal window — usually two to four hours after taking it — where focus is highest. I schedule my capture windows during that window when possible. This isn't always controllable, but when it is, it changes the quality of what I make.

If you don't use medication, the same principle applies with energy: most people have a natural focus window in the morning or early afternoon. Find yours and protect it for music.

**Video 4: How to Mix Your Track When You're Not a Mix Engineer**
**Channel:** Music Production
**Target length:** 10-12 min
**Hook (0-30s):** [Two audio waveforms on screen — one messy and flat, one cleaner] "This is a mix from six months ago. This is the same song type I would mix today. I'm not a mix engineer. **Intro (30s-2min):** "Mixing is one of those topics where the more you learn, the more paralyzed you get. There are infinite YouTube tutorials on frequency analysis, mid-side processing, dynamic EQ, and techniques that matter at the professional level but actively harm you when you're just trying to finish a track. This video is the minimal effective dose of mixing for producers who make beats and want them to sound decent without spending weeks on a mix."

**Main Content:**

**The Mindset Shift: Mixing for Completion, Not Perfection**
Most self-produced music doesn't fail because of bad mixing. It fails because it never gets finished. Perfect mix on a half-built track is worse than a decent mix on a complete track. Set your standard at "sounds good enough to share" not "sounds like a professional mix."

This shift changes every decision. You stop second-guessing and start finishing.

**Move 1: Volume Balance First, Everything Else Second**
Before touching EQ or compression, set volume levels so nothing is drowning anything else out. This alone solves 40 percent of mix problems. Most amateur mixes have the 808 too loud, the melody too quiet, and everything fighting in the same volume range.

Rule of thumb: kick and snare are the anchor. 808 sits slightly below the kick peak. Melody sits below the 808. Hi-hats and percussion are quieter than you think they should be.

Mix at low volume. If it sounds balanced at low volume, it will sound balanced loud. If you mix loud, your ears adjust and you'll make bad decisions.

**Move 2: EQ to Remove, Not to Add**
Beginner instinct is to add frequencies — boost the bass, add brightness, enhance the mids. Professional instinct is to remove: cut the frequencies that are causing problems. This is called subtractive EQ.

For the 808: use a high-pass filter around 40-50 Hz to remove sub-sub bass that sounds like mud. Cut around 300-500 Hz if it sounds boxy. Don't boost much.

For melodic elements: cut the low end on anything that isn't bass or kick. Everything above the bass register doesn't need frequencies below 200 Hz. High-pass filter everything except bass.

This prevents frequency buildup, which is the main reason mixes sound muddy.

**Move 3: Compression to Glue, Not to Shape**
One compressor on the master bus set conservatively: ratio 2:1, attack slow enough that the transients breathe, release medium. This glues the mix without squashing it. Don't use compression on every individual channel unless you know why you're doing it.

On the 808: a gentle compressor to even out the sustain. Not to add punch — the pitch and volume do that. Just to even it out.

**Move 4: Space with Reverb and Delay (Use Sparingly)**
Reverb on melodic elements only. Not on drums, not on 808. Small to medium room size. Keep the wet/dry ratio low — more dry than wet. If you can clearly hear the reverb, it's probably too much.

Delay on vocals or leads only, if at all. Short delay time synced to the BPM.

Trap and drill beats in particular should be mostly dry. The reverb should be subtle enough that listeners don't consciously hear it but the mix would feel flat without it.

**Move 5: Reference Track Comparison**
Before exporting, pull up a professional song in the same genre on SoundCloud or Spotify. Route it through your headphones or speakers at the same volume. Switch back and forth between your mix and the reference.

You're not trying to match it perfectly. You're checking for obvious imbalances. Is your 808 much louder or much quieter than the reference? Is your mix brighter or muddier? This gives you real data instead of ear fatigue guessing.

**The Export Settings That Matter**
WAV, 24-bit, 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz. If you're uploading to streaming platforms, most normalize loudness anyway — aim for a consistent, punchy mix rather than maximizing loudness. Overloud masters lose dynamics and sound crushed on earbuds.

**Video 5: The 5 Plugins Every Independent Producer Needs in 2025**
**Channel:** Music Production
**Target length:** 8-10 min

When you have five good tools that you know deeply, you make decisions faster and your sound gets more consistent. This is the list I'd give myself if I was starting over in 2025 — focused on plugins that are free or low cost and cover all the core functions you actually need."

**Main Content:**

**How I Define "Need"**
A plugin you need does one of three things: it solves a problem you can't solve with what's already in FL Studio, it significantly improves your speed on a common task, or it adds a sound quality improvement that you can actually hear.

Everything else is GAS — Gear Acquisition Syndrome. Buy it, install it, use it twice, forget it. We're cutting that out.

**Plugin 1: Valhalla Supermassive (Free)**
Reverb and delay plugin. Free. The reverb algorithms in here produce sounds that stock FL Studio reverb can't match — particularly for atmospheric, spacious sounds in melodic beats. The "Massive" and "Gemini" modes create the kind of huge spatial sound you hear in modern melodic trap and pop production.

Why it made the list: it's free, it's one of the most-used plugins in professional sessions, and it does one thing extremely well.

Use it on: melodic leads, pads, atmospheric elements. Not on drums or bass.

**Plugin 2: Serum (or Vital as a free alternative)**
Serum is the standard wavetable synthesizer. It's in almost every professional session across electronic music genres. The workflow is visual and intuitive once you learn it, and the presets alone cover most of the sounds you'll need. It's paid at around $200 or available through Splice at $10/month.

If you can't justify that cost yet, Vital is a free alternative with a similar architecture. It's not identical but it's close enough for most use cases.

Why it made the list: the difference between FL Studio's stock synths and Serum is audible immediately. If your melodies sound thin or cheap, this is likely the fix.

**Plugin 3: OTT (Free)**
OTT is a multiband compressor/distortion plugin by Xfer Records — the same company that makes Serum. It's free. It adds energy, harmonics, and presence to sounds that feel flat. It's used extensively in bass music, trap, and electronic production.

The catch: it's easy to overuse. A subtle setting adds life to a sound. A heavy setting turns everything into distorted noise. Start at 25 percent depth and work up.

Why it made the list: one plugin, free, immediately improves the energy of most sounds without complex setup.

**Plugin 4: FabFilter Pro-Q 3 (Paid, Worth It)**
This is the one paid plugin on the list that I consider non-negotiable for serious mixing. It's a parametric EQ with a visual frequency analyzer, dynamic EQ modes, and a collision detection feature that helps you separate elements in a mix.

It's around $179. If that's not in budget, TDR Nova is a free alternative that covers the core functionality.

Why it made the list: EQ is the most important mixing tool and the stock FL Studio EQ has limitations that Pro-Q 3 doesn't. For a producer who plans to sell or release music, this investment pays back quickly in finished quality.

**Plugin 5: Glue Compressor (Stock Ableton) / Molot (Free)**
If you're in FL Studio, Fruity Peak Controller and the stock Parametric EQ handle compression reasonably well, but for bus glue compression specifically, Molot by Tokyo Dawn Labs is a free plugin with a vintage character that adds warmth and cohesion to a mix bus.

Glue compression is what makes a mix feel like it belongs together rather than a collection of individual elements. A little goes a long way. Set ratio to 2:1, attack slow, threshold just barely touching.

Why it made the list: the final mix quality gap between "sounds like a demo" and "sounds like a release" often comes down to a single well-placed glue compressor.

**The Rule Moving Forward**
Before downloading another plugin, ask: does this replace one of my five, or is it GAS? If it replaces one, test it for a week and decide. If it's GAS, don't install it. Your plugin folder is not a collection — it's a toolbox.
