### Script 1 — How to Price Your Digital Products (Without Guessing)

**Thumbnail concept:** Split screen. Left side: "$5" with a red X. Right side: "$97" with a green checkmark. Clean dark background, white text.

**Hook (0-30s):**
Most people price their digital products by looking at what their competitors charge and going slightly lower. That is one of the fastest ways to make sure your product never makes real money. In the next 10 minutes I am going to show you the actual framework for pricing digital products so that you can charge more and sell more at the same time.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This video is for anyone who has built a digital product, an ebook, a template, a course, a guide, and genuinely does not know what number to put on the sales page. If you have been picking prices at random or defaulting to whatever feels "safe," this changes today.

I have priced products at $5 and sold almost nothing. I have priced products at $47 and sold consistently. The difference was not quality. It was how I thought about price. Here is what I learned.

**Section 1 — Value Anchoring, Not Cost Pricing (2-4min):**
The first mistake is pricing based on what it cost you to make the product. Digital products cost almost nothing to replicate. That math always leads you to undercharge.

The correct question is: what is the outcome worth to the buyer.

If your ebook teaches someone how to make $500/month on eBay, the value of that outcome is not $10. It is several hundred dollars minimum. Your price should sit at roughly 10-20% of the value the buyer receives. So if the outcome is worth $500, the product should be $47-$97.

This is called value anchoring. You anchor the price to the result, not the production cost.

Write down your product's main outcome. Put a dollar amount on that outcome. Divide by ten. That is your price floor.

**Section 2 — The Psychology of Price Points (4-7min):**
Certain numbers perform better than others. Not because of magic, but because of how buyers process decisions.

$9 signals low value. $97 signals real product. $47 is the entry trust price. $197 is the commitment price where buyers expect transformation.

Avoid round numbers like $50 or $100. They read as arbitrary. $49 and $97 read as deliberate.

There is also a concept called price as signal. When you charge $7, you are telling the buyer your product is worth $7. When you charge $97, you are telling them it is a serious investment. Buyers who pay more tend to take the product more seriously, complete it more, and leave better reviews.

Another lever is anchoring within your own product lineup. If you have a $27 product and a $97 product, the $97 starts to look reasonable next to the $27. Create a pricing ladder inside your own store.

**Section 3 — Testing and Adjusting (7-10min):**
Here is the practical part. You do not need to guess. You can test.

Launch at your calculated price. Run it for 30 days. Track your conversion rate. If you are getting 2-5% of landing page visitors buying, your price is working. If you are below 1%, you either have a copy problem or a price problem.

The way to tell the difference is to drop the price by 30% for one week. If conversions jump dramatically, you had a price problem. If they stay flat, you have a copy problem.

The other signal is refund rate. High refunds at a low price means the product is not delivering. High refunds at a high price means the expectation was set wrong in the copy.

One more thing: raise your prices every time you collect 10 new reviews. Social proof gives you permission to charge more.

### Script 2 — How to Build an Email List From Absolute Zero

**Thumbnail concept:** Empty inbox icon on the left, then an arrow pointing right to "1,247 subscribers" on the right. White background, bold black text, one accent color (green).

**Hook (0-30s):**
You do not need a website, a social following, or any money to start building an email list. I built my first 500 subscribers using three free tools and one lead magnet I made in an afternoon. This video shows you exactly how I did it and how you can do the same starting today.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
The email list is the only digital asset you fully control. Social platforms shadowban, demonetize, and get shut down. Your email list is yours. If Gumroad shuts down tomorrow, your list survives. If Instagram tanks your reach, your list survives.

This video is for people who are starting from scratch. No existing audience, no budget, no domain. We are going to build from zero.

**Section 1 — The Lead Magnet That Actually Works (2-4min):**
The first thing you need is a reason for someone to give you their email. That reason is a lead magnet.

Most people make lead magnets that are too broad. "10 business tips" gets ignored. The lead magnets that work are extremely specific and promise an immediate outcome.

Good example: "The 5 AI tools I use to write a week of content in 2 hours" with a free PDF checklist. Specific, time-bound, actionable.

You can build this in one afternoon. Use Notion to write it. Export as PDF. Done. No design skills needed. If you want it to look clean, put it into Canva and use any simple document template.

The format that converts best right now is a short checklist or a reference sheet. Not a 50-page ebook. Something they can use in 10 minutes.

**Section 2 — Where to Put It and How to Promote It (4-7min):**
You need three things: a landing page, an email service provider, and traffic.

For the landing page, use Beehiiv or ConvertKit. Both have free plans. Create one page with a headline, three bullet points explaining what they get, and an email input. That is it.

For traffic when you have zero following, here is what works. Post the lead magnet link in relevant Reddit threads. Find threads where people are asking questions your lead magnet answers, provide a real answer in the comments, then mention your freebie at the end. This alone can get you 50-100 subscribers in a week.

The second method is Twitter or X. Post a thread on the topic of your lead magnet. At the end, say "I made a free PDF on this. Reply and I will send it." Then DM the link to everyone who replies. This is called a reply gate and it consistently outperforms regular link posts.

Third: repurpose the lead magnet content into a short video, post it to TikTok or Instagram Reels, and put the landing page link in bio. Even a low-view video sends traffic if the topic is specific enough.

**Section 3 — Turning Subscribers Into Buyers (7-10min):**
Getting someone to subscribe is step one. The real work is the welcome sequence.

The first email should deliver what you promised, introduce yourself in two sentences, and tell them what is coming next. Do not pitch anything in email one.

Email two is your story. How did you get here, what did you figure out that they are still struggling with.

Email three is where you introduce a paid product. Not as a hard sell. As a resource. "I put together a more complete version of this into a paid guide if you want to go deeper."

This three-email sequence converts subscribers into buyers without being pushy. The key is that emails one and two build enough trust that email three reads as a recommendation, not an ad.

After the welcome sequence, send one email per week. One useful thing. That is all. Consistency matters more than volume.

### Script 3 — The Freelancer's Income Ceiling (And How to Break Through It)

**Thumbnail concept:** A person hitting their head against a glass ceiling labeled "$5K/mo." Below the ceiling, frustration. Above it, open sky. Bold typography.

**Hook (0-30s):**
If you have been freelancing for more than a year and your income has not grown in the last six months, you have hit the ceiling. The ceiling is not a skill problem. It is a business model problem. And there is a specific reason you hit it and a specific way to break through. I am going to show you both in this video.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
This is for freelancers who are working hard and not seeing income growth. You might be booked out, which feels like success, but being fully booked at your current rate just means you have built a job, not a business.

The income ceiling is real and predictable. Every freelancer hits it. The ones who break through are not the ones who work harder. They are the ones who change the model.

**Section 1 — Why the Ceiling Exists (2-4min):**
Freelancing trades time for money. There are only 24 hours in a day. At some point you run out of hours and income flatlines.

The ceiling shows up in three specific ways. First: you are fully booked but can not take more clients. Second: you want to raise rates but are afraid of losing clients. Third: you are making good money but feel like one client leaving could collapse everything.

All three are symptoms of the same problem. Your income depends entirely on your hourly or project rate multiplied by hours worked. That formula has a hard cap.

The people who break through the ceiling change the input. Instead of hours times rate, they build systems where value is decoupled from time.

**Section 2 — Three Ways to Break the Ceiling (4-7min):**
The first way is productized services. Instead of custom quotes on every project, you offer a fixed-scope service at a fixed price. One landing page for $1,500. Delivered in five business days. No discovery calls, no scope creep. This lets you process more clients in the same time because you have already solved the project once.

The second way is retainers. Monthly recurring revenue from clients who need consistent work. The goal is to move 50% of your income to retainers so your baseline is covered and project work becomes upside.

The third way is the most powerful: add a digital product track parallel to your service. Your services bring in active income. Your digital products bring in passive income. Over 12 months you grow the product side until it represents 30-40% of your total revenue. At that point your ceiling is effectively gone because you have two separate engines.

The freelancers who hit $10K/month almost always have some combination of these three. Pure hourly billing rarely scales past $5K without destroying your quality of life.

**Section 3 — The Practical Transition Plan (7-10min):**
You do not quit freelancing to do this. You build alongside it.

Month one: pick your highest-demand service and productize it. Define the exact scope, the price, and the timeline. Update your website and start quoting only productized packages to new clients.

Month two: identify two existing clients who would benefit from a retainer arrangement. Offer them a monthly package that covers what you already do for them. Most will say yes because it gives them predictability too.

Month three: turn the knowledge from your best service into a digital product. If you do copywriting, write a guide on how to write landing page copy. If you do design, make a template pack. Sell it for $27-$47.

This is a 90-day transition. You are not blowing up your freelance business. You are evolving it.
