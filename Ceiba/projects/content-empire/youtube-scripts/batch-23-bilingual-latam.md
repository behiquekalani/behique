**Format:** Screen recording + talking head hybrid

I write content once. It publishes in two languages. The Spanish version gets edited for idioms, scheduled for the right time zones, and goes out clean. Here is exactly how I built that pipeline and why it doubled my potential audience overnight.

---

### [0:15-1:00] SETUP — The inefficiency I was ignoring

For most of the time I was building Behike, I was writing in English and ignoring the Spanish market.

Not on purpose. Just by default. English is the default language of the internet for people in tech. You absorb it and you start producing in it.

Then I looked at my analytics one day and I saw exactly zero Spanish-speaking followers. Not because there are no Spanish speakers interested in AI. Because I had never published anything in Spanish. It was that simple.

I asked myself how long it would take me to run a parallel Spanish content operation. My first instinct was "too long." Separate scripts, separate editing, separate scheduling. It sounded like doubling the work.

Then I built a pipeline that made it take about fifteen extra minutes per piece.

---

### [1:00-2:00] THE PRINCIPLE — Write once, translate intentionally

The wrong version of this is: write in English, run through Google Translate, post.

Google Translate will give you grammatically correct Spanish that sounds like a legal document. Nobody who grew up speaking Caribbean Spanish reads that and feels like the content is for them.

The right version is: write once, translate with AI using a specific style guide, then do a fifteen-minute human edit for idioms and tone.

The first step is getting the translation right. That means giving the AI more than just the text. You give it context. You give it rules. You tell it who the audience is and how they talk.

I call these the Voice Bible ES rules. Short version: no Castilian formal register, no words that only Spaniards use for everyday things, no translated idioms that do not land in Caribbean Spanish, thesis first, short sentences, no filler phrases.

---

### [2:00-4:00] THE WORKFLOW — Step by step

[Screen recording begins]

Step one: write the original piece in English. I use the same Voice Bible rules I always use: thesis first, no banned words, short sentences, no em dashes.

Step two: open the AI tool of your choice. I use Claude. Paste the full piece and give it this prompt:

"Translate this to Spanish for a Puerto Rican/Caribbean audience. Use natural, conversational PR Spanish. Not formal Castilian. Keep the same structure. Thesis first, short sentences. If an idiom does not translate naturally, find the closest equivalent that a Caribbean Spanish speaker would actually say. Do not invent new structure. Translate the content."

Step three: read the output. This is where you catch things the AI will miss. American cultural references that do not land in PR. Idioms that got half-translated. Technical terms that have standard English usage even in Spanish-speaking tech communities.

In LATAM tech content, people say "startup" not "empresa emergente." They say "influencer" not "persona influyente." They say "AI" or "IA" depending on context, and knowing which one to use for which audience matters.

The edit takes fifteen minutes if the original was clean. Maybe twenty if the piece was complex.

Step four: schedule the English version for the primary window, then schedule the Spanish version for plus three hours. Latin American audiences have peak engagement windows that differ from US Eastern time. 7 PM in Puerto Rico is also 7 PM in Colombia and Venezuela and most of the Caribbean. Stagger it right and one piece of content runs twice without competing with itself.

[Screen recording ends]

---

### [4:00-5:30] THE RESULTS — What changed

Within three weeks of running parallel content, I had Spanish-speaking followers from Mexico, Colombia, Venezuela, the Dominican Republic, and Puerto Rico.

None of them followed me before because there was nothing in their language.

The content quality is the same. The distribution logic is the same. The only change is that the same asset now has two audiences instead of one.

Here is the counterintuitive part: the Spanish version sometimes performs better than the English version on certain posts. Content about AI for small businesses, content about building income streams, content about practical tools for people who are not in Silicon Valley. Those themes resonate harder in LATAM because there is less competition for that attention.

---

### [5:30-7:00] THE RULES — What I learned about writing for two audiences

A few things I adjusted after running this for a while.

First: some content is inherently English-first. A video about Claude's API or a tool that only has an English interface should stay in English and get a subtitle-translated version, not a full parallel script. Save the full bilingual treatment for educational content where the language is the whole product.

Second: the Spanish audience is more forgiving of the personal story. Caribbean and Latin American content culture skews warmer than American tech content. Stories about family, about the grind, about building from nothing land harder. The English version of a piece can be more functional. The Spanish version can lean into the narrative a bit more.

Third: do not localize idioms you do not actually know. If you are not from the culture, do not guess at slang. Use neutral but natural Spanish and let the audience accept the slight formality. It is better than sounding like you are performing at being local.

**Format:** Talking head + screen audit

**Thumbnail idea:** Screenshot of a Google search for "AI course in Spanish" with very few relevant results. Text overlay: "I searched for 3 hours. This is what I found."

---

### [0:00-0:15] HOOK

I spent three hours auditing every AI education resource available in Spanish. What I found is a gap so big that anyone who starts filling it right now has an almost unfair advantage. Here is the full breakdown.

---

### [0:15-1:00] SETUP — Why I did this audit

Before I committed fully to a bilingual content strategy, I wanted to know what already existed. I was not going to build a competitor to something that was already doing the job well.

So I searched. YouTube, Google, Reddit in Spanish, Telegram channels, Gumroad, Udemy, Coursera. Anywhere you could reasonably find AI education content in Spanish.

I gave myself three hours and a simple rubric: does this resource teach practical AI skills to someone who wants to build a business or automate their work? Not news. Not theory. Not a summary of what OpenAI announced last week. Does it teach someone how to actually do something?

The results were bleak.

---

### [1:00-3:00] WHAT EXISTS — The audit

[Screen recording begins]

Category one: news and commentary.

There are several decent Spanish-language YouTube channels that cover AI news. They summarize what GPT-4 can do. They react to new product launches. They explain what LLMs are at a conceptual level. This content exists and some of it is good.

The problem: news is not education. Knowing that a new model dropped does not teach you how to use it in your business.

Category two: Spanish dubs and subtitles of English content.

A significant portion of what shows up in Spanish search results is translated English content. Coursera courses with auto-generated Spanish subtitles. YouTube videos dubbed by a voice that clearly does not speak Spanish natively. These exist. The quality is variable. The pedagogical design was built for English speakers and it shows.

Category three: formal academic content.

Universities in Spain, Mexico, and Chile have published AI-related content in Spanish. Most of it is aimed at students in formal computer science programs. If you want to understand transformer architecture at a research level, some of this is useful. If you want to build an AI-assisted content business or automate your eBay store, it is not designed for you.

Category four: practical builder content.

This is the gap. After three hours I found fewer than five Spanish-language YouTube channels that consistently publish practical AI education aimed at entrepreneurs, creators, and small business owners.

Five. For 680 million people.

Compare that to the English market. Andrew Huberman has more AI-adjacent productivity content than the entire Spanish practical AI education space has total videos.

[Screen recording ends]

---

### [3:00-4:30] WHY THE GAP EXISTS — The structural reasons

This gap did not appear because Spanish speakers are less interested in AI or less technically capable. The gap exists for structural reasons.

First: English is the language of the AI research and development community. Every major model, paper, and tool launches in English first. Creators who speak English get early access to the concepts before they exist in Spanish.

Second: monetization infrastructure favors English audiences. YouTube ad rates in the US are significantly higher than in LATAM. A creator calculating CPM-based revenue has less financial incentive to serve a Spanish audience, even a large one. Sponsorship deals, affiliate programs, and digital product markets are also more developed in English.

Third: the bilingual advantage is invisible to monolingual creators. Most successful English AI educators are not bilingual. They see the LATAM market but they do not speak the language and they do not have the cultural context to enter it authentically. The door is open but they cannot walk through it.

All three of these factors are either irrelevant or advantageous to someone who grew up bilingual in a Caribbean context and is building a digital product business.

---

### [4:30-6:00] WHAT IS MISSING — The specific content gaps

Based on the audit, here is what does not exist in quality Spanish:

Practical prompt engineering tutorials. Not "here are prompts you can copy" but actual instruction on how to think in prompts, how to iterate, how to get consistent outputs across different use cases.

AI tool walkthroughs for small business operations. How to use AI to handle customer service in a small online store. How to use AI to generate and schedule content for a local business. How to use AI to research suppliers or competitors. These workflows exist in English on dozens of channels. In Spanish, almost nothing.

AI automation for non-technical users. n8n, Zapier, Make, and similar tools have almost no Spanish tutorial content that is practical and current. The demand is clearly there. The supply is not.

Building income streams with AI. The Dan Koe style of content, the idea that one person with AI tools can build meaningful income, exists in English. The equivalent for a Spanish-speaking audience who is thinking about these problems in a LATAM economic context does not exist at scale.

Personal brand building with AI. How to use AI tools to compress the work of building a public presence. How to batch content, maintain consistency, and grow an audience without a team. This entire genre is English-only.

---

### [6:00-7:30] THE OPPORTUNITY — What early builders capture

In any content market, the people who establish authority early keep it disproportionately long.

The English AI education space has its leaders. They built trust from 2020 to 2023 when the space was forming. A new English channel starting today is fighting for position in a crowded room.

The Spanish practical AI education space is in 2020 right now. The tools are accessible. The audience is forming. The market infrastructure is developing. The leaders have not been decided yet.

I am not saying there will be no competition. There will be. But the creators who show up consistently, with practical content, in the next eighteen months will have first-mover advantages that are very hard to overcome later.

That is the bet I am making with Behike.
