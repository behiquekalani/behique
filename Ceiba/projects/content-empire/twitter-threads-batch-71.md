## Thread 71-1: Personal Brand Is Not What You Think

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Most people building a "personal brand" in 2026 are actually building a performance.

They're not sharing ideas. They're cosplaying the idea of having ideas.

Here's what actually works:

**Tweet 2:**
A personal brand is not a logo, a color palette, or a niche.

It's a reputation that precedes you.

It's what people say about you when you're not in the room.

**Tweet 3:**
The trap most builders fall into:

They copy the aesthetic of successful creators without copying the thinking.

Aesthetics are easy to replicate. Perspective is not.

**Tweet 4:**
Your brand is built on a thesis.

One clear point of view on the world that you return to constantly.

Everything else — content, offers, products — is just evidence for that thesis.

**Tweet 5:**
If you can't fill in this sentence clearly, you don't have a brand yet:

"I help [specific person] achieve [specific outcome] by [specific mechanism]."

Vague brands attract vague audiences. Vague audiences don't buy.

The fastest way to build a brand in 2026:

Pick one platform. Write one idea per day. Respond to everyone who engages.

Do this for 90 days before thinking about anything else.

**Tweet 7:**
What most people skip:

The brand is downstream of the thinking. If your ideas are shallow, your brand will be shallow.

Read more. Build more. Write what you actually think, not what sounds good.

## Thread 71-2: The One-Person Brand Framework

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
You don't need a team, an agency, or a PR strategy to build a brand.

You need a framework. Here's the one I use:

**Tweet 2:**
Step 1: Pick one problem you've already solved for yourself.

Not a problem you want to solve. One you've actually been through and figured out.

Lived experience compounds. Theoretical knowledge doesn't.

**Tweet 3:**
Step 2: Document the process publicly.

Not the polished version. The actual messy process.

People follow people who show their work, not people who show their results.

**Tweet 4:**
Step 3: Have one consistent point of view.

You can talk about ten different topics. But you need one lens through which you see all of them.

That lens is your brand.

**Tweet 5:**
Step 4: Distribute more than you create.

Most creators spend 80% of their time making content and 20% distributing it.

Flip that ratio for the first year.

**Tweet 6:**
Step 5: Build in public, sell in private.

Your content builds trust. Your offers convert that trust into revenue.

Keep them separate. Blurring them kills both.

**Tweet 7 (CTA):**
Save this. Come back to it when you feel like you need to rebrand.

You don't need a new aesthetic. You need to go deeper on your thesis.

## Thread 71-3: Why Niching Down Is Misunderstood

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
"Niche down" is the most repeated advice in personal branding.

It's also the most misapplied.

Here's what it actually means:

**Tweet 2:**
Niching down doesn't mean limiting your topics.

It means being specific about who you're talking to and what outcome you're helping them reach.

You can talk about anything. You just need a specific reader in mind.

**Tweet 3:**
The wrong version:

"I talk about productivity for remote workers who use Notion and have ADHD and live in Europe."

That's not a niche. That's a search query.

**Tweet 4:**
The right version:

"I talk about building sustainable work systems for people who are smart but chronically overwhelmed."

That's a state of mind, not a demographic. States of mind scale.

**Tweet 5:**
The paradox of niching:

The more specific your point of view, the broader your actual reach.

Strong opinions attract people across demographics. Neutral content attracts no one.

**Tweet 6:**
Before you niche down on a topic, niche down on a transformation.

What does someone's life look like before finding your content? What does it look like after?

That before-after gap is your niche.

**Tweet 7:**
Most personal brands fail because they try to appeal to everyone.

The second you try to appeal to everyone, you're writing for no one.

Pick a person. Write for them every day.

## Thread 71-4: Content That Builds Trust vs. Content That Gets Likes

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
Getting likes and building trust are not the same thing.

Most creators optimize for one and wonder why the other never comes.

**Tweet 2:**
Content that gets likes:

- Inspirational quotes
- Hot takes with no depth
- "5 tools that will change your life" listicles
- Relatable memes about work-life balance

**Tweet 3:**
Content that builds trust:

- Specific frameworks with clear logic
- Lessons from things that failed
- Honest takes on hard topics in your industry
- Analysis of why something works, not just that it works

**Tweet 4:**
Likes are cheap. Trust is expensive.

You can buy likes. You can hack the algorithm for reach.

You cannot manufacture trust. It's earned through consistency over time.

**Tweet 5:**
The trust-building content formula:

1. Share what you actually believe
2. Show your reasoning, not just your conclusion
3. Be willing to be wrong publicly
4. Update your thinking when new evidence appears

**Tweet 6:**
The uncomfortable truth about personal branding:

The accounts that look the most polished often have the least trust.

Authenticity doesn't mean being messy. It means being honest.

**Tweet 7:**
Audit your last 10 posts.

How many were about what your audience believes vs. what you actually think?

If that ratio is off, that's why your engagement feels hollow.

## Thread 71-5: The 2026 Personal Brand Stack

**Tweet 1 (hook):**
The personal brand playbook from 2019 is dead.

Here's what actually works in 2026:

**Tweet 2:**
Old playbook:

- Pick one social platform
- Post 3x per week
- Grow slowly
- Sell course

New playbook:

- Build an idea engine first
- Distribute across platforms simultaneously
- Own your audience (email list)
- Sell access to your thinking

**Tweet 3:**
The platform strategy that works now:

1 long-form home base (newsletter, YouTube, or podcast)
2 short-form amplifiers (Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Instagram)

Everything short-form leads back to the long-form. The long-form is where you convert.

**Tweet 4:**
The most underrated asset in personal branding:

An email list you own.

Social platforms can change algorithms, ban accounts, or collapse overnight.

No one can take away your email list.

**Tweet 5:**
The content system that doesn't burn you out:

1 big idea per week
3 short-form posts that extract from the big idea
1 newsletter that goes deeper on it

That's it. Everything else is noise.

**Tweet 6:**
AI changes the game in one specific way:

It removes the friction between thinking and publishing.

If you have strong ideas, AI helps you get them out faster. It doesn't replace the ideas.

If your ideas are weak, AI makes weak content faster. That's not an advantage.

**Tweet 7:**
The 2026 personal brand is built on:

Depth of thinking. Consistency of distribution. Ownership of audience.

Everything else is window dressing.
