- **Slide 6:** "Principle 4: The transition from silence to music matters. A cold start with a loud track spikes your heart rate. Start with something ambient and build. Give your nervous system time to settle."
- **Slide 7:** "Principle 5: Match the music to the task type. Data entry and repetitive tasks benefit from rhythmic music. Writing and complex reasoning benefit from ambient or binaural. Do not use the same playlist for everything."

**CAPTION:**

Most people pick a playlist based on what they feel like listening to.

Focus DJ generates sessions matched to your task type and the kind of focus the work requires. The science behind how it works is in this carousel.

**GOAL:** Hot take / Engagement

**HOOK:** "Telling someone with ADHD to just use a to-do list is like telling someone with a broken leg to just walk it off."

**Visual description:** Bold statement on dark background. Minimal subtext. High contrast.

**CAPTION:**

Telling someone with ADHD to just use a to-do list is like telling someone with a broken leg to just walk it off.

The advice is not wrong in isolation. To-do lists are useful. The problem is assuming the only thing standing between the person and productivity is a better organizational system.

ADHD is not disorganization. It is a dysregulation of the systems that make starting, transitioning, and completing tasks feel internally rewarding. No list fixes that. The internal reward mechanism is the thing that does not fire reliably.

What actually helps: external triggers, environmental cues, emotional state monitoring, sensory management, body doubling.

None of that fits on a sticky note.
