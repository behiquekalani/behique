## Post 1 — The Validation Framework That Saved Me From Building the Wrong Thing
**Hook:** I almost spent 3 months building a product nobody wanted. A 48-hour test told me first.
**Slide 1:** I almost spent 3 months building a product nobody wanted. A 48-hour test told me first.
**Slide 2:** The mistake most builders make: they fall in love with the idea before they talk to anyone who might pay for it.
**Slide 3:** The 48-hour framework: Hour 0-8: define the problem and the person. Hour 8-24: find 10 real people with that problem. Hour 24-40: have 5 conversations. Hour 40-48: analyze what you heard and decide.
**Slide 4:** The only question that matters in those conversations: "Have you ever paid for a solution to this problem?" If the answer is no for every person, stop.
**Slide 5:** Not "would you pay?" Would you is a hypothetical. Have you paid is evidence. Evidence is what you are looking for.
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. The next post breaks down exactly how to find those 10 people.
**Caption:** The build-first instinct is natural for people who like making things. You get excited about the idea, you can already see how it would work, and the conversations feel slower than just starting. The problem is that building without validation is not faster. It is slower, because the worst case outcome is finishing a product and then discovering that the problem you solved was not one anyone was willing to pay you to fix. The 48-hour framework does not eliminate all risk. It eliminates the most avoidable risk: building for a problem that does not have a paying audience.

## Post 2 — Where to Find 10 People Who Have Your Problem
**Hook:** Finding 10 people who have the problem your product solves is the hardest part of validation. Here is where to look.
**Slide 1:** Finding 10 people who have the problem your product solves is the hardest part of validation. Here is where to look.
**Slide 2:** Start with communities, not search engines. Reddit, Facebook Groups, Discord servers, Slack workspaces. Go where the problem is already being discussed.
**Slide 3:** Look for the questions being asked, not just the communities themselves. A post titled "Is there a tool that does X automatically?" is a person with a problem and purchasing intent in the same sentence.
**Slide 4:** LinkedIn is underused for this. Search for people with a specific job title or situation. Send a cold message that is honest: you are doing research, you have 15 minutes of questions, no pitch at the end.
**Slide 5:** Your own audience if you have one. A story post or a tweet asking "does this problem resonate with you?" can surface the right people faster than cold outreach.
**Slide 6:** Find the people first. Then build the thing. Follow @behikeai for the full validation playbook.
**Caption:** The tactical challenge of validation is not the framework. It is finding the right people to validate with. Talking to the wrong audience gives you false negatives, people who do not have the problem say they would not buy, and you walk away thinking the idea is bad when you were just asking the wrong people. The places I go first are online communities where the problem is already being discussed. If people are posting about a pain point in a Reddit thread, the problem is real. If there is a 200-comment thread about a specific frustration, there is an audience. Your job is to find those threads and then turn the people in them into conversations.

## Post 3 — The 5 Questions That Tell You If an Idea Is Worth Building
**Hook:** These 5 questions will tell you more about a business idea than 3 months of market research.
**Slide 1:** These 5 questions will tell you more about a business idea than 3 months of market research.
**Slide 2:** Q1: Have you dealt with this problem before? (Confirms the problem is real for this person.)
**Slide 3:** Q2: What have you tried to solve it? (Tells you what the alternatives are and why they failed.)
**Slide 4:** Q3: What did that cost you, in money or time? (Establishes value. If it cost nothing, the problem might not be serious enough.)
**Slide 5:** Q4: If this problem disappeared tomorrow, what would change for you? (Reveals how much it actually matters.)
**Slide 6:** Q5: Who else do you know who has this problem? (Tests for a market, not just one person.)
**Caption:** Most validation conversations fail because people ask the wrong questions. They ask hypotheticals. "Would you use this?" "Would you pay $20 a month?" Those questions generate optimistic answers because people want to be helpful. They do not generate accurate signals about actual behavior. The five questions on this list are all about past behavior and real cost. People cannot lie convincingly about what they have already done. When someone tells you they have tried three solutions, spent $500 on one of them, and still has the problem, you have a business. When someone tells you they have never really looked for a solution, you have a hobby project.

## Post 4 — How to Pre-Sell Before You Build Anything
**Hook:** The best validation signal is a payment. Here is how to collect money before you build the product.
**Slide 1:** The best validation signal is a payment. Here is how to collect money before you build the product.
**Slide 2:** The pre-sell model: describe the product you intend to build, set a launch date 3 to 6 weeks out, price it at a discount for early buyers, and open a waitlist that accepts payment.
**Slide 3:** The tool stack: a landing page (Carrd or a simple HTML file), a payment processor (Gumroad, Stripe, or Lemon Squeezy), and an email to collect buyers.
**Slide 4:** The threshold: if you cannot get 10 paying customers before you build, the idea needs to be reworked. Not abandoned necessarily, but the positioning, the audience, or the price needs adjustment.
**Slide 5:** The delivery: when you hit your threshold, you build the thing. You already know the market is there. You already have feedback from buyers about what they expected.
**Slide 6:** Pre-sell first. Build second. Follow @behikeai for the landing page template.
**Caption:** Pre-selling feels uncomfortable because it seems like you are promising something you do not have yet. But framed honestly, it is the most respectful thing you can do for your future customers. You are telling them exactly what you are building, when it will be ready, and what it will cost. They make an informed decision. You get real market feedback before you invest weeks into building. The payment is not a trick. It is a signal. When someone gives you money for something that does not exist yet, they are telling you that the problem is real, your solution sounds credible, and the price is acceptable. That is the most valuable information you can have before you build.

## Post 5 — When Validation Says No
**Hook:** Validation is not just about confirming good ideas. It is about killing bad ones quickly.
**Slide 1:** Validation is not just about confirming good ideas. It is about killing bad ones quickly.
**Slide 2:** When validation says no, most people do one of two things: ignore it and build anyway, or give up entirely. Neither is the right response.
**Slide 3:** The right response: analyze why. Did you talk to the wrong audience? Was the price too high? Is the problem real but your solution wrong? Is the timing off?
**Slide 4:** The most common reason ideas fail validation: the problem exists but it is not painful enough. People have it, they tolerate it, but they are not actively looking for a solution. That is not your audience.
**Slide 5:** A failed validation is not a failed idea. It is a course correction. It tells you specifically what to adjust before you spend serious time building.
**Slide 6:** Kill ideas cheaply. Build winners confidently. Follow @behikeai for the full framework.
**Caption:** The purpose of validation is not to confirm that your idea is good. You probably think your idea is good. The purpose is to find out where the idea breaks down before you build it. A no from validation is not a judgment on your ability or your creativity. It is data. Specifically, it is data about whether the intersection of this problem, this audience, and this solution, at this price, at this time, adds up to something people will pay for. Every variable in that sentence can be adjusted. The audience can be narrowed. The price can be changed. The solution can be repositioned. Validation gives you the information to make those adjustments before you are committed.
