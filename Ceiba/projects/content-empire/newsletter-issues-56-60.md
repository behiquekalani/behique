## Issue 56: "Why I track everything"

Subject: Why I track everything

Most people track nothing. They run their business on feel. They can't tell you which product made the most money last month, how many hours they worked, or what percentage of their content got traction. They're flying blind and calling it flow.

I track everything. Revenue by product, daily tasks completed, content performance by format, money in and money out, habits kept versus habits broken. It started as an experiment. Now it's infrastructure.

Here's why it matters.

Data from your own life compounds. One month of tracking tells you almost nothing. Three months tells you which habits actually move your energy. Six months tells you which income streams are growing and which are coasting. A year tells you who you actually are versus who you think you are.

The gap between those two things is often large.

The metrics I track daily: tasks completed (tracked in Habitica), revenue from each active income stream, whether I wrote something, whether I moved. That's it. Takes two minutes. The daily log doesn't require analysis. It requires consistency. The analysis comes later.

The metrics I track weekly: content reach by platform and format, new revenue versus recurring, hours worked versus hours focused (they're not the same), and the BehiqueBot check-in review. One hour on Sunday. This is where patterns start to show up.

The metrics I track monthly: the full systems audit. Revenue trends, content trends, which systems are clean and which are broken. Two hours. This is where decisions get made.

The reason tracking works is not because data is magic. It's because measurement creates accountability without needing anyone else to hold you accountable. You can't rationalize away a number. You can rationalize away a feeling.

I've caught three dead habits by tracking. Habits I thought I was building but the data showed I was skipping four out of five days. I've also caught two revenue streams I was undervaluing, because when I looked at the numbers I realized they were more consistent than I'd assumed.

Tracking also removes the post-day shame spiral. When I don't know how my day went, the brain fills in the gaps with worst-case interpretation. When I have data, I know exactly what happened. Even on a bad day, "I completed two of five tasks" is better information than "I was useless today."

Start with one metric. The one that matters most to what you're building right now. Track it every day for 30 days. See what you find.

## Issue 57: "The student builder paradox"

Subject: The student builder paradox

Everyone assumes school is the obstacle. You're too busy studying to build. You don't have the time or the freedom that a full-time builder has. The curriculum is irrelevant to what you actually want to create.

I used to believe that. I was wrong.

Being a CS student in the middle of building a business is an advantage. A specific, underrated advantage that I only recognized after fighting against it for a year.

Here's what school actually gives you.

Hard constraints. I can't work on Behike all day. I have assignments, exams, lab sessions, office hours. This forces prioritization in a way that unstructured freedom doesn't. When you have infinite time, there's no pressure to choose. When you have two hours before a deadline, you find out what actually matters.

Technical foundation. Computer engineering is not directly applicable to building a Telegram bot or a Gumroad store. But the way you're taught to think about systems, about architecture, about debugging, transfers directly. I debug product research problems the same way I debug code. Break it into components, test each one, find where it fails.

The curriculum as signal. Every course I find boring is a data point about what I don't want to do for forty years. Every project I stay up late to finish is a data point about what actually engages me. School surfaces this faster than the market does, because the market doesn't let you fail for free.

Credibility without a resume. I'm building products while most people my age are building class projects. That gap compounds. By the time I graduate I'll have revenue history, product iterations, and distribution that most employed graduates spend their first years trying to create.

The paradox is that the constraints of being a student force the discipline that most builders claim to want but struggle to maintain when they have freedom.

The people who build nothing in school will also build nothing without it. The structure was never the problem.

## Issue 58: "What I got wrong about content"

Subject: What I got wrong about content

When I started posting I had two assumptions about content. Both were wrong.

Assumption one: posting more would build an audience faster.

I posted daily for a month. Engagement was flat. The volume wasn't the problem because the quality wasn't there. I was shipping fast and learning nothing between posts. The feedback loop was too compressed to produce insight.

The data showed me: my three best-performing posts came after weeks with the lowest output. The weeks where I slowed down and thought more carefully about what I was actually saying.

Frequency matters less than clarity. A post that says one true thing clearly will outperform five posts that say five mediocre things every time.

Assumption two: educational content would build the most trust.

I thought step-by-step tutorials would perform best. Teach something concrete, demonstrate value, build audience. Clean logic.

What actually performed: posts where I described a specific mistake I made and what I learned. Not tutorials. Stories with a lesson buried inside. The difference is that tutorials position you as the expert. Stories position you as the person who has been where the reader currently is. That's more compelling because it's more honest.

My best content follows one pattern now: here is a thing I believed, here is evidence that changed my view, here is what I actually think. Three parts. No intro fluff. No conclusion that restates the beginning. End on the insight.

The platform also changed what I learned. Instagram rewards saves. A post that gets saved is a post that someone found valuable enough to want to revisit. That metric is more honest than likes because it requires a decision, not just a reflex.

If I were starting the content work over, I'd post once a week for the first month and spend the rest of the time reading the comments and messages with obsessive attention. The audience tells you what they need. You just have to be going slow enough to hear it.

## Issue 59: "The long game"

Subject: The long game

Most people building online businesses are optimizing for this month's numbers. That's not wrong. Revenue is real and urgency is real. But the frame is too short to see what's actually being built.

An audience of 100 people who trust you is worth more than 10,000 followers who don't read your content. Revenue from five consistent buyers is more valuable than revenue from 100 one-time buyers. A reputation built over three years in a specific niche is more defensible than a product that went viral once.

This is not a motivational argument. It's a structural one. Compounding requires time. If you keep changing direction every 90 days you never accumulate anything. No trust, no data, no reputation.

Here is what compounding looks like in this specific context.

Month three of consistent posting: you start to see which content format generates the most saves and shares. You have 200-300 followers who found you for a specific reason.

Month six: the algorithm has enough data about your content to start distributing it more accurately. Your reach per post increases without increasing effort. The followers you have are more likely to see your next post because the ones who stayed are the ones who consistently engage.

Month twelve: you have a year of data. You know exactly which topics your audience responds to. You have a repeatable content system. The first products are generating consistent sales, not because of any single launch but because the content funnel is feeding discovery continuously.

Month twenty-four: the compounding is visible. The content you wrote a year ago is still discoverable. The reputation from consistent, specific work has accumulated to the point where people recommend you to others without being asked. Inbound replaces outbound.

None of this requires going viral. It requires showing up with something worth saying, consistently, in one place, for longer than most people are willing to.

The long game is not patient. It's strategic. The people winning it aren't waiting. They're building infrastructure that compounds while they sleep.

## Issue 60: "One year from now"

Subject: One year from now

March 2027. Twelve months from today. Here is what Behike should look like if the current trajectory holds and the right decisions get made.

This is not a vision board. It's a realistic extrapolation of what happens if the work that's already started continues at the same rate with the same intention.

Revenue. The current product catalog has 25 products ranging from $4.99 to $99. With consistent content distribution driving discovery, a 2% conversion rate on monthly traffic, and monthly visitors in the low thousands, the projected monthly revenue lands in the $2,000-$4,000 range from digital products alone. Adding the service tier ($499-$2,000 for automation builds) and one or two active Gumroad bundles, the realistic monthly ceiling is $5,000-$8,000. Not $10,000 yet. That requires either a major distribution event or a price-point increase in the service tier. Both are possible in 12 months.

Audience. Instagram at 3,000-5,000 followers if the content calendar executes consistently. YouTube at 1,000-2,500 subscribers if two to three videos per month ship. Newsletter at 500-1,500 subscribers if a free lead magnet is live and actively promoted. These numbers aren't large. They don't need to be. At that scale, with a responsive audience in a specific niche, the conversion math works.

Products. Three to five new digital products. Not twenty-five new ones. Better distribution of what already exists is more valuable than more products. The next product priority: a higher-ticket offer in the $149-$299 range that packages the most practical content into a structured format. One product. Priced correctly. Not rushed.

Operations. BehiqueBot expanded. Notion as the real database. A consistent weekly review habit that generates content and tracks all metrics. The eBay research pipeline running semi-automatically with the Niche Sniper system. Cobo (the second machine) handling background tasks.

What could break this. Not posting for more than two weeks. Chasing a new platform instead of staying consistent on the ones already working. Adding complexity (new tools, new systems, new products) before the current foundations are generating reliably. Finishing school and losing the constraint that forces prioritization.

March 2027 is achievable. It's not guaranteed. The gap between the projection and the reality is execution over twelve months, not one big move.

Start there.
