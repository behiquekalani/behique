## Thread 1: The 5 digital products I'd build first if starting over today

**1/**
Starting over with zero audience and zero money.

Here are the 5 digital products I'd build first, in order.

Not because they're popular. Because they have short build time, clear buyers, and real search demand.

---

**2/**
Product 1: A specific workflow template.

Not "productivity templates." A template for one exact task.

"The weekly review template for engineers who hate journaling."
"A client onboarding doc for solo designers."

Specificity is the whole product. Charge $7 to $12.

---

**3/**
Product 2: A cheat sheet for a tool with a steep learning curve.

People pay for compression.
If you've spent 40 hours learning something and can save someone 30 hours with a 15-page reference guide, that's a real product.

Obsidian, n8n, Notion, CapCut, Figma. Pick one you know well.

---

**4/**
Product 3: A research document with original sourcing.

This takes more time but almost no competition exists for it.

Pick a niche question people search for answers to.
Do the research.
Write it up with citations and structure.

Charge $15 to $25. A good research doc holds value for months.

---

**5/**
Product 4: A bilingual version of something that only exists in English.

If you speak Spanish and English, this is a wide-open lane.

Business vocabulary. AI tool guides. Email templates. Legal concepts explained simply.

The Spanish-language digital product market is underserved by a factor of 10.

---

**6/**
Product 5: A tool comparison for a specific decision.

"n8n vs Zapier for freelancers."
"Gumroad vs Lemon Squeezy: which to choose if you're starting from zero."
"The four AI writing tools I tested: what each one is actually good at."

People pay for a trusted opinion before making a decision. Be that opinion.

---

**7/**
The pattern across all five:

They're specific. They have a clear buyer. They can be built in a weekend.

None of them require fame. None of them require a following. They require knowing something and writing it down with enough clarity that a stranger can benefit from it.

---

**8/**
The product you're not building because it "doesn't seem impressive enough" is the one you should build first.

The $7 cheat sheet outperforms the $97 course you never shipped.

Start small. Ship it. Learn what the buyer actually wants. Build the next one better.

## Thread 2: How I think about pricing — the psychology behind $9.99, $29.99, and $99

**1/**
Pricing digital products is not math.

It's psychology. And most people get it wrong in the same direction: too low.

Here's how I actually think about it.

---

**2/**
$9.99 is not cheap. It's the "almost free" zone.

Buyers at this price point are low-risk buyers. They convert easily but they also expect less. They're less likely to read the product, less likely to implement it, and less likely to become repeat buyers.

Not a bad price point. Just understand who it attracts.

---

**3/**
$19 to $29 is where real buying decisions start.

At this range, the buyer has made a choice. They're not impulse-buying. They've decided this thing is worth their attention.

Completion rates go up. Feedback quality goes up. Conversions are lower, but the buyers are better.

---

**4/**
The jump from $29 to $49 is where most creators hesitate.

They think: "who would pay $49 for a PDF?"

The answer: anyone who has spent $500 on a problem this solves.

Price signals quality when the product is good. Low price sometimes signals low confidence.

---

**5/**
$99 is not a price point for a product. It's a price point for a transformation.

At $99, you're not selling information. You're selling the shortcut to a specific outcome.

"Here is exactly how I went from X to Y. Here are the exact steps, tools, and decisions."

That has a real value. Price it accordingly.

---

**6/**
The pricing framework I use:

What does this save the buyer in time?
What would they pay someone else to do this for them?
What's the cost of not having this?

A product that saves 10 hours of research time is worth at least $20 to someone who bills $50/hour.

Price to the value, not the page count.

---

**7/**
The charm pricing myth.

$9.99 doesn't feel meaningfully cheaper than $10 to most digital product buyers. They're not price-checking you against a competitor.

$9, $17, $27, $47 are all valid price points. Round numbers also work.

Don't overthink the last digit. Think about the tier.

---

**8/**
One counterintuitive thing: raising the price often increases perceived value enough to increase conversions.

I've tested this. The $12 product that converted at 3% converted at 4.5% at $17 with the same copy.

Higher price, more buyers. Because the product felt more serious.

---

**9/**
The real pricing rule:

Charge what makes you slightly uncomfortable.

If the price feels too easy to say, it's probably too low.
If you feel like you need to apologize for it, it might be right.

You can always run a sale. You can't easily raise without friction.

## Thread 3: What Gumroad analytics actually tell you about your audience

**1/**
Most people look at Gumroad revenue and nothing else.

The analytics panel has four signals that tell you more than the total number.

Here's what to actually look at.

---

**2/**
Signal 1: Traffic source breakdown.

Where are people coming from? Search, social, direct?

If 80% is direct, that means people are typing your URL or following a link. You have a real audience sending people.

If 80% is search, you have SEO working but no community. Different problem to solve.

---

**3/**
Signal 2: Conversion rate by source.

Traffic from Twitter converts at a different rate than traffic from Reddit.

Traffic from someone who already knows you converts at a different rate than cold search traffic.

If one source converts at 5% and another at 0.3%, that tells you where your real buyers are.

---

**4/**
Signal 3: Day-over-day traffic vs. sales.

Consistent traffic with zero sales means the product page is the problem.

Inconsistent traffic with high conversion on the days it spikes means you need more distribution, not a better product.

These are different problems. Don't fix the wrong one.

---

**5/**
Signal 4: Which products get traffic but not sales.

A product with views but no purchases tells you one of three things:

The price is too high for the audience landing on it.
The product description doesn't match what they expected.
The title is getting clicks from the wrong search intent.

Each has a different fix.

---

**6/**
What Gumroad analytics don't tell you:

What your buyers did with the product. Whether they were satisfied. What they wish it included.

That data lives in email replies, reviews, and refund requests.

The analytics tell you where the leak is in the funnel. The buyers tell you why.

---

**7/**
The metric that matters most in the first 90 days:

Cost per click divided by conversion rate.

Not revenue. Not traffic.

If you're spending time creating content to drive traffic, what's one buyer costing you in time?

That number tells you if the model is working.

---

**8/**
Review your Gumroad analytics once per week. Not daily.

Daily checking optimizes for anxiety, not action.

Weekly review gives you enough data to see trends and make one actual decision per week about what to change.

## Thread 4: Building a product in public vs. launching in private: my experience with both

**1/**
I've done both.

Built products in private, launched cold.
Built products in public, launched to a small audience that followed the build.

The results were different in ways I didn't expect.

---

**2/**
Building in private means you control everything until launch.

No feedback during the build. No expectation management. You ship when it's ready.

The risk: you spend 40 hours on something the market doesn't want and you find out at launch.

---

**3/**
Building in public means sharing the work as it happens.

The first screenshot. The outline. The problem you're trying to solve.
Each post is a small test of whether anyone cares.

The feedback comes in before you've committed everything. That's the real advantage.

---

**4/**
What I found: building in public forces clarity.

When you have to explain what you're building to a stranger in one tweet, you find out if the idea is actually clear.

Fuzzy ideas fail that test. Good ideas survive it.

---

**5/**
The audience you build during a public build is different from a launch audience.

They watched it get made. They saw the problem you were solving.
They're not evaluating the product on launch day. They already decided it was worth following.

Conversion rates are higher. Refund rates are lower.

---

**6/**
The downside of building in public:

You're committed to finishing. Walking away from a public build feels like failure in front of people.

That pressure is mostly positive. But if the build genuinely needs to pivot mid-way, the social contract makes it awkward.

---

**7/**
When to build in private:

The product is deeply personal or involves sensitive topics.
You genuinely don't know if the idea has legs and want to test privately first.
The build is so fast (two days) that there's no benefit to a slow public reveal.

---

**8/**
When to build in public:

The product solves a clear problem you can articulate in one sentence.
You want to grow an audience in parallel with building.
The build will take more than two weeks and you need external accountability.

---

**9/**
The honest takeaway:

Building in public worked better for me. Not because of reach. Because of clarity and accountability.

The audience is a side effect. The discipline is the real product.

## Thread 5: The content that actually drives product sales (data from my first 90 days)

**1/**
90 days of posting. Multiple platforms. Tracking what actually sent buyers to the product pages.

The results broke some things I thought I knew.

---

**2/**
The content that drove the most clicks: specific, tactical threads.

Not inspiration. Not "here's my story." Step-by-step threads about how to do a specific thing.

"How to set up n8n in 20 minutes" drove 4x more link clicks than "why I use n8n."

Specificity wins.

---

**3/**
The content that drove the most sales per click: personal story posts.

Fewer clicks than the tactical content. But higher conversion when people did click.

Someone who reads your story and then clicks has made a warmer decision than someone who clicked on a tactical tip.

---

**4/**
The content that drove zero sales: listicles without a hook.

"10 AI tools you should know about" performed fine on engagement.

It drove essentially no product sales because it didn't position me as someone with a specific, credible point of view.

Engagement and sales are not the same signal.

---

**5/**
The distribution finding that surprised me most:

One thread shared by someone with 5,000 followers in the exact niche drove more sales than 10 posts to my own audience.

Warm distribution from a trusted voice in the right community beats cold distribution to a large mixed audience every time.

---

**6/**
What worked on Instagram that didn't work on Twitter:

Before/after content. Showing a messy process and the clean result.

Instagram is more visual and more emotional. The "look at what I built" post resonated there in a way it didn't in a Twitter thread.

Platform calibration matters.

---

**7/**
The posting frequency finding:

Posting every day outperformed posting three times per week in terms of account growth.

It did not outperform in terms of sales per post.

The three-times-per-week schedule, when each post was sharper, drove more product clicks per post.

Quality with frequency is the target. If you can't do both, choose quality.

---

**8/**
The metric I stopped tracking after month one:

Likes.

Likes have almost zero correlation with link clicks or product sales in my data.

The posts with the most likes were often inspiration posts. The posts with the most sales were tactical threads that got 20% of the likes.

Optimize for the metric that matters.

---

**9/**
The content calendar I'd build from scratch knowing what I know now:

2 tactical threads per week (how-to, step-by-step, specific)
1 personal story post per week (something real, with a product mention if relevant)
1 product-focused post per week (outcome-led, no feature dump)
Daily short posts for reach and consistency

Simple. Sustainable. Built around what actually converts.
