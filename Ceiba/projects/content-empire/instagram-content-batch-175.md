## Post 3 — The Product Launch Email That Converts

**Hook:** Selling to your list is not about the product. It is about the sequence.

**Slide 1:** Selling to your list is not about the product. It is about the sequence.

**Slide 2:** The worst way to launch a product via email:
One email. "Hey, I have this product. Here is the link. Buy it."

Open rate: fine.
Click rate: low.
Conversion: near zero.

**Slide 3:** The 4-email launch sequence:
Email 1: The problem (send 7 days before launch)
Email 2: The possibility (send 4 days before launch)
Email 3: The product (launch day)
Email 4: The deadline (last day or last chance)

**Slide 4:** Email 1 — The problem email:
Do not mention the product.
Just write about the problem in detail.
Subscribers who feel seen by this email will buy when the offer appears.

**Slide 5:** Email 3 — The product email:
Short, direct, specific.
What it is. What it solves. What is included. Price. Link.
Fewer than 300 words.
No hype. No pressure. Just clarity.

**Slide 6:** Send a sequence. Not just an announcement.
Email List Accelerator at behike.gumroad.com
Link in bio.

**Caption:** The reason most product launches to email lists underperform is not the product and it is not the list size. It is that the launch is treated as an announcement rather than a conversation.

Subscribers do not wake up on the day you launch already primed to buy. You have to warm them up. The problem email does that by connecting their current struggle to the solution you are about to offer. By the time the product email arrives, the subscriber already knows why they need it.

That is the entire psychology of an effective email launch. Diagnose before you prescribe. The purchase is just the logical next step after the subscriber has already acknowledged the problem.

## Post 4 — The Affiliate Email That Does Not Feel Salesy

**Hook:** The best affiliate emails do not read like affiliate emails.

**Slide 1:** The best affiliate emails do not read like affiliate emails.

**Slide 2:** The affiliate email that gets ignored:
"This week's newsletter is sponsored by [tool]. Use my code for 20% off."

It reads like a banner ad.
Subscribers skip it without reading.

**Slide 3:** The affiliate email that converts:
"I have been using [tool] for 3 months. Here is the specific problem it solved and why I recommend it."

Personal. Specific. Earned.

**Slide 4:** The rule for affiliate recommendations in email:
Only recommend what you actually use.
Only recommend it when it is relevant to the email topic.
Never lead with the affiliate relationship.

The recommendation comes first. The disclosure comes after.

**Slide 5:** The format that works:
- Share a real story about using the tool
- Explain the specific result or problem it solved
- Give your honest opinion including the limitations
- Then include the affiliate link with a clear disclosure

Readers trust specific, honest reviews. They ignore generic promotions.

**Caption:** Affiliate marketing fails in email when the creator treats their list as an audience to monetize rather than a community to serve. Subscribers can tell the difference.

That specificity is what converts. Because it is not marketing. It is a genuine recommendation from someone the reader already trusts.

Your affiliate income will scale with your credibility. Protect the credibility first.
