## Script 1: Creating Digital Products With AI Assistance

**Hook (0-30s):**
AI has changed what is possible for a single person to create. Not because AI does the creative work for you, but because it removes most of the production friction that used to require teams, agencies, or years of accumulated skill. The person who understands this correctly can build a product library in months that would have taken years before. The person who misunderstands it will produce generic garbage that nobody buys.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
I want to be specific about what AI assistance actually means when it comes to digital products. Because there is a version of this conversation that is just about prompt templates and automation hacks. That version is not interesting and it does not produce good products.

The more important version is about how AI changes the economics of creation. When the cost of producing a first draft, designing a layout, formatting a document, or generating supporting materials drops dramatically, the constraint shifts. It is no longer "can I produce this?" It is "do I have something worth saying that someone will pay for?"

That shift is more significant than the technology itself. Because most people with AI access are using it to produce faster while ignoring the more fundamental question: is this worth producing at all?

Today I am going to walk through how to actually create digital products with AI assistance in a way that produces something real, something differentiated, and something people will actually buy.

### Section 1: Where AI Fits in the Product Creation Stack

There is a stack of decisions and tasks that go into creating a digital product. Let me map where AI genuinely helps and where you still have to do the work yourself.

At the top of the stack is the concept. What problem does this product solve? Who specifically has that problem? Why does your version of the solution matter? AI cannot answer these questions for you because the answers depend on your specific experience, your audience, and what you have actually observed about what people struggle with. If you ask AI to generate a product idea, you will get a generic idea. Generic ideas produce generic products that compete only on price.

The concept phase is on you. This is where the product's value is determined. Everything downstream depends on getting this right.

Once you have a clear concept, AI becomes enormously useful. The research phase, where you are gathering supporting information, finding examples, understanding the landscape of existing solutions, this is where AI assistants save significant time. The outline phase, where you are structuring the product's flow and deciding what goes where, is also a strong use case. You can iterate on structure rapidly.

The draft phase is where it gets more nuanced. AI can produce serviceable first drafts that you then revise, edit, and inject with your specific voice and perspective. This is faster than starting from a blank page. But the editing and voice layer is still your work. A product that reads like it was entirely written by AI will not build the trust that makes customers come back.

The production phase, formatting, layout, design elements, this is where AI tools have made the biggest practical difference. Tasks that used to require a designer or hours of manual work now take minutes.

### Section 2: The Differentiation Problem

Here is the real challenge with AI-assisted products: everyone has access to the same tools. Which means the raw production capacity is no longer a competitive advantage. What remains as a differentiator is perspective, specificity, and trust.

Perspective means your product reflects a real point of view. Not a compilation of generic best practices. An actual position on how to approach a problem based on what you have tested and concluded.

Specificity means your product is built for a defined person with a defined situation, not for everyone. The more specific the problem you solve, the less competition you face and the more intensely your ideal customer feels that the product was made for them.

Trust means your audience already knows your work and believes you have something valuable to say. A digital product from someone with no audience and no established perspective is hard to sell regardless of quality. But a product from someone who has been publishing consistently and building credibility over months, that product has a market before it even launches.

This is why the audience-building work and the product-creation work are not separate. They feed each other. Your content builds the trust that makes your products sell. Your products give your content a destination that it is moving readers toward.

### Section 3: A Practical Process

Let me give you the actual process I would follow to create a digital product with AI assistance.

Step one: write the core thesis manually. One paragraph that says what this product is, who it is for, what problem it solves, and what makes your approach different. This is your product's foundation. Do not use AI for this. It needs to come from your thinking.

Step two: use AI to expand the outline. Start with your thesis, then ask AI to help you build a comprehensive structure. Review it, cut anything generic, add anything it missed, and reorder it to match your actual thinking.

Step three: draft section by section with AI assistance, then edit each section yourself. Add examples from your experience. Replace generic phrases with specific ones. Cut anything that does not directly serve the reader.

Step four: use AI tools for production. Format the document, generate design elements, create cover graphics, build the table of contents. This is where you get the hours back.

Step five: get feedback before you finalize. Send it to five people who represent your target reader. What confused them? What did they want more of? What felt like filler? Use that input for a final revision pass.

The product you end up with will have your voice, your perspective, and your specific value. And it will have taken you a fraction of the time it would have taken without AI assistance.

## Script 2: Why Your Niche Is Not Too Saturated

**Hook (0-30s):**
Every person who has never started an online business thinks their niche is too saturated. And almost every person who has built one looks back and says that was the wrong thing to worry about. The saturation concern is real, but it is usually aimed at the wrong level. Let me show you what I mean.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Here is the test for whether a niche is actually too saturated: are people in that niche still spending money? Are they still searching for solutions, consuming content, buying products, and hiring people to help them? If yes, the market is alive. And if the market is alive, the question is never whether it is too crowded at the macro level. The question is whether there is a specific angle, a specific audience segment, a specific problem within that niche that is underserved.

The answer is almost always yes. Because even in the most crowded niches, most of the content and products are generic. They are made for everyone, which means they resonate deeply with no one. The opportunity is not at the broad level. It is at the specific one.

Today I want to break down why saturation thinking is usually a confidence problem dressed up as a market research problem, and what to do instead.

### Section 1: The Saturation Illusion

When you look at a niche from the outside, you see the most successful players. The people with the largest audiences, the most products, the most visibility. And your brain interprets that visibility as crowdedness. It looks like there is no room.

But visibility is not the same as market share. The top creators in any niche serve a fraction of the people who need help in that space. Most people in any given problem category never find the one creator who speaks directly to them. They settle for something close enough, or they do not find anything at all.

Think about the personal finance niche. It looks completely saturated. There are thousands of blogs, YouTube channels, podcasts, and courses about money management. And yet, there is still massive demand for personal finance content aimed specifically at recent college graduates, at freelancers with irregular income, at parents in their 40s who feel behind, at immigrants building wealth in a new country. Each of those sub-segments is underserved relative to the demand that exists there.

The same logic applies to every niche. What looks saturated at the general level has gaps at the specific level. Your job is not to compete at the general level. It is to find the specific level where you have something genuine to offer and the competition is thin.

### Section 2: The Actual Differentiators

Even in niches where the specific segment you target already has some established voices, you can still build something. Because differentiation is not just about topic. It is about perspective, format, depth, and trust.

Perspective is the most powerful differentiator. If you have a genuine point of view that differs meaningfully from the conventional wisdom in your space, you will attract the people who sense that the conventional wisdom has failed them. Those people are your core audience. They are not served by the mainstream voices. They need someone who sees things differently.

Format matters more than people acknowledge. The exact same information delivered as a long-form newsletter versus a short-form video series versus an interactive course produces very different experiences. Some people learn from reading. Some from watching. Some need structured exercises. If the dominant players in your niche favor one format, the others are open.

Depth is another angle. Most content in any niche is shallow. It covers the basics, the beginner frameworks, the entry-level vocabulary. Very few people go deep. If you are willing to go where most content creators do not, you will attract an audience that has exhausted the shallow content and is hungry for more.

### Section 3: What to Do With This

The practical implication is straightforward. Stop asking "is my niche too saturated?" and start asking "is my angle specific enough?"

If you can describe your ideal reader in one sentence that sounds like a real person, not a demographic category, you probably have a specific enough angle. "People who want to make money online" is not a specific enough angle. "Freelance writers who want to productize their expertise into scalable income without burning out on client work" is specific. You can write for that person. You can create products for that person. And most of the generic personal finance and online business content is not aimed at that person specifically.

Specificity is not a constraint on your market size. Counterintuitively, it is what makes your market findable. People search for specific solutions to specific problems. They do not search for "money advice." They search for "how to price freelance writing retainers" or "how to transition from hourly to productized services." If your content answers the specific question, you will be found by the people who are looking for exactly that.

The niche is not the problem. The specificity is. Get more specific, not less.

## Script 3: The Solopreneur's Guide to Affiliate Marketing

**Hook (0-30s):**
Affiliate marketing has a reputation problem. Most people hear the term and picture spam links, shady review sites, or people promoting products they have never used for a commission. That version of affiliate marketing exists and it is a race to the bottom. But there is another version, one that is built on genuine recommendation, earned trust, and real alignment between what you promote and what your audience actually needs. That version is a legitimate and sustainable revenue stream for solopreneurs.

**Intro (30s-2min):**
Let me define what I mean by affiliate marketing in the context of building a one-person business. I am not talking about building a site purely to capture search traffic and monetize with affiliate links. That model still works in some contexts, but it requires significant SEO investment and is vulnerable to algorithm changes.

The model I want to talk about is simpler and more durable. You build an audience around a specific problem or topic. You use tools and products yourself. You recommend the ones that actually work. When your recommendation converts, you earn a commission. The audience does not pay more. The company you are recommending rewards you for the referral.

The reason this works is the same reason word-of-mouth works. People trust recommendations from sources they already trust more than they trust advertising. Your affiliate recommendation is essentially the same as telling a friend about something that helped you. The difference is you get paid for it.

But the trust is the whole thing. Without trust, affiliate marketing does not work. It becomes noise that your audience filters out. So understanding how to maintain that trust while monetizing with affiliate recommendations is the core skill here.

### Section 1: Building an Affiliate Stack That Serves Your Audience

The first step is mapping what your audience needs and then finding affiliate products that genuinely serve those needs. Not finding affiliate programs and building content to justify them. That is the wrong order.

Think about the problem your content addresses. Think about the tools, products, services, and courses your ideal reader would naturally need as they work on that problem. Those are your affiliate candidates.

Then do the actual evaluation. Use the products if at all possible. Understand the quality, the support, the pricing, the reputation. If you would not recommend something to a friend unprompted, you should not recommend it to your audience for a commission.

The affiliate stack for most solopreneurs ends up being fairly small. Five to ten products that genuinely fit their content and their audience. That is fine. Depth of recommendation beats breadth of products. One tool that you have used for two years and can speak about with specific examples and genuine enthusiasm will convert better and produce more lasting trust than twenty tools you mention in passing.

### Section 2: How to Recommend Without It Feeling Like Advertising

The way most people do affiliate recommendations is transparent as advertising. A forced mention at the end of a piece of content that feels disconnected from everything else. The audience knows what it is. They filter it out.

The more effective approach is integration. Affiliate recommendations belong inside the content, at the natural moment where that tool or product becomes relevant. If you are writing about building a newsletter, the natural place to mention your email platform recommendation is when you are talking about platform selection, not in a footer disclaimer.

The framing matters enormously. "I have tried several email platforms over the years and the one I have settled on is X, here is specifically why" is a recommendation. "Check out this platform using my link for a discount" is an ad. The words, tone, and specificity of the first version create trust. The format of the second version erodes it.

Disclose clearly. Not just for legal compliance, though that matters. But because transparency is part of the trust contract. Your audience will respect you more for being upfront about affiliate relationships than for hiding them. Most readers are not bothered by you earning a commission. They are bothered by the feeling of being manipulated.

### Section 3: The Long-Term Math

Affiliate revenue does not usually start strong. In the early stages, when your audience is small, the commissions are small. This is why so many people give up on affiliate marketing before it becomes meaningful.

The math changes as your audience grows. And more importantly, the math changes as the trust deepens. A recommendation from someone your readers have been following for two years converts at a completely different rate than a recommendation from someone they discovered last month.

The strategy is to build the trust infrastructure first and let the affiliate revenue follow. Do not pick affiliate products because they pay the highest commission rates. Pick them because they are the best solution for your specific audience. Over time, that alignment produces better conversion rates, longer customer retention for the companies you recommend, and better commission structures as your referral volume grows.

Some of the most successful solopreneur affiliate relationships end up evolving into actual partnerships, sponsored content, and deeper integrations with the companies. That starts from genuine recommendation and earned trust. Not from chasing the highest payout.

**CTA & Outro:**
If you are at the stage where you are thinking about how to add revenue streams to your content business without compromising the trust you have built, affiliate marketing done correctly is one of the most natural fits. Start with what you already use and recommend genuinely.
