## Threads

**6. The $0 to $1 digital product system**

1/ The hardest part of selling digital products isn't building them. It's getting the first dollar. Here's the system I use:

2/ Start with a problem you've already solved for yourself. Document the solution. That's your first product.

3/ Price it low enough to remove friction. $7-$27. You're not trying to get rich. You're trying to validate demand.

4/ Put it in front of 100 people. Not 10,000. 100 targeted people who have the problem. See what happens.

5/ After 10 sales, raise the price. After 100 sales, build the next tier. The business grows in layers, not explosions.

**7. Why most content fails**

1/ Most content fails for one reason: it was created to fill a calendar instead of to say something true.

2/ The algorithm doesn't reward posting frequency as much as people think. It rewards engagement. And engagement requires the reader to feel something.

3/ You can't manufacture that feeling. You can only get it by writing from genuine conviction about something you actually know.

4/ One post that changes how someone thinks about their work beats 30 posts that just fill space. Quality over cadence.

---

**8. The solopreneur operating system**

1/ If your business only runs when you're working, you don't have a business. You have a job with extra steps.

2/ The shift happens when you build systems: content that keeps attracting, products that keep selling, automations that keep running.

3/ Each system you build buys back time. That time goes into building the next system. This is compounding applied to work.

4/ Most people never start because they're waiting for permission or the perfect moment. Start with the smallest repeatable unit and stack from there.

---

**9. Building in public: the honest version**

1/ Building in public isn't a marketing strategy. It's a commitment to reality over image.

2/ Most "building in public" accounts only share wins. That's just PR with extra steps.

3/ The version that builds real trust: share the actual numbers, the failed experiments, the decisions you're unsure about.

4/ People don't follow you because you're succeeding. They follow you because you're doing what they want to do and being honest about what it costs.

---

**10. On niching down**

1/ "Niche down" is the most repeated and least followed advice in creator circles. Why?

2/ Because specificity feels like exclusion. If you write for "fitness coaches who want to build a course," you're afraid of losing everyone else.

3/ But generality is invisible. Specificity makes you findable, quotable, and worth paying for.

4/ The narrower your focus, the easier it is for the right person to say "this is exactly for me." That's the sentence that converts.
