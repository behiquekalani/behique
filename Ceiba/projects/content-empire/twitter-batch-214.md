# Twitter Batch 214 — The Solopreneur's Sales Funnel

---

## Standalone Tweets

**Tweet 1**
A funnel is just a trust ladder. Each step earns more trust, which earns the right to ask for more commitment. Complexity is optional. The ladder is not.

**Tweet 2**
Most solopreneurs fail at sales because they skip straight from "free content" to "buy my $500 course." The gap is too wide. You need steps in between.

**Tweet 3**
Content is top of funnel. Email is middle of funnel. A conversation or a direct offer is bottom of funnel. Every piece you create should serve one of those three stages.

**Tweet 4**
The goal of a free post is not to sell. The goal is to earn the click to your profile. Then the bio sells the follow. Then the follow earns the email. Then the email sells the product.

**Tweet 5**
Your first product should not be your best product. It should be the easiest "yes." Low price, high speed-to-value, clear outcome. Get them in the door first.

## Threads

**Thread 1 — The simple solopreneur funnel that works (6 tweets)**

1/ Most solopreneur funnels are either overcomplicated or nonexistent. Here is the simple version that works at every scale.

2/ Stage 1: Awareness. Free content on social. One topic, one audience, consistent posting. This is where cold audiences discover you.

3/ Stage 2: Trust. Email newsletter. The people who cared enough to subscribe get deeper value. You stop performing and start having a conversation.

4/ Stage 3: Interest. A low-ticket offer. $9, $27, $47. Something that answers one specific question they already have. This filters serious people from browsers.

5/ Stage 4: Investment. Your core product. $97 to $497. The people who bought the entry offer are pre-qualified. Conversion rates here are significantly higher.

6/ Stage 5: Depth. A service, coaching, or community. High-touch, high-margin, for the people who want direct access to you. This is where the real income sits.

---

**Thread 2 — Why your free content is leaking revenue (5 tweets)**

1/ If your content is valuable but you are not making money, you have a funnel problem, not a content problem.

2/ Great content without a next step is charity. You give value, someone consumes it, and then they go back to scrolling. Nothing happens.

3/ Every piece of content needs a clear next step. A link, a CTA, a "reply to this," a "grab this." Not aggressive. Just present.

4/ The next step does not have to be a sale. It can be a follow, a save, a subscribe, a share. But it has to exist. Otherwise you are farming attention for someone else's benefit.

5/ Audit your last 10 posts. How many had a clear next step? That number is your funnel conversion rate. If it is zero, you now know the problem.

---

**Thread 3 — The psychology behind a funnel that converts (5 tweets)**

1/ Funnels work because of psychology, not technology. The tech is just the delivery mechanism. Here is what actually moves people.

2/ Reciprocity: give value first, genuinely and consistently. People feel a social pull to give something back. An email address is a small, easy way to do that.

3/ Specificity: vague offers do not convert. The more precisely you describe the problem you solve and the result you deliver, the faster people say yes.

4/ Social proof: "others like you made this decision and it worked out" removes friction. Testimonials, case studies, and numbers all serve this function.

5/ Scarcity and momentum: people act when they have a reason to act now. Deadlines, limited spots, and time-sensitive bonuses are not manipulation. They are honest friction-removers.

---

**Thread 4 — The one-product solopreneur funnel (4 tweets)**

1/ You do not need ten products to build a funnel. You can build a real business with one product and one email list.

2/ Step 1: Create one piece of free content per day that speaks directly to the person who needs your product.

3/ Step 2: Offer one lead magnet that gives them a taste of the transformation your product delivers. Collect their email.

4/ Step 3: Send a 5-email welcome sequence. Emails 1-4 deliver value and build trust. Email 5 presents the offer. This sequence does the selling for you, on autopilot, forever.

---

**Thread 5 — How to price your first offer (5 tweets)**

1/ Pricing is not math. It is positioning. Your price communicates the value of what you are selling before the buyer reads a word of the sales page.

2/ Pricing too low signals that you do not believe in your own work. Buyers notice this. A $9 product creates $9-level expectations.

3/ Pricing too high without proof creates friction that free content alone cannot overcome. You need trust before you can command premium prices.

4/ For a first offer: price it at the point where someone has to think for a second, but can say yes without consulting their budget. Usually $27 to $97.

5/ As you collect testimonials, as your audience grows, as your proof compounds — raise the price. Pricing is not fixed. It grows with your authority.
