## POST 5 — Single Image (Tracking vs Planning)

**GOAL:** Engagement / Hot take

**HOOK:** Tracking the past doesn't fix the future.

Most people use a budget to document how they failed last month. They review the numbers, feel bad, and resolve to do better next month.

That is not a system. That is a guilt ritual.

A budget that actually changes behavior looks forward. The question isn't "what did I spend." It's "what do I have left, and where does it go from here."

## POST 8 — Single Image (Zero-Based Adapted for ADHD)

**GOAL:** Education

**HOOK:** Zero-based budgeting, adapted for ADHD.

The original idea: every dollar gets a job before the month starts. Income minus expenses equals zero. Not because you spent everything. Because nothing was left unplanned.

The ADHD adaptation: do this weekly, not monthly. Monthly is too far away to feel real. A week is concrete. Seven days. You can hold a week.

Assign every dollar on Sunday. See where it went on Friday. Adjust the following Sunday.

**CTA:** Try it for one week before deciding it doesn't work for you.

## POST 9 — Single Image (Puerto Rican Personal Finance)

**GOAL:** Personal / Community

**HOOK:** Money in Puerto Rico hits different.

The cost of living here doesn't match the mainland comparisons. Groceries are mostly imported. Utilities run higher than most comparisons show. Salary ranges for most fields don't reflect what you'd see in Miami or New York for the same work.

And then there's the layer most personal finance content ignores entirely. Remesas. Family obligations. The cousin who needs help with rent. Your grandmother's medication. The culture where saying no to family feels like a failure of character.

A budget that doesn't account for these things was built for someone else's life.

**CTA:** Drop a comment if your financial reality doesn't match the content you see online.

## POST 10 — Single Image (Balance as Identity vs Information)

**GOAL:** Philosophical / Engagement

**HOOK:** Checking your balance is not a character assessment.

The number in your account tells you one thing: what is there right now. It doesn't tell you if you're responsible. It doesn't tell you if you're winning or losing. It is data. That's all it is.

The problem starts when the balance check becomes a referendum on who you are. You look, the number is lower than expected, and you feel like a failure instead of someone who just needs more information.

Separate the data from the identity. The number is neutral. You are not your balance.

**CTA:** Save this for the next time checking your account feels like a punishment.

## POST 12 — Single Image (The Good Enough Budget)

**GOAL:** Practical / Relatable

**HOOK:** A good enough budget beats a perfect one you quit.

The perfect budget has every transaction categorized correctly. Every receipt logged. Every variance explained.

Most people who try to build that system quit within three weeks.

A good enough budget means: you know roughly where your money is, roughly where it's going, and you catch what's leaking. That is enough to make better decisions. That is enough to build from.

Consistency at 70 percent accuracy is more useful than perfection you abandon.

**CTA:** Build the minimum version first. You can make it better once it's actually running.
