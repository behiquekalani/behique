## Threads

**6. What "build in public" actually requires**

1/ Building in public is popular advice. Very few people who try it stick with it past 60 days. Here's why, and how to make it work:

2/ Most people start building in public for the audience. That's the wrong reason. Start because you want external accountability and the act of narrating forces you to think more clearly.

3/ The content comes naturally when you're genuinely working on something. The problem is people try to manufacture "BIP content" when they're not actually building anything.

4/ Start by sharing one real decision, one real number, or one real lesson per week. That's it. The consistency of that over 6 months is worth more than any launch thread.

---

**7. The anti-funnel approach**

1/ Funnels work. But most solopreneurs build funnels before they've earned trust. Here's a simpler approach:

2/ Instead of a funnel: build a body of work. Write 50 posts that demonstrate what you know. Put your product at the end of the bio. Let the content do the pre-selling.

3/ The anti-funnel: no landing page sequences, no 7-email automation, no tripwire. Just: "here's my work, here's my product, here's the price."

4/ This works slower. It also builds a different kind of customer, one who bought because they already trusted you. Those customers refer, repeat-buy, and don't ask for refunds.

---

**8. How to write a tweet that gets shared**

1/ Shareable tweets have one thing in common: they articulate something the reader already believed but couldn't say clearly.

2/ That's why "hot takes" often perform well. They're not usually that extreme. They just say the quiet part loud.

3/ Find the belief in your niche that most people hold privately but rarely state publicly. Write a clean, confident version of that belief. That's your shareable tweet.

4/ This isn't manipulation. It's clarity. Your job as a writer is to say things more precisely than your reader can. When you do, they share it because it sounds like their own thought, improved.

---

**9. The compounding content effect**

1/ Content written today will still be attracting people 3 years from now. This is the compound interest of media.

2/ A blog post doesn't expire. A well-indexed YouTube video keeps getting found. A Twitter thread gets re-shared every few months by someone who discovers it fresh.

3/ This is why consistency over time is more powerful than any individual viral hit. The viral hit gets you attention once. The consistent body of work gets you discovered indefinitely.

4/ Think in years, not posts. Each piece you publish is infrastructure. It either earns its keep long-term or it doesn't. Prioritize depth.

---

**10. On selling without feeling like you're selling**

1/ Most creators are uncomfortable selling. They built an audience to share ideas, and selling feels like exploiting that relationship.

2/ Here's the reframe: if you have a product that solves a real problem your audience has, not mentioning it is doing them a disservice.

3/ Selling feels gross when the product isn't right for the audience. It feels natural when there's a genuine match between their problem and your solution.

4/ Fix the product-audience fit. Then selling becomes: "here's a thing I made that solves what I've been writing about." That's not a pitch. That's a conclusion.
