## Post 1 — Most Landing Pages Die in the First 5 Seconds

**Hook:** Your landing page has 5 seconds to answer one question. Most pages take 30 seconds to get there.

**Slide 1:** Your landing page has 5 seconds to answer one question. Most pages take 30 seconds to get there.

**Slide 2:** The question: "Is this for me?" Not "what is this?" Not "how much is it?" Those come later. The first question is always identity. "Am I the person this is built for?"

**Slide 3:** Most landing pages open with a product description. Features. Background on the company. The origin story. None of that answers the identity question. None of that tells the visitor whether to stay.

**Slide 4:** The opening line of your landing page should name your specific person. "For freelancers who want to charge more without working more." "For creators who are consistent on some platforms and invisible on the rest." Identity first.

**Slide 5:** If the right person reads your opening line and thinks "that's me," you have their attention. If the wrong person reads it and thinks "that's not me," they leave. Both outcomes are correct. Stop trying to keep everyone.

**Slide 6:** Rewrite your landing page headline right now. Make it name a person, not a product. See what happens to your time on page. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:**
The most expensive mistake on a landing page is writing for everyone.

When you try to appeal to everyone with your opening line, you connect with no one. The reader who would have paid you $200 without hesitation just bounced because your headline sounded like it was written for a different person.

The headline is a filter, not an advertisement. Its job is not to attract the maximum number of people. Its job is to confirm, immediately, that the exact right person has arrived at the right place.

"Build your business" keeps everyone. "Build a one-product digital business without an audience or ad budget" keeps the right person and sends everyone else away. The second headline will convert at a higher rate from a smaller audience. That's the tradeoff. Take it.

Niche headlines feel like they're leaving money on the table. In practice, they're the ones that generate the most revenue per visitor. Specificity is not a risk. Vagueness is the risk.

Name your person in the headline. Let the wrong people self-select out. Build for the ones who stay.

## Post 2 — The Above-the-Fold Formula

**Hook:** Everything above the scroll line on your landing page must do one thing: earn the scroll.

**Slide 1:** Everything above the scroll line on your landing page must do one thing: earn the scroll.

**Slide 2:** The above-the-fold section is not the whole sales pitch. It's the reason to read the sales pitch. Think of it as a movie trailer, not the movie.

**Slide 3:** The formula: [Headline naming the transformation] + [Subheadline naming the mechanism] + [One visual that proves it's real] + [CTA that names the next step clearly].

**Slide 4:** Example: "Write 10x Faster Without Sounding Like AI" + "The 4-part prompt system used by 800 solo creators for landing pages, emails, and posts" + screenshot of the product + "Get the Playbook for $19."

**Slide 5:** What does not belong above the fold: your bio, a long explanation of the problem, testimonials, pricing details, or FAQ. Those are below-the-fold content. They are for people who already decided they're interested.

**Slide 6:** Above the fold is only for people deciding whether to scroll. Give them one reason to scroll. Make it specific and make it fast. Follow @behikeai for the full landing page framework.

**Caption:**
I've audited a lot of landing pages. The single most common mistake is an above-the-fold section that contains everything except a clear reason to keep reading.

The founder puts their story there. Or a list of all the problems the product solves. Or a long explanation of the methodology. All of that belongs somewhere on the page. None of it belongs at the top.

The top of the page is real estate for first impressions. Your visitor has not yet decided whether this is worth their time. They have not scrolled, they have not read, they have not bought. They have only glanced.

You have one job with that glance: give them a reason to take a second look. One transformation. One mechanism. One signal that this is real. One action they can take.

Everything else is for people who already said yes to the first question. Don't try to close the sale above the fold. Earn the scroll. The sale happens below it.

## Post 3 — How to Write the Problem Section Without Fear

**Hook:** The problem section is where most landing pages go either too soft or too manipulative. The right path is between them.

**Slide 1:** The problem section is where most landing pages go either too soft or too manipulative. The right path is between them.

**Slide 2:** Too soft: "You might be wondering how to improve your content." This minimizes the problem. The reader doesn't feel seen. They don't feel understood. They don't feel compelled to read on.

**Slide 3:** Too manipulative: "You're falling behind every day you don't have a system. Your competitors are using AI and you're still writing by hand." This invents urgency. The reader feels pressured. They disengage or they buy with resentment.

**Slide 4:** The right path: describe the specific friction your reader has already felt, without inventing stakes they don't have. "You've tried five AI tools and none of them sound like you. The content comes out polished but forgettable." That's empathy, not pressure.

**Slide 5:** The test for your problem section: read it and ask "would my target reader's response be 'that's exactly my situation' or 'I feel scared'?" The first response means you've written empathy. The second means you've written fear. One builds trust. The other erodes it.

**Slide 6:** Write the problem section from memory. Write it the way you would describe the pain to a friend who is experiencing it. Not the way you would pitch it. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:**
The problem section of a landing page is where the reader decides whether you understand them.

It's not where they decide to buy. That happens later. The problem section is purely about trust. Does this person know what it's actually like to be me? Have they been in this room?

When you write it from a place of genuine empathy, the answer is yes. When you write it to maximize anxiety and urgency, the answer is: "This person is trying to sell me something."

The reader can tell the difference. They've seen thousands of landing pages. They know when a problem section is describing their reality and when it's weaponizing it.

The restraint required to write an honest problem section is the same restraint required to be a good advisor. You name the pain clearly. You don't amplify it. You show that you understand the cost of not solving it, but you don't manufacture that cost.

The reader who feels understood by your problem section is already halfway to a yes. That's enough. You don't need to scare them the rest of the way.

## Post 4 — The Feature vs. Outcome Mistake That Kills Sales

**Hook:** Your landing page is probably listing features when it should be listing outcomes. Here is the difference.

**Slide 1:** Your landing page is probably listing features when it should be listing outcomes. Here is the difference.

**Slide 2:** Feature: "30 ready-to-use prompts." Outcome: "Write a complete landing page in one afternoon using a proven prompt, without staring at a blank page for 45 minutes."

**Slide 3:** The reader does not buy the feature. They buy the life after the feature. The outcome is the life after. Always lead with that and offer the feature as proof that the outcome is real.

**Slide 4:** The formula: [Outcome statement] + [because] + [feature that delivers it]. "You'll stop spending 2 hours on email copy because each prompt is engineered for a specific use case: launch email, nurture email, re-engagement email."

**Slide 5:** Run your "What's Inside" section right now. For every bullet that starts with a noun (a guide, a template, a checklist), rewrite it to start with a verb and a transformation (stop wasting, build faster, write with confidence).

**Slide 6:** Your product's value is not in what it contains. It's in what your reader's life looks like after they use it. Write the after. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:**
The features are real. The features matter. But the features are not why people buy.

Nobody has ever woken up thinking "I need more prompts." They woke up thinking "I need to stop spending three hours on content that performs worse than posts I wrote in 20 minutes."

The prompts are the mechanism that solves that problem. But the buyer doesn't care about the mechanism until they've already bought into the outcome. So lead with the outcome and offer the mechanism as the evidence that the outcome is achievable.

This shift is uncomfortable for founders and creators because we're proud of what we built. We want to describe it. We want to list everything that's in there. We worked hard on it.

But the reader is not evaluating your effort. They're evaluating whether their life changes if they buy. Lead them to the change. The features are the proof that the change is real. They go second, not first.

Outcome first. Feature as evidence. That's the formula.

## Post 5 — The CTA Section That Gets Clicked

**Hook:** The pricing section of your landing page is not about the price. It's about making the decision feel easy.

**Slide 1:** The pricing section of your landing page is not about the price. It's about making the decision feel easy.

**Slide 2:** By the time your reader reaches the pricing section, they know what the product is, who it's for, and what changes after they buy. The price is not the variable. The perceived risk is the variable.

**Slide 3:** Reduce perceived risk with four elements: a specific statement of what's included, one sentence on who gets the most out of it, a note on how fast they'll see a result, and one social proof signal.

**Slide 4:** Example: "The AI Copywriting Playbook. 4-part prompt framework, 30 prompts, email and landing page templates, and the human editing layer. Best for solopreneurs and freelancers who publish regularly. Most users finish their first draft in one afternoon. 800+ copies sold."

**Slide 5:** The CTA button text is the last thing they read before clicking. "Buy Now" is the weakest option. "Get the Playbook" is better. "Write Faster Starting Today" is best. The button text should name what they're getting, not the transaction they're making.

**Slide 6:** Test your CTA button text. Change it to a transformation, not a command. Track what happens to your click-through rate. Follow @behikeai for more.

**Caption:**
Here's a reframe that changed how I write pricing sections: the reader is not deciding whether the price is acceptable. They are deciding whether the risk is acceptable.

Price and risk are not the same thing. A $200 product with clear deliverables, clear results, and social proof feels like low risk. A $10 product with vague promises and no evidence feels like high risk. The $10 product will convert worse.

Risk is the variable. Your job is to eliminate it in the pricing section.

You do this by being specific about what they get, being honest about who it's best for, giving them a realistic timeframe for seeing a result, and providing at least one signal that other people already trusted this enough to buy.

Those four elements, done well, turn the pricing section from a hesitation point into a confirmation point. The reader has already said yes in their head. The pricing section is just the last check. Make it feel like a formality, not a barrier.

And change your button text. "Buy Now" is for Amazon. You're building a relationship. Name what they're getting.
