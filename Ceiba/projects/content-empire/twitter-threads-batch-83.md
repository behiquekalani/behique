## Thread 83.1 — Why $27 Outsells $25

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
$27 outsells $25.

Not because of the number itself.

Because of what the number signals.

Here's the psychology of pricing that most solopreneurs never learn:

---

**Tweet 2:**
$25 feels like a guess.

$27 feels like a calculation.

When a price ends in an odd, specific number, buyers subconsciously believe it was derived from actual cost analysis.

It signals thought. Thought signals legitimacy.

---

**Tweet 3:**
Round numbers feel arbitrary.

$20, $50, $100 say: "I picked something that sounds nice."

Specific numbers feel deliberate.

$23, $47, $97 say: "This is what it costs to deliver this value."

One price makes buyers comfortable. The other makes them question your authority.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The same logic applies at higher price points.

$497 outsells $500 for software and courses.

$1,997 outsells $2,000 for coaching and services.

The gap in perceived value is larger than the $3 or $3 actual difference.

---

**Tweet 5:**
The anchor price matters more than the final price.

If you show a $97 price crossed out next to your $27 price, buyers evaluate $27 against $97, not against zero.

The reference point is the anchor. Never present a price without an anchor.

---

**Tweet 6:**
Pain of paying decreases with specificity.

$27 feels like it was earned. $25 feels like it was rounded.

When buyers feel a price was calculated carefully, they feel they're getting a fair deal even if the price is objectively the same.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The practical rule: avoid round numbers below $200.

At $500+, round numbers signal premium positioning and simplicity.

Below $200, specific numbers signal value and thoughtfulness.

Know which game you're playing before you set a price.

---

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
Pricing is not math. It's psychology.

The same product at $25 vs $27 can have meaningfully different conversion rates.

## Thread 83.2 — The Three Tiers That Print Revenue

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
Selling one price point is leaving money on the table.

Your audience has three types of buyers.

Here's how to capture all three with a tiered pricing structure:

---

**Tweet 2:**
The three tiers:

Entry: $7-$27. Removes the financial barrier. Builds buyer trust.
Core: $47-$97. Your main offer. Where most revenue lives.
Premium: $197-$497. For buyers who want more access, more support, or faster results.

One product. Three price points. Three different buyers served.

---

**Tweet 3:**
The entry tier is not a loss leader.

It is a trust accelerator.

Someone who buys a $17 product from you is 5x more likely to buy your $97 product than someone who never bought anything.

The entry tier filters serious people from window shoppers.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The core tier is where most solopreneurs stop.

This is a mistake.

10-20% of your core buyers would have paid 3-5x more for additional access, community, or done-with-you support.

If you don't offer a premium tier, those buyers just leave money unspent.

---

**Tweet 5:**
The premium tier is not about price.

It's about outcome proximity.

Entry: information only.
Core: information plus structure.
Premium: information plus structure plus access to you.

The more directly the buyer gets access to outcomes, the more the tier should cost.

---

**Tweet 6:**
The middle option wins by default.

In a three-tier pricing structure, the middle option gets the most sales.

Not because it's the best deal. Because it is the psychologically "safe" choice.

Buyers avoid extremes. Design your core offer to be the obvious middle.

---

**Tweet 7:**
Build your tiers in this order:

1. Define the premium outcome first.
2. Strip it down for the core offer.
3. Strip it further for the entry offer.

Working top-down ensures every tier is a real step down, not just an arbitrary price change.

---

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
Three tiers. Three buyers. One product ecosystem.

Most solopreneurs build one and wonder why revenue plateaus.

Build the structure. Let the buyers self-select.

## Thread 83.3 — Price Anchoring Done Right

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
Every product needs an anchor price.

Without one, buyers evaluate your price against their own reference points.

That's a negotiation you will always lose.

Here's how price anchoring works:

---

**Tweet 2:**
The anchor is the first number a buyer sees.

It sets the reference frame for everything that follows.

If your anchor is $97 and your offer is $27, $27 feels cheap.

If your anchor is $19 and your offer is $27, $27 feels expensive.

---

**Tweet 3:**
Three ways to anchor your price:

1. Show the "original" price crossed out.
2. Compare to an alternative cost ("hiring a consultant costs $500/hour").
3. Break down the component value ("this includes X worth $47, Y worth $27, Z worth $37").

All three make the buyer evaluate your price against a higher reference.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The comparison anchor is the most underused.

"A 1-hour coaching call on this topic costs $200. This guide gives you the same framework for $27."

Buyers now evaluate $27 against $200, not against other $27 products.

The category comparison matters as much as the number.

---

**Tweet 5:**
The component breakdown anchor works for bundles.

List every item with its standalone value.

Total standalone value: $147. Your price today: $37.

The math doesn't need to be perfect. The perception of value stacked is what drives the conversion.

---

**Tweet 6:**
What not to do: anchor too high.

If your anchor is $500 and your price is $27, buyers become suspicious.

The gap needs to feel plausible.

A 3x-5x anchor is believable. A 20x anchor triggers skepticism.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The best anchor is a competitor or alternative that buyers already know.

"Courses on this topic sell for $300-$500. This guide gets you the core framework for $37."

That anchor exists in the buyer's real world. It's more credible than made-up original prices.

---

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
Never present a price in isolation.

Every price needs a reference point.

Give buyers the right reference and you control how they evaluate value.

## Thread 83.4 — Why Charging More Makes Products Sell Better

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
Lowering your price is usually the wrong move.

Here's why charging more often increases sales, not decreases them:

---

**Tweet 2:**
Price is a quality signal.

Buyers cannot see inside your product before they buy.

So they use price as a proxy for quality.

A $7 product and a $97 product on the same topic signal completely different quality levels, regardless of actual content.

---

**Tweet 3:**
Low prices attract the wrong buyers.

The buyer at $7 expects a quick fix.

The buyer at $97 shows up ready to do the work.

Higher price filters for commitment. Committed buyers get better results. Better results generate better reviews and referrals.

---

**Tweet 4:**
Price and support expectation are linked.

A $7 buyer will email you at 2am expecting instant support.

A $97 buyer expects a structured experience.

Your price sets the expectation. Low prices create high-maintenance customers.

---

**Tweet 5:**
The math is also better.

To make $1,000 at $7: 143 buyers.
To make $1,000 at $47: 22 buyers.
To make $1,000 at $97: 11 buyers.

Serving 11 buyers well is a better business than managing 143.

Revenue scales slower at low prices. Problems scale faster.

---

**Tweet 6:**
The sweet spot for most digital products: $27-$97.

Below $27: friction is low but so is commitment. Works for lead-generation products.
$27-$97: high commitment, reasonable barrier, scalable without heavy support.
$97+: requires stronger positioning but attracts the best buyers.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The fear of charging more is a story you tell yourself.

Most solopreneurs undercharge because they secretly believe their work isn't worth the price.

The solution is not to lower the price. It's to build conviction in the value, then let the market confirm it.

---

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
Raise the price before you lower it.

Test $47 before you drop to $25.

You might be surprised.

## Thread 83.5 — The Urgency and Scarcity Pricing Playbook

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
Urgency is the most powerful pricing lever most solopreneurs ignore.

Not fake urgency. Real deadline-based offers that force a decision.

Here's how it works:

---

**Tweet 2:**
The human default is to delay.

"I'll buy it later" is the most common reason products don't sell.

Urgency removes "later" as an option.

A real deadline forces a real decision.

---

**Tweet 3:**
Types of urgency that work:

1. Launch window pricing (price goes up after X days).
2. Cohort-based access (this round closes Friday).
3. Bonus removal (bonuses disappear at midnight).
4. Quantity cap (only 20 copies at this price).

All four are legitimate. All four require follow-through. If you say the price goes up, it goes up.

---

**Tweet 4:**
Types of urgency that don't work:

Fake countdown timers that reset when you reload the page.

Buyers have seen this. They test it. When they catch you, you lose the sale and the trust permanently.

Fake urgency is a short-term tactic with long-term damage.

---

**Tweet 5:**
The launch window is the most repeatable.

Announce a launch price. Set a deadline. Raise the price.

The second time you run this, buyers who missed the first window will move faster.

Consistency makes urgency credible over time.

---

**Tweet 6:**
Scarcity is different from urgency.

Urgency is time-limited.
Scarcity is quantity-limited.

"Only 20 copies at this price" works because buyers don't know which number you're currently on.

Use scarcity for cohort access, live workshops, or done-with-you programs where capacity is genuinely limited.

---

**Tweet 7:**
Combine them sparingly.

"This cohort opens Friday and closes Sunday at midnight, and I'm only accepting 15 people."

Both time and quantity limits. Both real. Both communicated clearly.

This structure converts better than either alone.

---

**Tweet 8 (CTA):**
Build urgency into your pricing strategy.

Not as a trick. As a structural feature of how you launch.
