## Script 68.1 — Building in Public: What to Share and What to Keep Private

**Hook (0-3s)**
"Building in public is the best marketing strategy most people execute wrong."

**Body (3-50s)**
There's a version of building in public that grows your audience and a version that wastes your energy. Know the difference.

Point 1: Share the process, the numbers, and the lessons. People follow along because they want a map. Revenue milestones, experiments that failed, tools you tried, decisions you made. Real data with honest context is more valuable than any how-to post.

Point 2: Keep your strategy, your unreleased products, and your half-formed ideas private until they're ready. Sharing incomplete ideas invites noise before you have clarity. Finish the thinking, then share the result.

Point 3: Share the struggle without oversharing the crisis. "Here's what I'm working through and what I'm trying" is useful content. "Here's my entire mental breakdown" is a different category of content and requires a different platform and a different audience relationship.

Point 4: The rule is simple. Share what you already understand. Keep private what you're still figuring out. Transparency is a feature, not an obligation.

Building in public works because it turns your work into an audience asset. Know what you're building before you build it in public.

**Caption**
Building in public isn't about sharing everything. It's about sharing the right things at the right time. Process yes, panic no.

## Script 68.2 — Lead Magnets That Actually Work

**Hook (0-3s)**
"Your lead magnet isn't failing because of design. It's failing because of specificity."

**Body (3-50s)**
Most lead magnets are too broad to convert anyone.

Point 1: The best lead magnets solve one very specific problem for one very specific person. Not "10 tips for productivity." More like "the 20-minute morning routine that eliminates decision fatigue before 9am." Specific beats comprehensive every time.

Point 2: The format matters less than people think. PDF, template, checklist, email course, short video. What matters is that the person gets a result immediately. Lead magnets that deliver instant value convert better and produce better email subscribers.

Point 3: The lead magnet should preview the paid product. The free thing solves problem A. The paid thing solves problems B, C, and D that naturally follow A. The subscriber who used your lead magnet is already pre-qualified for your offer.

Point 4: Test the headline before you build the resource. Create a landing page, drive traffic to it, measure the opt-in rate. If it converts well, build the actual content. If it doesn't, you changed one sentence, not 40 pages.

The best lead magnet is the most specific thing that solves the most urgent problem your ideal customer has right now.

**Caption**
A lead magnet that tries to help everyone helps no one. Go specific. Solve one problem completely. Build trust before you build the offer.

## Script 68.3 — Twitter Growth in 2026

**Hook (0-3s)**
"Twitter is still the best platform for building a following if you have ideas."

**Body (3-50s)**
The strategy hasn't changed as much as people claim.

Point 1: Twitter rewards consistency and specificity. If you tweet about everything, you attract no one. Pick a lane. Niche accounts grow faster than general ones because the algorithm can categorize you and the right people can find you.

Point 2: The best format in 2026 is the thread. Three to eight tweets that take someone from a question to an answer. Threads get shared, saved, and recommended. Single tweets get reactions. Know the difference in what you're optimizing for.

Point 3: Reply more than you post. The fastest growth on Twitter has always come from being visible in conversations where your target audience is already talking. Thoughtful replies that add real value bring followers back to your profile without any extra production effort.

Point 4: Post your best content at least twice. Twitter has a short half-life. A thread that performed well in March can be reposted in September to a different segment of your audience with the same engagement. Reuse the content that worked.

Twitter still builds authority faster than almost any other platform. The entry cost is writing well and showing up consistently.

**Caption**
Twitter growth in 2026 is still about ideas, specificity, and consistency. The algorithm is smarter now. So the bar for clarity is higher.

## Script 68.4 — The $100/Day Product Math

**Hook (0-3s)**
"$100 a day is $36,500 a year. The math to get there is simpler than you think."

**Body (3-50s)**
Let's work backward from the number.

Point 1: At $27 per product, you need 4 sales per day to hit $100. At $47, you need 3. At $97, you need 2. At $197, you need 1. The higher your price, the fewer transactions you need. This changes what your content and distribution strategy should look like.

Point 2: 4 sales per day from a $27 product with a 3% conversion rate means you need 133 people seeing your offer daily. That's not a big audience. It's a focused funnel that sends the right traffic to the right page consistently.

Point 3: The math breaks when the offer is vague or the traffic is untargeted. A specific offer to a specific person converts at 5 to 10 percent. A generic offer to a general audience converts at under 1 percent. You can't traffic your way out of a bad offer.

Point 4: The path to $100 per day isn't one product. It's building the funnel correctly with one product, then adding the second product that your existing buyers are already asking for. Repeat. The math compounds.

$100 per day is not a big business goal. It's a proof of concept that then becomes $1,000 per day with the same framework.

## Script 68.5 — Creator Burnout Prevention

**Hook (0-3s)**
"Most creator burnout is not from working too much. It's from working without a system."

**Body (3-50s)**
Burnout is a systems problem before it's a mental health problem.

Point 1: Creating without a content library means every post requires creative effort from scratch. That's exhausting and unsustainable. Build a swipe file of ideas, frameworks, and past content. The library reduces the energy cost of each new piece by giving you a starting point.

Point 2: Tying your identity to metrics is the fastest path to burnout. Views, followers, and revenue are lagging indicators. They measure past work. If your self-worth fluctuates with the dashboard, you'll burn out regardless of how well things are going.

Point 3: Batch your production. One day of writing, editing, and scheduling per week is less taxing than trying to create something new every day. The creative brain needs time to fill back up. Batching creates that space.

Point 4: Know what you're building toward. Creators who burn out often can't articulate why they're building. The content becomes a performance without a purpose. The purpose is what sustains you through the months where nothing seems to be working.

Sustainability is the strategy. You can't build a long-term business on a short-term pace.

**Caption**
Creator burnout is preventable. It requires a system, not more discipline. Build the library, batch the work, remember why you started.
