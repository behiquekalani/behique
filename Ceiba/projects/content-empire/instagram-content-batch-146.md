## Post 1 — You Already Have the Product

**Hook:** Your knowledge is not worthless. It is just unpackaged.

**Slide 1:** Your knowledge is not worthless. It is just unpackaged.

**Slide 2:** Most people separate "what I know" from "what I sell." That separation is the problem.

**Slide 3:** The things you learned by doing are the most valuable. Not the things you learned from a course.

**Slide 4:** Packaging looks like this: a PDF, a template, a checklist, a short guide. Not a book. Not a course. Not a masterclass.

**Slide 5:** Start with what took you the most time to figure out. That is the product. Someone else is three months behind you, looking for exactly that.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. Build what you know.

**Caption:**
Every person reading this has spent years building skills, solving problems, and figuring things out the hard way. That collection of hard-won knowledge is not a hobby. It is inventory.

The gap between knowing something and selling that knowledge is smaller than most people think. A five-page PDF that answers one specific question can sell for $9.99. A ten-slide template that saves someone four hours of work can sell for $19. You do not need a course platform, a studio, or a team.

What you need is to stop treating your knowledge as background and start treating it as the thing. Most people build products around something external. The builders who move fast build around something they actually lived through.

Your lived experience is not generic. Generic is everywhere. Specific is rare.

Package the thing you already know. Price it honestly. Ship it.

## Post 2 — The Monetization Order Nobody Talks About

**Hook:** Sequence matters more than strategy.

**Slide 1:** Sequence matters more than strategy.

**Slide 2:** Most people try to monetize in the wrong order. They build the big thing first. Then they wonder why nobody buys.

**Slide 3:** The right order: Audience first. Trust second. Offer third. Scale fourth.

**Slide 4:** Audience does not mean followers. It means people who read your words because they want to, not because an algorithm pushed it.

**Slide 5:** Trust means they have seen you be right about something small before they commit to something big.

**Slide 6:** Offer means one thing, priced clearly, solving one problem. Not a suite of products. One.

**Slide 7:** @behikeai. Sequence first, then speed.

**Caption:**
The most common reason a creator's first product does not sell is not the product. It is the order of operations. They built the product before they built trust. They built trust before they had an audience. They had an audience before they knew what that audience actually needed.

The sequence is the strategy. Not the other way around.

Start by saying true things consistently for 60 days. On one platform. About one topic. Watch who responds. Those are your buyers. Then ask them what problem they are stuck on. Then build the smallest possible thing that solves that problem. Then price it at what an hour of your time costs.

That is the whole model. It does not require a funnel. It does not require a team. It requires patience with the sequence.

## Post 3 — The $9 to $99 Ladder

**Hook:** Nobody buys $500 from a stranger.

**Slide 1:** Nobody buys $500 from a stranger.

**Slide 2:** Every purchase is a trust test. The lower the price, the lower the trust required.

**Slide 3:** The ladder works like this:
- Free: lead magnet, newsletter
- $9-$19: PDF, template, checklist
- $29-$49: guide, mini-course, system
- $97-$199: deep-dive course, toolkit
- $500+: service, coaching, done-for-you

**Slide 4:** Most creators skip the bottom of the ladder. They launch $197 before anyone has bought $9 from them. It does not work.

**Slide 5:** The cheap product is not a sacrifice. It is a proof of value. People who buy your $9 guide buy your $97 course at 8x the rate of cold traffic.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. Build the ladder, not just the top.

**Caption:**
Price is not just a number. It is a signal of how much trust the transaction requires.

A $9 PDF requires almost no trust. The buyer thinks: if it is bad, I wasted less than lunch. A $97 course requires 10x the trust. The buyer thinks: this needs to actually work or I feel burned.

The mistake creators make is launching at $97 before anyone has bought at $9. No trust has been established. No proof of value exists. The conversion rate reflects that.

Build the bottom of the ladder first. A checklist. A short guide. A template. Something that delivers a win quickly and costs less than two coffees. Let people experience what it feels like to buy from you and get something real back.

The ladder fills from the bottom up. The $500 offer closes because twelve people already bought the $9 thing.

## Post 4 — The Fastest Path to First Dollar Online

**Hook:** First dollar matters more than first thousand.

**Slide 1:** First dollar matters more than first thousand.

**Slide 2:** Most people overthink the first sale. The product is not perfect. The page is not designed. The audience is not big enough.

**Slide 3:** The fastest path to first dollar: solve one specific problem, write it up honestly, price it at $9, tell twenty people about it directly.

**Slide 4:** Not via a post. Via a message. "I built this for people stuck on X. You mentioned X last month. Here's the link."

**Slide 5:** The first sale is not about scale. It is about proof that someone will pay for what you built.

**Slide 6:** After the first sale, everything changes. Now you know it is real.

**Slide 7:** @behikeai. First sale first.

**Caption:**
The first dollar online is not about money. It is about evidence.

Evidence that the thing you built has value to someone outside your own head. Evidence that you can package knowledge into something sellable. Evidence that the model works.

Most people wait until conditions are perfect before trying to sell. The audience is bigger. The product is more polished. The branding is cleaner. That wait costs six months and still does not guarantee the first sale.

The move is different. Pick one thing you know well. Write it up in 5-10 pages, specific and direct. Price it at $9. Then message twenty people who you know are stuck on that problem. Not a post. A direct message. That is the fastest path from zero to first sale.

The first sale is proof. Once you have proof, you build on it.

## Post 5 — The Compound Effect of Content Plus Products

**Hook:** Content markets. Products convert. Both need to exist.

**Slide 1:** Content markets. Products convert. Both need to exist.

**Slide 2:** Content without a product is entertainment. A product without content is invisible. The pairing is where income compounds.

**Slide 3:** The system: post content on one topic consistently. Link to one product consistently. Let search and algorithm send traffic. Let the product capture it.

**Slide 4:** This does not require a big audience. A post that reaches 500 people with high relevance beats a post that reaches 50,000 people with low relevance.

**Slide 5:** The creator who pairs 90 days of honest content with one well-built $19 product is ahead of most people running paid ads.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. The pairing is the strategy.

**Caption:**
Two things compound over time in this model: the reach of content and the trust embedded in a back catalog of products people keep buying.

Content does the discovery work. Someone sees a post, reads a caption, decides this person understands their problem. Products do the revenue work. Someone who already trusts you pays to get more of your thinking in a format they can use.

Neither works well alone. A content creator with no product has an audience with nowhere to go. A seller with no content has a product with no trust behind it.

The pairing looks like this: pick one problem your target person has. Post about that problem consistently, from five different angles, over ninety days. Have one product that solves that problem sitting at the end of every content piece. That is a business model.

It is not complex. It is just consistent.
