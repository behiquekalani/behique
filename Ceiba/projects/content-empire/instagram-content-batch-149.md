## Post 1 — Followers Are Not the Business

**Hook:** Followers do not pay bills. Buyers do.

**Slide 1:** Followers do not pay bills. Buyers do.

**Slide 2:** In 2026, you can have 50,000 followers and make zero money. You can have 800 subscribers on an email list and make $3,000 a month.

**Slide 3:** The metric that matters is not reach. It is trust density. How much does the person reading this trust that you understand their problem?

**Slide 4:** Trust density is built by being specific, consistent, and honest over time. Not by going viral once.

**Slide 5:** A small audience with high trust density outperforms a large audience with low trust density in conversion, retention, and word of mouth. Every time.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. Build trust, not just reach.

**Caption:**
The audience-building conversation in 2026 is still mostly about follower counts. It should be about something else.

The question is not how many people follow you. The question is how many people would actually buy something you built, based on trust alone, without needing a sale or a discount.

That number is usually much smaller than the follower count. And that number is the real business.

A creator with 900 email subscribers who open every issue, respond to questions, and buy when something launches has a stronger business than a creator with 40,000 followers who barely read the captions.

Focus on the quality of the relationship, not the size of the list. Specificity builds quality. General content about general topics builds size. Size without quality is just noise.

## Post 2 — The Algorithm Is Not Your Enemy. Consistency Is Your Edge.

**Hook:** Every creator complains about the algorithm. Few understand it.

**Slide 1:** Every creator complains about the algorithm. Few understand it.

**Slide 2:** Algorithms in 2026 do one thing: show content that keeps people on the platform. That is the whole job.

**Slide 3:** Content that teaches, entertains, or tells a true story in a specific voice tends to keep people on the platform. Content that could have been written by anyone tends not to.

**Slide 4:** The algorithm is not biased against you. It is biased against generic. If your content is specific and honest, algorithms work in your favor over time.

**Slide 5:** Consistency compounds. The algorithm learns your audience over time. Six months of weekly posting gives the algorithm enough data to find the right people for your content.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. Be specific. Be consistent. The algorithm does the rest.

**Caption:**
Most creators treat the algorithm as an adversary. Something to outsmart, hack, or work around. That framing leads to chasing formats, sounds, and trends that have nothing to do with what you are actually building.

The more accurate framing is that the algorithm is a distribution tool. It distributes content that works. Your job is to make content that works for your specific audience.

What works is not a formula. It is specificity plus consistency over time. Specific enough that the right people feel seen. Consistent enough that the algorithm has enough data to find them.

One year of posting about one topic, from one specific perspective, on one platform is enough to build something real. Most creators do not make it that far because they change topics, platforms, and formats before the compound effect has time to show up.

Stay the course. The algorithm rewards patience more than it rewards tactics.

## Post 3 — The Niche Is Not a Prison

**Hook:** A specific niche is not a limit. It is a signal.

**Slide 1:** A specific niche is not a limit. It is a signal.

**Slide 2:** The creator who talks about everything talks to no one. The algorithm does not know who to show it to. The reader does not know whether to trust it.

**Slide 3:** Specificity says: this is for you, specifically, not for everyone generally. That signal attracts the right people and repels the wrong ones.

**Slide 4:** The niche expands naturally as the audience grows. What starts as "AI tools for solopreneurs" becomes "building a one-person business with AI" becomes "the future of independent work."

**Slide 5:** Start specific. Expand from a position of trust, not from a position of trying to include everyone.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. Start narrow. Build wide.

**Caption:**
One of the most common pieces of creator advice is "niche down." Most people who hear it resist it because it sounds like limiting what they can talk about.

The resistance comes from misunderstanding what a niche does. A niche is not a content restriction. It is a trust accelerator.

When someone arrives at your page and sees that everything you post is about one specific thing they care about, they follow faster, engage more, and buy sooner. You have immediately communicated: this person understands my problem.

That signal is what builds an audience in 2026. Not volume of content. Not frequency of posting. Specificity of positioning.

Pick the one thing you understand better than most. Build from there. The audience that finds you because of that specific thing will stay, engage, and buy. The broad audience that follows you for everything tends to do none of those things.

## Post 4 — Building in Public Changes Everything

**Hook:** Sharing the process pulls the right people toward you.

**Slide 1:** Sharing the process pulls the right people toward you.

**Slide 2:** Building in public means sharing what you are making, what is working, what is not, and what you are learning. Not the polished result. The actual process.

**Slide 3:** Most creators only share wins. Building in public creators share the full picture. That honesty creates trust faster than any amount of polished content.

**Slide 4:** The audience you attract when you build in public is different. They are invested in the outcome. They follow because they are watching something real get built.

**Slide 5:** They also buy differently. An audience that watched you build something has a stake in its success. Conversion rates reflect that.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. Share the build, not just the result.

**Caption:**
There are two ways to attract an audience. You can show up as the person who already has the result, positioning yourself as someone to learn from. Or you can show up as the person building the result in real time, inviting people to watch and follow along.

Both work. But building in public creates a different kind of audience.

When people follow the process, they are not just consuming content. They are invested. They care whether you ship. They follow up. They send messages. They buy when you launch because they feel like they were part of building the thing.

This is not a content strategy. It is an honesty strategy. Share the real numbers, the actual failures, the problems you are solving live. The audience you attract through that honesty is the audience that actually supports the business.

The polished highlight reel is everywhere. The real build is rare.

## Post 5 — The Long Game Is Not Slow

**Hook:** Consistency for 12 months moves faster than tactics for 12 months.

**Slide 1:** Consistency for 12 months moves faster than tactics for 12 months.

**Slide 2:** In month one, consistent posting looks identical to sporadic posting in terms of results. The algorithm has no data. The audience is zero. The difference is invisible.

**Slide 3:** By month six, the gap is measurable. The consistent creator has a library, an algorithm that knows their audience, and a pattern of trust with regular readers.

**Slide 4:** By month twelve, the gap is dramatic. The consistent creator has compounding search traffic, a warmed audience, and enough back catalog that new visitors can go deep.

**Slide 5:** The long game is not slow. It is front-loaded. The return on each post increases as the archive grows. By month twelve, post 300 benefits from posts 1 through 299.

**Slide 6:** @behikeai. The return on consistency compounds. Start now, not later.

**Caption:**
Audience building looks slow at the start because the compounding has not kicked in yet.

Month one of consistent posting feels like shouting into empty space. Month three feels slightly less empty. Month six, you start seeing familiar names in the comments. Month twelve, posts start reaching people through search and recommendation who have never encountered you before.

That is not slow. That is compound interest on attention. The early posts have been building context. The algorithm has been learning. The audience has been developing a relationship with your voice.

The creators who quit before month six never see this. They see the early silence and conclude the strategy does not work. The strategy works. It just works on a longer time horizon than most people give it.

Build consistently. Measure at six months, not six days.
