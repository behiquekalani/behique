## THREAD 1

**Hook:** The Overnight Machine: how I find 5 eBay products every morning without touching my computer

---

1/ Every morning I wake up to a scored list of eBay products I should consider listing. I did not research them. My pipeline did. Here is how the Overnight Machine works.

2/ The problem it solves: eBay reselling requires product research. The manual version is time-consuming and repetitive. Repetitive work that follows consistent rules is exactly what you automate.

3/ The pipeline runs on a schedule. It pulls sold listing data for monitored categories and keywords. It checks those prices against supplier sources. It calculates estimated margins.

4/ Each product gets scored on three factors: sell-through rate, margin percentage, and competition density. The score filters out the noise. Only items above the threshold make the list.

5/ By the time I am awake, hundreds of potential products have been processed and filtered down to the five to ten that are worth a second look. I did not touch the computer. The machine ran overnight.

6/ My job in the morning is not research. It is a decision from a prepared list. I check supplier stock on the top items. I decide which ones to list. Total time: under 20 minutes.

7/ The pipeline does not guarantee profit on every item. It filters for items that have historically sold at acceptable margins. Some work. Some do not. The signal quality is higher than what I find in 20 minutes of manual browsing.

8/ Over time, the categories I monitor get refined based on actual outcomes. Items that sold well get their category weighted higher. Items that underperformed get deprioritized. The machine learns from the data.

9/ The core components: a way to pull sold listing data, supplier price checking, margin calculation, and a scheduler. The scheduler is the part most people forget. The value is in running it overnight automatically.

10/ Start with a manual version to confirm the logic works. Build the automated version once the manual version produces useful output. The mechanical parts automate cleanly. The final decision stays human.

## THREAD 2

**Hook:** Digital product pricing: the anchoring framework that doubled my average order value

---

1/ Most people price digital products by asking: how long did this take me to make? That is the wrong question. Here is the anchoring framework I use instead.

2/ Nobody pays for your hours. They pay for their outcome. The first question is always: what is the buyer trying to achieve, and what is that outcome worth to them?

3/ The second question: what are the alternatives? Not just competing products. The full alternative set. Hiring someone to do it. Learning it themselves. Not solving the problem at all. Each alternative has a cost.

4/ Your price anchors to the alternative. It does not have to be cheaper. It has to be clearly better value than the alternative when the buyer considers the comparison.

5/ Example: a $14.99 template. The buyer's alternative is spending four hours researching the same thing. If they value their time at $15/hour, four hours is worth $60 to them. You are not competing with free. You are competing with $60 of their time.

6/ The second anchoring mechanism is what sits next to your price. A $14.99 product next to a $29.99 bundle looks cheap. Not because the number changed. Because the context changed. Price psychology runs on relative perception.

7/ Underpricing does not make you more accessible. It signals the product is not worth much. Buyers calibrate quality to price faster than they calibrate price to quality.

8/ Do not price based on what feels humble. Price based on value delivered, anchored to alternatives, and positioned against the right reference point.

9/ The first price is a test. It will change based on what the data shows about conversion and buyer satisfaction. Do not treat it as permanent. Treat it as version one.

10/ The framework in one sentence: start with the outcome value, anchor to alternatives, use tier context to shift perception, and treat the first price as a hypothesis.

## THREAD 3

**Hook:** Study Buddy: I built a Pomodoro app specifically for ADHD brains. Here's why it's different.

---

1/ I built Study Buddy, a Pomodoro app specifically for ADHD brains. Most focus apps are built for neurotypical users and add an ADHD label. This one was designed differently from the first decision.

2/ The core design difference: standard Pomodoro assumes you can start when the timer starts. ADHD brains cannot always do that. Study Buddy has an initiation mode. You declare what you are working on before the timer starts. The declaration creates the initiation cue.

3/ The session setup is intentional friction, not simplification. You name the task. You set the difficulty level. You choose your mode: high focus, creative, or review. The setup takes 60 seconds and it does cognitive work that the standard timer skips.

4/ State-matched music is built in. High focus mode plays mid-tempo instrumental with rhythm. Creative mode plays ambient with minimal structure. Review mode plays low-tempo background. The music is not decoration. It is an arousal regulation tool.

5/ The break protocol is different. Standard Pomodoro gives you 5 minutes. Study Buddy's breaks are structured: physical prompt first, then re-declaration of the next task before the break ends. The transition back is built into the break.

6/ Distraction logging is integrated. When your attention pulls elsewhere, you do not fight it. You log it in one tap and return. The log becomes data about your most common distraction patterns. Patterns can be addressed. Invisible impulses cannot.

7/ The streak mechanic is modified. Standard habit trackers punish broken streaks, which creates a second failure event that compounds with the first. Study Buddy resets within the session, not the day. A distraction does not break the streak. It becomes a data point.

8/ The session summary shows you actual focus time versus declared time. Not to judge. To give you real data about your attention patterns so you can design your environment better over time.

9/ ADHD brains are not broken. They need different inputs to produce consistent output. The tools built for neurotypical users create friction where ADHD brains need support and support where neurotypical users need none.

10/ Study Buddy is available at behike.shop. If you have ADHD and standard Pomodoro apps have never stuck, try something built for how your brain actually works.

## THREAD 4

**Hook:** The Public Build: why documenting everything is the best content strategy

---

1/ The best content strategy I have found is to document what you are building in public. Not because it is efficient. Because it is true. Here is why it works.

2/ The problem with most content strategies is that they require you to produce content separate from your work. You work, then you create content about the work. Two jobs instead of one.

3/ Public building collapses that. Every decision you make, every problem you solve, every thing you learn is content. The documentation is the content. One job.

4/ The documentation compounds. A tweet about a problem you solved today gets indexed. Six months from now, someone searching for that problem finds it. You did not do additional work for that reach. The archive did it.

5/ It builds trust faster than polished content. People trust someone they have watched work more than someone who shows up with finished results. The process is the proof. Showing the process is showing the work.

6/ It keeps you accountable. When you say publicly that you are building something, you have a reason to finish it. Not just personal motivation. A record that you said it.

7/ The content does not have to be perfect. It has to be true. A thread about what went wrong this week, why you changed direction, what you learned from something that did not work. That is more valuable to your audience than a polished success story.

8/ Over time, the body of documentation becomes a case study of your entire operation. Every new person who finds you can scroll back and see how you built. That is a different kind of credibility than a highlight reel.

9/ The specific formats that work for public building: threads about decisions and why you made them, posts about things you learned the hard way, honest numbers when you have them, and questions you are working through in real time.

10/ The summary: your work already generates the content. You just have to capture it. Stop producing content about building. Document the build.

## THREAD 5

**Hook:** Puerto Rico + internet business: the case for building from the island

---

1/ I build an internet business from Puerto Rico. People act like this is unusual. It is not. Here is the case for why the island is an underrated place to build from.

2/ The timezone works. Puerto Rico runs on Atlantic Standard Time year-round, no daylight saving. That puts me 1 hour ahead of Eastern, 4 hours ahead of Pacific, and in decent overlap with Europe. Async-friendly.

3/ The cost of living is lower than the mainland US in most categories that matter for a solo builder. Housing, food, basics. The cost of a productive month here is less than the cost of a productive month in most major US cities.

4/ The Spanish is native and the English is fluent. That bilingual combination is a distribution advantage. Two markets, two languages, one creator. The incremental cost of the second market is close to zero when you are already native to both languages.

5/ The culture has a relationship to resilience that shapes how I build. Puerto Rico has been through hurricanes, economic crises, federal policy that was not in our favor. You learn to build things that work when the infrastructure is unreliable. That is good training for any builder.

6/ The diaspora is a network. Puerto Rican communities in the US, Latin America, and Europe are significant. Building with a recognizable cultural identity creates an in-group signal that travels across those communities.

7/ The scenery is the office. I can build from the beach if I want. That is not a small thing for mental health and creative output. Environment affects cognitive state. I can control my environment.

8/ The tax structure has specific advantages for certain business types. Act 60 in Puerto Rico was designed to attract knowledge workers and digital businesses. It is worth understanding if you are building a digital product business here.

9/ The narrative of "you have to be in a major city to build something real" is false and getting more false every year. The infrastructure for remote internet business is complete. Location is a preference now, not a requirement.

10/ I build from Puerto Rico because this is home, this is my people, and the conditions here are genuinely good for the kind of work I do. The island does not limit the reach. The internet handles that.
