## THREAD 4: "The ADHD tax most budgeting apps ignore"
**Account:** @behikeai
**CTA products:** ADHD Finance App ($9.99), ADHD Budget Playbook ($9.99)

---

**Hook tweet:**
There is a tax that ADHD brains pay that no budgeting app accounts for.

It shows up in four specific ways. And the solutions have nothing to do with "try harder."

(thread)

**Tweet 2:**
Tax 1: Impulse purchases.

Not just "oh I bought coffee." Full category: Impulse buys mid-task, late-night Amazon spirals, buying things because the deal ends today.

ADHD brains have impaired response inhibition. The pause between "I want this" and "should I buy this" is shorter than in neurotypical brains. This is documented neuroscience, not a character flaw.

**Tweet 3:**
The solution is not "use willpower."

The solution is a cooling-off timer built into your buying process.

If it's under $50, wait 24 hours. If it's over $50, wait 72. Put the item in a note, not a cart. If you still want it after the timer, buy it.

The urge passes 80% of the time.

**Tweet 4:**
Tax 2: Forgotten subscriptions.

ADHD brains sign up for free trials and forget to cancel. They keep tools they haven't opened in 4 months. They have 3 overlapping services doing the same thing.

The average person with ADHD has $60-$120/month in forgotten subscriptions. I had $94 when I audited mine.

**Tweet 5:**
The solution is a quarterly subscription audit. Not monthly (too often), not yearly (too late).

Every 90 days: list every recurring charge, score each one (use it/doesn't justify the cost/forgot it existed), cancel everything in the third category.

One 30-minute session every quarter saves $500-$1,000/year.

**Tweet 6:**
Tax 3: Dopamine spending.

This one is harder to talk about because it sounds like a personal failing. It isn't.

Dopamine spending is buying things to feel something. Not because you need them. Not even because you want them. Because the purchase itself provides a temporary dopamine hit.

**Tweet 7:**
The fix: build cheaper dopamine triggers into your routine before you're bored enough to spend.

Physical movement. A creative task. A conversation. Things that provide the same neurochemical reward without the financial cost.

You can't remove the need. You can redirect it.

**Tweet 8:**
Tax 4: Decision paralysis on financial decisions.

ADHD + financial complexity = doing nothing. The decision feels too big, options feel overwhelming, so the money just sits or gets spent impulsively instead of intentionally.

**Tweet 9:**
The fix is reducing choices.

One savings account, not four. One investment account, not research paralysis. One budget category for "fun money" with a hard weekly cap.

Fewer decisions means fewer opportunities to freeze or overspend.

Both are $9.99 on Gumroad.

**CTA products:** Free lead magnets (5 AI Tools, AI Safety Checklist, Zero Dollar Stack)

**Hook tweet:**
My best marketing strategy is giving things away for free.

Not ads. Not cold outreach. Not influencer partnerships.

Free.

**Tweet 2:**
Most people think about free as a cost. "I'm giving away something I could charge for."

The reframe: free is not a cost. It's the cheapest distribution channel that exists.

A $0 product can spread further than any ad budget you have at $0 to $5K/month revenue.

**Tweet 3:**
Compare these two scenarios.

Scenario A: You spend $100 on ads. You get 100 cold visitors to a $9.99 product page. Industry average conversion is 1-3%. You make $10-$30.

**Tweet 4:**
The math only works if the free thing is actually good.

Not "good for free." Just good. Something people would share even if they had to pay for it.

Bad free content doesn't build trust. It signals that your paid content will also be bad.

**Tweet 5:**
What I give away:

"5 AI Tools That Replaced My Team" -- practical, specific, immediately usable.
"AI Safety Checklist" -- solves a real fear people have.
"Zero Dollar AI Stack" -- shows exactly how I run my setup for nothing per month.

All three solve a real problem. All three capture email.

**Tweet 6:**
The number that matters is not downloads. It's what percentage of free downloaders eventually buy something.

Warm audiences convert. Cold traffic does not.

100 people who trust you are worth more than 10,000 who don't know you exist.

When someone has to pay, they ask "is this worth it?" That question slows everything down.
