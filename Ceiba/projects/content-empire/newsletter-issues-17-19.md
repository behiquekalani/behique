# Issue 17: The First $997

**Subject:** The first $997 from AI services
**Preview:** What actually happened when I landed my first paying client, from the outreach message to the call to the deliverable.

---

Hey,

The first service sale is the hardest one. Not because of the work. Because you don't actually know what you're selling until someone pays for it.

Here's exactly how it happened.

---

I found the client through a local business Facebook group. Small restaurant owner complaining that he was spending Sundays manually answering the same 30 customer questions in his DMs. Menu, hours, reservations. The same things, every week, for years.

I sent him one message. This is close to the exact wording:

"I noticed you're handling a lot of customer questions manually. I build AI systems that handle that automatically. I could show you how it would work for your restaurant in 20 minutes, no obligation."

That was it. No portfolio. No website. No deck. He responded in four hours.

---

The audit call is where most people lose clients before they start.

The mistake is going in with a solution. He didn't need a solution yet. He needed to feel heard.

I asked three things: What takes the most time? What tasks feel like they shouldn't need you? What would you do with two extra hours on Sunday?

He talked for fifteen minutes. I took notes.

What he said he wanted: "Something like ChatGPT for my business."

What he actually needed: A way to handle after-hours DMs without hiring someone. That was the whole problem.

The gap between those two things is where the project scope lives.

---

I priced it at $997 for the build, $97/month to maintain. He said yes the same day.

The deliverable was a Telegram bot connected to a knowledge base I built from his FAQ document and menu PDF. Setup took a weekend. The first Sunday it ran without him, he sent me a voice message at 11pm saying it had answered 23 questions while he was at dinner.

That message was worth more than the invoice.

---

A few honest notes:

The outreach only worked because it was specific to his actual problem. Copy-paste the same message to 50 people and it reads like spam. Specific wins.

I underpriced it. For a real automation with maintenance, $997 is low. I charged that because I wasn't sure it would work. Now I know it does.

The client didn't care about AI. He cared about Sunday dinners with his family. That's what I was actually selling.

# Issue 18: What Clients Actually Need From AI

**Subject:** What clients actually want from AI
**Preview:** Business owners ask for ChatGPT. What they actually want is to stop doing a specific task. Here's how to find it.

---

Hey,

Every new client conversation starts the same way.

"I want AI in my business." "I want something like ChatGPT." "I've been hearing about automation and I think it could help us."

These are not requirements. They're directions. Your job is to turn the direction into a specific problem.

---

The gap is almost always this: business owners know AI exists, and they know it does things, but they haven't had time to figure out which things apply to them. They're not being vague to make your life hard. They're vague because they're busy.

A good needs assessment doesn't start with AI. It starts with time.

---

Three questions that reveal the real problem:

**One. "What task do you do every week that you wish someone else could handle?"**

This question bypasses the "I want AI" framing completely. It goes straight to the pain. The answer is almost always specific: replying to emails, writing product descriptions, processing invoices, updating spreadsheets. Real tasks with real time costs.

**Two. "If that task just disappeared, what would you do with the recovered time?"**

This question reveals what success looks like to them. Some clients say "I'd focus on sales." Some say "I'd actually leave work at a reasonable hour." The answer tells you how motivated they are to solve it, and what you're really delivering. Hint: you're not delivering automation. You're delivering the thing the recovered time buys.

**Three. "What's stopped you from solving this already?"**

This one surfaces the real blockers. Budget. Past bad experiences with tech. Fear of disruption. Lack of trust in AI outputs. If you skip this question, you'll build something they won't use because you never addressed what was actually in the way.

---

After those three questions, you should know:

- The specific task
- What success looks like to them personally
- What objections exist before you've even proposed anything

From there, you're not pitching AI. You're prescribing a solution to a named problem. That's a completely different conversation.

---

One thing worth naming directly: most small business owners don't want to understand how the AI works. They want to know it works. Your technical explanation is for your own confidence, not theirs. Keep the proposal simple. One problem, one solution, one price.

The audit call is also where you decide whether to take the project. Some problems aren't worth automating at the client's current scale. Be honest about that. Turning down a bad fit early is how you avoid bad projects and bad relationships.

# Issue 19: Products vs Services: The Real Math

**Subject:** Products vs services: the real math
**Preview:** Services pay now, products pay later. Here's why you need both, and the exact sequence to run them simultaneously.

---

Hey,

Most people treat this as a choice. Products or services. Passive income or client work. They pick one and wonder why it's not working fast enough.

The real answer is that they're solving different problems. The mistake is treating them as competition.

---

Here's the math as honestly as I can put it.

**Services:**

One client at $997 is $997 in your account within days of the conversation. No audience required. No product-market fit research. No waiting for a funnel to mature. You find the problem, you solve it, you get paid.

The limit is time. There are only so many projects you can run. A solo operator doing client AI builds can reasonably handle 3-5 concurrent clients, depending on complexity. That's a ceiling. The income is real, but it doesn't compound.

Services are how you fund everything else.

**Products:**

A $27 guide on Gumroad can sit there for 18 months and keep selling. One hour of traffic to the page every few weeks. That's the upside. Low variable cost. Zero client management. The math scales in your favor as your audience grows.

The catch: it takes time to build audience trust before products convert consistently. Month one with zero subscribers, a product launch makes $0. Month eighteen with 5,000 email subscribers, that same product might move 50 units in a week on nothing but an email.

Products don't pay now. They pay later, with interest.

---

The sequence that makes sense:

Start with services. Land the first client, then the second. Build a track record and some cash. Learn what problems are consistent across multiple businesses.

The consistent problems become your products.

If you audit ten small businesses and eight of them have the same gap, that gap is a product. You've already built the solution once as a service. Package it, document it, price it at a fraction of the service rate, and let it work while you're taking on the next client.

This is not theory. The AI Agent Installer service exists because I've built the same setup multiple times. The documentation from those builds became the product foundation.

---

The math at a realistic target:

Two service clients at $500/month maintenance each: $1,000/month, predictable.

Two hundred product sales at $27 over three months: $5,400, front-loaded then steady.

Combined, you're looking at $2,500-3,000/month recurring before you've done anything aggressive. That number compounds as the audience grows. The service income stays steady while the product side builds.

That's the architecture. Not passive income, not client dependency. Both, running simultaneously, feeding each other.
