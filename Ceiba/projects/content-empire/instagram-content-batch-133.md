# Instagram Content Batch 133: Why Your Offer Isn't Selling and How to Diagnose It

## Post 1 — An Offer That Is Not Selling Has One of Four Problems

**Hook:** Before you redesign your landing page, run this four-part diagnostic on your offer.

**Slide 1:** An Offer That Is Not Selling Has One of Four Problems

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Problem 1 — Wrong audience. The right product in front of the wrong person will never convert. Audience fit comes before everything.
- Slide 3: Problem 2 — Weak positioning. People cannot see themselves in the outcome. Your copy describes the product instead of the transformation.
- Slide 4: Problem 3 — Price-value mismatch. The price feels high relative to how the offer is explained. This is almost always a copy problem, not a price problem.
- Slide 5: Problem 4 — Not enough volume. The offer is fine but only 40 people have seen it. Distribution is the actual constraint.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for offer frameworks and business diagnostics for solopreneurs.

**Caption:** Most solopreneurs who are not getting sales from their digital product assume the product is bad. The product is rarely the problem. There are four distinct failure modes for an offer that is not converting: wrong audience, weak positioning, price-value mismatch, or insufficient volume. Before you rebuild the offer from scratch, diagnose which of the four you are dealing with. If sales are nonexistent despite real traffic, it is likely a positioning or price-value issue. If traffic is low, it is a distribution problem. If you are getting clicks but no purchases, it is usually the copy or the price anchor. Each of these has a specific fix. The mistake is treating them all as the same problem and doing a full rebuild every time a launch underperforms.

## Post 2 — The Positioning Problem Nobody Talks About

**Hook:** Your offer is failing because you are describing what it is, not what it does for the buyer.

**Slide 1:** The Positioning Problem Nobody Talks About

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: "A 30-page guide to content creation" is a description. "How to post every day without running out of ideas" is positioning.
- Slide 3: Positioning is: here is the specific person I am for, here is the specific problem I solve, here is the specific outcome they get.
- Slide 4: The fastest way to test your positioning: show it to someone outside your niche. Can they tell you exactly who it is for? If they cannot, buyers cannot either.
- Slide 5: Rewrite your headline as a before-and-after. What state is the buyer in before? What state are they in after? That is your positioning.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for positioning and copy frameworks that make your offer impossible to misunderstand.

**Caption:** The positioning problem is the most common reason a solid product fails to sell. Most creators default to describing the product: what it contains, how many pages, what topics it covers. That information answers a question the buyer has not asked yet. The question they are actually asking is: will this solve my specific problem? Your positioning needs to answer that question before they get to the product details. Specificity is your advantage. "A guide to freelancing" is easy to ignore. "How to land your first $1,000 client in 30 days without a portfolio" is a specific offer for a specific person with a specific problem. The clearer you can make the before-and-after transformation in your headline and subhead, the less work the rest of your sales page has to do.

## Post 3 — Why "It Is Too Expensive" Is Almost Never About the Price

**Hook:** When a buyer says the price is too high, they usually mean the value was not communicated clearly enough.

**Slide 1:** Why "It Is Too Expensive" Is Almost Never About the Price

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Price objections are value objections in disguise. The buyer could not see enough of the outcome to justify the number.
- Slide 3: The fix is rarely lowering the price. It is raising the perceived value through better copy, social proof, and outcome specificity.
- Slide 4: If your $19 product is not selling, lowering it to $12 will not fix it. The conversion problem is in the positioning, not the number.
- Slide 5: Add a risk reversal. Add a testimonial. Add a specific outcome. Every one of these increases perceived value without changing the price.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for pricing and conversion frameworks built for solo product creators.

**Caption:** The instinct to lower prices when a product is not selling is understandable and almost always wrong. Price objections are usually value communication failures. The buyer sees $29 and asks themselves: is the outcome I see described on this page worth $29? If the copy is vague, the testimonials are missing, and the outcome is described in terms of content instead of transformation, the answer will be no at almost any price. The fix is to make the outcome so vivid and specific that the price feels small relative to it. Add the number: how many hours does this save? How much money could this generate? What is the cost of not solving this problem? When the answer to those questions is visible in your copy, objections to the price drop significantly.

## Post 4 — Your Offer Is Fine. Your Distribution Is the Problem.

**Hook:** A thousand people need to see your offer before you conclude it does not work.

**Slide 1:** Your Offer Is Fine. Your Distribution Is the Problem.

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Most offers that "fail" have been seen by fewer than 100 people. That is not a test. That is a launch in a room with no one in it.
- Slide 3: Volume is a prerequisite for conversion data. You cannot optimize what has not been seen enough times to generate signal.
- Slide 4: Before reworking the offer: double the number of people seeing it. Post more. Share more directly. Collaborate with someone who has the right audience.
- Slide 5: 1,000 people see offer. 2% convert. That is 20 sales. 50 people see it. 2% convert. That is 1 sale. The math is the problem, not the offer.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for distribution strategies that get your offer in front of the right audience at volume.

**Caption:** One of the most common and most frustrating mistakes in digital product launches is calling an offer a failure before it has had enough exposure to generate meaningful data. If your product page has had 80 visitors and zero sales, you do not have enough information to know whether the offer is broken. You need at minimum 500 to 1,000 unique visitors to your product page before a conversion rate means anything. Before you redesign, rewrite, or reprice, the first question is: how many people have actually seen this? If the answer is under a few hundred, the problem is distribution. Get more people to the page. Post daily. Collaborate. Run a simple promotion. Then look at the numbers.

## Post 5 — The Fastest Way to Fix a Dead Offer Is to Ask the People Who Looked and Left

**Hook:** The people who visited your product page and did not buy have the answer you are looking for.

**Slide 1:** The Fastest Way to Fix a Dead Offer Is to Ask the People Who Looked and Left

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Post a question on social: "What would make you not buy this?" The answers will tell you what your copy is not addressing.
- Slide 3: Run a poll: "Would you buy a guide that does X for $Y?" The price point and framing that gets the most yes votes is your new headline.
- Slide 4: DM the people who clicked but did not buy. Ask one question: "What stopped you?" Most people will tell you exactly.
- Slide 5: Customer research is the most underused tool in solopreneur marketing. The answers are almost always simpler than you expect.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for the exact questions and frameworks that unlock what your audience actually needs.

**Caption:** The fastest path to a better-converting offer is not a new landing page template or a split test on your headline color. It is talking to the people who almost bought. They already know your offer exists. They had enough interest to click. Something in the final stage of the decision stopped them. That something is your conversion problem. The easiest way to find it is to ask directly. A post that says "I built this product, what would stop you from buying it?" will give you more useful data than a month of analytics. People are surprisingly honest when you ask with genuine curiosity instead of defensiveness. Take the answers and address the most common objection directly in your copy. That one change often doubles conversion.
