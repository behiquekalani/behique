## Post 1 — The Passive Voice Problem
**Hook:** Passive voice does not just weaken your writing. It weakens your conversions.
**Slide 1:** Passive voice does not just weaken your writing. It weakens your conversions.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Active voice creates clarity and urgency. "I built this system in 30 days" moves faster than "This system was built over a period of 30 days."
- Slide 3: Passive voice hides the actor. In copy, hiding the actor removes the sense of human involvement and replaces it with institutional distance. Readers disengage.
- Slide 4: The audit: read your last sales page and highlight every passive construction. Replace each one with an active verb. The page will feel different after 10 minutes of edits.
- Slide 5: Active copy is also shorter copy. Removing passive constructions almost always reduces word count without reducing meaning.
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. Copy audits, writing frameworks, and conversion strategy.
**Caption:** Passive voice is the most common structural problem in copy written by smart people. It creeps in because it sounds formal and balanced. But in copy, formal and balanced reads as distant and unconvincing. When you use active voice, you put a person in the story. When you use passive voice, you remove them. And people buy from people. They are not persuaded by institutional phrasing. They are persuaded by someone who sounds like they did the thing they are describing, believes it works, and wants the reader to experience the same result. Active voice is what that sounds like on the page. Passive voice is what it sounds like when someone is trying not to say something too directly.

## Post 2 — Filler Words That Make Readers Stop Trusting You
**Hook:** Certain words in your copy are actively working against you.
**Slide 1:** Certain words in your copy are actively working against you.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: "Just." It minimizes what you are asking. "Just click here to get started" makes the action feel small. If the action is worth taking, do not minimize it.
- Slide 3: "Obviously" and "clearly." These words signal that the writer thinks the reader should already know this. They create defensiveness instead of openness.
- Slide 4: "Very," "really," and "truly." These are intensifiers that signal the writer does not trust the noun they are modifying. Replace with a stronger noun or a specific detail.
- Slide 5: "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "disruptive." These have been used so many times they carry no information. Replace with specific proof of what changed.
**Slide 6:** Audit your last piece of copy for these words. Remove every one and reread.
**Caption:** The words that kill conversions are almost never the big dramatic ones. They are the small, invisible qualifiers and intensifiers that accumulate across a page and signal uncertainty. When a writer says "just fill out the form," the reader hears "this is a small, perhaps unimportant action." When a writer says "obviously this approach works better," the reader hears condescension. When a writer calls something "truly revolutionary," the reader hears a claim that has been made so many times it means nothing. The cure is specificity. Instead of "truly revolutionary," write the specific outcome that would feel revolutionary to your reader. Let the result carry the weight that the adjective was trying to borrow.

## Post 3 — Hedging Language and Why It Destroys Credibility
**Hook:** "I think this might possibly be helpful for some people." That sentence convinced no one.
**Slide 1:** "I think this might possibly be helpful for some people." That sentence convinced no one.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Hedging language: "I think," "it seems like," "this might," "in my experience," "for some people." These phrases signal that the writer does not fully believe what they are saying.
- Slide 3: Some hedging is honest. When you genuinely do not know something, say so directly. What kills credibility is hedging on things you actually know to be true.
- Slide 4: The test: if you have evidence, cite it. If you have experience, state it as experience. If you have a strong opinion, own it. Save the hedges for genuine uncertainty.
- Slide 5: "In my experience" is fine when used once. When it appears in every other sentence, it reads as someone who does not trust their own knowledge. Own what you know.
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. Writing that builds trust through clarity, not qualification.
**Caption:** The irony of hedging language is that it is usually deployed to sound humble but has the opposite effect. When you hedge constantly, you signal to the reader that you do not trust your own perspective. And if you do not trust your own perspective, why should they? Genuine humility in writing does not come from surrounding every claim with qualifiers. It comes from being honest about the limits of your knowledge and direct about the things within them. Know the difference between the two and write accordingly. State what you know as what you know. State what you believe as what you believe. State what is uncertain as uncertain. That combination is more trustworthy than a hedge-filled paragraph trying to cover all possibilities at once.

## Post 4 — The Jargon Trap: Why Industry Language Loses Your Buyer
**Hook:** The more industry-specific your language, the smaller your potential buyer pool.
**Slide 1:** The more industry-specific your language, the smaller your potential buyer pool.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Jargon creates in-group signals. It tells people who already know what you mean that you are one of them. But it loses everyone else before they can understand the value.
- Slide 3: The test for any term: if your ideal buyer has been solving this problem for less than one year, do they know this word? If not, replace it with plain language.
- Slide 4: "Optimize your conversion funnel through strategic segmentation and A/B testing of your value proposition." Translation: "Test different headlines to find which one sells more."
- Slide 5: Plain language is not dumbing it down. It is removing friction between your idea and the reader's understanding. Clarity is not a concession. It is a competitive advantage.
**Slide 6:** Save this. Rewrite your top landing page headline in plain language and compare.
**Caption:** There is a version of industry language that signals expertise and earns the reader's trust. There is another version that signals the writer cares more about sounding sophisticated than about being understood. In copy, the second version always loses. Your buyer does not need to be impressed by your vocabulary. They need to feel that you understand their problem and have a credible solution. The clearest sign that copy is working is when a reader says "yes, that is exactly what I needed." That reaction never comes from jargon. It comes from precision in plain language. The goal is not simpler writing. It is writing so clear that nothing stands between the idea and the reader's comprehension.

## Post 5 — The False Urgency Problem: Why Fake Scarcity Destroys Trust
**Hook:** Fake urgency does not create more buyers. It creates fewer repeat buyers.
**Slide 1:** Fake urgency does not create more buyers. It creates fewer repeat buyers.
**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: False urgency: countdown timers that reset when you refresh, "only 3 left" for a digital product, "this offer expires tonight" that appears again next week.
- Slide 3: Readers in 2025 are sophisticated. They have seen these tactics used dishonestly. When they spot fake urgency, they lose trust in everything else on the page.
- Slide 4: Real urgency exists and it converts. If you have a genuine deadline, a real cohort limit, or an authentic price increase coming, say so with specifics. People respond to real constraints.
- Slide 5: The alternative to fake urgency is real relevance. If your offer is solving an actual problem the reader has right now, the urgency is already there. You do not need to manufacture it.
**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai. Copy strategy built on trust, not tricks.
**Caption:** Every fake urgency tactic you use trains your audience to distrust your next message. The short-term conversion lift from a manufactured countdown is not worth the long-term damage to the relationship with everyone who recognized the tactic. The most sustainable copy is honest copy. It acknowledges that not everyone is ready to buy right now, offers real value to those who are, and does not manufacture pressure for those who are not. The buyers who come in through real urgency and real relevance are the ones who buy again, recommend you to others, and trust the next offer without needing to be pushed. Build for those people. They are more valuable than the ones you pressure into a single purchase.
