---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
ADHD doesn't make you bad with money.

The systems you're using do.

Every mainstream budgeting tool was designed for neurotypical brains. Yours works differently.

**Slide 2 . The mismatch:**
Standard budget advice: track every transaction, review weekly, build habits over time.

For an ADHD brain, that advice creates failure by design.

Tracking every transaction requires sustained attention to low-reward tasks. ADHD brains have a dopamine system that resists exactly this. The system breaks down in week two, not because of weakness, but because of friction.

**Slide 3 . The 5 categories that fix it:**
Instead of dozens of tracking categories, use five.

Needs. Wants. Future. Buffer. Play.

Needs: fixed obligations that do not move. Wants: variable spending you choose. Future: savings and investments. Buffer: irregular expenses you can predict. Play: guilt-free spending with a hard cap.

Five categories. No ambiguity. No decision fatigue at every transaction.

**Slide 4 . Why this works for ADHD brains:**
The buffer category is the most important one.

ADHD spending patterns include irregular, often-forgotten costs. Annual subscriptions. Car maintenance. Gifts. These are not surprises. They are predictable costs that standard budgets ignore until they hit.

A buffer category with a fixed monthly contribution means these costs are already covered before they arrive.

**Slide 5 . The weekly 10-minute review:**
Not a daily log. Not a tracking app. One review per week.

Ten minutes. Four questions: Did needs get covered? Did I stay inside the wants cap? Did the future contribution happen? Did the buffer absorb anything unexpected?

That is it. Everything else is noise.

## POST 3 . The Thing Nobody Tells ADHD People About Impulse Buying (Carousel)
**Account:** @kalaniandrez
**Format:** Carousel (5 slides)

---

**Slide 1 (Hook):**
The thing nobody tells ADHD people about impulse buying:

It is not a willpower problem.

It is a dopamine problem.

**Slide 2 . What is actually happening:**
When an ADHD brain encounters a purchase opportunity, the dopamine system registers it as a reward signal.

The item looks interesting. The click feels good. The add-to-cart feels like progress.

This is not impulsive behavior in the pejorative sense. It is the brain seeking stimulation it is chronically under-supplied with. The purchase is not the goal. The dopamine hit is.

Telling someone with ADHD to "just resist" the impulse is like telling someone who hasn't eaten in 12 hours to just not feel hungry.

**Slide 3 . The 24-hour protocol:**
One rule: you cannot buy anything non-essential within 24 hours of finding it.

Add it to a list. Set a reminder for tomorrow. Walk away.

That is the entire protocol.

**Slide 4 . What happens after 24 hours:**
Most impulse urges do not survive 24 hours.

The dopamine spike that made the item feel urgent dissipates. What was "I need this right now" becomes "why did I want that."

This is not willpower. It is giving the dopamine response time to normalize before the purchase decision is made.

The items that still feel worth buying after 24 hours are the ones that were worth buying. The list filters itself.
