## ISSUE 44: "The Month I Finally Saw Where My Money Was Going"

**Subject line options:**
- "The month I finally saw where my money was going"
- "I thought I knew. I was wrong by $280."
- "Ninety days of tracking taught me one thing"

---

Something happened around month three of tracking my spending.

The anxiety went away.

Not because my finances got better immediately. Not because I figured out some magic category to cut. Because I could finally see the actual shape of the problem, and the actual problem turned out to be smaller than the one I'd been imagining.

Here's what happened.

I had been avoiding looking at my money in any serious way for about a year. I knew roughly what came in. I had a vague sense of what went out. The math felt like it should work. But there was always that mid-month friction where things felt tighter than they should, and I couldn't explain it.

So I started logging. Not with an app. Just a spreadsheet I built over an afternoon. Every transaction. Every dollar. No exceptions.

The first discovery was food. Not restaurants. Groceries. I thought I was spending around $200 a month. The actual number was $340. The gap wasn't recklessness. It was just not knowing. I had been buying in a way that felt reasonable each individual trip and then being surprised by the monthly total.

The second discovery was subscriptions. I found seven I had either forgotten about or stopped using. One was a tool I'd tried for two weeks in 2024. It had been billing me $12 a month for over a year. Another was a streaming service I hadn't opened since before summer.

Total subscription leak: $94 a month.

But the thing that actually changed my behavior wasn't the specific numbers. It was the pattern that showed up on Sundays.

My spending spiked every Sunday. Not dramatically, but consistently. Always food and small impulse purchases. Something about the end of the week, the transition into the new one, triggered a spending pattern I didn't know I had.

Seeing it didn't automatically fix it. But it meant I could prepare for it. Now Sunday has a soft cap. A number I'm allowed to spend without touching the rest of the week's plan. And the spike stopped.

The spreadsheet I used for all of this is the one I cleaned up and made available as a template. Monthly budget tab. Weekly cash flow tab. Subscription audit. Nothing fancy. It runs locally. You own the file.

The tool didn't solve the problem. Visibility did. The tool just gave me visibility.

If you want the template, it's linked at the end of this issue. $9. One-time. Takes about 20 minutes to set up.

The first month of data you put in it will probably show you something you didn't know. That's the whole point.

## ISSUE 45: "Systems Are Not Willpower"

**Subject line options:**
- "Systems are not willpower"
- "Financial discipline is a design problem"
- "The reason your budget keeps failing isn't you"

---

There's a version of financial advice that frames every money problem as a character problem.

You overspent because you lack discipline. You forgot the subscription because you're irresponsible. You impulse-bought because you have weak willpower. The implied solution is always the same: try harder. Be better. Want it more.

This framing is not just unhelpful. It's incorrect.

Financial behavior is a systems design problem. The environment you put yourself in, the tools you use, the checkpoints you build or don't build. These determine the outcome far more reliably than motivation.

This becomes clearer when you add ADHD to the picture.

ADHD doesn't make you bad with money. It makes the standard money tools break in specific, predictable ways.

Variable attention means you'll maintain any system perfectly for two weeks and then forget it entirely for three. Not because you stopped caring. Because attention is the input the system runs on, and ADHD attention isn't steady-state.

Impulsive decision-making means the buffer between impulse and action is shorter. The $30 thing you didn't plan to buy gets bought before the part of your brain that tracks the budget gets involved. Not weakness. Architecture.

Subscription amnesia is almost a feature of the way ADHD works. You sign up, you use it intensely for a week, attention moves on, and the subscription becomes invisible. Monthly billing is small enough to scroll past. There's no moment that triggers a reconsideration. It just runs.

So when I see people say "I've tried budgeting and it never works for me," I don't hear a discipline failure. I hear a system design failure. The system they tried required consistent attention, daily maintenance, and ongoing motivation. None of those are reliable inputs for ADHD brains.

The system that actually works for me has none of those requirements.

Weekly instead of monthly, because a week is concrete and a month is abstract. Visual color signals instead of precise calculations, because ADHD brains respond to cues, not numbers in isolation. A subscription audit list, because if you don't have a list, subscriptions will hide from you. Local storage, because opening your own file has less friction than logging into another app.

These are design decisions. Not motivation strategies.

The goal is to build a system where good decisions are the path of least resistance. Not to become the kind of person who makes good decisions through sheer force of will.

Systems don't require you to want it enough. They require you to set them up once.
