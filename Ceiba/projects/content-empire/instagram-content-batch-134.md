## Post 1 — There Are Only 4 Types of Digital Products. Here Is How They Differ.

**Hook:** Before you build anything, understand which type of digital product you are actually making.

**Slide 1:** There Are Only 4 Types of Digital Products. Here Is How They Differ.

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Type 1 — Information products. Guides, ebooks, reports. You package knowledge into a downloadable format. Low overhead, fast to build.
- Slide 3: Type 2 — Templates and tools. Notion dashboards, spreadsheets, prompt libraries, code snippets. Buyers get something they use directly.
- Slide 4: Type 3 — Courses and cohorts. Video or structured written curriculum. Higher price, higher production cost, bigger support commitment.
- Slide 5: Type 4 — Memberships and communities. Recurring revenue, ongoing access, requires consistent delivery. Highest relationship requirement.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for digital product strategy and solopreneur frameworks.

**Caption:** Most people who want to start selling digital products do not know which category of product fits where they are right now. They see a successful course creator and build a $997 course before they have validated that anyone will pay for their knowledge. Or they build a community before they have an audience to put in it. The four types of digital products have different time requirements, price ceilings, audience size requirements, and ongoing commitment levels. Knowing which type you are building before you build it saves months of work pointed in the wrong direction. The simple rule: start with information products and templates. They are fastest to build, easiest to price, and do not require you to show up live to deliver value.

## Post 2 — Start With an Information Product. Here Is Why.

**Hook:** The fastest path to your first online sale is an information product you can build this week.

**Slide 1:** Start With an Information Product. Here Is Why.

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Information products: an ebook, guide, checklist, or report. You take what you know and package it into a PDF or document. That is the whole build.
- Slide 3: Build time: 1-5 days. Price range: $9-$49. No ongoing delivery required. No customer support beyond the download link.
- Slide 4: The goal of your first product is not revenue. It is proof of concept. Did someone pay money for something you made? Yes. Now you know the path is real.
- Slide 5: Pick one thing you know that someone else needs to know. Write it clearly. Put a cover on it. Price it at $19. That is your first product.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for the exact workflow to go from idea to published digital product in 5 days.

**Caption:** The information product is where almost every successful digital product creator starts, and it is where you should start too. Not because it is the most profitable type of product in the long run, but because it has the lowest barrier to entry, the fastest time to market, and the clearest proof-of-concept signal. You are not building a platform. You are building evidence that someone will pay you for what you know. Write down what you know that other people in your target audience do not know yet. Pick the narrowest, most specific version of that knowledge. Package it as a PDF guide. Price it between $9 and $29. Launch it to whatever audience you have, no matter how small. Your first sale will tell you more about your market than any research you could do.

## Post 3 — Templates Are Underrated. The Buyer Gets Instant Utility.

**Hook:** Templates convert better than ebooks because buyers can use them immediately, not someday.

**Slide 1:** Templates Are Underrated. The Buyer Gets Instant Utility.

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: A template solves the blank page problem for the buyer. They open it and start working. The value is immediate and tangible.
- Slide 3: What qualifies as a template: Notion dashboards, Canva designs, spreadsheet trackers, prompt packs, email sequences, content calendars.
- Slide 4: Templates often command the same price as guides despite lower word count because the utility is higher. $19-$49 is a standard range.
- Slide 5: If you use a personal system that saves you time, it is probably a template product. Build once, sell without limit.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for templates and solopreneur systems that turn one person into a full operation.

**Caption:** Template products are among the best first digital products to build because the value proposition is immediate and concrete. An ebook requires the buyer to read it, apply it, and trust that the information is accurate. A template requires them to open it and start using it. The perceived risk of buying is lower because the utility is obvious before purchase. If you have built a Notion setup that manages your content calendar, your client projects, your finances, and your weekly review, that is a product. If you have a spreadsheet that tracks your eBay sourcing profitability automatically, that is a product. If you have a prompt library that saves you 2 hours a week on writing, that is a product. Package what already works in your life and sell the system to people who want the same result.

## Post 4 — Courses Are Worth It at the Right Stage. Not Stage One.

**Hook:** A course is the right product after you have proven people will pay for your knowledge. Not before.

**Slide 1:** Courses Are Worth It at the Right Stage. Not Stage One.

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Courses require significant time to produce, a curriculum with a clear transformation, and ongoing student support. The overhead is real.
- Slide 3: The price ceiling is higher: $97-$997 is standard. But you need an audience that trusts you enough to pay that amount before building it.
- Slide 4: The staging rule: sell the information product first. If buyers ask for more depth, build the course. Demand-first, then build.
- Slide 5: A course built for an audience that already paid for your guide has a conversion rate 3-5x higher than a course launched cold.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for the product ladder strategy that moves buyers from $9 to $997 naturally.

**Caption:** The most common mistake in digital product creation is starting with a course. The instinct makes sense: courses have higher price points, more perceived value, and the format feels more serious than a PDF guide. But courses take weeks to build properly, require video production or structured written curriculum, and need an audience willing to spend $97 or more on a creator they trust. That trust takes time and smaller purchases to build. The right sequence is: sell a low-priced information product, build trust, collect feedback, understand exactly what the audience needs more depth on, then build the course that answers the follow-up questions from your existing buyers. That sequence produces a course with a built-in warm audience. Starting with the course produces a polished product with no one to sell it to.

## Post 5 — Memberships Require an Audience First. Build One Before You Build the Community.

**Hook:** A paid community with no members is just a subscription to an empty room.

**Slide 1:** Memberships Require an Audience First. Build One Before You Build the Community.

**Slides 2-5:**
- Slide 2: Memberships work when people already trust you enough to pay monthly for ongoing access to you and each other.
- Slide 3: The recurring revenue of a membership is real. A $19/month community with 200 members is $3,800/month. But those 200 members need to exist first.
- Slide 4: The prerequisite: a free community, a consistent newsletter, or a social following with genuine engagement. Audience comes before monetization.
- Slide 5: Start with free, build trust, validate that people want to pay for more access. Then price the membership and launch to the people who already showed up for free.

**Slide 6:** Follow @behikeai for audience-first product strategy and the right sequence for solopreneur growth.

**Caption:** Memberships are the most attractive digital product model on paper because of the recurring revenue. Sell it once, collect monthly. That math is real. But it requires something that every other product type also requires, to a degree, and that memberships require absolutely: an existing audience that trusts you. A paid community launched to a cold audience with no track record will churn within 60 days even if the first members convert. People pay monthly for ongoing access to people and communities that consistently deliver value. The way to build a membership that retains is to give the value away free first, long enough that people are asking you to let them pay. That signal is your launch condition.
