## Thread 1081: The Two Types of Work Every Creator Must Know

**Hook tweet:** Most creators work hard and stay broke. The problem is not effort. It is which type of work they choose.

1/ Most creators work hard and stay broke. The problem is not effort. It is which type of work they choose.

There are two types of work. One resets every week. One compounds forever. Here is the difference:

2/ Type 1: Expiring work.
Client calls. Service delivery. One-off tasks. Admin.

You do it, you get paid, the clock resets.
Nothing carries forward.
You start from zero every Monday.

3/ Type 2: Compound work.
Written guides. Systems. Products. Content.

You do it once.
It keeps working.
Every sale, every read, every share happens without you doing it again.

4/ The math is brutal.
A creator who does 80% expiring work and 20% compound work will always lose to a creator who does the reverse.

Not because of talent. Because of the type of effort they are investing.

5/ The trap: compound work feels unproductive at first.
No immediate feedback. No instant payment.

Your guide gets 3 reads. Your product gets 0 sales.
Most people quit here and go back to expiring work.

6/ But compound work has a delay, not a limit.
Six months of consistent compound work looks like nothing.

Then it looks like everything.
The creators who seem to explode overnight were compounding in silence.

7/ The audit:
Look at your last week.
How much of what you did will still be generating value in 12 months?

If the answer is less than 20%, your business model is a job with extra steps.

8/ Start shifting the ratio.
Pick one thing you do repeatedly and turn it into a product, system, or published piece.

Do it this week. Ship it. Then do it again next week.
Compound work is just expiring work, packaged better. Start packaging.

## Thread 1082: Why You Keep Procrastinating (It Is Not What You Think)

**Hook tweet:** Procrastination is not a character flaw. It is a signal. Here is what it is actually telling you.

1/ Procrastination is not a character flaw. It is a signal. Here is what it is actually telling you.

2/ The standard advice: just start. Use the 2-minute rule. Build discipline.

All fine. All missing the actual cause.

Most procrastination is not laziness.
It is ambiguity.

3/ When you do not know exactly what to do next, your brain registers it as a threat.

It responds with avoidance.
You call it procrastination.
Your brain calls it self-preservation.

Same behavior. Very different root cause.

4/ Test this: pick a task you have been putting off.

Is the problem that you do not want to do it?
Or is it that you do not know where to start because the task is too vague?

"Work on the business" is not a task.
"Write the opening paragraph" is a task.

5/ The fix is not motivation. It is specificity.

Break the vague task into the smallest possible concrete action.
Not "build the course." Not "outline the course." Not "outline module one."

"Write the three outcomes module one will teach."
That is a task. That is startable.

6/ Notice what happens when you have a specific task:
The resistance drops.
The starting is easier.
The doing takes care of itself.

Clarity creates momentum. Vagueness creates paralysis.

7/ The system:
End every day by writing tomorrow's three most specific next actions.

Not goals. Not areas of focus. Next actions.
Physical, visible, specific.

Your brain gets through the night without processing ambiguity. You sit down and go.

8/ Stop trying to feel motivated.
Start trying to feel clear.

Write the specific next action. Then do it.
Motivation follows. It never leads.

## Thread 1083: The Beginner's Guide to Building a Content System

**Hook tweet:** I spent a year publishing randomly and burning out every month. Then I built a system. Here is the whole thing.

1/ I spent a year publishing randomly and burning out every month. Then I built a system. Here is the whole thing.

2/ The burnout pattern:
Post every day when motivated.
Disappear for a week when not.
Guilt spiral. Restart. Repeat.

The problem: I was producing and publishing at the same time every day. That is unsustainable.

3/ The fix starts with separating creation from publishing.

You do not write the post the day it goes live.
You write it days before.
This single change eliminates most of the daily pressure.

4/ Step 1: Build an idea library.
Every time a thought hits you, it goes into one place. Notes app, Google Doc, voice memo.

You are not writing yet. You are collecting.
Do this for one week before you write a single piece.

5/ Step 2: Batch create.
Set two hours once a week. Write everything you will publish that week.

Not inspired daily writing. Focused weekly production.
You get better content and eliminate the daily "what do I write today" spiral.

6/ Step 3: Build a buffer.
Never publish from this week's batch. Publish from last week's.

A two-week buffer means a sick day or hard week never breaks your streak.
Your audience never notices. You never panic.

7/ Step 4: Repurpose before you abandon.
Every idea you have is not one piece. It is three.

Newsletter section becomes tweet thread.
Tweet thread becomes LinkedIn post.
LinkedIn post becomes short-form video script.

One idea. Three assets. Same work.

8/ The system runs without willpower.
Willpower fails. Systems do not.

Build the four-step system. Protect the batch session.
Publish from the buffer. Repurpose everything.
Your consistency is a system problem. Solve it like one.

## Thread 1084: What Pricing Your Work Too Low Is Actually Costing You

**Hook tweet:** The creator who charges $7 is not humble. They are sending a message. And the market is listening.

1/ The creator who charges $7 is not humble. They are sending a message. And the market is listening.

2/ Price is not just a number. It is communication.

Before anyone reads your sales page, your price tells a story about:
- What kind of product this is
- Whether the creator believes in it
- Whether the buyer should take it seriously

3/ Low prices attract low-commitment buyers.
Not always. But often.

The person who pays $9 for a guide usually does not read it.
The person who pays $97 reads it twice.

Cost creates commitment. No cost, no skin in the game.

4/ You are also competing on price with the wrong market.

Every $9 product competes with a $12 coffee and a $15 lunch.
Every $97 product competes with a $100 problem the buyer already has.

Price signals value category. Know which one you are in.

5/ The math:
$7 x 100 buyers = $700
$97 x 10 buyers = $970

Same revenue. 10x fewer sales needed.
Plus: the $97 buyers are more engaged, get better results, leave better testimonials.

6/ Why creators underprice:
- They do not believe in the product
- They fear rejection
- They confuse "accessible" with "low cost"

Accessibility is about payment plans and reach. It is not about charging less than the outcome is worth.

7/ The repricing exercise:
List your products. For each one, ask:
"What is the transformation this creates worth to the buyer?"

Then ask: "Does my current price reflect that, or does it reflect my anxiety?"

8/ Raise one price this week.
Test it for 30 days.
Observe the buyer quality, engagement, and results.

The price was not lowering the barrier for good buyers. It was lowering your confidence.
Charge what the transformation is worth.

## Thread 1085: The Three-Phase Audience Journey Nobody Maps

**Hook tweet:** Your audience does not go from stranger to buyer in one step. There are three phases. Most creators only build for one of them.

1/ Your audience does not go from stranger to buyer in one step. There are three phases. Most creators only build for one of them.

2/ Phase 1: Discovery.
Someone finds your work for the first time.

They do not know you. They do not trust you. They have one question:
"Is this worth my attention?"

Your job here: be specific enough to be relevant, clear enough to be interesting.

3/ Phase 2: Trust.
They come back a second time. Then a third.

Now they are asking a different question:
"Is this person consistent? Do they know what they are talking about? Do they care?"

Your job here: deliver more than promised, consistently.

4/ Phase 3: Conversion.
They have been reading your work for 90+ days. Trust is built.

When you offer something, the question is not "should I trust this creator?"
It is simply "does this solve my specific problem right now?"

5/ Most creators only optimize for Phase 1.
Better hooks. Better titles. More reach.

The top of the funnel is full. The middle is empty.
Lots of discovery. No trust being built.

The result: high follower count, low sales.

6/ The fix is a Phase 2 investment.
A welcome email sequence.
A consistent newsletter.
Long-form content that rewards the people who have been reading longest.

The stranger already found you. Now give them a reason to stay.

7/ Phase 3 does not require convincing.
If you have built Phase 2 correctly, the sale is almost frictionless.

Your buyer already knows you, trusts you, and wants what you make.
They just need a clear offer at the right moment.

8/ Audit your business:
- What are you doing to optimize discovery?
- What are you doing to build trust over time?
- What triggers the offer for someone who is already ready?

Build for all three. The buyers you want are in Phase 2 right now.
Serve them better.
