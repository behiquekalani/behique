## Tweet 777
**Type:** Thread opener
There are two types of solopreneur businesses. Most people never consciously choose between them. (Thread)

1/ A lifestyle business optimizes for freedom. Maximum quality of life. Minimum overhead. You work the hours you want, with the clients you choose, on topics you care about. Revenue is a tool, not a scoreboard.

2/ An exit-oriented business optimizes for transferable value. Systems. Recurring revenue. Documented processes. A brand that could run without you. You are building an asset, not just an income.

3/ Both are valid. Neither is more ambitious than the other. A deliberately run lifestyle business is one of the most underrated forms of freedom that exists. An exit-oriented business creates leverage most people never touch.

4/ The problem: accidentally running one while thinking you're building the other. If you want lifestyle, stop adding complexity. Every offering and platform that doesn't increase your enjoyment or income per hour is a mistake.

5/ If you're building for exit, start documenting everything now. Processes. Playbooks. Client frameworks. Revenue that depends on your personality has no sale value. Revenue that depends on your system does. Pick one. Then build accordingly.

## Tweet 780
**Type:** Thread opener
How to make your first outsourcing hire without wasting money or losing control. (Thread)

1/ Track where your time actually goes for one week. Not where you think it goes. Actually log it. You will find that 20-30% of your hours are on tasks that are documented, repeatable, and do not require your judgment.

2/ That 20-30% is your first hire list. The first outsourced role should almost never be someone creative or strategic. It should be an operator. Inbox management, repurposing, formatting, uploading, basic research. Low risk. High time return.

3/ Start with five hours per week. Not a full-time VA. Not a retainer. Five hours. A clear scope. A paid test project first. Hire for reliability and communication above skill. Consistent 80% beats unreliable 100% every time.

4/ The math that makes this obvious: if your hourly output is worth $100 and you're spending 10 hours a week on $15-per-hour tasks, you are losing $850 per week by not delegating. That is not frugality. That is expensive.

5/ Outsourcing is not a luxury for when you can afford it. It is a leverage multiplier you can turn on before you feel ready. Most people wait too long because it feels like a leap. It is a five-hour test project.
