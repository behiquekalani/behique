## Thread 1321: The thing about overnight success

1/ Every "overnight success" story you've heard has a hidden first chapter. The 10 years of preparation that made the overnight look effortless. Here's what that first chapter actually looks like:

2/ It looks like publishing for two years with minimal engagement. Writing emails to a list of 80 people. Selling a product to 12 buyers and treating all 12 like they're the most important people on earth.

3/ It looks like improving your craft when nobody is paying attention. Learning the thing deeply because you genuinely want to know it, not because an audience is watching.

4/ It looks like staying when everything logical says quit. When the metrics are flat. When the comparisons are unfavorable. When the ROI on the time invested does not seem to add up.

5/ The visible success is real. The overnight part is just the public debut of private work. The world sees the launch. Nobody saw the three years of failing in private.

6/ This matters for your timeline. You are almost certainly earlier in the process than you think you should be. The impatience you feel is normal. It's not evidence that you're doing the wrong thing.

7/ The question is not "why hasn't it worked yet?" The question is "am I doing the right work consistently enough for long enough?" In most cases, the answer is yes. Keep going.

## Thread 1322: How to pick a digital product that will actually sell

1/ Most people pick a product topic based on what they know best. That's the second-best approach. Here's the first: pick based on what your specific audience is already paying for.

2/ The market is already telling you what it wants to pay for. Look at what products in your niche are selling. Look at what questions your audience keeps asking. Look at where they're spending money with other creators.

3/ The overlap between what you know and what people are already buying is the sweet spot. Not everything you know. Not everything the market buys. The specific intersection.

4/ Validate before you build. Write the sales page first. Post a tweet or a newsletter asking if people would want this. See if anyone says yes with enough urgency to pre-buy.

5/ Price the product based on outcome value, not on time invested. A two-hour guide that solves a $5,000 problem is worth more than a 40-hour course that teaches something people could Google.

6/ Start with something you can build in less than two weeks. Not because the product has to be small forever, but because speed of execution matters more than scale of ambition at the beginning.

7/ The first product teaches you everything. What people actually need from you. How to explain your work. What questions come up after purchase. Use that data to build the second product better.

8/ Follow for more on building digital products that match what real people want to buy. Repost for anyone staring at a blank Notion doc wondering what product they should build next.

## Thread 1323: The creator who builds systems wins

1/ Two creators start with the same idea, the same audience, and the same skill level. Five years later, one is thriving and one is burned out. The difference almost always comes down to systems.

2/ The burned-out creator optimizes for quality on every individual piece. They hand-craft each post. Each email takes hours. They are proud of the output, but the process is unsustainable at scale.

3/ The thriving creator builds systems around their best work. They identify what works, create templates and frameworks from it, and execute from those templates consistently.

4/ Systems don't kill creativity. They protect it. When the structure handles the logistics, your creative energy goes toward the thing only you can bring: your specific insight and voice.

5/ Start with the parts of your workflow that are repetitive. Content formats that work. Subject line templates that convert. Offer structures that close. Each of those is a candidate for a system.

6/ A system can be as simple as a document with the question you ask yourself before writing. Or a checklist before you publish. Or a weekly routine for content planning. Simple beats complicated.

7/ Document as you go. Every time you figure out a better way to do something, write it down. Build a personal operating system. Revisit it quarterly. Refine it continuously.

8/ Follow for more on building a creator business that scales without burning you out. Repost for anyone who feels like they are constantly reinventing the wheel with their content.

## Thread 1324: The uncomfortable truth about growing on social media

1/ Most social media growth advice is optimized for vanity metrics, not for building a real business. Here's the truth nobody says loudly enough:

2/ Follower count is a lagging indicator of trust. Not a leading one. You can have 50,000 followers and zero buyers if those followers came from viral content that doesn't relate to your offer.

3/ The best growth strategy and the best business-building strategy are the same: create content that attracts your ideal buyer, consistently, over a long enough period of time.

4/ "Ideal buyer" is not the same as "most people." The more specifically you speak to the exact person you are trying to serve, the slower the follower count grows and the higher the conversion rate climbs.

5/ Going viral to a misaligned audience is worse than not going viral at all. It inflates your metrics, dilutes your engagement rate, and fills your DMs with people who will never buy.

6/ The right metric to optimize: are the right people finding me? Not how many people. Are the people who find me the ones who have the problem I solve and the willingness to pay?

7/ Post for your buyer, not for the algorithm. Trust that the algorithm will show your content to more people when it resonates. But resonance with the wrong people is not the goal.

8/ Follow for a more honest take on building an audience that actually supports a business. Repost for any creator chasing numbers who is starting to wonder why the numbers don't feel like they're adding up.

## Thread 1325: You don't need more ideas. You need more depth.

1/ The idea that you need a constant stream of fresh ideas to succeed as a creator is one of the biggest myths in the space. Here's what actually works better:

2/ Most successful creators are not idea machines. They are depth machines. They take a small number of ideas and explore them from every possible angle until those ideas are understood completely.

3/ Dan Koe writes about human potential from multiple angles. James Clear writes about habits from multiple angles. Naval writes about wealth and freedom. None of them are running out of content because they don't need new ideas every week. They go deeper.

4/ Going deep has compounding returns. Your tenth piece on a topic is better than your first because you've thought about it more, received feedback, and found the edges of the idea.

5/ Going deep also creates clarity. When you write about the same core idea from multiple angles over time, you find the through-lines. You develop an actual philosophy instead of a collection of disconnected takes.

6/ The practical move: identify your three core beliefs about your topic. For the next 90 days, write exclusively about those three. Every piece of content explores one of them from a new angle.

7/ At the end of 90 days, you will have a body of work with coherence, depth, and a clear point of view. That is more valuable than 90 random pieces of content on 90 different topics.

8/ Follow for more on building a content body of work that actually means something. Repost for any creator who feels like they are running out of things to say, when the real issue is they haven't gone deep enough yet.
