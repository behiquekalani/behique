## Tweet 627
**Type:** Thread opener
**Hook:** I studied 50 creators who make $10K+/month from digital products. Here is the pattern nobody talks about. (Thread)

1/ None of them have the biggest audience in their niche. Most have under 50K followers. Audience size is almost irrelevant past a certain floor.

2/ They all have at least one offer above $97. Low-ticket products keep you broke. You need volume that one person cannot produce alone.

3/ They own their distribution. Email list, community, something they control. Platform reach is rented. Their email list is property.

4/ They create less content than you think, but each piece is more intentional. Fewer posts. Stronger points. One clear takeaway per piece.

5/ They reinvest in the business. Better tools, better systems, occasionally hired help. They treat it like a business because it is one.

The lesson: small audience, clear offer, owned channel, intentional content. That formula beats big following plus no system every time.

## Tweet 630
**Type:** Thread opener
**Hook:** Nobody tells you the real cost of staying cheap with your pricing. Here it is. (Thread)

1/ Low prices attract the wrong buyers. Not always, but often. People who shop primarily on price are more likely to complain, refund, and demand extra work.

2/ Low prices force you to sell volume. Selling 200 units of a $27 product is harder than selling 20 units of a $270 product. And the economics are the same.

3/ Low prices position you as a commodity. When you compete on price, you are telling the market "my main advantage is being cheap." That is not a brand. It is a race to the bottom.

4/ Low prices require low-margin work. You spend more time in customer support, more time marketing, less time improving the actual product.

5/ The fix is not raising prices arbitrarily. It is improving positioning until the higher price feels obvious to the buyer.

Price is a signal. Make sure yours says what you actually mean.
