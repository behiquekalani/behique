## Tweet 1052

THREAD: The 3 recurring revenue models for solo operators (and when to use each)

1/ Every recurring revenue model comes down to one question:

What keeps the customer paying month after month?

There are exactly three good answers.

2/ Answer 1: Ongoing results.

This is the productized retainer.

You deliver the same thing every month. Client pays the same amount every month.

No scope creep. No custom proposals. No hourly tracking.

Example: 4 LinkedIn posts per week, $2,500/month, one revision per post.

3/ Answer 2: Ongoing access.

This is the membership.

Members pay for the community, the calls, the archive, or all three.

Churn is managed by making the community itself the product, not just you.

4/ Answer 3: Ongoing fresh content.

This is the subscription product.

New templates, new research, new prompts every month.

The customer stays because stopping means falling behind.

5/ The mistake: trying to run all three before any of them works.

Pick one. Get to 50 paying customers.

50 customers is proof. 1 customer is hope.

6/ Which one fits your business depends on what you are already doing.

If you do service work: retainer.
If you have an audience: membership.
If you make content: subscription product.

Start there.

## Tweet 1055

THREAD: How to build a content moat that makes you impossible to replace

1/ A content moat is the gap between you and anyone who tries to do what you do.

It is not built with frequency. It is built with depth.

2/ Most creators go wide.

They cover productivity, business, mindset, tech, creativity. Relevant to everyone. Essential to no one.

The algorithm rewards them occasionally. Buyers never find them.

3/ Moat builders go narrow.

They pick one specific intersection and go deeper than anyone else alive.

Not "email marketing." Something like "email sequences for course creators who hate selling."

4/ When you go deep on a narrow thing, three things happen:

- Search finds you first
- People who know the topic trust you immediately
- Buyers do not need to compare options. You are the obvious answer.

5/ The moat compounds.

Every new piece makes the old pieces more valuable.

A new case study validates your framework. A new framework gives context to your case studies.

The whole grows stronger with each addition.

6/ The question to ask yourself:

"If someone became obsessed with [your specific topic], is my content the thing they would reach for first?"

If yes, you have a moat.

If no, you are still building one.

Keep going.
