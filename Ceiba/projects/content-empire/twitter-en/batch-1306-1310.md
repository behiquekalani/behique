## Thread 1306: Most creators are building the wrong thing first

1/ Most creators spend 6 months building a product before they have 100 followers. That's why they fail. Here's the order that actually works:

2/ The instinct is to create something and then find people who want it. That feels logical. It's backwards. You find people first, then build for them.

3/ An audience of 500 highly aligned people will tell you exactly what they want to pay for. They'll ask questions. They'll complain about their problems. Your product almost writes itself.

4/ Without that audience, you're guessing. You build based on what you think people want. Sometimes you're right. Usually you're not. And you won't know for months.

5/ The fastest way to a successful product: talk publicly about a specific problem. Attract people who have that problem. Ask them what solution they'd buy. Build that.

6/ Presell it before you build it. If five people pay before it exists, you build. If zero people pay, you adjust the positioning or the topic. Fast feedback. No wasted months.

7/ Audience first. Product second. Launch third. Every creator who skips step one makes the other steps ten times harder than they need to be.

## Thread 1307: The price is not your problem

1/ Your product isn't failing because the price is too high. It's failing because the value isn't clear enough. A pricing problem is almost always a communication problem.

2/ People make price decisions relative to perceived value. A $500 product feels cheap if the outcome is worth $5,000. A $10 product feels expensive if you can't explain why it exists.

3/ Before you lower the price, ask: have I clearly stated what changes for the buyer after they use this? Not what the product is. What it does. What it fixes. What it enables.

4/ The most common sales page mistake is leading with format. "A 12-module course with 4 hours of video." Nobody buys hours of video. They buy the outcome the video delivers.

5/ Rewrite your offer around the before and after. Before: they have this problem. After: they have this result. The product is just the mechanism that gets them from one to the other.

6/ Once the value is clear, test the price upward. Most creators underprice because they are comparing their product to what they would personally pay. That's the wrong benchmark.

7/ The right benchmark is what the outcome is worth to the buyer. Not what it cost you to create. Not what you'd pay. What your specific customer would pay to have the problem solved.

8/ Follow for weekly breakdowns on pricing, positioning, and building digital products that actually sell. Repost if you've ever blamed the price when the real issue was the pitch.

## Thread 1308: Nobody tells you about the middle

1/ Everyone talks about the start of building a business and the moment it succeeds. Nobody talks about the 18 months in the middle when nothing is working and you can't tell if you're close or delusional.

2/ The beginning has novelty. You're figuring things out. Everything is a first. Energy is high because the possibility feels unlimited.

3/ Success has validation. Revenue, followers, evidence that the work is landing. You finally have proof you weren't wrong about yourself.

4/ The middle has neither. You have enough experience to know how hard this is. You don't have enough results to know it's going to work. That gap is where most people quit.

5/ The quitting looks rational from the inside. "I've been at this for a year and nothing is happening." But the timelines for this kind of work are longer than that. Usually 2-4 years.

6/ The people who make it through the middle have one thing in common: they focus on inputs they control instead of outcomes they don't. Post count. Email list size. Products shipped.

7/ You cannot control when it clicks. You can control whether you showed up today. Build an identity around the inputs and the outputs eventually follow.

8/ If you're in the middle right now, follow. Most of what I write is for you. Repost for anyone who needs to hear that the middle is normal, not a sign to stop.

## Thread 1309: The newsletter is the best business move most creators avoid

1/ Everyone is building on platforms they don't own. Instagram, TikTok, YouTube. One algorithm change and years of work can stop converting overnight. The newsletter is the only exception.

2/ An email list is the only audience asset you own outright. No algorithm decides who sees it. No platform can de-rank you. The subscriber chose to receive your work directly.

3/ The open rates on a quality newsletter destroy the reach of any social post. A 40% open rate on 1,000 subscribers is 400 people reading your full message. That beats most social posts at 10,000 followers.

4/ But the real value is conversion. Email converts to sales at 3-5x the rate of social media. The people on your list are not passive scrollers. They opted in. They read. They trust you more.

5/ The common objection is "nobody reads email anymore." That's said by people who send bad emails. If your newsletter consistently delivers something worth reading, your list will open it.

6/ You don't need a massive list to make real money. 500 engaged subscribers who trust you will generate more revenue than 50,000 passive social followers who don't.

7/ Start the newsletter before you feel ready. One email per week. Something useful. Something honest. Grow the list slowly and correctly instead of fast and without trust.

## Thread 1310: Working in public is the best marketing strategy that feels like cheating

1/ The best marketing I know: write about what you're building as you build it. Document the decisions, the failures, the numbers. The people this is for will find you.

2/ Traditional marketing is about reaching strangers with a message. Working in public is about attracting the right people through the work itself. Different mechanism. Different results.

3/ When you share the real process, you do three things at once. You build an audience. You get feedback that improves the work. You create content without manufacturing it from nothing.

4/ The fear is that showing unfinished work makes you look incompetent. The reality is it makes you look human, trustworthy, and genuinely working on the thing you claim to be working on.

5/ People want to follow a process. The destination is less interesting than the journey. Show where you are right now. Show what you are figuring out. Show what you got wrong this week.

6/ The audience that finds you through documented process is also more qualified. They know what you do, how you think, and what kind of work you produce. They are pre-sold before they ever see an offer.

7/ You do not need to perform. You need to document. One honest update per week about where you are in the build. That is enough to attract the people you are trying to reach.
