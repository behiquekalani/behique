## Thread 1376: The Content Nobody Is Making

1/ Everyone is making the same content. Here's the category that's wide open and pays the best: deeply specific, genuinely useful, and honest about tradeoffs.

2/ Most content is either too generic to be useful or too promotional to be trusted. The middle ground is real insight from someone who actually did the thing.

3/ "Here's what worked and why" beats "here are 10 tips" every time. Specificity creates trust. Trust converts into audience and sales.

4/ The person who documents their real process, including what failed, becomes a reference point. Reference points get revisited. Generic tips get scrolled past.

5/ You don't need a large audience to start this. You need a real point of view and a specific person you're trying to help. Write for that person. Be specific enough that they feel seen.

6/ This approach also compounds differently. Authoritative, specific content earns backlinks, shares, and long-tail traffic that generic content doesn't. It has a longer half-life.

7/ The gap in the market isn't more information. It's more clarity. More specific. More honest. That's the content that builds real audiences.

## Thread 1377: Identity Before Strategy

1/ Most people try to change their behavior before changing their identity. That's why change doesn't stick. You can't outperform your self-image for long.

2/ If you see yourself as someone who struggles with money, you'll sabotage financial wins without realizing it. The identity corrects toward what it believes is true.

3/ This isn't abstract. It's neurological. Your brain filters reality through what it expects to be true and creates behavior that confirms the expectation.

4/ Identity change is simpler than it sounds. It starts with a decision and then gets reinforced by evidence. "I'm the kind of person who does X" followed by doing X, even once.

5/ Every small win is evidence for the new identity. Every small action reinforces the belief. Over time, the new belief becomes the default lens.

6/ The question to ask isn't "how do I get myself to do this." It's "who is the person who naturally does this, and what do they believe about themselves?"

7/ Start there. The behavior follows. Not perfectly and not immediately. But more reliably than grinding against an identity that resists what you're trying to do.

## Thread 1378: The Hidden Cost of Keeping Options Open

1/ Keeping all your options open feels smart. It's actually one of the most expensive choices you can make when you're trying to build something real.

2/ Options have a carrying cost. Every option you're keeping open requires mental bandwidth to maintain. A dozen open options is a dozen ongoing decisions you haven't made.

3/ Commitment is not the enemy of opportunity. Commitment is what generates opportunity. When you go deep on one thing, you become a reference point. Reference points attract collaborators, clients, and doors that don't open for generalists.

4/ The person who does six things adequately will always be outcompeted by the person who does one thing exceptionally. Depth beats breadth in almost every market worth being in.

5/ This doesn't mean closing options forever. It means closing them for a defined period. Pick a lane. Run hard. Reassess in 12 months. That's different from permanent commitment.

6/ The fear of missing out makes people spread too thin. But you can't miss out on the things you would have built if you'd gone all in on one thing. Those futures are invisible.

7/ Close some options. Go deep. Trust that the constraint will generate the focus that generates the results. That's how most meaningful things get built.

8/ Follow for more. Repost for the person who's been "keeping options open" for two years without building anything.

## Thread 1379: Why Your Morning Routine Isn't Working

1/ You've read about morning routines. You've tried them. They lasted two weeks. Here's why, and what to do instead.

2/ Most morning routine advice is a highlight reel of a specific person's optimal life, not a template for yours. You adopted their system without adapting it to your context.

3/ The fundamentals of a good morning are simpler than the gurus suggest: avoid external inputs (phone, news, email) until you've done one thing for yourself. That's the core rule.

4/ The "one thing" can be 10 minutes. It can be writing, exercise, planning, reading, or silence. The specific activity matters far less than the order of operations. You first. Then the world.

5/ The second mistake: designing a 90-minute routine you can only do under ideal conditions. Design for your worst day. If you can do it when things are hard, it will stick.

6/ Start with a 10-minute version. Protect it obsessively. Expand only after the base habit is solid. Most people design the perfect morning and then fail to show up for it.

7/ The goal is not a perfect morning. The goal is a morning that centers you before the day pulls you in twenty directions. Even a partial version of that is worth having.

## Thread 1380: The Simplest Business Model Online

1/ People overcomplicate online business. The simplest model that works: make something useful for a specific person, tell that person it exists, repeat.

2/ The "specific person" part is where most people go wrong. They try to help everyone, which means the product helps no one well enough to pay for.

3/ Specificity creates the ability to make something genuinely excellent for someone. A generic solution for everyone competes with a thousand generic solutions. A specific solution for someone specific has far less competition.

4/ "Tell that person it exists" means marketing. Not manipulation. Not deception. Just showing up where they are and saying: here's what I made and here's who it's for.

5/ Content is the most scalable form of that. Every piece of content is a message to a specific person saying: I understand your problem and I have something that might help.

6/ "Repeat" is the compound engine. Each product teaches you more about your audience. Each piece of content finds new versions of the right person. The system improves with use.

7/ You don't need a team. You don't need funding. You need something real, someone who needs it, and enough consistency to keep showing up.
