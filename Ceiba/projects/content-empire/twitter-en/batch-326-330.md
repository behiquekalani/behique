Sell before you have served.
Disappear when things get hard.

**Batch 330**
Topic: Digital product pricing strategy

Tweet 1:
Your price is a positioning statement.
A $7 product says "this is a snack."
A $500 product says "this changes something."
Price accordingly, then build the product to match what the price implies.

Tweet 2:
Most creators underprice because they are thinking about what they would pay.
But your buyer is not you.
They are in a different situation, with a different problem, with a different sense of what that solution is worth.
Price for the outcome, not the hours.

Tweet 3:
The psychology of low pricing:
Under $20 creates impulse buyers with low commitment.
$50-$200 creates considered buyers with higher completion rates.
$500 and above creates invested buyers who actually do the work.
What kind of customer do you want.

Tweet 4:
Raising your price is often the most effective marketing move you can make.
It signals confidence.
It filters out buyers who will drain your energy.
It attracts buyers who take the solution seriously.
The math usually works out in your favor.

Tweet 5:
A pricing framework for your first digital product:
1. What problem does it solve.
2. What is that problem costing the buyer in time or money.
3. Price at roughly 10 percent of that cost.
4. Add a guarantee to remove the friction.
5. Test, then adjust.
Simple is better than sophisticated when you are starting out.
