## Tweet 767
**Type:** Thread opener
Most creators have the wrong goal for their welcome sequence. Here is what it should actually do. (Thread)

1/ The welcome sequence is not for selling. It is for earning the right to sell later. Most creators skip this and wonder why their list doesn't buy anything.

2/ Email 1: deliver what you promised. The lead magnet, the resource, the reason they subscribed. Do it in the first 10 minutes. Every minute of delay is a degree of trust lost.

3/ Email 2: tell your story. Not your polished bio. The version with the failure, the pivot, the thing you figured out the hard way. People don't subscribe to information. They subscribe to people who've been where they are.

4/ Email 3: address the biggest objection they have about their problem. Not your product. Their problem. Show you understand it better than they do. That's positioning before it's selling.

5/ By email 5, you've earned a soft offer. Not a hard close. A door left open. "If you want to go deeper, here's where to start." Five emails. One relationship built that a thousand cold traffic ads can't buy.

## Tweet 770
**Type:** Thread opener
Building a media business is not the same as being a content creator. Here is the mental shift that changes everything. (Thread)

1/ A content creator makes posts. A media business makes systems that produce posts. The creator is the bottleneck. The media business is not.

2/ Owned distribution first. Every piece of content on a platform you don't control should drive toward your email list. When a platform owns your audience, you're a renter. When your list owns it, you're a landlord.

3/ An editorial calendar with a point of view. Not "what to post this week." A thematic, seasonal, compounding content plan. Every piece teaches something. Taken together, it builds a worldview.

4/ Revenue that doesn't require individual transaction energy. Subscriptions, bundles, recurring digital sales. When every dollar requires active selling, you're not a media business. You're a high-volume freelancer.

5/ The shift: think like an editor-in-chief, not a writer. Your job is the system and the point of view. The system produces the content. The point of view is what makes it worth reading.
