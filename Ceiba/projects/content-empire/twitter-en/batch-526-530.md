## Thread 527: How to Price Your Expertise

**Tweet 1:**
Most people underprice themselves for years.

Then one day they raise their rates and realize the market was never the problem.

Here's how to price your expertise correctly: 🧵

**Tweet 2:**
The wrong way to price: time-based.

"I charge $50 an hour." This frames you as a commodity measured in units of time.

The buyer optimizes to buy fewer hours. You optimize to work more. Both incentives are wrong.

**Tweet 3:**
The right way to price: outcome-based.

What does the client have after working with you that they didn't have before?

Price the gap between their current state and the result.

**Tweet 4:**
Outcome pricing requires you to know your client's problem better than they do.

Not just the surface problem.

The real cost of the problem. The time it wastes. The revenue it blocks. The stress it creates.

**Tweet 5:**
If a business owner spends 15 hours a week on a problem you can eliminate, that's not a $500 problem.

That's a $15,000 per year problem if they bill at $200 an hour.

Price accordingly.

**Tweet 6:**
The fear behind underpricing is almost always "what if they say no."

Most people would rather be busy at low rates than risk rejection at high rates.

That's a fear problem dressed up as a pricing problem.

**Tweet 7:**
When you raise your rates, two things happen.

Some people leave. Better clients appear.

This is not a coincidence. Higher prices filter for buyers who value results over cost.

**Tweet 8:**
Your pricing is a positioning statement.

Low prices say: I'm affordable.

High prices say: I'm the person who gets results, and serious buyers come to me.

Which message do you want to send?

**Tweet 9:**
You don't need to justify your price to the right client.

The right client has already done the math.

If you're constantly justifying, you're talking to the wrong people.

**Tweet 10:**
The fastest way to test a higher price: charge it to the next person who asks.

Not in three months. The next person.

The data you get from a real ask is worth more than a month of thinking about it.

**Final Tweet:**
Raise your prices.

Not 20%. Consider doubling them.

The discomfort you feel is not a signal that you're wrong. It's a signal that you've been undercharging.

"Here's what I tried, here's what broke, here's what I changed."

Every post is a lesson. Your audience learns and you earn credibility.

**Tweet 9:**

Weekly updates over six months build more trust than three viral posts.

People trust the pattern, not the performance.

That's not luck. That's what consistent building in public produces.

Keep the thesis clear. Keep the lessons coming.

The audience builds itself around a story worth following.

You trade active time for a period of years to build an asset.

That asset then produces income at a ratio better than 1:1 time-to-dollar.

**Tweet 6:**

They want the output without the build phase.

That's why most passive income attempts fail inside six months.

**Tweet 7:**

Then the asset starts returning.

**Tweet 8:**

Content is the distribution channel. Most people don't build it.

**Tweet 9:**

But you need to stop calling it passive in the build phase.

It's deferred income. You're buying your future time with your present time.

**Tweet 10:**

**Final Tweet:**
Passive income is not a shortcut.

It's a long trade. Present labor for future freedom.

Make the trade with your eyes open and it's one of the best decisions you can make.

## Thread 530: Escaping the Freelance Trap

**Tweet 1:**
Most freelancers don't have a business.

They have a job where they're the boss and also the employee.

Here's how to escape the trap and build something that actually scales: 🧵

**Tweet 2:**
The freelance trap has a clear definition.

You cannot take a week off without losing income.

If that's true for you, you have a job. The client is just different every month.

**Tweet 3:**
The trap closes when your income is tied entirely to your time.

Every dollar you earn requires a direct hour of your labor.

This caps your income and eliminates your freedom. That's not what you left the 9-5 for.

**Tweet 4:**
The first escape route: productize your service.

Instead of custom work for every client, you build a defined scope, fixed price, repeatable process.

You do the same thing every time, better every time, faster every time.

**Tweet 5:**
Productized services are easier to sell, easier to deliver, and easier to scale.

The client knows exactly what they're getting.

You know exactly what you're delivering. Friction drops on both sides.

**Tweet 6:**
The second escape route: build a digital product alongside your service.

Take the most common question your clients ask you.

Turn the answer into a product. Sell it to people who can't afford you yet.

**Tweet 7:**
Now you have two income streams.

Service income: high per client, time-limited.

Product income: low per unit, no time cap.

Over time, you shift the ratio in favor of the product.

**Tweet 8:**
The third escape route: build an audience while you work.

Document what you learn. Share your process. Teach the concepts behind your skill.

Your audience becomes a pipeline that doesn't require cold outreach.

**Tweet 9:**
Most freelancers resist these routes because they feel like more work.

They are more work. For a while.

The question is whether you want to still be trading time for dollars in five years.

**Tweet 10:**
The identity shift is the hardest part.

Freelancer: I sell my time.
Business owner: I build systems that produce value.

The work changes when the identity changes.

**Final Tweet:**
You started freelancing for freedom.

Make sure the business you're building actually delivers that.

Productize. Build assets. Grow an audience. Then you have a business.
