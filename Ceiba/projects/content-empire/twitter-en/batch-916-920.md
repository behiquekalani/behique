## Tweet 918

Thread: What I learned from studying people who actually changed their lives.

1/ They did not wait until conditions were perfect. They adjusted conditions to make starting possible.

2/ They did not overhaul everything at once. They changed one thing. Held it. Let it change adjacent things. Repeated.

3/ They were honest with themselves about what was avoidance versus what was legitimate caution. Most people cannot make that distinction under pressure.

4/ They had a reason that was not about looking impressive. Identity shift, not performance. The ones performing eventually ran out of audience energy to sustain it.

5/ They failed in ways that informed the next attempt rather than ending the pursuit entirely. The failure was a data point, not a verdict.

6/ None of them had a straight line. The trajectory only looked clean in retrospect. In real time it was a mess that they kept moving through.

## Tweet 920

Thread: The problem with optimizing everything.

1/ Optimization culture tells you to measure everything, improve every variable, eliminate every inefficiency. This sounds rigorous. It causes a specific kind of damage.

2/ Over-optimization kills the experimental mindset. You start running plays instead of exploring territory. The map becomes more real than the ground.

3/ Some of the best things you build will come from wandering. From following something that interested you without a clear ROI. You cannot optimize your way to those.

4/ High optimization is appropriate when the direction is confirmed and the goal is execution. It is destructive when the direction is still being discovered.

5/ Know which phase you are in. Exploration requires loose hands. Execution requires tight ones. Applying execution mode during exploration is how you build the right thing in the wrong direction.

6/ Keep some territory in your life that is unoptimized. That is where you find the things worth optimizing later.
