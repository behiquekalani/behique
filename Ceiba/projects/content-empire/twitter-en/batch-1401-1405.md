## Thread 1401: Stop optimizing for the wrong thing

1/ Most people optimize for the thing they can measure, not the thing that actually matters. That gap between measurable and meaningful is where most creative businesses die.

2/ You can measure likes. You cannot measure trust. You optimize for likes and wonder why people are not buying.

3/ You can measure post frequency. You cannot measure retention. You post every day and wonder why people are not coming back.

4/ You can measure traffic. You cannot measure intent. You chase SEO and wonder why your email list has a 10% open rate.

5/ The answer is not to stop measuring. It is to get better at measuring the right things. Open rate over follower count. Replies over likes. Repeat purchases over new customers.

6/ The metrics closest to money are the ones most people ignore because they are harder to grow. Growing trust is slow. Growing a loyal audience is slow. Growing repeat customers is slow.

7/ Slow is fine. Slow and real beats fast and hollow every time. Build toward the metrics that are hard to game.

8/ Follow for thinking on what actually builds a creative business. Repost this to someone optimizing for vanity metrics.

## Thread 1402: The morning routine no one talks about

1/ Every high-performer has a morning routine. But most people copy the ritual without understanding the purpose behind it. Here is what the routine is actually doing.

2/ A morning routine is not about productivity. It is about signal. It tells your nervous system that today is intentional, not reactive. It sets the tone before the world has a chance to set it for you.

3/ The specific activities matter less than the consistency. Whether you journal, exercise, read, or sit quietly, the point is that you chose how to start. That choice is the practice.

4/ When you start your day reactively, checking email and social media before you have done anything intentional, you hand the first hour of your cognition to other people's priorities.

5/ That first hour shapes the cognitive tone of the entire day. Reactive starts produce reactive days. Intentional starts produce intentional days. The math is that simple.

6/ Your morning routine does not need to be long or complicated. It needs to be yours. 20 minutes of something you chose, before anything that someone else asked of you.

7/ The most protected part of your day is the part before the world wakes up. Do not give it away by default.

8/ Follow for thinking on designing your day with intention. Share this with someone who checks their phone the second they wake up.

## Thread 1403: Why most writers quit before they get good

1/ Most writers quit before they produce their best work. The timing of the quit is not random. There is a specific phase where almost everyone considers stopping.

2/ It happens around month 4 to 6. The initial excitement is gone. The audience is still small. The improvement feels invisible. The gap between what you can see and what you can produce is painful.

3/ This gap is called the taste gap. Ira Glass described it. You have developed enough taste to recognize that your writing is not yet where you want it to be, but you have not yet built the skill to close that gap.

4/ Most people experience this as proof they are not a writer. It is the opposite. The taste gap exists because you are becoming a writer. You cannot see the gap until your taste is developed enough to see it.

5/ The only way through the taste gap is to keep writing. Not to write better on the first try. To write more often until skill catches up to taste.

6/ Every writer who produces work you love lived through this phase. They are not telling you about it because they are on the other side now. But they were here.

7/ Write your way through. The taste gap is a milestone, not a dead end.

8/ Follow for honest talk about what the writing process actually looks like. Repost this for a writer who is thinking about quitting.

## Thread 1404: The real reason you are not making money online

1/ You are not making money online because of a misdiagnosis. Most people think they have a traffic problem or a skills problem. They actually have a trust problem.

2/ Trust is the only thing that converts attention into money. Without it, you can have all the traffic in the world and still earn nothing.

3/ Trust is built through three things: showing up consistently, being honest about what you know and what you do not, and delivering more value than people expect.

4/ Most creators do one of these. The best creators do all three consistently over a long enough time that trust becomes a durable asset.

5/ Here is what kills trust: disappearing for months, then reappearing to sell something. Overselling results. Hiding failures. Posting content that is not connected to a real point of view.

6/ Here is what builds trust: being there every week. Sharing real thinking, not just curated wins. Saying "I do not know" when you do not know. Delivering on every promise you make in your content.

7/ If you are not earning, audit your trust. Are you showing up consistently? Are you honest? Are you delivering? Fix the trust and the money follows.

8/ Follow for direct thinking on building a creator business that actually converts. Repost this to a creator who is getting traffic but not sales.

## Thread 1405: The thing about energy that nobody explains

1/ Time management is dead. The new constraint is not hours. It is energy. You can have all the time in the world and produce nothing if you are running on empty.

2/ High energy and low energy hours are not equal. An hour of focused, high-energy work produces more than three hours of distracted, depleted work. The difference is not effort. It is state.

3/ Most people spend their highest energy hours on the wrong tasks. They clear easy things first to build momentum. Then they attempt their most important work on leftover energy.

4/ Flip it. Protect your high-energy hours for high-value work. Use low-energy hours for tasks that require less cognitive load. Administration, email, scheduling, and consumption can wait.

5/ Energy also requires active management, not just passive conservation. Sleep, movement, real nutrition, real rest. These are not nice-to-haves. They are inputs to your most important output.

6/ Track your energy for one week. When are you sharpest? When do you hit the wall? Design your work schedule around those patterns. The calendar should serve your biology, not the other way around.

7/ The highest output version of you is not the one working the most hours. It is the one protecting the right hours.

8/ Follow for frameworks on designing work around how you actually function. Share this with someone who is exhausted and confusing busyness with productivity.
