## Thread 1406: Most people will never build anything because they're too busy consuming content about building things.

1/ Most people will never build anything because they're too busy consuming content about building things.

2/ There's a version of learning that is actually avoidance. You feel productive. You're not building anything. The feeling of progress is not the same as progress.

3/ Consuming content about writing is not writing. Watching videos about business is not building a business. Reading about fitness is not training. The activity and the preparation for the activity are not the same thing.

4/ The internet has made this trap more comfortable than ever. You can spend 12 hours a day feeling productive without ever creating anything real. Every recommendation algorithm is designed to keep you consuming more, not producing more.

5/ The sign you've crossed from learning into avoidance: you already know what to do next, but you're looking for one more piece of information before you start. That is not learning. That is fear with good justifications.

6/ The fix is uncomfortable. Stop consuming in the area you're avoiding. For 30 days, no more content about the thing you're trying to build. Just build. The discomfort will be proportional to how long you've been hiding.

7/ Real learning happens in the doing. You will learn more from one week of building than from one month of studying. The feedback loop of shipping is the education system nobody is selling you.

8/ If this thread felt uncomfortable, that's the signal. Follow for more. Share this with someone still in the preparation phase.

## Thread 1407: Charging more is the most underused growth strategy for service businesses.

1/ Charging more is the most underused growth strategy for service businesses. Most people compete on price when they should compete on results.

2/ When you're cheap, you attract clients who chose you because of the price. Those clients are the most demanding, the least trusting, and the most likely to nickel-and-dime every decision. You end up doing more work for less money and less respect.

3/ Premium clients are different. They hired you because they believe you can get the result. They trust your judgment. They give you space to work. They're not watching every dollar. They're watching the outcome.

4/ The counterintuitive reality: raising your prices often makes your business easier to run, not harder. You work with fewer clients, but better ones. Your revenue stays the same or grows. Your stress decreases.

5/ You don't earn premium pricing by having a better website. You earn it by having better proof. Case studies. Testimonials. Track record. Every piece of evidence you have makes the higher number harder to argue with.

6/ The psychological block is simple. You don't believe you're worth the higher price yet. That belief will not fix itself through more thinking. It fixes through raising the price, getting clients anyway, and updating the belief with evidence.

7/ Start by raising your rate by 20% on the next new client. Not a current client. A new one. See what happens. Most people are surprised by how little resistance they encounter.

8/ Follow for more on building a sustainable service business. RT if you've been undercharging longer than you want to admit.

## Thread 1408: You don't need a large audience. You need a trusted one.

1/ You don't need a large audience. You need a trusted one. These are not the same thing and most creators chase the wrong one.

2/ A large audience that doesn't trust you is just a vanity metric. You can have 50,000 followers and launch a product to crickets. It happens constantly. Big numbers, zero conversion.

3/ A small audience that trusts you deeply is a business. 500 people who open every email, engage with every post, and buy what you recommend will generate more revenue than 50,000 passive scrollers.

4/ Trust is built through consistency, honesty, and demonstrated expertise. It takes time. There are no shortcuts. The creators who skip this step and try to monetize too early usually burn the relationship before it's worth anything.

5/ The metric to track is not follower count. It's engagement rate. Open rates. Reply rates. The number of people who actually respond when you post something. That's the real audience size.

6/ When you have trust, launching something is a conversation with people who already believe in you. When you don't have trust, it's a cold pitch to strangers. One of these is significantly easier.

7/ Build the trust first. Be consistent. Be honest. Give more than you take. When you eventually ask for something, the people who trust you will show up.

8/ Follow if you're building a real audience, not just a big one. Share this with creators who are chasing numbers over trust.

## Thread 1409: The difference between people who build and people who plan to build is one decision.

1/ The difference between people who build and people who plan to build is one decision made at a specific moment. Usually it's not a dramatic one.

2/ There is no moment when you feel completely ready. No point where the plan is perfect, the timing is ideal, and the fear disappears. That moment doesn't exist. People who wait for it don't build anything.

3/ The people who do build things make a decision to start before they're ready. Not because they're braver. Because they understand that readiness is earned through doing, not through preparing.

4/ The first version of everything is bad. This is a feature, not a bug. The first version exists only to generate the feedback that makes the second version better. You cannot get to version two without publishing version one.

5/ Fear doesn't go away before you start. It goes away after you start, if you're lucky. More often it just becomes less relevant as the work gets interesting. The doing overrides the fear.

6/ Every person you admire who has built something had a moment where they clicked publish, sent the email, or launched the product with a knot in their stomach. The knot is not a warning. It's the feeling of something real.

7/ The decision is not "should I do this?" You already know the answer. The decision is "am I going to do this today or am I going to keep waiting?" Today is the right answer.

8/ Follow for more on building things that matter. RT if you've been waiting for a readiness that was never coming.

## Thread 1410: Writing online is the highest return activity for most knowledge workers. Most don't do it.

1/ Writing online is the highest return activity for most knowledge workers. Most don't do it because it's uncomfortable and the results aren't immediate.

2/ When you write online consistently, several things happen simultaneously. You clarify your thinking. You build a track record. You attract people who resonate with your ideas. You create assets that compound over time.

3/ A blog post or Twitter thread published today can generate traffic, leads, and opportunities for years. Your hourly rate at a job stops paying the moment you stop working. Content keeps paying after you're done creating it.

4/ Writing also builds credibility in a way that nothing else does. When someone searches your name and finds a year of consistent, thoughtful writing, they trust you before you ever speak. That trust is pre-sold authority.

5/ The quality bar is lower than you think. You do not need to be a professional writer. You need to be someone who thinks clearly and shares honestly. Most people are capable of that. Most people don't try.

6/ Start with what you know. The things that feel obvious to you because you've been living them are not obvious to someone who hasn't. Your experience is content. Your perspective is content. Your lessons are content.

7/ The only thing writing online requires is starting. Publish something imperfect. See what happens. Adjust. Repeat. The compounding starts the moment you publish the first thing.

8/ Follow if you're building a writing habit. Share this with someone who keeps saying they'll start soon.
