## Thread 546: The Substack vs Self-Hosted Newsletter Decision
**Tweet 1:** Substack or self-hosted? This is one of the most common questions in the creator economy. The answer depends on one thing: what stage you are actually at.

**Tweet 2:** Substack's value proposition is distribution plus simplicity. You get discovery features, a built-in reader app, and zero setup time. You can publish in ten minutes.

**Tweet 3:** The tradeoff: Substack takes 10% of paid revenue. You do not own your subscriber list in a fully portable way. And the platform owns the interface.

**Tweet 4:** Self-hosted (ConvertKit, Beehiiv, Ghost) gives you full ownership. Custom domain. Full data portability. Better monetization rates. More control over design and automation.

**Tweet 5:** The tradeoff: you handle growth entirely on your own. No discovery algorithm. No built-in reader network. You are responsible for every new subscriber.

**Tweet 6:** Here is the actual decision framework. If you have under 500 subscribers and are still validating whether you can write consistently, use Substack. The friction of setup will stop you before you start.

**Tweet 7:** If you are over 1,000 subscribers and have a consistent publishing habit, migrate to a self-hosted platform. The 10% cut on paid subscriptions alone justifies the move at that scale.

**Tweet 8:** If you have any serious plans to build a business around your newsletter, self-hosted is not optional. You need email automation, segmentation, and the ability to run sequences. Substack does not do this.

**Tweet 9:** The one thing Substack has that nothing else does: the Notes feed. If you are growing purely through organic discovery and Substack's network, that has real value. Weigh it honestly.

**Tweet 10:** Many serious newsletter operators run both. A Substack for discovery. A self-hosted list for monetization and automation. It is more work, but it is a hedge worth considering at scale.

**Tweet 11:** The worst move: spending three months optimizing your platform choice before writing a single issue. Platform is infrastructure. Content is the business. Start writing.

**Final Tweet:** Substack for starting. Self-hosted for scaling. The best newsletter platform is the one you actually publish on consistently. The rest is noise.

## Thread 547: LinkedIn as a Creator Platform in 2025
**Tweet 1:** LinkedIn in 2025 is the most underrated platform for solopreneurs and B2B creators. Most people are using it wrong. Here is what actually works.

**Tweet 2:** LinkedIn is not a resume platform. It has not been that for years. It is a professional content feed with organic reach that most other platforms stopped offering in 2018.

**Tweet 3:** The audience profile is different from Twitter or Instagram. LinkedIn readers have budgets. They make decisions. They are specifically looking for vendors, tools, and perspectives that apply to their work.

**Tweet 4:** The content that performs: short-form professional insights, career lessons framed as business principles, contrarian takes on industry norms, honest process breakdowns.

**Tweet 5:** The content that gets ignored: repurposed motivational quotes, fake humility posts, excessive self-promotion, anything that looks like it was written for a press release.

**Tweet 6:** LinkedIn's algorithm rewards comments over likes. A post with 20 substantive comments will outperform a post with 200 likes. Write posts that generate actual responses.

**Tweet 7:** The best format for LinkedIn in 2025 is the single-point essay. One idea. Three to five short paragraphs. A clear thesis in the first line that people will see before clicking "see more."

**Tweet 8:** Personal stories with business lessons still outperform pure information posts. Not because people want your life story, but because narrative creates context. Context creates retention.

**Tweet 9:** The most underused LinkedIn strategy: direct outreach after a post performs. A post gets 1,000 impressions. Ten people engage meaningfully. Message those ten people. That is warmer than any cold email.

**Tweet 10:** Consistency on LinkedIn compounds differently than other platforms. Six months of weekly publishing will build you a reputation in your industry that is very hard to replicate anywhere else.

**Tweet 11:** The creators making serious revenue from LinkedIn are not treating it as a broadcast channel. They are treating it as a trust-building engine that eventually converts readers into clients.

**Final Tweet:** LinkedIn is not the exciting platform. It is the profitable one. If your work has a B2B application, you should be publishing there consistently. The competition is not as strong as it looks.

## Thread 548: The Micro-Course vs Full Course Debate
**Tweet 1:** Should you build a full course or a micro-course? Most creators default to the full course because it sounds more substantial. That is usually the wrong call.

**Tweet 2:** A full course is 4-8 hours of content, typically priced $200-$500. It takes months to build. Most students never finish it. Your completion rate is probably under 20%.

**Tweet 3:** A micro-course is one specific transformation in under two hours, priced $27-$97. It takes weeks to build. Students finish it. They feel like they got what they paid for.

**Tweet 4:** The business case for micro-courses: faster to validate, faster to build, lower buyer friction, higher completion rates, and easier to use as a front-end offer to a higher-ticket product.

**Tweet 5:** Full courses make sense when the transformation requires depth. Learning to code. Building a financial model. Developing a creative practice. These are inherently multi-week commitments.

**Tweet 6:** Micro-courses make sense when there is a discrete problem with a discrete solution. How to write a cold email. How to set up your Notion workspace. How to price your first offer.

**Tweet 7:** The validation test: before building either, can you sell 10 people on the outcome in a 30-minute conversation? If not, the problem is not course length. The problem is market fit.

**Tweet 8:** A common trap: creating a full course as the first product because it feels more legitimate. Legitimacy comes from the result you produce, not the hour count of your content.

**Tweet 9:** Revenue math that creators ignore. A $47 micro-course sold to 100 people equals $4,700. A $497 full course sold to 100 people equals $49,700. The full course wins on paper. In practice, the $47 course is 20x easier to sell.

**Tweet 10:** The best model for 2025: start with a micro-course. Use it to identify who your best students are. Build the full course for those people, using their questions and gaps as your outline.

**Final Tweet:** Do not build a full course to feel serious. Build the micro-course first, sell it, and let your students tell you what they want next. The market will write your curriculum if you let it.

## Thread 549: Ghostwriting as a Business Model
**Tweet 1:** Ghostwriting is one of the cleanest service businesses you can build in 2025. Low overhead. High margin. Genuine demand. Most people dismiss it. Here is why they are wrong.

**Tweet 2:** The demand for ghostwriting has never been higher. LinkedIn, newsletters, Twitter, podcast scripts, books. Every executive, founder, and expert with an audience and no time is a potential client.

**Tweet 3:** The common objection: "I am writing their content, not mine. Does that count?" Yes. Your name is not on it, but your skill is building it. You are paid for the outcome. The attribution is the client's business, not yours.

**Tweet 4:** Ghostwriting rates in 2025. Twitter ghostwriting: $1,500-$5,000/month per client. LinkedIn: $2,000-$6,000/month. Newsletter ghostwriting: $2,000-$8,000/month. Book ghostwriting: $15,000-$50,000 per project.

**Tweet 5:** You do not need a portfolio of named work to get started. You need a sample. Write five threads, three newsletter issues, or two LinkedIn posts in the voice of a hypothetical client. That is your portfolio.

**Tweet 6:** How to find clients: start with your existing network. Who do you know that posts inconsistently, has something worth saying, and a business that benefits from their visibility? That is your first pitch.

**Tweet 7:** The skill gap most ghostwriters have: they can write well, but they cannot capture someone else's voice. Voice capture is the actual service. The writing comes after.

**Tweet 8:** Voice capture process: read 50 pieces of their existing content, interview them for 60 minutes about their beliefs and vocabulary, write a draft, expect to revise. Repeat until you can predict what they would say.

**Tweet 9:** The business model works best as a retainer, not project work. Monthly retainers mean predictable revenue for you and consistent output for the client. Project work means constant reselling.

**Tweet 10:** The ceiling on ghostwriting is real if you stay solo. Two to four clients at $3,000-$5,000/month is roughly $100K/year. To scale further, you productize, hire writers, or raise prices for a smaller, higher-paying client list.

**Tweet 11:** Many ghostwriters eventually build their own audience on the side. The irony: writing for others every day makes you a better writer faster than almost anything else.

**Final Tweet:** Ghostwriting is not a compromise. It is a business. One with low startup costs, real demand, and a skill set that compounds. If you can write, you can start tomorrow.

## Thread 550: The Annual Business Review Framework
**Tweet 1:** Most solopreneurs never do a real annual review. They look at revenue, feel something, and move on. Here is the framework that actually surfaces what matters.

**Tweet 2:** The purpose of an annual review is not to celebrate or punish yourself. It is to see clearly. What worked, what did not, and what assumptions you were carrying that turned out to be wrong.

**Tweet 3:** Start with the revenue audit. Total revenue by source. Which offers generated what percentage? Which clients took the most time relative to what they paid? Where was the highest ROI per hour?

**Tweet 4:** Then do the time audit. Where did your hours actually go? Not where you intended them to go. Where they went. Use your calendar, your invoices, your memory. Most people discover they spent more time on low-value work than they realized.

**Tweet 5:** Next: offers audit. Which offers are you proud of? Which ones do you feel mixed about when you deliver them? What did you promise versus what you actually delivered? The gap between those two is your product roadmap.

**Tweet 6:** Then: energy audit. Which clients energized you? Which drained you? Which projects felt meaningful? Which felt like you were going through motions? This is data. Not just feelings.

**Tweet 7:** After that: belief audit. What did you believe at the start of the year that you no longer believe? What assumptions drove decisions that did not pan out? Write them down. They are expensive if they carry into next year.

**Tweet 8:** Now look forward. Based on everything above, what is the single most important thing you could change in the next 90 days? Not a list. One thing. The clearest leverage point.

**Tweet 9:** Most people skip directly to goals. Goals without a clear audit behind them are just wishes. The audit is what makes the goal real because it tells you what you are actually building on.

**Tweet 10:** The annual review also forces you to acknowledge what you have built. Builders with ADHD and high ambition often skip past wins so fast they cannot feel the momentum. The review slows that down intentionally.

**Tweet 11:** Schedule this review annually, but run a lighter version quarterly. The discipline of regular reflection is what separates builders who compound from builders who restart every year.

**Final Tweet:** You do not need a retreat or a 40-page template. You need honest answers to hard questions. Revenue, time, offers, energy, beliefs. One sitting. Two hours. Do it before the new year starts without you.
