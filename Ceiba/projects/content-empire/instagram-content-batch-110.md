## Post 1 — Stop Building. Start Validating.

**Hook:** Most people build for 3 months and then find out nobody wants it. Validation takes 3 days.

**Slide 1:** Most digital products are built in secret and launched to silence. Validation prevents that.

**Slide 2:** The mistake: assume you know what people want, build the full product, then try to sell it. This is backwards.

**Slide 3:** The right order: find a real problem first, confirm people would pay to solve it, then build exactly what they told you they'd pay for.

**Slide 4:** Validation does not require a finished product. It requires a clear problem statement and a way to gauge whether people would trade money for the solution.

**Slide 5:** Three days of validation is worth more than three months of building in the wrong direction.

**Slide 6:** Validate before you build. Every time. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:** The most common reason digital products don't sell is not bad design or bad copy. It's the wrong product. The creator built what they thought people wanted instead of what the market confirmed it needed.

Validation is the step that makes everything else work. It is not optional. It is not something you do after you build. It is the first thing you do.

The validation does not need to be complex. It needs to confirm one thing: are there real people with this real problem who would pay real money to solve it.

If yes, build. If no, find a different problem.

The three-day validation sprint beats the three-month build-in-secret approach by removing the biggest risk in product creation: building something nobody wants.

## Post 2 — The 5-Step Validation Sprint

**Hook:** You can validate a digital product idea in under 72 hours. Here's the process.

**Slide 1:** The 72-hour product validation sprint. No product required.

**Slide 2:** Step 1 — Search the problem. Reddit, Facebook groups, YouTube comments. Are people actively complaining about this problem? If nobody is talking about it, it's not a burning problem.

**Slide 3:** Step 2 — Check what's already selling. Gumroad, Etsy, Amazon. If similar products exist and have reviews, demand is confirmed. If nothing exists, be suspicious.

**Slide 4:** Step 3 — Post the idea. Share the concept with your audience. Not "I'm building this" — share the problem and ask if it resonates. Measure comments, DMs, and saves.

**Slide 5:** Step 4 — Presell. Write a simple description of the product, set a price, and link to a waitlist or payment page before the product exists. Real sales are real validation.

**Slide 6:** Step 5 — Decide. If you got responses, build it. If you didn't, move to the next idea. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:** Five steps. Seventy-two hours. You either have validation or you have a reason to move to the next idea.

The most important step is the presell. Not a waitlist. Not a survey. An actual payment page with a real price. If someone puts money down before the product exists, you have the strongest possible validation signal.

Most people are afraid to presell because they feel it's dishonest to sell something that isn't built yet. It's not. It's how most professional product teams confirm demand before committing resources to a build. It's also how you confirm your price point before it's too late to change it.

If nobody buys the presell, that is not a failure. That is the system working correctly. You found out in 72 hours instead of 3 months.

## Post 3 — How to Read the Market Before You Build

**Hook:** The market is always telling you what it wants to buy. Most people don't know how to listen.

**Slide 1:** The market tells you what to build. Most people never listen to it.

**Slide 2:** Signal 1 — Reddit and forums. Search your niche on Reddit. Sort by top posts of all time. The most upvoted posts show you what the community cares most about. The problems in the comments are your product brief.

**Slide 3:** Signal 2 — YouTube comments. Find the top videos in your niche. Read the comment sections, especially on beginner content. "I wish this video also covered..." is a product idea.

**Slide 4:** Signal 3 — Gumroad and Etsy bestsellers. What's selling and at what price? What do the reviews say the product did well and what did it miss? That gap is your product.

**Slide 5:** Signal 4 — What people DM you about. If you already have any audience at all, the questions people ask you are your best product roadmap. You are already the person they want to learn from.

**Slide 6:** The market is not silent. You just need to know where to look. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:** Product research is not guessing. It's listening.

The market has already told you what it wants through the content people engage with most, the products people buy and review, the questions people ask in communities, and the comments people leave on videos that almost cover what they need.

You don't need to invent a new problem. You need to find an existing, painful, widespread problem that the current solutions don't fully solve.

The combination of Reddit research, YouTube comment mining, and competitor product review analysis gives you a clearer product brief than any focus group could produce. It's real people, in real time, describing their real frustrations in their own words.

Read the market before you build. Then build exactly what you read.

## Post 4 — The Presell Test and Why It's the Only Validation That Counts

**Hook:** The only real validation is money. Everything else is feedback.

**Slide 1:** Surveys lie. Comments mislead. A presale does not.

**Slide 2:** When you ask people if they'd buy your product, they say yes out of politeness. When you give them a payment link, the truth reveals itself.

**Slide 3:** How to run a presell: write a clear product description with price and delivery timeline. Share it with your warmest audience first. Collect real payments or genuine pre-orders.

**Slide 4:** What counts as validation: 3-10 real sales before the product is finished. Not 100 survey responses. Not 50 likes. Real money from real people.

**Slide 5:** What to do if nobody buys: don't build the product. Adjust the problem, the price, or the audience, then test again. This is the process working correctly.

**Slide 6:** Soft validation feels good. Hard validation builds businesses. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:** There is a hierarchy of validation signals and money is at the top.

Someone following your account is weak validation. Someone liking a post about your product idea is weak validation. Someone filling out a survey saying they'd be interested is weak validation. Someone clicking a payment link and putting in their credit card details is strong validation.

The gap between "I'd probably buy that" and "here is my money" is enormous. It measures real intent, real price sensitivity, and real timing.

Most solopreneurs are afraid of the presell because they fear no one will buy. That fear is exactly the reason to run the presell. If no one buys, you learned the most valuable thing you could possibly learn before spending weeks building the product.

Run the presell. Accept the result. Build or pivot accordingly.

## Post 5 — The 3 Signs a Product Idea Is Worth Building

**Hook:** 3 specific signals that tell you an idea is worth your time. Stop guessing.

**Slide 1:** You don't have to guess whether a product idea is worth building. There are 3 measurable signals.

**Slide 2:** Signal 1 — Existing paid demand. Similar products with real reviews already exist. This confirms people pay to solve this problem. Your job is to do it better, cheaper, or for a more specific audience.

**Slide 3:** Signal 2 — Search volume. The problem is searched for actively. Google Trends, YouTube search suggestions, or Reddit thread volume confirms that people are looking for solutions. If nobody is searching, nobody is suffering enough to pay.

**Slide 4:** Signal 3 — Personal experience. You have solved this problem yourself or for others. You are not speculating about the pain. You've lived it or watched someone else live it. Products built from real experience convert better.

**Slide 5:** All three signals together: this is a strong build. Two out of three: build it with lower confidence. One out of three: validate harder before committing.

**Slide 6:** Check all three before building anything. Follow @behikeai.

**Caption:** Most product ideas fail the three-signal test and their creators build them anyway based on intuition and enthusiasm.

Intuition and enthusiasm are useful. They're not sufficient.

Existing paid demand tells you the market is real. This is the most important signal because it means real people have already voted with real money on this category of problem.

Search volume tells you the problem is active. If people are searching for it regularly, the pain is ongoing, not situational.

Personal experience tells you you're the right person to solve it. You understand the problem at a depth that shows in the product. Buyers can feel the difference between a guide written by someone who's lived the problem and one written by someone who researched it.

Three signals. Check all three. Then build.
