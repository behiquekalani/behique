## 1. COPYRIGHT VS TRADEMARK VS PATENT

| Protection | What It Covers | Duration | Cost | Best For |
|-----------|---------------|----------|------|----------|
| **Copyright** | Original creative works (text, images, code, audio, video) | Life of author + 70 years | $45-$85 online | Ebooks, courses, templates, guides, code |
| **Trademark** | Brand names, logos, slogans used in commerce | Indefinite (renew every 10 years) | $350+ per class | Brand names like "Behike", logos |
| **Patent** | Inventions, novel processes, useful machines | 20 years | $1,500-$15,000+ | Software inventions, unique algorithms |
| **Trade Secret** | Confidential business info with economic value | Indefinite (as long as secret) | $0 (just keep it secret) | Proprietary methods, client lists, pricing |

**For digital creators selling ebooks, templates, and courses: copyright and trademark are your two main tools.** Patents are rarely relevant unless you invent a novel technical process.

## 2. COPYRIGHT FOR DIGITAL PRODUCTS

### What qualifies for copyright protection:

- Ebooks and written guides (yes, including PDFs)
- Online course content (scripts, slides, worksheets, video recordings)
- Excel/Google Sheets templates with original structure and design
- HTML/CSS/JavaScript applications (like a meditation app)
- Original images, graphics, and design work
- Audio content (narration, music, sound design)
- Software code

### What does NOT qualify:

- Facts, data, or information by themselves
- Ideas, concepts, or methods (more on this below)
- Titles, names, short phrases (these may qualify for trademark instead)
- Commonly used layouts or standard templates with no creative originality
- Government works

### The key test: Is it an "original work of authorship fixed in a tangible medium"?

If you wrote it, designed it, or coded it, and it shows some minimal creativity, it qualifies. The bar for "originality" is low. It does not need to be good, novel, or unique. It just needs to be yours and not copied.

## 3. AUTOMATIC COPYRIGHT VS REGISTRATION

### Automatic copyright (you already have this)

The moment you create an original work and save it to a file, you own the copyright. No registration needed. This has been the law since 1978.

**What automatic copyright gives you:**
- Ownership of the work
- The right to control reproduction, distribution, and derivative works
- The ability to license or sell the work

**What automatic copyright does NOT give you:**
- The ability to sue for infringement in federal court (registration required first)
- Eligibility for statutory damages ($750 to $150,000 per infringement)
- Eligibility for attorney's fees recovery
- A public record of your ownership
- Legal presumption of validity (makes proving ownership easier)

### Registration (strongly recommended for anything you sell)

Registration is optional but powerful. It is the difference between "I own this" and "I can prove I own this and make someone pay for stealing it."

**When to register:**
- Before you publish/sell (ideal, gives maximum protection)
- Within 3 months of publication (still gets you statutory damages)
- After infringement happens (you can still register, but lose statutory damages for past infringement)

**Bottom line:** If you are selling a digital product for money, register it. The $45-$65 cost is nothing compared to what you lose if someone steals it and you can't sue effectively.

## 4. HOW TO REGISTER A COPYRIGHT

### Step-by-step process:

**1. Go to the US Copyright Office Electronic Copyright Office (eCO) portal**
- URL: https://www.copyright.gov/registration/
- Create an account if you don't have one

**2. Choose your application type:**
- **Single Application ($45):** One work, one author, one claimant. Use this for a single ebook, single template, etc.
- **Standard Application ($65):** Multiple authors, works made for hire, or anything more complex.
- **Group of Unpublished Works ($85):** Up to 10 unpublished works by the same author. Good for batching templates or guides before launch.

**3. Fill out the application:**
- Type of work (Literary Work for ebooks/guides, Visual Arts for templates with heavy design, Computer Program for code)
- Title of work
- Author information (your legal name)
- Year of creation and publication
- Rights and permissions contact

**4. Upload your deposit copy:**
- For digital-only works: upload the actual file (PDF, EPUB, etc.)
- For published works: upload the "best edition" in digital format
- File size limits apply. Check current limits on the portal.

**5. Pay the fee**

**6. Wait:**
- Current processing time: 3-8 months for online applications
- You will receive a certificate of registration by mail/email
- Your registration is effective from the date you submitted, not when it's processed

### Costs summary:

| Type | Filing Fee | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Single application (online) | $45 | One work, one author |
| Standard application (online) | $65 | Most common |
| Group of unpublished works (online) | $85 | Up to 10 works |
| Paper filing | $125 | Slower, not recommended |
| Expedited processing (special handling) | $800 | Only for litigation or urgent needs |

## 5. COPYRIGHTING FRAMEWORKS AND METHODOLOGIES

### The hard truth: You cannot copyright a framework, method, or system.

This is explicitly stated in 17 U.S.C. Section 102(b):

> "In no case does copyright protection for an original work of authorship extend to any idea, procedure, process, system, method of operation, concept, principle, or discovery."

### What this means for something like the "One Person Business" framework:

- You CANNOT copyright the concept of a "one person business" or the steps in a methodology
- You CANNOT stop someone from teaching the same concepts
- You CAN copyright the specific way you express those ideas (your exact words, your specific diagrams, your unique examples, your particular arrangement of content)

### How to protect a framework in practice:

1. **Copyright your expression.** Write the framework in your own words with your own examples. Register the written/video/audio content. Nobody can copy your specific text or presentation.

2. **Trademark the name.** If you create a branded framework name (like "OPB Framework" or "Behike Method"), trademark that name. Nobody can use your branded name in commerce.

3. **Trade secret the proprietary parts.** If your framework includes specific tools, templates, or processes that give competitive advantage, keep the details confidential. Share the concept publicly but keep the implementation behind a paywall.

4. **Terms of service on your products.** Include license terms that restrict redistribution, reselling, and derivative works. This is contractual protection, not IP law, but it is enforceable.

5. **Build a brand, not just a product.** The real moat is your brand, your audience, and your unique perspective. Ideas are cheap. Execution and trust are not.

## 6. TRADEMARKING A BRAND NAME

### Should you trademark "Behike" or "BehikeAI"?

**Yes, if:**
- You are actively using the name in commerce (selling products/services under it)
- You plan to build a recognizable brand around it
- You want to prevent others from using the same or confusingly similar name

**Before you apply, do a trademark search:**
- USPTO Trademark Search: https://tmsearch.uspto.gov/
- Search for "Behike" and variations (Behique, BehikeAI, etc.)
- Also search Google, social media, and domain registrars for conflicts
- Note: "Cohiba Behike" is a well-known Cuban cigar brand. This could be a conflict depending on your goods/services class. Research this carefully.

### USPTO trademark registration process:

**1. Determine your filing basis:**
- **Section 1(a) "Use in Commerce":** You are already using the mark in commerce. Requires proof of use (screenshot of website, product listing, etc.)
- **Section 1(b) "Intent to Use":** You plan to use the mark but haven't started yet. Adds $150-$200 in extra fees later when you prove actual use.

**2. Identify your class(es) of goods/services:**
- Class 9: Downloadable electronic publications, software, digital content
- Class 16: Printed matter, books
- Class 35: Advertising, business management, online retail services
- Class 41: Education, training, entertainment services
- Class 42: Computer services, SaaS, technology services
- Each class costs a separate filing fee ($350 each)

**3. File through the USPTO Trademark Electronic Application System (TEAS):**
- URL: https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks
- Base fee: $350 per class (if you use pre-approved descriptions from the ID Manual)
- Surcharges: +$100 for insufficient info, +$200 for free-text descriptions not in ID Manual

**4. Timeline:**
- Initial review by examining attorney: 3-4 months
- If approved, published for opposition: 30 days
- If no opposition, registration issues: 2-3 months after publication
- Total timeline: ~8-12 months if no issues
- If you filed intent-to-use, add time for Statement of Use filing

**5. Maintenance:**
- Between year 5-6: File Declaration of Use ($325/class)
- Every 10 years: Renewal ($325 Declaration + $325 Renewal = $650/class)

### Cost breakdown for trademarking "Behike":

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| Filing fee (1 class) | $350 |
| Filing fee (2 classes) | $700 |
| Attorney (optional but recommended) | $500-$2,000 |
| Declaration of Use (year 5-6) | $325/class |
| 10-year renewal | $650/class |

**Practical advice:** Start with one class (Class 9 for downloadable digital products). Add classes later as you expand. You can file yourself to save money, but a trademark attorney can catch conflicts you might miss.

## 7. AI-GENERATED CONTENT AND COPYRIGHT

### This is the most critical section for your situation.

**Current law (as of March 2026):**

The US Copyright Office and courts have established clear rules:

1. **Purely AI-generated content is NOT copyrightable.** If you type a prompt and an AI generates the entire output, you do not own a copyright on that output. The Supreme Court declined to hear the Thaler v. Perlmutter case in March 2026, effectively confirming this.

2. **AI-assisted content CAN be copyrightable, if human creative input is substantial.** Using AI as a tool (like using Photoshop or a calculator) does not disqualify the work. What matters is that a human made the meaningful creative decisions.

3. **Prompts alone are generally NOT sufficient authorship.** Simply writing detailed prompts does not make you the author of the output, because you don't control the specific expressive elements.

4. **The test is: did the human determine the "sufficient expressive elements"?**

### What this means for your products:

| Scenario | Copyrightable? | Why |
|----------|---------------|-----|
| You write an ebook and use AI to check grammar/spelling | YES | You are the author. AI is just a tool. |
| You outline an ebook, write key sections, use AI to draft others, then heavily edit everything | LIKELY YES | Substantial human creative control over final expression. |
| You prompt AI to "write a complete guide on X" and sell the raw output | NO | AI generated the expression. You just provided the idea. |
| You use AI to generate images for your course | PROBABLY NO for the images alone | Unless you significantly modify/arrange them. |
| You create a composite work: human-written text + AI-generated images arranged creatively | PARTIAL | The text is copyrightable. The arrangement may be copyrightable. Individual AI images probably not. |
| You use AI to brainstorm ideas, then write everything yourself | YES | Ideas aren't copyrightable anyway. The expression is yours. |

### Practical rules for protecting your content:

1. **Always do substantial human editing.** Don't sell raw AI output. Rewrite, restructure, add your own examples, inject your personal experience. The more "you" is in the final product, the stronger your copyright claim.

2. **Document your creative process.** Keep drafts, notes, outlines. If challenged, you need to show that a human (you) made the creative decisions.

3. **When registering with the Copyright Office, disclose AI involvement.** As of February 2023, the Copyright Office requires applicants to disclose AI-generated content. Failing to disclose can invalidate your registration. Mark which parts are AI-generated and which are human-authored.

4. **For products where AI generated significant portions:** Consider registering only the human-authored portions, or register the work as a compilation/composite where the selection and arrangement is your creative contribution.

5. **Add anti-AI-training notices.** Include a statement that your content may not be used to train AI models. Not legally tested yet, but establishes your position.

### The bottom line for your products:

Your ebooks, guides, and templates are likely copyrightable if you:
- Created the structure and outline yourself
- Wrote or substantially edited all the text
- Added your own examples, insights, and personal experience
- Made the creative decisions about what to include and how to present it
- Used AI as a drafting assistant, not as the author

## 9. FAIR USE VS DERIVATIVE WORKS

### This matters because some of your content is inspired by Dan Koe's OPB framework and similar creators.

**Fair Use** is a legal defense that allows limited use of copyrighted material without permission. Courts evaluate four factors:

1. **Purpose and character of the use.** Commercial use weighs against fair use. Transformative use (adding new meaning, commentary, criticism) weighs in favor.

2. **Nature of the copyrighted work.** Using factual/informational works is more likely fair use than using creative/artistic works.

3. **Amount and substantiality used.** Using a small portion is more likely fair use. Using the "heart" of a work is not, even if it's a small portion.

4. **Effect on the market.** If your product competes with or replaces the original, it's less likely fair use.

### What you CAN do safely:

- Teach the same general concepts (ideas are not copyrightable)
- Reference another creator's framework by name (factual statement)
- Build on publicly known business principles and strategies
- Create your own framework that covers similar ground with your own structure, examples, and expression
- Quote briefly for commentary or criticism (with attribution)

### What you should NOT do:

- Copy or closely paraphrase someone's specific text
- Replicate their exact framework structure, step names, and proprietary terminology
- Use their branded framework name as if it's yours
- Reproduce their worksheets, templates, or course materials
- Create something that could replace the need to buy their product

### The "inspired by" test:

If someone looked at your product and Dan Koe's product side by side, would they say yours is:

- **A new product that covers similar topics?** SAFE. Ideas are free. You can teach about one-person businesses, content creation, or personal branding. Thousands of people do.
- **A reorganized version of his specific material?** RISKY. This could be a derivative work.
- **A copy with different words?** DANGEROUS. This is infringement even if you changed the wording.

### Practical approach for your content:

1. Learn the concepts from multiple sources, not just one
2. Develop your own framework with your own terminology
3. Use your own examples from your own experience
4. If you reference someone else's work, credit them and keep it brief
5. Make your product clearly reflect YOUR perspective and experience
6. If in doubt, focus on what makes your approach different, not what you borrowed

## 10. PROTECTING IDEAS WITHOUT REGISTRATION

### Methods available to you today at $0 cost:

**1. Terms of Service / License Agreements**
- Every digital product you sell should include license terms
- Restrict: redistribution, reselling, derivative works, AI training
- These are enforceable as contracts between you and the buyer
- Gumroad allows you to add custom license terms

**2. Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs)**
- Use before sharing unpublished work with contractors, partners, or collaborators
- Free NDA templates available at Rocket Lawyer, LegalZoom, etc.
- Especially important before sharing product ideas with freelancers or developers

**3. Trade Secret Protection**
- Keep proprietary processes, pricing strategies, and internal tools confidential
- Document what is confidential and who has access
- No registration needed. Protection lasts as long as secrecy is maintained.

**4. Document Everything**
- Keep dated copies of all drafts, outlines, and work-in-progress
- Use version control (git) for code-based products. You already do this.
- Save emails, messages, and notes that show when you created something
- This creates a paper trail proving you created it first

**5. Publish Strategically**
- Publishing your work creates a public timestamp
- Blog posts, social media posts, and product listings all create dated records
- If someone copies you later, you can prove prior creation

**6. Digital Timestamps**
- Services like ScoreDetect, OriginStamp, or similar provide blockchain-based timestamps
- Upload your work to create an immutable proof of creation date
- Costs are minimal ($0-$10/month for basic plans)

**7. Poor Man's Copyright (limited value)**
- Emailing yourself a copy of your work creates a timestamp
- Better than nothing but not a substitute for real registration
- Courts give it minimal weight

## 11. PUERTO RICO SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS

### The good news: Federal IP law fully applies in Puerto Rico.

As a US territory, Puerto Rico is covered by:
- The Copyright Act (federal, same as all 50 states)
- The Lanham Act (federal trademark law)
- The Patent Act
- The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA)

You register copyrights and trademarks with the same federal agencies (US Copyright Office, USPTO) as anyone on the mainland. Same forms, same fees, same process.

### Puerto Rico has ADDITIONAL protections:

**1. Puerto Rico Trademark Act (local)**
- PR has its own separate trademark registration system
- A federal registration IS enforceable in PR (federal preemption)
- But registering at the PR level too provides extra local protection
- File with: Puerto Rico State Department, Trademarks Division
- Consider this as a second layer if you're doing business specifically in PR

**2. Moral Rights Act (Ley de Derechos Morales)**
- PR law provides moral rights to authors: the right of attribution and the right of integrity
- This means even if you sell or transfer your copyright, you retain the right to be identified as the author and to prevent distortion of your work
- Mainland US has very limited moral rights (only for visual art under VARA). PR goes further.
- This is a real advantage for PR-based creators.

**3. Right of Publicity Act**
- Protects your name, image, and likeness from unauthorized commercial use
- Relevant if you build a personal brand (which you are doing)

**4. Trade Secrets Act (PR local)**
- Mirrors the federal Defend Trade Secrets Act but provides local remedies too

### Practical implication:

For your situation, file everything federally. That gives you nationwide (and PR) protection. The local PR registrations are a nice-to-have second layer but not urgent. Federal first.
