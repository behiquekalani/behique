# Chapter 1: Why Contracts Are Not Bureaucracy, They Are Clarity

Most solopreneurs avoid contracts for two reasons. The first is that writing them feels complicated and legal. The second is that asking a client to sign something feels like implying you do not trust them.

Both of those beliefs are costing you money.

Contracts are not documents for lawyers. They are documents for you and your client. They exist to answer one question clearly before it becomes a dispute: what did we agree to? The value of a well-written contract is not that it wins you a lawsuit. It is that it prevents the situation that would lead to a lawsuit from ever developing in the first place.

Here is what happens when you work without a contract. A client assumes revisions are unlimited. You assume they are capped at two. Neither of you said it out loud. Three months into the project, the client is requesting their twelfth round of changes and you have worked 80 extra hours for free. There is no dispute. There is no bad actor. There is just an assumption that never got written down.

Scope creep is not primarily a client problem. It is a communication problem. Clients who add to the scope are often doing what they think is reasonable. From their perspective, they hired you for a result, not a fixed list of tasks. The contract is what draws the line between those two interpretations.

The second protection a contract provides is payment security. Net-60 payment terms, payment disputes, and non-payment are three of the most common problems solopreneurs face. A contract with clear payment terms, late fees, and a kill fee clause gives you legal standing and negotiating position. Most payment problems resolve before they escalate when the terms are documented.

The third protection is less discussed but equally important: professional positioning. Clients who receive a professional contract from a solopreneur treat that solopreneur differently. The contract signals that you have standards, that you have been through this before, and that you operate a real business. Clients who planned to push your rates or add scope without discussion often do not. The contract changes the dynamic before the work begins.

This guide gives you five templates. Each one is written in plain language. Each one contains the clauses that matter most for the situations where solopreneurs face the most risk. You do not need to modify all of them. You need to read them once, understand what each clause does, and adapt the template to your specific service.

# Chapter 2: The Scope of Work Contract

The Scope of Work agreement is the most important contract in your toolkit. It is the foundation of every client engagement. Everything else you need protection from, from scope creep to payment disputes, comes back to whether the scope was clear from the beginning.

A strong Scope of Work has six sections.

**Section 1: Parties and Project Description.** Name both parties clearly. Use legal business names, not informal names. Describe the project in one to three sentences at a high level. This section should be readable by someone who has no context for the project and give them a clear picture of what was agreed.

**Section 2: Deliverables.** This is the most important section of the entire document. List every deliverable specifically. Not "website design" but "five custom-designed website pages in Figma, including home, about, services, contact, and one interior page of the client's choosing." Not "content writing" but "eight 1,000-word blog posts on topics provided by the client, with one round of revisions included." Specificity is protection. Vagueness is exposure.

**Section 3: Timeline and Milestones.** Define when deliverables are due and what the client's responsibilities are to hit those dates. Include a clause that states if client feedback is delayed beyond a specified window (typically 5 to 7 business days), the project timeline will shift accordingly. This clause alone prevents most timeline disputes.

**Section 4: Revision Policy.** Define what counts as a revision and how many rounds are included. A revision is a change to an existing deliverable. A new deliverable is out of scope. State this clearly. "Up to two rounds of revisions per deliverable are included. Additional revisions are available at the rate of $XX per hour."

**Section 5: Compensation and Payment Terms.** State the total project fee. State when deposits are due (typically 50% upfront). State when final payment is due. Include a late payment clause: "Payments not received within 7 days of the due date will incur a late fee of 1.5% per month." Include a work-stoppage clause: "Work may be paused on projects where payment is 14 or more days overdue."

**Section 6: Ownership and Rights.** Define who owns the work product and when. The cleanest approach: full ownership transfers to the client upon receipt of final payment. Until that payment is received, the work product is your property. This clause gives you standing when payment is late.

**Scope of Work Template:**

---

SCOPE OF WORK AGREEMENT

This Scope of Work Agreement (Agreement) is entered into as of [DATE] by and between [YOUR FULL NAME OR BUSINESS NAME] (Service Provider) and [CLIENT FULL NAME OR BUSINESS NAME] (Client).

PROJECT DESCRIPTION
Service Provider agrees to provide the following services to Client: [One to three sentence description].

DELIVERABLES
Service Provider will deliver the following, in the formats specified:
1. [Specific deliverable 1]
2. [Specific deliverable 2]
3. [Specific deliverable 3]

TIMELINE
Estimated start date: [DATE]
Estimated completion date: [DATE]
Client agrees to provide requested information and feedback within [5] business days. Delays beyond this window will result in a proportional extension of the project timeline.

REVISIONS
Up to [2] rounds of revisions per deliverable are included. Additional revisions will be billed at [YOUR HOURLY RATE] per hour.

COMPENSATION
Total project fee: $[AMOUNT]
Payment schedule: 50% due upon signing. Remaining 50% due upon final delivery.
Late payments incur a 1.5% monthly late fee.

OWNERSHIP
Full ownership of deliverables transfers to Client upon receipt of final payment. Until full payment is received, all work product remains the property of Service Provider.

SIGNATURES

Client: ________________ Date: ________

# Chapter 3: The Retainer Agreement

A retainer is the most valuable contract structure available to a solopreneur. It converts one-time client relationships into recurring revenue. It provides income predictability. And it positions you as a long-term partner rather than a vendor.

Most solopreneurs wait too long to offer retainers. They assume the client needs to see project work first. Sometimes that is true. Often, the right client will agree to a retainer from the start if the offer is structured correctly.

A retainer agreement should contain four critical elements that differ from a standard project contract.

**The monthly deliverables definition.** A retainer is not a commitment to be available. It is a commitment to deliver specific outputs each month. Define them precisely: "eight pieces of written content per month" or "20 hours of consulting time per month" or "weekly 60-minute strategy calls plus email support." Undefined retainers lead to scope creep faster than any other engagement structure.

**The rollover policy.** Define what happens to unused hours or deliverables. The two most common approaches are: use it or lose it (unused deliverables do not roll over) and partial rollover (up to 50% of unused hours may be carried into the following month). Use-it-or-lose-it protects your capacity. Partial rollover appeals to clients who want flexibility. Choose based on your workflow.

**The cancellation terms.** Both parties should be able to exit the retainer. Define the notice period. Thirty days is standard for most retainer arrangements. Define whether a kill fee applies if the client terminates early without cause.

**Retainer Agreement Template:**

---

RETAINER AGREEMENT

This Retainer Agreement is entered into as of [DATE] by and between [SERVICE PROVIDER] and [CLIENT].

SERVICES
Service Provider will provide the following services on a monthly basis:
[List specific monthly deliverables here]

TERM
This agreement begins on [START DATE] and continues month-to-month until terminated by either party with [30] days written notice.

COMPENSATION
Monthly retainer fee: $[AMOUNT], due on the [1st] of each month.
Late payments incur a 1.5% monthly late fee.

ROLLOVER POLICY
Unused deliverables [do not roll over / may roll over up to 50% into the following month].

TERMINATION
Either party may terminate this agreement with 30 days written notice. Early termination by Client without cause will result in a kill fee equal to one month's retainer fee.

SIGNATURES
Service Provider: ________________ Date: ________
Client: ________________ Date: ________

# Chapter 4: The NDA and Partnership Agreement

There are two more situations where solopreneurs routinely work without documentation: when they share proprietary information with a potential client before a deal is signed, and when they collaborate with another independent professional.

Both situations require a contract.

**The Non-Disclosure Agreement.**

An NDA protects confidential information that one or both parties share during a business discussion. Solopreneurs need NDAs in two scenarios. First, when a client shares sensitive business information before signing a work agreement. Second, when you share your process, methodology, or pricing structure with a client who might share it with competitors.

A functional NDA for solopreneurs has three parts.

Definition of confidential information. Be specific. "Business plans, financial data, customer lists, product roadmaps, pricing information, and any other information designated as confidential by either party" is more defensible than a vague reference to "sensitive information."

The term of confidentiality. Standard NDA terms range from one to three years. Define when the obligation expires.

Permitted disclosures. Information that is already public, that the receiving party already knew, or that was independently developed is not protected. Include this carve-out.

**NDA Template:**

---

NON-DISCLOSURE AGREEMENT

This NDA is entered into as of [DATE] by and between [PARTY 1] and [PARTY 2].

CONFIDENTIAL INFORMATION
"Confidential Information" means any information disclosed by either party that is designated as confidential or that reasonably should be understood to be confidential, including but not limited to business plans, financial data, customer information, pricing, and proprietary processes.

OBLIGATIONS
Each party agrees to: (1) keep all Confidential Information strictly confidential; (2) not disclose Confidential Information to any third party without prior written consent; (3) use Confidential Information only for the purpose of evaluating or conducting business with the other party.

TERM
This agreement is effective for [2] years from the date above.

SIGNATURES
Party 1: ________________ Date: ________
Party 2: ________________ Date: ________

**The Partnership Agreement.**

Collaborations between solopreneurs are common and commonly undocumented. When two independent professionals agree to build something together, the questions that go unanswered at the start become the arguments that end the partnership later. Who owns the intellectual property? How are revenues split? What happens if one partner wants to exit?

A partnership agreement answers those questions in writing before they become disputes.

The three non-negotiable clauses in a solopreneur partnership agreement:

Ownership percentages. Define exactly what percentage of the business, product, or project each partner owns. Default to 50/50 only if both partners are contributing equivalent value. If one partner is doing more of the work, reflect that in the split.

Decision rights. Define who makes which decisions. Operational decisions (daily execution) can belong to one partner. Strategic decisions (pricing, major pivots, new hires) should require both partners to agree.

Exit terms. Define how a partner can exit and what happens to their ownership stake. Can they sell their share? To whom? At what valuation method? What happens if both partners want to dissolve the partnership entirely?

# Chapter 5: The Invoice with Payment Terms and the Negotiation Framework

**The Invoice as a Contract.**

An invoice is not just a request for payment. When it includes payment terms, it is a document with legal weight. Most solopreneurs send invoices that contain only the amount and a bank transfer detail. That is a payment request. An invoice with terms is something different.

The clauses that transform an invoice into a protected document:

Payment due date. "Due upon receipt" is too vague. "Due within 7 business days of invoice date" is specific and enforceable.

Accepted payment methods. List the methods you accept and the timeline for each. Checks take longer to clear than wire transfers. ACH has different timing than PayPal. Clarity prevents the "I thought I had more time" conversation.

Late payment fee. State it on every invoice. "Invoices unpaid after the due date are subject to a 1.5% monthly fee on the outstanding balance." Most clients pay on time when the fee is stated. The ones who do not are the ones who needed the clause.

Work product and rights clause on invoice. "Full rights to deliverables described in the associated Scope of Work transfer upon clearance of this payment." This mirrors the ownership clause in your SOW and reinforces it in the payment request itself.

**Invoice Template:**

---

INVOICE

Invoice Number: [INV-001]
Date: [DATE]
Due Date: [DATE + 7 DAYS]

From: [YOUR NAME / BUSINESS]
To: [CLIENT NAME / BUSINESS]

SERVICES RENDERED:
[Description of deliverables] . $[AMOUNT]

SUBTOTAL: $[AMOUNT]
TOTAL DUE: $[AMOUNT]

Payment Methods: [Bank transfer / PayPal / other]
Late Payment Fee: 1.5% per month on balances unpaid after due date.
Ownership Transfer: Full rights to deliverables transfer upon clearance of this payment.

Thank you for your business.

**The Negotiation Framework.**

Clients push back on contracts. They push back on payment terms, revision caps, late fees, and kill fees. Knowing how to respond without losing the client is a skill worth developing.

The three most common objections and how to handle them:

"We don't use outside contracts." This is a power move. It usually means the client prefers their own terms, which will favor them. Respond: "I understand. I can review your standard agreement. In the meantime, here is mine for your reference, so we can compare the key terms." Do not abandon your contract. Negotiate from it.

"The late fee seems high." Respond: "The late fee only applies if payment is past the due date, which I expect won't be an issue for us. It is standard language I include for protection on both sides." This response is calm, reasonable, and holds the position.

"We don't pay a deposit." This is a red flag. Respond: "I understand some clients prefer different structures. My standard is 50% upfront because it ensures I can commit my schedule fully to your project. I am happy to discuss the structure if there is a reason the deposit creates a problem for you." Listen to the answer. If the reason is a cash flow timing issue, you can negotiate. If the reason is that they are not committed, that is information.

## Conclusion

Every contract you send is a signal. It tells a client how you operate, what your standards are, and how seriously you run your own business.

Solopreneurs who use contracts consistently report three things: fewer scope disputes, faster payments, and higher-quality client relationships. The clients who refuse to sign reasonable agreements self-select out. That is a feature, not a problem. Those clients were always going to be difficult.

You have five templates now. The Scope of Work, the Retainer Agreement, the NDA, the Partnership Agreement, and the Invoice with Terms. Use them in that order for each new client. Review them once a year and update any clause that no longer reflects how you work.

The goal is not to be a harder person to work with. The goal is to be clearer. Clients who want clarity will value your contracts. Clients who resist clarity are telling you something important about what working with them will be like.
