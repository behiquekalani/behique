# Solopreneur's Freelance Rates Guide

**How to price your work so the math works and the client respects it.**

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike

---

## Before We Start

The income ceiling for most freelancers is not a skill problem. It is a pricing problem.

They learned their craft. They built a portfolio. They found clients. Then they priced based on what felt safe, what they thought the market would tolerate, or what someone else charged, and they have been constrained by that ceiling ever since.

This guide is about changing the math from the inside. Not about charging more because you believe in yourself. About charging more because you know what your work is worth, how to calculate it, how to present it, and how to hold the line when clients push back.

Every section here has a practical application. By the end you will have a clear method for setting rates, a framework for the pricing conversation, and a system for raising rates without losing clients who matter.

# Part One: The Pricing Foundation

## The Hourly Rate Trap and Why Project Pricing Is Almost Always Better

The hourly rate is the default for new freelancers because it feels safe and fair. You track time, you multiply by your rate, you send the invoice. Clean and simple.

The problem is what it does to your incentives.

When you charge hourly, every efficiency gain you make penalizes you. You get faster at a skill, you earn less for the same outcome. You find a better tool that saves two hours, the client pays less. You are financially rewarded for being slow and penalized for being good.

Hourly pricing also creates a negotiating problem. The client knows exactly how much each unit of your time costs. Every conversation about scope becomes a negotiation about hours. Every efficiency you demonstrate triggers an expectation of lower invoices.

Project pricing fixes both problems. The client pays for the outcome, not the time. If you solve the problem in six hours instead of twelve, that is your efficiency gain, not theirs. You are incentivized to get better. They are incentivized to scope clearly.

The one case where hourly makes sense: work with genuinely undefined scope where neither party can reasonably estimate the deliverable. Even here, a capped hourly or a phased approach is usually better than open-ended hourly billing.

## How to Calculate Your Minimum Viable Rate

Before you can think about value-based pricing, you need a floor. This is the number below which you cannot sustainably operate. Most freelancers skip this calculation and then wonder why the money feels tight even when business seems good.

The calculation has four parts.

**Part 1: Monthly operating expenses.**

List everything you pay for to run your business and your life. This is not what you spend. It is what you need. Rent or mortgage, utilities, food, insurance, software subscriptions, equipment, professional development. If you are in the US, add self-employment taxes, which are approximately 15.3% on top of your income tax liability.

Be honest. People consistently underestimate this number.

**Part 2: Business expenses and reinvestment.**

Add the costs specific to operating as a freelancer: accounting software, professional tools, any marketing you pay for, the time cost of unpaid administrative work. If you pay a bookkeeper or accountant, add that. If you have professional insurance, add that.

**Part 3: Profit target.**

This is not a nice-to-have. You need a buffer. Freelance income is variable. Without a profit margin built into your pricing, every slow month becomes a financial emergency. A minimum target of 20% above your expenses is a starting point. More is better.

**Part 4: Billable hours reality.**

You do not work 40 billable hours per week. Nobody does. Account for business development, administrative work, unpaid client communication, downtime between projects. A realistic figure for a solo freelancer is 20 to 25 billable hours per week, or approximately 80 to 100 per month.

The formula: (Monthly expenses + profit target) / billable hours per month = minimum hourly floor.

This number is not what you charge. It is what you cannot go below. Everything above it is actual margin.

# Part Two: The Pricing Models

## Value-Based Pricing: Pricing to Outcome, Not Time

Value-based pricing means your fee is tied to what the work is worth to the client, not what it costs you to produce. It requires understanding the client's economics well enough to anchor your fee to their gain.

The principle: if your work generates or saves significantly more than your fee, the fee is defensible regardless of how long the work takes.

A website redesign that takes 40 hours to produce might generate $200,000 in additional revenue for the client over its first year. Charging $4,000 for 40 hours at $100/hour is underpricing by a significant margin. Charging $15,000 for the outcome, with clear documentation of what that outcome enables, is closer to value-aligned.

How to apply it:

Ask the right questions early. What happens if this problem is not solved? What is the cost of the current situation? What does success look like in revenue, conversions, time saved, or risk reduced? These questions reframe the conversation from "how much will this cost" to "what is this outcome worth."

Research the client's context. A small business owner and a funded startup have different economic realities. Your fee should reflect the size of the problem you are solving and the scale of the client who benefits.

Anchor to outcomes, not deliverables. The deliverable is a website. The outcome is a sales pipeline. Price the pipeline.

## Market Rate Research: How to Find What Clients Actually Pay

Surveys lie. Not intentionally, but the data in freelancing income surveys is almost always skewed toward what people report rather than what they receive. The rates published on job boards are what clients want to pay. Neither tells you what the market actually clears at.

**Better sources:**

Private communities. Find freelance communities in your specific niche, whether forums, Slack groups, or Discord servers. The rates people discuss in private, among peers, are more accurate than any published number. Lurk, contribute, and pay attention to how experienced people talk about fees.

Direct conversations. Ask peers what they charge. Most people will not share exact numbers but will tell you if you are significantly above or below the norm. Build a peer network before you need the information.

Client inquiries. Pay close attention to how quickly clients accept your rate. If every client accepts immediately without hesitation, you are undercharging. If you are losing half your proposals, you may be overpriced for the market segment you are targeting, or your positioning does not justify the rate yet.

Proposal data. Track your proposals over time: rate, outcome, client type, project scope. Your own data is the most accurate market research you have.

Anchoring exercises. When you quote a rate, note the client's immediate response. Not whether they accept, but whether there is a pause, a question, or no reaction at all. That information is valuable regardless of the final outcome.

# Part Three: The Pricing Conversation

## How to Present Pricing Without Flinching

The rate conversation fails for most freelancers before a word is spoken. The failure is in the posture.

When you present a price with hesitation, apology, or a trailing explanation, you signal that you are uncertain about the value of your own work. Clients read this accurately. If you are not confident in your rate, why should they be?

The presentation itself:

State the number clearly and completely. "$8,500 for the full project" is a complete sentence. Do not immediately follow it with justifications, alternatives, or discounts. Let the number land.

Silence after the number is not your enemy. Most freelancers fill it with concessions before the client has had a chance to think. Wait.

When you do explain, explain in terms of outcome, not effort. "This includes strategy, design, copy, and a 60-day performance review" is better than "this is about 80 hours of work at my standard rate." The first explains value. The second invites the client to do math about your hourly rate.

Have your walk-away number clear before you enter the conversation. Knowing your floor prevents you from making decisions under pressure. If a client counters below your floor, the answer is no. That decision should be made before the conversation, not during it.

## Handling "That's Too Expensive": The 3 Real Objections Behind Price Resistance

"That's too expensive" is almost never a complete sentence. It is the start of a conversation about something else. Understanding what it actually means determines how you respond.

**Objection 1: "I don't have the budget."**

This is a real constraint for some clients. The honest response: "What is your budget?" If they name a number, you have a choice. You can decline, you can scope the project to fit the budget, or you can offer a phased approach. What you should not do is immediately reduce your original price without changing the scope. That signals that the original price was inflated.

**Objection 2: "I don't believe the value justifies the price."**

This is a positioning problem, not a price problem. The client does not yet understand why your work is worth what you are asking. Your response here is to make the case more clearly: outcomes, track record, what the alternative costs them, what success looks like. If you have done this well before the price conversation, this objection appears less frequently.

**Objection 3: "I can find this cheaper somewhere else."**

This is true in almost every market. Someone will always do the work for less. Your response is not to compete on price but to make visible what distinguishes your work. Track record, specialization, process, communication quality, the cost of a bad outcome. If you are genuinely not differentiated, that is a positioning problem worth solving before you revisit pricing.

The trap to avoid: taking price resistance at face value and reducing your rate without understanding which objection you are actually dealing with. This trains clients to negotiate down and signals that your prices are not anchored in real value.

# Part Four: Rate Management

## Raising Rates: Timing, Wording, and What to Do When Clients Push Back

Raising rates is uncomfortable enough that most freelancers avoid it until the discomfort of staying flat outweighs the discomfort of the conversation. By then they are significantly underpaid.

**Timing.**

The right time to raise rates is before you feel the pressure to. Annual rate reviews prevent rates from becoming anchored. Project transitions are natural opportunities. When demand for your time exceeds your available hours, that is a clear market signal that your rates are below clearing price.

Give existing clients 60 days notice on rate increases. This is both respectful and practical. It gives them time to budget and gives you time to find replacements if they do not continue.

**Wording.**

Keep it simple and confident. "Beginning [date], my rate for [work type] will be [new rate]. I wanted to give you advance notice." You do not need to justify a rate increase in detail. Prices go up. Your value has increased. That is sufficient.

If you feel a reason is necessary: "My rates are increasing to reflect my expanded experience in [area] and the current demand for my availability." Specific and professional.

**When clients push back.**

Some will. The response depends on how much you value the relationship versus the revenue gap.

For clients you want to keep: "I understand that's a significant change. I'd rather keep working together, so if the new rate creates a real constraint, let's talk about what scope makes sense at the current level." This opens a conversation without abandoning the rate increase.

For clients you are willing to lose: "I understand this may not work for you. I appreciate the relationship and wish you well." Then hold the line. Discounting your rate increase for one client signals to all clients that rate increases are negotiable. They are not.

## Day Rates, Retainers, and Project Rates: When to Use Each

No single pricing model is optimal for every engagement type. The right model depends on the nature of the work, the relationship with the client, and how predictable the scope is.

**Day rates.**

Best for: short-term engagements with defined time requirements, consulting work where the client needs flexible access to your time, situations where the scope is genuinely unclear at the start.

The advantage of a day rate over an hourly rate is that it removes the granularity of hour-by-hour tracking while still tying your income to time. A day rate is also psychologically easier for clients who are not comfortable with project pricing but find hourly rates granular and stressful.

**Retainers.**

Best for: ongoing relationships with predictable monthly scope, clients who need consistent availability rather than project-by-project delivery, work types that do not produce discrete deliverables (strategy, advisory, content).

Retainers are the most income-stable pricing model for freelancers. The risk is scope creep: clients who interpret a monthly retainer as unlimited access. Define what the retainer includes and what is out of scope before signing. Revisit this annually.

**Project rates.**

Best for: scoped work with a defined deliverable and a beginning and end, clients who are sophisticated enough to think in outcomes, work types where your efficiency benefits you rather than penalizing you.

Project rates require good discovery work. The more clearly you understand the scope before quoting, the more accurately you can price. Build in a contingency for scope changes. State clearly what constitutes a change in scope and what the process is for handling one.

## The Annual Rate Review: Building In Systematic Increases

A freelance business without a rate review process is a business that gradually falls behind inflation, market rates, and its own rising skill level.

The annual review does not have to be complicated. Once per year, answer four questions:

1. What did I charge last year? What am I delivering now that I was not delivering then?
2. What is the current market rate for my work, based on actual conversations and data?
3. What is my current cost of operating, and has it increased?
4. Where is my time overallocated relative to what I earn from each client?

From there: adjust new client rates first. New engagements do not have the psychological anchor of an existing relationship and are the easiest place to implement increases.

Adjust existing clients on the schedule you decide before the conversation, not when they push back. A client who learns your rate is increasing when you tell them, rather than when they receive a surprise invoice, is far more likely to accept the change.

The principle: systematic rate increases applied consistently and professionally are a sign of a healthy, growing freelance practice. The alternative is working more and more hours to maintain the same income as your expenses and skill level both increase.

---

# Final Note

Pricing is a skill. Like any skill, it improves with deliberate practice and honest self-assessment.

The goal is not to extract maximum short-term value from every client. It is to price in a way that reflects what your work actually accomplishes, sustains your business over the long term, and creates relationships where clients pay you what you are worth rather than what they can get away with.

That requires knowing your numbers, knowing your value, and knowing how to hold a conversation without flinching.

You do not have to be aggressive. You have to be clear.
