# Solopreneur's Outsourcing Guide

## How to Delegate Without Losing Control, Quality, or Your Mind

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike

---

## Introduction: The Trap of Doing Everything Yourself

There is a certain identity that forms in solopreneurs. The builder identity. The person who figures things out. The one who does not need help because needing help feels like weakness, or inefficiency, or loss of control.

This identity is an asset in the early days. It is why you survived. It is why you built the thing. But at some point it becomes a ceiling.

The solopreneur who cannot outsource anything is not free. They are the highest-paid employee of a business they own. Their revenue is permanently capped by their personal bandwidth. Every hour they spend on bookkeeping is an hour not spent on product development. Every hour on social media is an hour not spent on sales. Every hour in the weeds of execution is an hour not spent on the strategic decisions only they can make.

Outsourcing is the mechanism for breaking that ceiling. But most solopreneurs approach it wrong, which is why most of them have outsourcing horror stories. The contractor who disappeared with half a project done. The VA who misunderstood every task. The developer who built the wrong thing and billed for 40 hours. These experiences create scar tissue. Solopreneurs who have been burned once conclude that outsourcing does not work for them. That it is easier to just do it themselves.

This guide exists to address that conclusion directly. The problem was almost certainly not outsourcing itself. The problem was the absence of a system. No clear SOPs. No proper contractor selection. No quality framework. No cost-benefit analysis that justified the investment. When those systems are in place, outsourcing works. When they are absent, it produces exactly the horror stories that keep solopreneurs stuck.

This guide builds those systems, from the decision framework for what to outsource first, through contractor selection and onboarding, to the SOP infrastructure that makes quality sustainable without micromanaging every task.

# Chapter 1: The Decision Framework for What to Outsource

Not everything should be outsourced. The goal is not to get as many things off your plate as possible. The goal is to free up your highest-leverage time while maintaining quality on everything that affects your business outcomes.

The decision about what to outsource starts with a clear-eyed audit of how you currently spend your time.

## The Time Audit

Spend one week tracking every work activity in 15-minute increments. Do not estimate from memory. Estimation is almost always wrong. Track it in real time.

At the end of the week, categorize every activity into one of three buckets:

Zone of Genius: Activities that only you can do and that directly create your highest-value output. For most solopreneurs, this includes strategic decisions, relationship building, core creative work, and direct customer interaction.

Zone of Competence: Activities you can do well but that do not require your specific genius. Writing routine emails. Managing your calendar. Basic editing. Formatting documents. Someone else could do these with the right training.

Zone of Incompetence: Activities you do poorly and that would be done significantly better by a specialist. Graphic design if you have no visual skill. Bookkeeping if you struggle with numbers. Technical SEO if you do not understand it well.

The outsourcing priority order is: Zone of Incompetence first, then Zone of Competence, then never outsource Zone of Genius.

## The Frequency Filter

After categorizing activities, apply a frequency filter. One-time tasks are rarely worth building an outsourcing relationship over. The tasks that benefit most from outsourcing are recurring and repetitive: they happen weekly or monthly, they follow a consistent pattern, and they consume time disproportionate to their strategic value.

Recurring tasks in the Zone of Competence are the primary targets. If you spend 6 hours per week on social media scheduling, formatting, and posting and this falls into your Zone of Competence, it is a strong outsourcing candidate. If you spend 6 hours on a one-time project build, the overhead of bringing in a contractor may not be worth it.

## The Emotional Resistance Test

There is a category of tasks that solopreneurs resist not because they are high-leverage but because they are uncomfortable. Accounting. Following up on unpaid invoices. Rejection-prone outreach. Tasks that produce anxiety or consistent procrastination.

These are also strong outsourcing candidates, not because of the time they take but because of the drag they create. A task that takes 2 hours but that you avoid for 3 weeks while it sits on your mental load is consuming far more than 2 hours of energy. Outsourcing it removes both the time and the psychological weight.

## The Output Test: Can It Be Defined?

Before outsourcing any task, you need to be able to define the output. This sounds obvious but is consistently where outsourcing fails. "Help with content" is not a definable output. "Write 3 Instagram captions per week based on the blog post I publish, using this tone guide and these example posts, in this format, delivered by Thursday at noon" is a definable output.

If you cannot clearly define what success looks like for a task, you are not ready to outsource it yet. The work required is not finding a contractor. The work required is defining the output. Do that first.

# Chapter 2: Finding the Right Contractors

The marketplace for freelance talent has never been more efficient or more confusing. There are more platforms, more profiles, more options than at any previous point. This abundance creates a selection problem. How do you find contractors who can actually deliver what you need?

## The Platform Landscape

The major platforms serve different use cases and different talent pools.

Upwork is the largest and most diverse. Strong for: development, design, writing, virtual assistance, data work. The review system is robust and works reasonably well as a quality signal. Expect to spend time filtering through irrelevant applications if your job post is not precise.

Toptal is a curated network that claims to select the top 3 percent of applicants. Rates are higher. For senior technical work where quality is critical and time-to-ramp is short, the premium can be worth it.

Fiverr works for bounded, well-defined tasks with a clear deliverable. Logo design, copywriting samples, short video editing, social media graphics. The "gig" format keeps scope tight and prevents the scope creep that plagues hourly engagements.

LinkedIn is underused for freelance contractor discovery. A direct message to a professional with relevant experience who is listed as "open to work" or who has a history of freelance engagements can produce better fits than any platform, because you can see their full professional history and mutual connections.

Referrals from trusted peers are still the highest-signal method. When someone whose business judgment you respect recommends a contractor, the risk profile drops substantially. Build a habit of asking, when you see good work, who did this.

## Evaluating Candidates

The profile review process should focus on two things: evidence of relevant output and evidence of professional behavior.

Evidence of relevant output means portfolio samples that demonstrate they have done the specific type of work you need, not adjacent work. If you need product description copywriting, samples of blog posts do not tell you what you need to know. Ask for samples of exactly what you will be asking them to produce.

Evidence of professional behavior includes how they respond to your initial outreach, how clearly they communicate about their process and timeline, and whether their questions demonstrate that they read your brief carefully. A contractor who asks good questions before starting is nearly always better than one who dives in immediately. The questions reveal how they think about a problem.

## The Test Project

Do not give a new contractor a large engagement without a paid test project first. This is the single highest-leverage practice in contractor selection. A test project reveals more about work quality, communication style, and reliability than any amount of portfolio review.

The test project should be real work, paid at the contractor's rate, scoped to roughly 2 to 5 hours of work. Pick something that will be representative of the ongoing engagement. Pay what you agreed, deliver timely feedback, and observe closely.

What you are evaluating:

Does the output match the brief? If you gave clear instructions and the output does not match, that is a signal about either comprehension or attention to detail.

How do they handle ambiguity? If there was something unclear in your brief, did they ask before proceeding or did they make an assumption and deliver something that missed the mark?

How do they take feedback? The response to your feedback notes is often more revealing than the initial work. A contractor who absorbs specific feedback and implements it precisely is far more valuable than one who delivers perfect work on round one but becomes defensive when you have corrections.

Are they on time? Simple but determinative. A contractor who delivers the test project 2 days late with no communication about the delay will behave exactly the same way on your ongoing engagement.

## Red Flags

Contractors who do not read briefs carefully. If your job post says "no agencies" and an agency responds, they failed the first test.

Contractors who are vague about process and timeline. "I will get it done soon" is not a timeline. Vagueness about process usually means they do not have one.

Contractors who compete on price alone. The cheapest option is almost never the best value. The cheapest option is usually the least experienced, the least reliable, or both.

Contractors who cannot provide samples relevant to your specific need. If they have never done the thing you are hiring them to do, they are not a contractor. They are a student, and you will pay the tuition.

# Chapter 3: Creating SOPs So You Do Not Have to Babysit

The most common reason outsourcing fails for solopreneurs is the absence of documented processes. The solopreneur has all the context in their head. The contractor has none of it. Without a system to transfer that context, every task requires extensive back-and-forth, constant checking, and eventually the solopreneur concludes it is faster to just do it themselves.

This conclusion is correct in the short term and catastrophic in the long term.

The solution is Standard Operating Procedures: documented, step-by-step instructions that allow a contractor to execute a task to your standard without requiring your direct involvement at every step.

## What a Good SOP Contains

A useful SOP is not a vague description of a task. It is a complete operating manual for a specific repeatable process. It contains:

The purpose: A one-sentence description of why this task exists and what outcome it produces for the business.

The trigger: What initiates this process? A new week starting, a new piece of content going live, a client submission received.

The input: What materials, data, or resources does the person executing the SOP need to start? Where are they located?

The steps: Numbered, sequential, specific. Each step should be described at a level of detail where someone who has never seen this process before could complete it without asking for clarification. If a step involves a judgment call, specify the criteria for making that judgment.

The output: What does the completed task look like? Where does it go? What format is it in?

The quality check: What specific things should the person completing the task verify before marking it done?

## The Recording Method

The fastest way to create SOPs is to record yourself doing the task. Screen recording with voiceover while you walk through the process produces a complete SOP draft in real time. Tools like Loom make this easy. The recording captures not just the steps but the reasoning behind decisions, which is often the hardest thing to document in text.

After recording, have someone else watch the video and attempt to follow the process. The gaps between what you assumed were obvious in the recording and what they needed clarification on become the additions to your written SOP.

## SOP Library Structure

Store SOPs in a central location accessible to all contractors. Notion, Google Drive, or a dedicated wiki all work. The structure should mirror your business operations:

Content creation SOPs: How to format blog posts, how to create social media graphics, how to repurpose long-form content into shorter formats, how to schedule and publish.

Client management SOPs: How to onboard a new client, how to send deliverables, how to follow up on feedback, how to handle revision requests.

Administrative SOPs: How to process invoices, how to manage the project management system, how to handle routine email responses.

Operational SOPs: Any recurring process that happens in your business that someone else could be handling.

## Keeping SOPs Current

An SOP written six months ago may no longer reflect how you do something. The process of updating SOPs needs to be built into your operating rhythm. A monthly review of the most frequently used SOPs, with notes on any changes, keeps the library accurate.

The most effective way to catch outdated SOPs is to have the contractor note any discrepancies between the SOP and what actually happened during execution. "The SOP says to do X, but when I did X, the system showed Y instead. I improvised by doing Z. Should the SOP be updated?" This feedback loop is how your SOP library stays current without requiring you to proactively audit it constantly.

# Chapter 4: Managing Quality Without Micromanaging

Micromanagement is a failure of systems. When a solopreneur is checking in on every task, approving every decision, and reviewing every output before it goes anywhere, the outsourcing relationship has created more overhead than it saved. The problem is never that the contractor needs this level of oversight. The problem is that the system was not built to make that level of oversight unnecessary.

## The Quality Framework

Before delegating any task, define what acceptable quality looks like in specific, observable terms. "Good writing" is not a quality standard. "Clear, one-sentence paragraphs, no jargon, no passive voice, no more than 500 words, ends with a clear call to action that links to [specific page]" is a quality standard.

When the quality standard is specific and observable, quality review becomes quick and objective. You are not making a judgment call. You are checking against a checklist.

Build quality checklists for every recurring output your contractors produce. The checklist lives in the SOP. Before any output is submitted to you, the contractor checks it against the list. This catches the obvious problems before they reach you.

## The Feedback Cadence

Micromanagement often emerges from anxiety about what contractors are doing between check-ins. The solution is a structured feedback cadence that gives you visibility without requiring constant contact.

For ongoing engagements, a brief weekly check-in works for most tasks. Not a long meeting. A short update message or a structured status report. "Completed X this week. Working on Y next week. Have a question about Z." This keeps you informed without creating dependency.

For project-based work, milestone check-ins at pre-defined points in the project provide visibility without micromanaging. A development project might have three milestones: architecture review, first working build, final testing. You review at each milestone, provide feedback, and the contractor continues.

## Outcome-Based Evaluation

The key mental shift in managing contractors without micromanaging is moving from monitoring activity to evaluating outcomes. You do not need to know how many hours they worked or what their process looked like. You need to know whether the output met the standard.

This is harder than it sounds for solopreneurs who are used to doing everything themselves and who have deep context on how tasks should be done. The instinct is to evaluate process because you know your process well. But the contractor's process may be different from yours and still produce the same or better outcome. Let the outcome be the standard.

## When to Let Go of a Contractor

There is a specific pattern that signals a contractor relationship is not working and is unlikely to improve: repeated corrections on the same issues. If you have provided specific, clear feedback on the same problem more than twice and the contractor has not corrected it, the problem is not a training problem. It is a fit problem.

The sunk cost thinking that keeps solopreneurs in bad contractor relationships is the same thinking that keeps people in bad jobs. The time already invested in onboarding, training, and feedback is gone regardless of what you decide. The question is only whether continuing the relationship will produce better outcomes going forward. If the pattern of repeated failures on the same issues is established, the answer is almost certainly no.

End the relationship cleanly, document what went wrong to inform the next hire, and restart the selection process with better-defined requirements.

# Chapter 5: The True Cost Calculation of Outsourcing vs. Doing It Yourself

The financial analysis most solopreneurs do when considering outsourcing is incomplete. They look at the contractor's rate, compare it to their own effective hourly rate, and conclude that outsourcing is expensive. This analysis misses several important factors.

## Your Actual Hourly Rate

Most solopreneurs significantly overestimate their effective hourly rate when doing the outsourcing calculus. They divide annual revenue by a 40-hour week, 50-week year and arrive at a number that sounds impressive. But that calculation includes all the hours they work, including the Zone of Incompetence and Zone of Competence hours they are considering outsourcing.

The relevant comparison is not your average hourly rate. It is your marginal hourly rate: what is an additional hour of your highest-leverage work worth?

If spending an additional hour on product development, client acquisition, or strategic planning produces $200 in value, then outsourcing any task that costs less than $200 per hour to a contractor is economically rational. Most administrative and operational tasks can be outsourced for $15 to $50 per hour. The math is clear.

## The Cost of Not Outsourcing

The hidden cost of doing everything yourself is not just the time you spend on low-leverage tasks. It is the opportunity cost: the high-leverage work you did not do because you were occupied with low-leverage work.

A solopreneur who spends 10 hours per week on administrative tasks and operational work they could outsource for $20 per hour is spending $200 per week of their time on that work. If their highest-leverage work generates $100 per hour, the opportunity cost is $1,000 per week minus the $200 outsourcing cost. They are losing $800 per week by not outsourcing.

This calculation is rarely done explicitly, which is why the decision to outsource often feels like an expense rather than an investment.

## Quality-Adjusted Cost

The cheapest contractor is rarely the lowest-cost option when quality adjustment is applied. A contractor who charges $15 per hour but requires three rounds of revision and produces output you need to significantly rework is more expensive than a contractor who charges $35 per hour and delivers usable work on the first pass.

When evaluating contractor costs, estimate the total cost including your time spent on briefing, reviewing, giving feedback, and processing revisions. The all-in cost of a cheap contractor can easily exceed the all-in cost of a more expensive one.

## The Ramp-Up Investment

The first few engagements with a new contractor always cost more than subsequent ones. The time you spend creating SOPs, onboarding, explaining your preferences, and reviewing output carefully in the beginning is an investment that produces returns over the life of the relationship.

This ramp-up cost is another reason why solopreneurs often conclude that outsourcing is not worth it. They try one contractor, spend a lot of time on onboarding and corrections, and conclude it was more trouble than it was worth. But the ramp-up cost is a one-time investment. Amortized over six or twelve months of reliable output, it is usually worth it.

The contractors who are worth developing a long-term relationship with are valuable precisely because the ramp-up cost has been paid. Solopreneurs who constantly switch contractors for marginal cost savings pay the ramp-up cost repeatedly and never capture the long-term efficiency.

## Building a True Outsourcing Budget

Once you have done the true cost calculation and identified what you want to outsource, build an explicit outsourcing budget. A line item in your business finances for contractor costs, treated as a business investment rather than an expense, changes the psychological relationship to outsourcing.

A reasonable starting target: allocate 15 to 25 percent of your business revenue to outsourcing. This is the range where most solopreneurs find the balance between maintaining control over core work and freeing up enough high-leverage time to grow.

# Chapter 6: Staying Lean While Delegating Smartly

Outsourcing creates a gravitational pull toward bloat. Once you discover that things can be delegated, it is easy to delegate more and more until your contractor costs are significant, your coordination overhead is substantial, and you are managing people rather than building your business.

The lean outsourcing discipline is about maintaining the smallest possible team that delivers the maximum possible output, with the minimum coordination overhead.

## The Minimum Effective Delegation

Before adding any new contractor, ask whether the task genuinely requires a person. Automation handles a significant portion of what solopreneurs delegate to humans, often more reliably and at lower cost. Social media scheduling, email autoresponders, invoice generation, simple data formatting, file organization. All of these can be automated.

The distinction that matters: automation handles deterministic tasks (if X, then Y). Humans handle judgment tasks (assess whether X, then decide between Y and Z). If the task requires judgment, you need a human. If it is deterministic, you need a tool, not a person.

Audit your contractor tasks for automation candidates before each engagement renewal.

## The Project vs. Retainer Decision

Most solopreneurs default to retainer engagements: paying a contractor a fixed monthly fee for ongoing availability. Retainers create cost certainty and contractor availability, but they also create the overhead of managing an ongoing relationship whether or not you have enough work to fill it.

Project-based engagements are often more cost-effective for work that is not truly weekly. If you need a designer for two projects per year, a retainer is not the right structure. Project-based pricing with a trusted contractor on call produces better economics.

The right structure for retainers is when you have truly recurring, weekly work that requires consistent output: content production, virtual assistant work, ongoing development. For sporadic needs, project pricing is usually better.

## The Asynchronous First Principle

Synchronous communication with contractors (meetings, calls, real-time messaging) is expensive relative to asynchronous communication (email, written briefs, recorded feedback). Every 30-minute check-in call consumes 30 minutes of your time plus context-switching overhead.

Default to asynchronous. Write briefs instead of calling. Record feedback videos instead of scheduling review calls. Use project management tools where tasks and comments are documented rather than discussed in real time.

The exception is contractor onboarding. A brief synchronous call at the start of a new relationship to align on expectations, communication preferences, and the first project is usually time well spent. It prevents the accumulated misunderstandings that come from purely text-based onboarding. After that initial alignment, default to async.

## Avoiding Contractor Dependency

The risk of a lean, efficient contractor relationship is that the contractor becomes a single point of failure. If your best writer disappears, you lose a critical capability. If your primary developer goes unavailable, your product pipeline stops.

Mitigate this by maintaining documented SOPs for every function you outsource. The SOP means you can bring a new contractor up to speed relatively quickly if you need to replace someone. It also means the capability is not entirely locked in one person's head.

For critical functions, consider having a primary and a backup contractor relationship. The backup gets a small recurring project to maintain the relationship and their familiarity with your work. The cost is modest. The downside protection is significant.

# Chapter 7: Building Toward a Team Without Becoming a Manager

The logical endpoint of successful outsourcing is a small, effective team. But most solopreneurs who reach this point find that managing people is a fundamentally different skill than building things. The transition from maker to manager is one of the hardest in business, and it is even harder when it happens implicitly rather than by design.

## The Maker-Manager Distinction

A maker's most productive state is deep, uninterrupted focus. A manager's most productive state is available, responsive, and connected to what their team is working on. These two states are nearly incompatible. Trying to be both simultaneously usually means doing neither well.

Solopreneurs who build toward a team need to make an explicit decision about their primary role. If you are the primary value creator in your business, and your work requires deep, uninterrupted focus, then managing a team directly will degrade your output. In that case, the team needs a layer of project coordination between you and the contractors: a part-time project manager, an operations contractor, or a lead contractor who manages the others.

If you are transitioning toward a role that is more strategic and less hands-on, then taking on more direct management makes sense.

## The Contractor Coordinator Role

For solopreneurs building toward a small team, the highest-leverage single hire is often a coordinator or operations person. This person does not produce output themselves. They coordinate the people who do. They own the SOP library. They manage the project management system. They are the first line of quality review. They handle the communication overhead that would otherwise fall on you.

A competent coordinator at 20 hours per week can multiply the output of a team of 3 to 5 contractors while freeing you from the management overhead that would otherwise keep you from your highest-leverage work.

## Staying Strategic

The discipline required to stay in your Zone of Genius as your team grows is largely the discipline of saying no. No to being the person who handles exceptions. No to being pulled into operational decisions that your SOPs should cover. No to being the bottleneck that everything flows through.

Build systems that make these decisions without you. SOPs for common exceptions. Escalation criteria that define what requires your input and what does not. Contractor empowerment to make certain decisions within defined parameters.

The test of a well-built delegation system is whether your business can operate at 80 percent capacity for a week while you are unreachable. If it can, you have built something real. If it cannot, you have built a business that still depends entirely on you, regardless of how many contractors are on the roster.

## The Feedback Loop That Improves Everything

The best contractor relationships are not static. They improve over time as the contractor learns your preferences, your standards, and your context, and as you improve your ability to brief clearly and give useful feedback.

Build a deliberate feedback loop into every ongoing contractor relationship. Monthly: a brief written review of what is working and what needs improvement. Quarterly: a slightly more structured review of the overall engagement, including whether the scope is right, whether the rate reflects the value being delivered, and what each side needs from the relationship going forward.

Contractors who are treated as partners, given honest feedback, and invested in over time produce dramatically better output than contractors treated as interchangeable vendors. The relationships that compound over 12 or 24 months are worth far more than the marginal savings from constantly finding cheaper alternatives.

# Conclusion: The Solopreneur Who Delegates Is the One Who Scales

The solopreneur who does everything themselves has a ceiling. It is set by the number of hours they can work and the number of tasks they can hold in their head simultaneously. No amount of productivity optimization, time management, or efficiency hacking permanently removes that ceiling. It only pushes it slightly higher.

Outsourcing removes the ceiling. When you have built the systems, you are not limited by your personal bandwidth. You are limited by the quality of your architecture: your SOPs, your contractor relationships, your quality standards, your feedback loops.

This guide has given you the structure to build that architecture. The decision framework for what to outsource. The process for finding contractors who are actually capable. The SOP system that transfers context without constant supervision. The quality management approach that does not require micromanagement. The true cost analysis that makes the investment decision clear. The lean discipline that keeps you from building bureaucracy. The transition framework for growing toward a team without becoming a full-time manager.

What it has not given you is the willingness to start.

That is the actual bottleneck for most solopreneurs. Not knowledge. Willingness. The willingness to invest time in creating an SOP for a task that would take less time to just do once. The willingness to pay for a test project and risk having it come back wrong. The willingness to trust another person with work that has your name on it.

All of those things are uncomfortable. They are also the exact things that separate solopreneurs who scale from those who stay permanently on the ceiling.

The architecture is here. The decision is yours.

## Appendix: Quick-Reference Checklist for Your First Outsourcing Hire

Use this checklist the first time you bring on a contractor. It consolidates the key decision points from each chapter into a single sequence.

**Before you post the job:**

- Complete your time audit for at least one full work week
- Categorize every recurring task into Zone of Genius, Zone of Competence, or Zone of Incompetence
- Apply the frequency filter: is the task recurring, or one-time?
- Apply the emotional resistance test: is this task generating psychological drag?
- Define the output in specific, observable terms before posting anything

**While evaluating candidates:**

- Request portfolio samples that match the exact task type, not adjacent work
- Evaluate response quality: does their first message suggest they read your brief carefully?
- Check the review history on platform profiles for patterns, not just average scores
- Shortlist 2 to 3 candidates before making any decisions

**The test project:**

- Scope it to 2 to 5 hours of real work at their stated rate
- Write the brief as precisely as you would write it for any ongoing engagement
- Observe the output, the timeline adherence, and the response to your feedback
- Only advance to an ongoing engagement if all three pass

**Before the first ongoing task:**

- Create the SOP using the recording method: screen record yourself doing the task with voiceover
- Store the SOP in your central library with the trigger, input, steps, output, and quality check
- Walk the contractor through the SOP on a single synchronous call
- Establish the feedback cadence: weekly async update, monthly written review

**Ongoing quality management:**

- Check output against the quality checklist in the SOP, not against your gut
- Give specific, written feedback tied to observable standards
- Track the feedback-to-correction cycle: if the same issue appears three times without correction, address the fit question directly
- Review the SOP for accuracy every 30 days and update based on contractor notes

**The cost check (quarterly):**

- Calculate the all-in cost of each contractor relationship including your review time
- Calculate the marginal value of the hours freed by the delegation
- Compare the two numbers. If the freed hours are generating more value than the all-in cost, the relationship has positive economics
- If not, investigate whether the issue is the contractor, the SOP, or the task selection

This checklist will not prevent every outsourcing problem. No checklist does. What it prevents is the most common failure mode: starting an outsourcing relationship without the systems that make it work. The scar tissue most solopreneurs carry from bad contractor experiences is almost always traceable to one or more of the steps above being skipped.

Start systematically. Build the system once. Then run every new hire through it. The overhead of building the system is paid once. The returns compound indefinitely.
