## Introduction: The Inbox Is Still the Most Valuable Real Estate on the Internet

Social platforms rent you attention. Email gives you ownership.

When your Instagram account gets shadowbanned or TikTok changes its algorithm, your followers become inaccessible overnight. Your email list is different. It is a direct line to people who raised their hand and said they want to hear from you. No algorithm decides who sees your message. No platform can throttle your reach. You own it.

But most solopreneurs treat email like a newsletter obligation. They send updates when they feel inspired. They blast announcements with no structure. They wonder why their list of 3,000 subscribers generates almost no revenue.

The problem is not the size of the list. It is the architecture.

Email works when it is engineered, not improvised. The difference between a list that generates $500/month and one that generates $5,000/month is not the number of subscribers. It is the system behind the sequences.

This playbook is a complete architecture. Not theory. Not a motivational overview. A working system.

You will get the specific sequences, the logic behind each one, the tools to build them, and the metrics to measure them. By the end, you will have a clear map of every email your business needs, when it sends, what it says, and why.

One disclaimer before we start: email automation is not a substitute for genuine value. Automation amplifies. If what you are automating is weak, you will automate your way to unsubscribes. This playbook assumes you have something worth selling. Your job is to build the infrastructure that connects it to the people who need it.

## Chapter 1: The Architecture Before the Emails

Most people start by writing emails. That is the wrong starting point.

Before you write a single subject line, you need to understand the full map. Email sequences are not just individual messages. They are a conversation architecture. Each email connects to a reader's emotional state and moves them from one place to another.

The architecture has four layers.

**Layer 1: The List**

Your list is segmented by intent. Every subscriber falls into one of three categories: curious (they downloaded something free), warm (they have engaged with multiple emails or bought before), and active buyers (they have purchased and are ready for the next thing).

Most people treat all subscribers the same. That is the first mistake. Someone who downloaded a free template two years ago and never opened another email should not receive the same sequence as someone who just bought your $49 course.

**Layer 2: The Triggers**

Automation runs on triggers. A trigger is an event that starts a sequence. Common triggers include a form submission, a link click, a purchase, a tag being applied, a date, or a lack of action (the absence of an open or click).

Understand your triggers before you build. Map out every entry point in your business. Free lead magnet downloads. Product purchases. Webinar registrations. Quiz completions. Each one is a trigger, and each trigger should start a specific, intentional conversation.

**Layer 3: The Sequences**

A sequence is a series of emails attached to a trigger. Each sequence has a job. The welcome sequence builds trust. The nurture sequence builds belief. The launch sequence creates urgency. The abandoned cart sequence recovers revenue. The re-engagement sequence cleans your list.

You do not need all of these on day one. You build them in order of impact. Start with the welcome sequence. Then add nurture. Then launch. Then recovery.

**Layer 4: The Exits**

Every sequence needs an exit condition. What happens to a subscriber when they complete a sequence? Where do they go next? Most systems dump people into a general newsletter by default. That is fine. But intentional exits, where you move people into a new sequence based on their behavior, are where the compounding begins.

Someone who clicks a link about a specific product in your nurture sequence should automatically exit into a sales sequence for that product. That is automation working as it should.

Draw this map before you build anything. A whiteboard, a Notion doc, a napkin. The medium does not matter. The clarity does.

## Chapter 2: The Welcome Sequence

The welcome sequence is the most important automation you will ever build. It sets the expectation for everything that follows.

Most people ruin it in two ways. They send one generic "thanks for subscribing" email and go silent for weeks. Or they immediately pitch their product before the reader knows who they are.

Both approaches treat email like broadcasting. Email is not broadcasting. It is conversation.

**The Welcome Sequence Framework: 5 Emails Over 7 Days**

**Email 1: Day 0 (Immediate) — Deliver + Introduce**

This email sends the moment someone subscribes. Its job is to deliver what you promised (the lead magnet, the free resource, the thing they signed up for) and introduce yourself in one sentence.

Not your full story. One sentence that establishes what you do and who you do it for.

Subject line formula: "[What they asked for] is here — plus one thing about me"

Keep this email under 150 words. Deliver the thing. Tell them what to expect from future emails. That is it.

**Email 2: Day 1 — The Origin Story**

This is where you earn credibility without claiming it. Tell the story of the specific moment you realized the thing your product helps with was a real problem.

Not your full biography. Not your credentials. The one moment of friction or failure that led you to build what you built.

This email should feel personal. It should make the reader think "this person has actually been through this." That recognition is the foundation of trust.

Keep it under 300 words. End with a question that invites a reply. Replies signal engagement to email providers and move your messages out of the promotions tab.

**Email 3: Day 3 — The Belief Shift**

Every product you sell is attached to a belief. Your reader currently holds a belief that prevents them from solving their problem. Your job is to show them that belief is wrong.

If you sell a course on email marketing, the limiting belief might be: "My audience is too small to make email worth it." Your Email 3 challenges that belief with evidence.

Not by arguing. By showing. Use a specific example, a case study, a data point, or a short story that demonstrates the alternative.

This email plants the seed for your eventual offer. It does not mention the offer yet.

**Email 4: Day 5 — The Useful Thing**

Send something genuinely useful with no strings attached. A checklist. A process. A framework. A recommendation. Something they can use right now.

This email builds what marketers call "reciprocity." When you give before you ask, the eventual ask lands differently.

The useful thing should be adjacent to your product but not a replacement for it. Give them the map. Sell them the terrain.

**Email 5: Day 7 — The Soft Offer**

After building trust for six days, you can mention your product. Not with pressure. With context.

"I've been sharing [topic] with you this week. If you want to go deeper, here's what I built for that."

One link. Clear value proposition. No countdown timer, no manufactured urgency. Just a direct, honest offer.

If they do not buy, they flow into your nurture sequence. The conversation continues.

**Key rules for the welcome sequence:**

- Every email should feel like it was written to one person, not a list
- Avoid images in the first email (they increase load time and trigger spam filters)
- Plain text or minimal formatting outperforms heavy HTML in early sequences
- Never mention unsubscribing in the first email (it plants the idea)

## Chapter 3: Nurture Sequences — Building Belief Over Time

The welcome sequence builds initial trust. The nurture sequence deepens it.

A nurture sequence is a long-running set of emails, typically sent weekly or biweekly, that continues to deliver value while slowly building the case for your products. Think of it as a magazine subscription that also happens to occasionally recommend things worth buying.

The failure mode here is staying in pure content mode forever. Some creators nurture for months and never make an offer. Their list loves them but does not buy from them. Generosity without structure is just expensive content creation.

**The Nurture Sequence Architecture**

A well-built nurture sequence alternates between three email types:

**Type 1: The Insight Email**

One idea, explored with depth. Not a listicle. A single insight about your topic, developed over 300-500 words.

The format: introduce the idea, challenge the conventional view, show the alternative, leave them with something to act on.

Readers forward insight emails. They reply to them. They save them. This is the email type that builds the kind of relationship where people feel like they know you even before they buy.

**Type 2: The Story Email**

A case study, customer result, or personal experience that demonstrates a principle in action.

Stories do the selling that arguments cannot. When a reader sees themselves in your story, the product becomes obvious. You are not convincing them. You are showing them evidence they can apply.

Keep story emails narrative-first. The lesson can come at the end, but the story should stand on its own.

**Type 3: The Direct Offer Email**

Every four to six weeks, send a direct offer email. Not disguised as content. Openly a pitch.

This sounds counterintuitive. Most marketers bury their offers in content to avoid seeming salesy. The problem is that buried offers get buried results.

When you have built real trust through insight and story emails, a direct offer email does not feel manipulative. It feels like a natural next step. "We've been talking about [problem]. Here's the most direct way I know to solve it."

**Nurture Sequence Cadence**

For most solopreneurs, one email per week is the right cadence. More than that, and you are fighting for attention against everything else in the inbox. Less than that, and you become a stranger between emails.

Plan your nurture sequence in 90-day blocks. Map out 12 emails: 8 insight or story emails, 4 offer emails. Review and update the block every quarter.

**Behavioral Branching in Nurture**

Once your platform supports it, add behavioral branching. This means: if a subscriber clicks a link about Product A in a nurture email, they automatically get tagged as "interested in Product A" and enter a short product-specific sequence.

This is where email automation starts compounding. The same list, more intelligently segmented, generates more revenue without more subscribers.

## Chapter 4: Product Launch Sequences

A product launch sequence is the engine of a launch. Without it, a launch is just a price drop announcement. With it, a launch becomes an event.

The sequence has a specific job: take someone from unaware or mildly curious to ready to buy over a defined period of time. Usually 5 to 10 days.

**The Pre-Launch Phase (Days 1-3)**

Before you announce the product, announce the problem.

Send 2-3 emails that deepen the reader's awareness of the problem your product solves. Do not mention the product yet. Make the problem feel real and immediate.

Email 1: "Why [problem] is harder than it looks" — validate their struggle
Email 2: "The thing most people miss about [problem]" — reframe the issue
Email 3: "What actually works" — introduce the framework (not the product)

By the end of the pre-launch phase, your reader should be thinking: "Yes, this is exactly my problem, and I need a real solution."

**The Open Cart Phase (Days 4-7)**

Now you introduce the product. Three emails over four days.

**Open Cart Email (Day 4):** Announce the product. State the transformation, not the features. Make the price clear. Link to the sales page. Do not manufacture false urgency here.

**Social Proof Email (Day 5 or 6):** If you have early buyers, testimonials, or beta feedback, send a story about a specific person's result. If this is a new product, use a story about your own experience with the problem.

**FAQ Email (Day 6 or 7):** Before cart close, address the top objections. "Is this for beginners?" "What if I've already tried [competing approach]?" "How long does it take?" Honest answers to real questions.

**Close Email (Day 7 or 8):** Cart closes. Be direct. Tell them why today is the last day (if it genuinely is). Give them one final reason to act. Keep this email short.

**Post-Launch**

After the launch window closes, move buyers into a buyer sequence (onboarding, delivery, upsells). Move non-buyers back into nurture. Tag them as "saw launch, did not buy" so you can adjust your messaging in future sequences.

**Evergreen Launch Sequences**

Once you have run a live launch and refined the sequence, you can convert it to evergreen. An evergreen launch uses a countdown timer that starts when a subscriber enters the sequence, not a fixed calendar date.

Platforms like ConvertKit allow this through visual automations. A new subscriber can enter your welcome sequence, flow into nurture, and then automatically enter an evergreen launch sequence 30 days later. From the subscriber's perspective, it feels like a live launch. For you, it runs without intervention.

The key to ethical evergreen launches: the scarcity must be real or the timer must be clearly stated as an individual window, not a fake "offer ends midnight." Readers notice when the timer resets. It erodes trust immediately.

## Chapter 5: Abandoned Cart Recovery

Abandoned cart sequences are the most overlooked revenue in any digital product business.

The conversion rate on a checkout page typically sits between 1% and 3%. That means 97% of people who visit your checkout page leave without buying. A percentage of those people intended to buy. They got distracted, had second thoughts, lost internet connection, or found the process confusing.

An abandoned cart sequence recovers a portion of that revenue automatically.

**How Abandoned Cart Tracking Works**

For digital products, abandoned cart recovery requires either:

1. A checkout platform that has native abandonment detection (ThriveCart, Gumroad Pro, Kajabi)
2. A tag applied when someone clicks your checkout link, which you then compare against purchase tags

The second method is simpler for solopreneurs. When someone clicks your checkout link, apply a tag called "checkout-started." When they purchase, apply a tag called "purchased." Anyone with the first tag but not the second after 2 hours is an abandoned cart.

Your email platform can run an automation that checks this condition and sends a sequence to people who match.

**The Three-Email Abandoned Cart Sequence**

**Email 1 — 2 Hours After Abandonment: The Gentle Check-In**

Do not assume they intended to buy and got distracted. Approach this as a helpful check-in.

Subject: "Did something go wrong?"

Body: Acknowledge that they started checkout. Ask if they ran into a technical issue. Provide a direct link back. Keep this under 100 words. No pressure.

**Email 2 — 24 Hours After Abandonment: Address the Hesitation**

By 24 hours, if they have not returned, hesitation is the likely reason. This email addresses the top objection for your specific product.

If you sell a course: "Is [outcome] actually achievable for someone in [their situation]?" Then answer it honestly with evidence.

End with a clear call to action and the link.

**Email 3 — 48 Hours After Abandonment: The Final Nudge**

This email closes the loop. Tell them you are removing them from this follow-up sequence so you are not cluttering their inbox. Make one last clear offer.

If you have a money-back guarantee, mention it here. A risk reversal at the moment of hesitation moves a specific segment of non-buyers.

**What You Should Not Do**

Do not send more than three emails in this sequence. More than three starts to feel like pressure rather than helpfulness. Do not offer a discount in the first email. Trained buyers will abandon carts intentionally if they learn discounts follow. If you offer a discount at all, make it the third email and make it a one-time offer.

## Chapter 6: Segmentation Basics

Segmentation is the practice of dividing your list into groups based on behavior, interest, or stage. It is how you make one list feel like it is sending personalized emails to every individual.

**The Three Segmentation Methods**

**Method 1: Tag-Based Segmentation**

Tags are labels applied to subscribers based on actions. Downloaded a specific lead magnet: tag. Clicked a link about a specific topic: tag. Made a purchase: tag. Attended a webinar: tag.

Tags are the most flexible segmentation method. They accumulate over time and paint a detailed picture of each subscriber's interests and behaviors.

Start with five core tags: source (where they came from), product interest (which product area they have shown interest in), purchase status (what they have bought), engagement level (active vs. dormant), and lead magnet (which freebie brought them in).

**Method 2: Segment-Based Segmentation**

Segments are dynamic filters that pull subscribers who currently match a set of criteria. A segment might be: "subscribed in the last 30 days AND tagged as interested in email marketing AND has not purchased."

Segments are useful for broadcast emails. Instead of emailing your full list, you send to the segment that is most likely to respond to a specific message.

**Method 3: Custom Fields**

Custom fields store data points about subscribers that are not yes/no. Job title, company size, country, annual revenue. These are useful if your audience is professionally segmented.

For most solopreneurs selling digital products, tags plus basic segments cover 90% of segmentation needs.

**The Engagement Segmentation You Are Probably Missing**

The most impactful segmentation most solopreneurs ignore is engagement scoring.

An "engaged subscriber" is one who has opened at least one email in the last 90 days. A "dormant subscriber" has not opened anything in 90 days.

You should treat these groups differently. Engaged subscribers get your full content. Dormant subscribers get a re-engagement sequence (covered later in this chapter). Those who do not re-engage get removed.

Clean lists perform better. A list of 2,000 active subscribers generates more revenue than a list of 10,000 where 80% are dormant. Email providers also charge by list size, and poor engagement rates hurt deliverability for everyone.

**Re-Engagement Sequences**

A re-engagement sequence is a 3-email series sent to dormant subscribers.

Email 1: "Are we still good?" — low-pressure check-in, remind them of value
Email 2: "Here's what you've missed" — surface your best content from the last 90 days
Email 3: "Last call" — tell them you are removing them unless they click to stay

The subject line for Email 3 is the most important of the three. It should be short and honest: "I'm removing you from this list (unless you want to stay)."

This email generates surprisingly high click rates because the subject line creates genuine curiosity and mild FOMO. Everyone who clicks gets tagged as re-engaged and moves back into active sequences.

Everyone who does not click gets removed.

## Chapter 7: Automation Tools Comparison

The right tool depends on where you are in your business. Not every platform is worth the price at every stage.

**ConvertKit (now Kit)**

ConvertKit is built for creators. Its visual automation builder is the best in class for most solopreneurs. You can build complex multi-step sequences with conditional branching using a drag-and-drop interface that actually makes sense.

Strengths: Visual automation builder, creator-focused features, strong landing page builder, good deliverability, excellent tagging system. The commerce features (selling directly through ConvertKit) are improving rapidly.

Weaknesses: More expensive than alternatives at scale. The free plan (up to 1,000 subscribers) does not include automations. The email editor is minimal, which is a strength for plain-text emails but a limitation for designed campaigns.

Price: Free up to 1,000 subscribers (no automations). Creator plan starts at $29/month for up to 3,000 subscribers. Creator Pro adds subscriber scoring and priority support.

Best for: Creators who are serious about email and want the best automation builder at a reasonable scale.

**Beehiiv**

Beehiiv is built for newsletter businesses. Its differentiator is monetization: the platform has a built-in ad network, paid subscription support, and referral program. If your business model centers on a newsletter rather than product sales, Beehiiv offers infrastructure ConvertKit does not.

Strengths: Superior newsletter editor, built-in paid subscriptions, ad network for monetization, excellent growth tools (referral programs, recommendation network), generous free tier.

Weaknesses: Automation is less mature than ConvertKit. The tagging system is simpler. If you need complex behavioral automations, you will hit walls faster. Less suited for product-first businesses.

Price: Free up to 2,500 subscribers with full features. Scale plan at $42/month adds advanced analytics and monetization.

Best for: Creators whose primary product is the newsletter itself. Not the best choice if your primary goal is automating product sales.

**MailerLite**

MailerLite is the budget-first option that punches significantly above its price. For solopreneurs who want solid automations without the ConvertKit pricing, MailerLite delivers.

Strengths: Excellent price-to-feature ratio, solid automation builder with behavioral triggers, landing pages and pop-ups included, e-commerce integrations, good deliverability.

Weaknesses: Interface can feel cluttered. The automation builder is functional but not as intuitive as ConvertKit's. Customer support at lower tiers is slower. The free tier (up to 1,000 subscribers) has MailerLite branding on forms and emails.

Price: Free up to 1,000 subscribers. Growing Business plan at $9/month for up to 500 subscribers (or $18/month for 2,500). Significantly cheaper than ConvertKit at equivalent subscriber counts.

Best for: Solopreneurs who need solid automation capabilities without a large budget. A rational choice before you are generating consistent revenue from email.

**Which Tool to Choose**

If you are starting from zero: MailerLite free tier. Learn the fundamentals without spending anything.

If you are generating $1,000+ per month and email is a primary channel: ConvertKit Creator plan. The automation builder will pay for itself.

If your business model is the newsletter: Beehiiv Scale plan once you have traction.

Switching platforms is painful. Pick based on where you are going, not just where you are now.

**Integrations Worth Knowing**

All three platforms integrate with Gumroad, ThriveCart, and Stripe. When a customer purchases through any of these, your email platform can automatically apply purchase tags and trigger buyer sequences.

Zapier connects everything that does not have a native integration. If you run a Gumroad store and use MailerLite, a Zap that fires "apply tag purchased-[product-name] when new Gumroad sale" takes 10 minutes to set up and runs forever.

## Chapter 8: Metrics That Matter

Most creators look at open rates and feel good or bad based on a number with no context. That is not measurement. That is noise.

Here are the metrics that actually tell you whether your email system is working.

**Deliverability Rate**

The percentage of emails that actually reach an inbox (not spam or junk). A healthy deliverability rate is above 95%. Below 90% is a signal that something is wrong with your domain reputation, list hygiene, or content.

To maintain good deliverability: use a custom sending domain, never buy email lists, clean dormant subscribers regularly, and avoid spam trigger words in subject lines (free, guarantee, act now, winner, etc.).

**Open Rate**

Industry average for creator email sits between 25% and 40%. Below 20% signals a list health problem or deliverability issue. Above 40% typically means you have a highly engaged niche audience.

One important caveat: Apple's Mail Privacy Protection (introduced in 2021) artificially inflates open rates by pre-loading email pixels. Many senders see inflated open rates on Apple devices. Use open rate as a relative metric (is it trending up or down?) rather than an absolute one.

**Click-Through Rate (CTR)**

The percentage of recipients who clicked a link. A healthy CTR for a product-focused email sits between 2% and 5%. Higher for launch emails targeting warm audiences.

CTR is a more reliable metric than open rate because clicks are not affected by privacy proxies. If your CTR is dropping while your open rate stays flat, your content is not compelling enough to act on.

**Click-to-Open Rate (CTOR)**

CTR divided by open rate. This tells you how compelling your content is for people who actually read it. A strong CTOR is above 20%. Low CTOR with high open rate means your subject lines are strong but your content is not delivering on the promise.

**Revenue Per Subscriber (RPS)**

The most important metric in your entire email operation.

Total email-attributed revenue divided by total subscribers. A healthy RPS for a digital product business ranges from $1 to $5 per subscriber per month. Top-tier email operations see $5 to $20.

If your RPS is below $1, you have an offer problem, a sequence problem, or a list quality problem. This is the metric that tells you what your list is actually worth in dollar terms.

Calculate it monthly. Watch the trend over quarters.

**Unsubscribe Rate**

Healthy unsubscribe rate is below 0.5% per email. Above 0.5% means you are sending to the wrong people, sending too frequently, or the content is not matching expectations.

Do not fear unsubscribes. Someone who removes themselves from your list is not your customer. Keeping them artificially inflates your subscriber count and hurts your metrics.

**Conversion Rate on Sequences**

For each sequence, track the percentage of subscribers who complete the sequence and take the desired action. For a welcome sequence, the desired action might be a product click. For a launch sequence, it is a purchase.

Low conversion rate on a launch sequence that has good open rates tells you the offer is the problem. Low open rates on a nurture sequence tell you the subject lines are weak. These are actionable diagnostics.

**How to Set Up Tracking**

Use UTM parameters on all links in your emails. A UTM parameter is a tag appended to a URL that tells your analytics platform where a visitor came from.

Format: `yourdomain.com/product?utm_source=email&utm_medium=automation&utm_campaign=launch-wave1`

When someone clicks this link and makes a purchase, your analytics shows that sale came from your email automation, launch wave 1. Over time, you build a clear picture of which sequences generate which revenue.

Most email platforms have built-in link tracking. Use both their native tracking and UTM parameters for redundancy.

## Conclusion: The System Is the Product

Here is the honest truth about email automation: the system you build is worth more than any individual email you write.

A single brilliant email generates a spike of revenue. A well-architected automation system generates revenue every day, from subscribers who joined six months ago, from abandoned carts at 2am, from launches that run without you touching them.

The compounding effect is real. Each sequence you add multiplies the work of every sequence already running.

Most solopreneurs never build this because it feels like too much to set up all at once. The antidote to that feeling is sequencing the work.

**90-Day Action Plan**

**Days 1-14: Foundation**

- Choose your email platform (use the criteria in Chapter 7)
- Set up a custom sending domain
- Build your welcome sequence (5 emails)
- Write and schedule all 5 emails
- Connect to your lead magnet delivery

**Days 15-30: Nurture**

- Map your first 90-day nurture block (12 emails)
- Write the first 4 and schedule them
- Add behavioral tags to your lead magnet links
- Set up your first direct offer email (week 4 of nurture)

**Days 31-60: Launch Infrastructure**

- Build your first launch sequence (8 emails)
- Set up purchase tags on your checkout platform
- Build a basic abandoned cart sequence (3 emails) using the tag method
- Run your first live launch

**Days 61-90: Optimization and Evergreen**

- Review metrics from your launch (open rate, CTR, CTOR, conversion rate, RPS)
- Identify the two weakest emails and rewrite them
- Set up re-engagement sequence for dormant subscribers
- Convert your launch sequence to evergreen
- Plan your next 90-day nurture block

By day 90, you have a working system. Not perfect. But running.

After that, your job shifts from builder to optimizer. You test subject lines. You rewrite low-CTR emails. You add new entry points to the system. You build sequences for new products.

The inbox is still the most valuable real estate on the internet. Most people leave it empty or let it fill with noise. Build the architecture. Write sequences worth reading. Show up consistently.

The gap between the people who make email work and those who do not is almost never about the size of their list. It is about whether they treated email as a system worth building.

You have the blueprint. Start building.

## Appendix B: Automation Checklist

Before any sequence goes live, run through this checklist:

- [ ] All links tested and working
- [ ] Unsubscribe link present in every email
- [ ] Custom sending domain configured and authenticated (SPF, DKIM, DMARC)
- [ ] Tags applied correctly on entry and exit
- [ ] Exit conditions defined (what happens after the last email)
- [ ] Behavioral branches set up for key links
- [ ] Test email sent to your own address and reviewed
- [ ] Sequence reviewed from subscriber perspective (read them in order)
- [ ] UTM parameters on all product links
- [ ] Confirmation that sequence does not overlap with other active sequences for the same subscriber

## Appendix C: Glossary of Terms

**Automation:** A set of emails triggered by a subscriber action, sent automatically without manual intervention.

**Bounce Rate:** The percentage of emails that could not be delivered. Hard bounces (invalid addresses) should be removed immediately. Soft bounces (temporary issues) can be retried.

**DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail):** An email authentication method that verifies the sender's identity. Required for good deliverability.

**DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication):** A policy that tells receiving servers how to handle emails that fail authentication checks.

**Double Opt-In:** A subscription process that requires a confirmation click before adding someone to your list. Produces a smaller but more engaged list.

**Lead Magnet:** A free resource offered in exchange for an email address. The entry point to your email system.

**Open Rate:** The percentage of delivered emails that were opened by recipients.

**Sequence:** A series of emails that send in a specific order, usually triggered by a subscriber action.

**SPF (Sender Policy Framework):** An email authentication record that specifies which servers are authorized to send email on behalf of your domain.

**Tag:** A label applied to a subscriber to indicate a specific behavior, interest, or status.

**UTM Parameters:** Tags appended to URLs that allow analytics tools to track the source of traffic and conversions.
