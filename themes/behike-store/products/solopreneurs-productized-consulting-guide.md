# The Solopreneur's Productized Consulting Guide
### How to Stop Selling Time and Start Selling Outcomes

---

**By Behike**

---

## The Thesis

Custom consulting is a trap. You solve a unique problem for a unique client, invoice them, then start from scratch with the next one. Every engagement is a new negotiation, a new scope, a new delivery process. Your income is high per hour but your business is not scalable, because you are the product.

Productized consulting is the alternative. You take one specific problem, one specific solution, one specific delivery format, and you repeat that process at scale. Same input, same output, reliably. You are still a consultant, but you have built a product around the work instead of selling the work raw.

This guide covers the mechanics of making that shift. Not the philosophy, the mechanics: what to productize, how to scope it, how to price it, how to sell it, how to deliver it without your life unraveling, and how to build from one productized service into a portfolio.

---

## Section 1: What Productized Consulting Actually Means

The term gets used loosely. Let's be precise.

Productized consulting means packaging a consulting service into a fixed deliverable, fixed price, and fixed timeline. The client does not hire you for your time or your expertise in the abstract. They buy a specific thing with a specific outcome.

Examples of the difference:

**Custom consulting:** "I'll audit your content strategy and give you recommendations." Price: $150/hour. Timeline: depends on what we find. Deliverable: a document, probably.

**Productized consulting:** "Content Strategy Audit: I review your last 90 days of content, benchmark against three competitors, and deliver a 10-point action plan in a recorded Loom walkthrough. $750 flat. 5 business days." Fixed. Clear. Repeatable.

The client knows exactly what they are getting. You know exactly what you are delivering. Neither party is guessing.

This is not the same as selling digital products. You are still doing real work. The difference is that the work is scoped, templated, and bounded. Your judgment and expertise are inside the process, not sold separately at an hourly rate.

The business model shift this creates: instead of your income depending on how many hours you work, it depends on how many units you sell and how efficiently you deliver each one. That is a fundamentally different kind of leverage.

## Section 2: The 3 Criteria for a Service Worth Productizing

Not every service can be productized. Some work is genuinely custom by nature and should stay that way. Before you invest in building a productized offer, run it through these three criteria.

**Criterion 1: The problem is specific and recurring.**

A service worth productizing solves a problem that many clients in your target market face in roughly the same form. If every client comes to you with a completely different situation requiring a completely different approach, productizing is premature. You need enough repetition in the problem to build a repeatable solution.

Ask: do my last five clients have the same core problem, even if the surface details differ? If yes, you have the raw material for a productized offer.

**Criterion 2: The output is definable.**

Can you describe what the client receives in one to three sentences, including the format, quantity, and what they can do with it afterward? If your deliverable description requires a phone call to explain, it is not defined enough to productize.

"A 12-page SEO audit with priority-ranked recommendations and a 3-month implementation checklist, delivered as a formatted PDF" is defined. "A comprehensive analysis of your digital presence" is not.

**Criterion 3: The delivery can be templated.**

You need to be able to build a repeatable process for doing the work. Not identical work, but an identical workflow. The same intake form, the same research steps, the same structure for the output, the same QA checklist before delivery.

If the process is so variable that every engagement requires you to reinvent how you work, the delivery cost will stay high and the quality will stay inconsistent. Both kill the productized model.

If a service passes all three criteria, it is ready to be scoped.

## Section 3: Scoping a Productized Offer

Scoping is where most productized consulting efforts go wrong. People either scope too broadly (and create a custom engagement with a fixed price, which means they lose money on complex clients) or too narrowly (and create something so small it is hard to justify the price).

The goal of scoping is to find the minimum complete unit of value.

A minimum complete unit of value is the smallest version of the work that produces a meaningful outcome for the client. Not a starter kit. Not a teaser. The full thing, just scoped tightly.

**How to find it:**

Start with the outcome, not the deliverable. What does the client have or know or be able to do after the engagement that they could not before? Write that down in one specific sentence.

Then work backward: what is the minimum set of work required to produce that outcome reliably? Cut everything else.

Example: a freelance copywriter wants to productize.

Outcome: "The client has a high-converting homepage that clearly communicates their offer to their ideal customer."

Minimum work: a 90-minute interview, competitor research on three brands, one homepage draft, two rounds of revision.

Not included: a full website audit, brand strategy, email sequence, social media copy.

The scope is tight. The outcome is real. The deliverable is clear.

**Fixed deliverable, fixed price, fixed time:**

These three things must all be defined before you sell a single unit.

- Fixed deliverable: the exact thing the client receives
- Fixed price: the number, no negotiation, no add-ons without a separate SKU
- Fixed time: the turnaround, stated in business days, and held

If you cannot commit to all three, the offer is not ready.

## Section 4: Pricing for Profit, Not Just Revenue

Most service providers underprice because they calculate hourly rate times estimated hours and add a modest margin. This is the wrong math.

Productized consulting should be priced on value delivered, not time spent.

**The value-based pricing framework:**

Step 1: Define the outcome in concrete terms. What does the client gain? Revenue increase, time saved, a specific asset they can use.

Step 2: Estimate the value of that outcome to the client. Not to you, to them. A homepage that converts 2% better for an e-commerce brand doing $500K/year is worth $10,000/year in additional revenue.

Step 3: Price at a fraction of that value. Convention in professional services is 10 to 20% of the first year's value. A $10,000/year outcome justifies $1,000 to $2,000 for the engagement.

Step 4: Check against your delivery cost and margin. How long does the work actually take? At your productized rate, is the hourly implied rate acceptable? It should be substantially higher than you would charge by the hour, because you are being compensated for the system you built, not just the hours you work.

**On pricing too low:**

Low prices do not attract more clients. They attract the wrong clients. The client paying $200 for a strategy session is almost always more difficult, more demanding, and less likely to implement than the client paying $1,200. Price filters for seriousness.

**On pricing increases:**

When you have delivered 10 units of a productized service and the process is smooth, raise the price. You have now proven the system works. That proof has value. The price should reflect it.

## Section 5: Building the Delivery Process

The delivery process is the IP of your productized consulting business. It is what makes the outcome repeatable and the work scalable. A well-built delivery process is also what allows you to eventually delegate parts of the work without quality dropping.

**The four components of a delivery process:**

**1. Intake.** A form or brief that collects everything you need before you start work. The intake should be comprehensive enough that you can begin without a kickoff call. If you need a call to clarify the brief, the form is incomplete.

**2. Production workflow.** A documented sequence of steps you follow every time. This is the internal recipe. Step one: review intake. Step two: run competitor analysis using [specific tool] with [specific parameters]. Step three: complete section one of the output template. Continue through delivery.

**3. Output template.** The structure of the deliverable, built once and reused for every client. Sections are predefined. The consultant fills in the content, not the format. This reduces cognitive load and keeps quality consistent.

**4. QA checklist.** Before delivering, check against a list of quality criteria. Does the deliverable meet the scope? Is every section complete? Is the recommendation specific enough to act on? Does it address what the intake brief asked for?

When all four components are built, you have a delivery machine. Any unit that goes through it produces roughly the same quality output, regardless of how busy you are or where you are in the day.

This is the difference between a productized service and a productized consulting business.

## Section 6: Sales Page Design for Productized Services

A productized service sales page has a different job than a typical service page. It does not need to convince someone you are a good consultant. It needs to answer four questions:

1. What exactly will I get?
2. What outcome does it produce?
3. Who is this for (and who is it not for)?
4. How do I buy it right now?

**What not to include:**

Long "about me" sections. Testimonials that are not specific to this exact service. FAQ sections with more than five questions. Anything that creates ambiguity about scope or price.

**Structure that works:**

Headline: the specific outcome, stated clearly. Not "let me help you grow" but "Your complete content audit, delivered in 5 days."

Subheadline: the mechanism. One sentence explaining how you produce the outcome.

What you get: a bulleted list of the exact deliverables. Be specific. Page counts, formats, turnaround times.

Who this is for: two to four sentences describing the ideal client. Specificity here increases conversion because the right person sees themselves immediately.

Price and buy button: visible without scrolling on most screens. No "contact me for a quote." The price is the price.

Proof: one to three specific testimonials about this specific service, or case study results in plain numbers.

The page should be short. Two to three scrolls on desktop. A productized offer is simple by design. The sales page should be, too.

## Section 7: The Waitlist Strategy

One of the most effective ways to launch a productized service is to sell it before you have capacity to deliver it. This sounds backwards. It is actually how you validate demand without overcommitting.

**How the waitlist strategy works:**

Build the sales page. Set the price. Add a waitlist form instead of a buy button. Run a small amount of traffic or announce to your existing audience.

Measure the sign-up rate. If 30 people join the waitlist within two weeks of a soft launch to 500 people, you have a conversion rate worth building for. If 3 people join, the offer or the audience is wrong.

The waitlist also creates scarcity without manufactured urgency. You are not saying "only 10 spots left" as a tactic. You are genuinely controlling capacity to protect your ability to deliver well. When you open the first cohort, waitlist members get first access. They have already made a partial commitment by joining, so conversion from waitlist to buyer is high.

**What to do while the waitlist grows:**

Build the delivery process. Complete the intake form, the output template, the QA checklist. When you open enrollment, you should be able to start delivering immediately.

Collect context from waitlist members. A simple form asking "what is the one thing you are hoping this will solve for you?" gives you the exact language to use in your sales copy and reveals if your target audience is interpreting the offer correctly.

The waitlist strategy is not about hype. It is about demand validation and delivery preparation happening simultaneously.

## Section 8: Handling Scope Creep in a Productized Model

Scope creep is the enemy of margin. A client asks for "one small addition." Then another. Then a revision that was not in the spec. Before long, a $1,200 engagement has taken three times the estimated hours.

In custom consulting, scope creep is managed through change orders and renegotiation. In productized consulting, it is managed structurally, before the engagement begins.

**Structural defenses against scope creep:**

**Clear intake forms.** If the client specifies what they want during intake and you confirm before starting, there is no ambiguity about what is included. "Based on your brief, I will be auditing your homepage and three product pages. Blog posts and email sequences are outside this scope." Write that confirmation in the delivery kickoff message.

**Fixed revision limits.** State the number of revisions included in the scope on the sales page and in the intake confirmation. "This service includes two rounds of revisions. Additional rounds are available at $150 each." This is not punitive. It is clear.

**Out-of-scope SKUs.** When a client asks for something beyond the scope, you have two options: decline or offer it as an add-on at a separate price. Build a list of common add-ons with fixed prices. "You mentioned you also need an email sequence. I offer a 5-email welcome sequence as a separate service for $550."

The psychology shift required here: you are not being difficult when you hold scope. You are being professional. Clients who respect the process will appreciate the clarity. Clients who push back on every boundary are telling you something important about whether they belong in your client roster.

## Section 9: Scaling from One Service to a Suite

The first productized service is the hardest to build because you are building the methodology from scratch. Every subsequent service in the suite is easier because you already understand how to scope, price, template, and deliver.

The strategic question is not "what else can I offer?" but "what do my current clients need next?"

**The logical ascension model:**

Your productized services should have a natural sequence. After a client completes the audit, they need the implementation. After the implementation, they need the optimization. Each service is the logical next step from the one before it.

This means you are not selling to cold audiences repeatedly. You are deepening the relationship with clients who already trust your work. The cost of acquisition is near zero for repeat clients, and the lifetime value grows with each additional service in the suite.

**When to add a second service:**

You are ready to add a second productized service when:

- The first service has been delivered at least 10 times with consistent results
- You have a documented, repeatable delivery process for the first service
- You have identified the most common "what next" question from clients who complete the first service

Do not add a second service before the first one is running smoothly. A suite built on an unstable first service will collapse the moment the workload doubles.

**The ceiling of one:**

One productized service, delivered 20 times a month, priced at $1,000, is $20,000 in monthly revenue. One consultant, working four days a week, delivering each service in an average of four hours, can hit that number while working 80 hours per month on billable work. That is a real business.

A suite of three to five productized services at similar pricing creates multiple entry points, natural upsells, and resilience. If demand for one service softens, the others carry the load.

The ceiling of a well-built productized consulting suite is not set by your hours. It is set by your marketing reach and the depth of the problems you solve.

---

## Closing

The shift from custom consulting to productized consulting is not about doing less creative work. The creative work is still there. It is about building a container for that work that makes it repeatable, saleable, and deliverable at scale.

Custom consulting sells your judgment hour by hour. Productized consulting sells your judgment packaged into a system that clients can understand, buy, and receive without a lengthy discovery process.

The clients you want are not looking for a consultant who will figure it out with them. They are looking for someone who has figured it out and can deliver the result. Productized consulting is the structure that communicates that confidence.

Build the scope. Build the process. Build the sales page. Sell it. Deliver it. Improve it. Repeat.

That is the whole model.
