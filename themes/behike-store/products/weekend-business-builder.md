# The Weekend Business Builder

### How to build a profitable digital business in the hours you already have, without quitting your job, burning out, or sacrificing your weekends forever.

**By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin**

---

## Introduction: The Thesis

You do not need to quit your job to build a business.

You need 10 hours a week and the right sequence.

That is the entire argument. Everything in this guide is built around proving it.

Most people who fail at building a business on the side do not fail because they lacked time. They fail because they spent the time they had on the wrong things. Learning instead of doing. Planning instead of shipping. Optimizing something that does not yet exist. Waiting for the right moment, the right clarity, the right confidence.

The right moment does not arrive. It gets built.

Here is what actually happens: someone decides they want to build a business. They spend the first three months reading books, watching YouTube videos, taking courses. They tell themselves this is preparation. It is not. It is productive procrastination. The outcome of that phase is the same as doing nothing, except you now know more vocabulary for why you have not started.

Then they start building something, but they start with the wrong thing. They build the product before they have proof anyone wants it. They build a brand before they have an audience. They spend six months on a website before they have made a single dollar.

Then they burn out. Then they quit. Then they tell people that "building a side business is not realistic."

The problem was never time. The problem was sequence.

This guide is the sequence that works. It is built around a simple premise: there is an order of operations for building a business, and if you follow it, 10 hours a week is more than enough. If you ignore it, 40 hours a week will not save you.

Eight chapters. Real numbers. Specific timelines. No theory that does not connect to action.

Let's begin.

# Chapter 1: The 10-Hour Business Week

## The Time Audit

Before you can build anything, you need to know where your hours actually go.

Most people believe they do not have time. When they track their time for a week, they discover that is not true. They have time. It is fragmented, it is low-quality, it is easily pulled by distraction, but it exists.

Here is a simple audit. For one week, track every hour you are awake that is not committed. Committed means work, sleep, family obligation, health maintenance. Everything else is discretionary. Write it down.

What most people find:

- 1.5 to 2 hours per weekday evening (after dinner, before bed) that is currently spent on scrolling, TV, or passive entertainment.
- 3 to 5 hours on Saturday morning or afternoon that goes to low-value activities.
- 2 to 3 hours on Sunday that could be redirected without significant sacrifice.

That is 14 to 18 potential hours per week. You do not need all of them. You need 10.

## Where the 10 Hours Come From

The 10-hour model is not about stealing time from rest or family. It is about redirecting time you are already spending unintentionally.

The breakdown that works for most people:

**Monday through Friday:** 90 minutes per evening. That is 7.5 hours across five days.

**Saturday:** 1.5 to 2.5 hours in the morning, before the day gets loud.

**Total:** 9 to 10 hours.

That is it. No 4am alarm. No sacrificed weekends. No destroyed family dinners. Ninety minutes after the dishes are done. Saturday morning before the house wakes up.

The two rules for these blocks:

1. They are non-negotiable by default. You do not decide each day whether to show up. The block is already committed. You are deciding what to work on during it, not whether it happens.

2. They are protected from passive consumption. No course-watching. No book reading. No researching tools. The block is for building and shipping only.

## The Non-Negotiable Blocks

This is where most people fail before they even start. They treat their business time as optional. They fit it in when things are quiet. Things are never quiet. There will always be something.

You need to treat these 10 hours the way you treat your job. You show up whether or not you feel ready. You show up when you are tired. You show up when results are not yet visible.

The non-negotiable block does three things:

First, it removes the decision. Decision fatigue is real. Every time you ask yourself "should I work on this tonight?" you are burning cognitive fuel. Remove the question. The block exists. Show up.

Second, it creates a feedback loop. When you show up consistently, you build evidence that you are the kind of person who ships. That identity compounds. It becomes easier to show up, because you are reinforcing who you are.

Third, it compounds over time in ways that are invisible at first. Ninety minutes five days a week for a year is 390 hours. That is nearly 10 full-time work weeks. Inside 10 hours a week, compounded over 12 months, is a real business.

## Why Evenings and Weekends Are Enough

There is a persistent idea that building a business requires full-time focus. That you need to be all in. That dabbling does not work.

This is not accurate.

Here is what full-time focus actually gives you: volume and speed. If you can build something in 3 months working full-time, you can build the same thing in 12 months on the side. The business does not care. The market does not care. Your first client does not ask how many hours per week you spent building before you found them.

The constraint of part-time building is actually useful. It forces discipline. It forces ruthless prioritization. It forces you to do only the things that matter, because you do not have time for anything else.

Full-time founders often waste time precisely because they have it. The person with 90 minutes uses them. The person with 8 hours fills them.

The goal of this chapter is not to convince you that 10 hours is ideal. The goal is to establish that 10 hours is enough if you use them correctly. The rest of this guide is about how.

# Chapter 2: The Right Sequencing

## Why Most People Start in the Wrong Order

There is a natural, intuitive sequence that almost everyone follows when they decide to build a business:

1. Have an idea.
2. Build the product.
3. Build a brand around it.
4. Try to find customers.
5. Fail to find customers.
6. Assume the idea was wrong.

The problem is not the idea. The problem is that the sequence is backwards. Products built before proof of demand are guesses. Brands built before audiences are performances no one is watching. The person who builds first and finds customers second is spending weeks of their 10-hour blocks building something no one wants.

The correct sequence starts with the market, not the idea.

## The 4-Phase Sequence: Income, Audience, Product, Scale

This is the sequence that works. It is not new. It is what every successful one-person business follows, whether they articulate it this way or not.

**Phase 1: Income**

Before you build anything, you need proof that someone will pay for your skill or knowledge. The fastest way to get that proof is to offer a service. Not a productized service with a fancy landing page. A direct offer to a real person: "I can do X for you. Here is what it costs."

The goal of Phase 1 is to make your first dollar. Not to build a brand. Not to grow an audience. Not to build a system. To make one dollar with your hands. This validates the core assumption: your skill has market value.

Phase 1 ends when you have made $500 to $1,000 from direct service work.

**Phase 2: Audience**

Once you have proof that people pay for what you do, you start building an audience around the knowledge that underlies the service. Not about your business. About the problem you solve and the results you create.

The audience is the distribution channel for everything that comes later. Products need an audience to sell to. Services at scale need inbound leads. Content compounds in ways that paid advertising does not.

Phase 2 runs in parallel with Phase 1 after the first month. You are still doing service work. You are also posting consistently on one platform, writing emails, or building a list.

Phase 2 ends when you have 500 to 1,000 engaged followers or email subscribers who care about the topic you work in.

**Phase 3: Product**

With an audience who knows you and a service that has already validated demand, you build your first digital product. Not a course. A focused, specific guide or framework that solves one problem clearly. Priced between $19 and $49.

The product serves two purposes. It generates revenue that does not require your direct time. It also acts as a filter, identifying the people in your audience who are most serious about solving the problem you address.

Phase 3 ends when the product is launched and has made at least $500. This is usually achievable with a list of 500 to 1,000 subscribers and a 1 to 2% conversion rate on a well-written launch.

**Phase 4: Scale**

With income from services, a growing audience, and a product that sells without your presence, you now have the foundation to scale. Scale means: systematize the service so it can be delivered more efficiently, raise the price, build a second product, develop a higher-ticket offer, or begin transitioning away from the job.

Most people never reach Phase 4 on the side. Not because it is impossible, but because they skip Phase 1 and 2 and go directly to Phase 3 without validation or distribution.

The sequence matters because each phase builds what the next phase requires. Income builds confidence and proof. Audience builds distribution. Product builds leverage. Scale builds freedom.

## What to Build First

Start with the service.

Not the brand. Not the product. Not the website. Not the logo. Not the course.

The service.

Why? Because a service requires no capital, no audience, and no product to build. It requires only a skill and someone who needs it. You can start this week. You can close your first client in 7 to 14 days if you reach out directly to people who need what you can do.

The service also teaches you things about your market that no amount of research can replicate. You learn what people actually struggle with, not what they say they struggle with. You learn what objections they have, what language they use, what outcomes they actually want. This information is worth thousands of dollars and you cannot buy it. You earn it by doing the work.

# Chapter 3: The Minimum Viable Business

## The 3 Ramp-Up Stages

There is a clean ramp-up path for a one-person digital business. It has three stages, each with a specific financial milestone and a specific set of actions that get you there.

### Stage 1: First $100

The goal of Stage 1 is proof. Not $100 in the bank. Proof that you can create value and someone will pay for it.

The fastest path to $100:

1. Write down 3 to 5 skills you have that other people would pay to have done. Be honest. Not "I know a little about marketing." More like "I build WordPress sites" or "I write email sequences" or "I help coaches package their offers."

2. Write one sentence for each: "[Person] who [has problem] pays me [price] to [get result]."

3. Send a direct message or email to 10 people in your network who might know someone who needs that.

4. Close one job. Do it well.

That is Stage 1. The money is almost secondary. The proof is primary. You now know: someone paid for this. That means it is real.

Timeline: 1 to 4 weeks from decision to first payment.

### Stage 2: $1,000 Per Month

Moving from one job to $1,000/month recurring requires two things: repeatability and positioning.

Repeatability means you can deliver the same result consistently, not just once. You have a process. You know the steps. The output is predictable.

Positioning means you have said clearly who you help and what you help them achieve. Not "I do marketing." More like "I help e-commerce brands grow their email list."

At $1,000/month you need 2 to 4 clients paying $250 to $500/month for an ongoing service, or 1 client at $1,000/month for a project-based engagement.

How to get there from Stage 1:

- Complete the first job and ask for a referral or testimonial.
- Raise the price before taking the next client. Every time you close a new client, raise the rate 20 to 30%.
- Post about your results and process once per week on LinkedIn or Twitter/X.
- Reach out proactively to 5 new potential clients per week.

Timeline: 2 to 4 months from Stage 1.

### Stage 3: $3,000 Per Month

At $3,000/month on the side, you are earning roughly the equivalent of a second full-time income for most people in the United States. This is the threshold where the decision to leave the job becomes real.

To reach $3,000/month, you need one of three things:

Option A: 4 to 6 clients at $500 to $750/month each.
Option B: 2 to 3 clients at $1,000 to $1,500/month each.
Option C: 1 to 2 high-ticket clients at $1,500 to $3,000/month plus passive product income.

Option C is the most sustainable. High-ticket clients require less time per dollar. Product income does not require your time at all. Combining them means your income is not dependent on client retention.

Timeline: 6 to 12 months from Stage 2, depending on niche, outreach consistency, and whether you have begun building an audience.

## The Lowest-Friction Path

Here is the most direct route:

Week 1: Identify the skill. Send 10 outreach messages.
Week 2 to 4: Close first client, deliver, collect testimonial.
Month 2 to 3: Raise rate, close second client, start posting one time per week.
Month 3 to 6: Reach $1,000/month in service income, start building email list.
Month 6 to 9: Launch first digital product to list, reach $2,000 to $3,000/month combined.
Month 9 to 12: Evaluate job transition.

None of this requires quitting your job first. None of it requires startup capital. The first 10 outreach messages are free. The first product can be built in 20 hours of work.

# Chapter 4: The Service-First Model

## Why Services Beat Products at the Start

The conventional wisdom about passive income tells you to build a product and sell it while you sleep. That sounds correct. Here is the problem: products require an audience to sell to, and audiences take time to build.

If you build a product before you have an audience, you will spend 20 hours writing it and then fail to sell it because no one knows it exists. This is demoralizing. Many people give up here.

A service requires no audience. It requires one person who needs what you do. You can find that person through direct outreach, a warm introduction, a single post, or a conversation. You do not need thousands of followers. You need one person.

Services also pay more per unit of time than low-ticket products. A $500/month service client pays more in one month than 50 sales of a $9.99 ebook. And you can close your first service client in two weeks. You cannot get 50 ebook sales in two weeks without an audience that does not yet exist.

## The One-Offer Service Business

The mistake most service businesses make is trying to offer too much. They build a menu of services, write pages of copy for each one, and create a complicated pricing structure. This is a mistake at the start.

One service. One price. One outcome.

Example: "I write 4 email newsletters per month for founders with a list under 5,000 subscribers. $600/month."

That is it. One offer. Clear price. Clear deliverable. Clear target client.

Why this works:
- It is easy to say yes to. The client knows exactly what they are buying.
- It is easy to deliver. You do the same thing every month. You get better at it.
- It is easy to sell. You know exactly who to talk to.

Do not add services until you have fully booked out on the first one.

## The Capacity Ceiling

Every service business hits a capacity ceiling. There are only so many hours in a week, and each client takes some of them. On 10 hours per week, you might have capacity for 3 to 5 ongoing clients depending on the service. Above that, you are running out of room.

This is not a problem. It is a checkpoint.

When you hit the capacity ceiling, you have several options:

1. Raise prices. If you are fully booked, you can charge more. Raise rates by 25 to 50% and see if clients accept. If they do, you are making more per hour. If some leave, you have created space for higher-value work.

2. Productize. Take the most repeatable part of your service and turn it into a template, guide, or course. Sell it to people who cannot afford the done-for-you service.

3. Transition. Stop taking new clients. Focus on building the product layer.

Most people on 10 hours per week will hit the ceiling at $2,000 to $3,500/month in service income. This is when Phase 3 becomes urgent.

## The Transition to Products

The transition from service to product is not a clean handoff. It is a slow shift of income from time-based to asset-based.

The process:

**Month 1 to 3 of Phase 3:** Build the first product while still delivering service work. Use your client work as the research. What questions do clients always ask? What framework do you use every time? What would save a client 5 hours if they had it documented? That is your first product.

**Month 3 to 6:** Launch the product to your email list. Sell it at $19 to $49. Use the first launch to collect feedback and iterate. Do not try to build a funnel yet. Just send an email, tell people what it is, link to a simple sales page.

**Month 6 to 9:** Optimize the product based on feedback. Build the second product. Begin raising service prices while selectively reducing client count.

**Month 9 to 12:** Target 50% of income from products, 50% from services. This ratio creates resilience. If a client drops, the business does not collapse.

# Chapter 5: Protecting Your Energy

## Building Alongside a Job Without Burning Out

The most common reason side businesses fail is not lack of strategy. It is burnout.

The person works a full-time job, comes home, works on the business for 90 minutes, sleeps less than they should, repeats this for 3 months, starts resenting both the job and the business, stops working on the business, and eventually concludes that "it was not the right time."

The problem was not the time. The problem was that they treated business building as a second job with the same intensity and urgency as the first job. It cannot be both. You do not have two full jobs' worth of energy.

Building on the side requires a different relationship with output. You are playing a longer game. The nights where you write two hundred words instead of eight hundred still count. The weeks where you only show up four times out of five still count. The months where progress is invisible still count.

## The 4 Rules

**Rule 1: One priority per work block.**

Do not sit down at the block with a list of 10 things to do. Have one thing. Write it down before you start. When the block ends, you either finished it or you did not. No open loops.

**Rule 2: No decisions during work blocks.**

The block is for execution, not planning. Planning happens in the 10 minutes before the block. During the block, you build. If you find yourself deciding what to do during the block, that is a sign you did not plan the block correctly. Stop. Set a timer for 5 minutes. Write the single most important action. Then start.

**Rule 3: The block ends when the time ends.**

Even if you are in flow. Even if you want to keep going. End it. Go be present with your family, your rest, your evening. The business benefits from this. You return to the next block fresh. Extending blocks destroys consistency.

**Rule 4: Results lag effort by 6 to 8 weeks.**

You will not see results immediately. You will post 12 times and have no followers. You will write 4 newsletters and have 40 subscribers. You will send 20 outreach messages and close zero clients. This is not failure. This is lag. The efforts are real. The results are delayed. Most people quit during the lag. That is the only difference between people who succeed and people who do not.

## The Off-Switch Protocol

You need a clear end to the work day. Not just the business block. The entire day.

At the same time each evening, 30 to 60 minutes before sleep, close every work tab. Close every business-related notification. Put the phone in another room. The protocol exists so that your nervous system gets the signal: work is done.

This is not about productivity optimization. It is about survival over a 12 to 24 month timeline. A person who can sustain 10 hours per week for 18 months outproduces a person who sprints at 20 hours per week for 4 months and burns out.

The off-switch is not a luxury. It is a business strategy.

## The "Don't Break the Chain" System

Jerry Seinfeld's system is simple and it works: mark each day you do the work on a calendar. Do not break the chain. Over time, the chain itself becomes motivating. You are not working to make money. You are working to not break the chain.

Applied to a 10-hour business week:

Set 5 weekday blocks on your calendar. Each one you complete, mark it. Each Saturday morning block you complete, mark it. At the end of the week, if you completed 5 of 6 blocks, that is a successful week.

The standard is not perfection. The standard is consistency. 80% consistency over 52 weeks builds a business. 100% intensity for 6 weeks followed by collapse does not.

# Chapter 6: The Digital Product Layer

## When to Add Products

The most common mistake is adding products too early. You build a product before you have an audience, before you have proof of demand, before you have made money from the underlying skill. Then you wonder why it does not sell.

The right time to add products is when:

1. You have at least 500 engaged email subscribers or social followers who care about the specific problem your product addresses.
2. You have at least 3 months of service work or consulting behind you in the same area.
3. You have heard at least 10 to 20 people describe the problem your product solves, in their own words.

At that point, you are not guessing. You are packaging what you already know the market wants.

## The Product That Pays You While You Sleep

Not all products are equal. The one that works best at the start is a focused, specific guide or playbook priced at $19 to $49.

Why this price point? Because it is an easy yes. The buyer does not need to think about it. The perceived risk is low. The conversion rate on a well-written sales page at $19 to $49 is 2 to 5% from a warm audience, compared to 0.5 to 1% for a $97+ product from the same audience.

Why this format? Because a 3,000 to 6,000 word guide can be built in 10 to 20 hours. A full course takes 60 to 100 hours. For a first product, the speed of getting to market matters more than the depth of the product.

What makes a good first product:

- It solves one specific problem for one specific person.
- The title names the outcome, not the process.
- The content is 80% actionable, 20% conceptual.
- It is based on real work you have done, not research.

## The Content-to-Product Pipeline

The most efficient product creation system does not start with the product. It starts with content.

Here is how it works:

You post consistently about your expertise. Over time, you notice which posts get the most engagement, the most replies, the most questions. Those topics are signals. They tell you what your audience is struggling with.

The posts that get the most engagement become the table of contents for your product. You have already written the rough draft, distributed it as free content, and received market validation. You now package it into a coherent guide, add the implementation framework and templates, and sell it.

This pipeline works because your product already has an existing audience that has seen the core ideas. They are not reading something new. They are buying the systematized, practical version of ideas they already found valuable.

The pipeline:

- Week 1 to 12: Post consistently on one platform. Build a small email list from the posts.
- Week 12 to 16: Identify the 3 to 5 topics with the most engagement. Write the guide based on these.
- Week 16 to 20: Launch the guide to your email list. Collect feedback.
- Week 20 to 24: Create v2 based on feedback. Begin promoting through content.
- Month 6 onwards: Build second product based on the next most engaged topic.

# Chapter 7: The Transition Timeline

## The 3 Financial Checkpoints

The decision to leave a job should not be emotional. It should be mathematical. There are 3 checkpoints before the transition becomes responsible.

**Checkpoint 1: Replacement income.**

Your business income should equal at least 75% of your job income before you consider leaving. Not gross revenue. Net income after platform fees, tools, taxes, and other business expenses.

Why 75% and not 100%? Because leaving the job removes the ceiling on your time. With 40 additional hours per week, a business at 75% replacement income will typically reach 100% within 90 days.

**Checkpoint 2: 3 months of consistency.**

The income needs to be consistent for at least 3 consecutive months, not just one good month. One spike means you had a good month. Three consecutive months means you have a business.

This also gives you time to see whether the income is trending up or plateauing. A business growing at 15 to 20% per month is different from one stuck at the same number for 3 months.

**Checkpoint 3: Pipeline visibility.**

Before you leave the job, you should be able to see where the next 60 to 90 days of income is coming from. Active client contracts. Product sales data with a trend. Leads in conversation. Not hope. Visibility.

If you cannot answer the question "where is my income coming from in 60 days?" do not leave yet. The answer does not need to be certain. It needs to be grounded in real data.

## The 6-Month Runway Rule

The 6-month runway rule is simple: before leaving your job, have 6 months of personal living expenses in liquid savings that is separate from your business income.

This is not the emergency fund that financial advisors recommend. This is a strategic runway that removes desperation from the decision-making process.

Desperation is the most dangerous state for a new full-time founder. When you need money immediately, you take bad clients. You make decisions from scarcity. You lower prices when you should hold them. You say yes to wrong-fit work that pulls you away from building.

With 6 months of runway, you can afford to be selective. You can afford to wait 30 days for the right client instead of taking the wrong one immediately. You can afford a bad month. You can afford to focus on building instead of panicking.

Building the runway while working a job is the move. Use every month of business income to fund the runway, not lifestyle upgrades.

## The Risk Calculation for Solo Founders

The risk of leaving a job to build a business is frequently overstated. Here is the actual calculation:

You have a skill set. That skill set does not disappear if the business fails. If you spend 18 months building a business and it fails, you still have 18 more months of experience and a stronger skill set than when you started. Re-employment is almost always available.

The asymmetry is this: the upside of succeeding is uncapped. The downside of failing is bounded. You return to the job market with a better story, more skills, and more self-knowledge than when you left.

The risk that is real: depleting the runway and taking on debt to sustain a business that has no clear path to profitability. That is a different situation. The solution is to not deplete the runway and to build a business that is profitable before leaving.

The risk that is imagined: what if the business fails and I am unemployable? This does not happen. Professionals who build businesses, even those that fail, are more employable, not less.

# Chapter 8: The Compounding Years

## Year 1: Building the Foundation

Year 1 is the hardest and the most important. The work feels disproportionate to the results. This is normal. You are building infrastructure that will compound later.

What Year 1 looks like if you follow the sequence:

**Months 1 to 3:** First client, first dollar, beginning of the email list.
**Months 3 to 6:** $500 to $1,500/month in service income, 200 to 500 email subscribers, first product in development.
**Months 6 to 9:** $1,500 to $2,500/month combined, first product launched, 500 to 1,000 subscribers.
**Months 9 to 12:** $2,000 to $3,500/month combined, second product in development, evaluating job transition.

By the end of Year 1, you have:
- A validated service offer.
- At least one digital product with real sales.
- An email list with real subscribers.
- A track record of consistency.
- A clear view of what Year 2 looks like.

The number most people reach by the end of Year 1 on the 10-hour model: $1,500 to $3,000/month. Not every month. Not the first month. But by month 12, that range is realistic.

## Year 2: Building Momentum

Year 2 is when the compounding becomes visible. Your audience is larger. Your products are better. Your positioning is sharper. The service clients are higher quality because your reputation has grown.

What Year 2 looks like:

**Income:** $3,000 to $6,000/month. Many people cross $5,000/month in Year 2. This is the threshold where the job transition starts to feel practical rather than theoretical.

**Audience:** 2,000 to 5,000 email subscribers who have been engaged for a year or more. These are not cold leads. They know who you are and have been reading your work for months. Conversion on new products is higher than Year 1.

**Products:** 2 to 4 products. The first ones have been iterated and improved. You understand your customers better. Products are priced higher and the sales page is better.

**Time:** If you transitioned from the job in Year 2, you now have 30 to 40 hours per week instead of 10. This does not double your income immediately, but it accelerates the trajectory.

## Year 3: The Acceleration

Year 3 is when most people who stayed consistent reach a point they did not believe was possible when they started.

**Income:** $5,000 to $12,000/month. Some people are above this. Some are below. The range depends heavily on niche, pricing strategy, and whether the jump to full-time was made.

**Audience:** 5,000 to 15,000 email subscribers. At this point, a product launch to this list generates $5,000 to $30,000 in a single week. This is not a theory. This is what happens when you compound a real audience over a real timeline.

**Products:** A product suite. A flagship guide. A higher-ticket course. Possibly a community. The service may still exist but at a higher price with fewer clients.

**Business structure:** At Year 3, most people formalize the business. LLC, business account, simple accounting. Not because they have to, but because the numbers make it worth it.

## Year 5: The Long Game

Year 5 is where the original vision starts to feel small.

The person who started with 10 hours per week and $0 in Year 1 now has:
- $8,000 to $25,000+/month depending on niche and execution.
- An email list of 10,000 to 50,000 people.
- A product catalog that generates revenue daily without direct involvement.
- A personal brand with real reach.
- The option to stop, sell, scale, or pivot from a position of strength.

The math:

If you grow at a modest 10% per month for 24 months from a starting point of $500/month:
- Month 12: ~$1,570/month
- Month 18: ~$2,800/month
- Month 24: ~$5,000/month

At 10% monthly growth, which is conservative for someone following a clear sequence, $500/month becomes $5,000/month in two years. This does not require viral content. It does not require millions of followers. It requires consistency and the right sequence.

The mental model: you are planting trees. Year 1, you plant. Year 2, the roots deepen. Year 3, the branches spread. Year 5, you are sitting in the shade.

# Appendix

## Weekly Schedule Template

**Monday through Friday:**
- 7:00pm to 8:30pm: Business block. One task. No planning, only building.
- Priority order: Client deliverables first, outreach second, content third, product work fourth.

**Saturday:**
- 7:00am to 9:00am: Extended business block. Strategy and longer-form work.
- Use this block for product development, content batching, or outreach campaigns.

**Sunday:**
- Protected day. No business block unless you missed 3 or more weekday blocks.
- Use Sunday to plan the week's single priority tasks for each block.

**Weekly review (15 minutes every Sunday evening):**
- Did I complete 5 of 6 blocks?
- What was the one result this week?
- What is the single priority for each block next week?

## Service-to-Product Transition Checklist

Before building your first product, confirm all of the following:

- [ ] I have at least 3 months of service work in this area.
- [ ] I have at least 500 email subscribers or social followers who engage with this topic.
- [ ] I have heard at least 10 people describe this problem in their own words.
- [ ] I can write the table of contents from memory without researching.
- [ ] I have a specific person in mind who would buy this.
- [ ] I know the one outcome this product delivers.
- [ ] I have named the product based on the outcome, not the process.
- [ ] I can build the first version in 15 to 25 hours.
- [ ] I have a place to sell it (Gumroad, Lemon Squeezy, or a simple checkout page).
- [ ] I have a list to send the launch announcement to.

If fewer than 8 of these are true, do not build the product yet. Continue building the service and the audience.
