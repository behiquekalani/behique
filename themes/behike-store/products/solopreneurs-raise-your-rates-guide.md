# Raise Your Rates Guide

## Why Solopreneurs Wait Too Long

Most solopreneurs who read this guide are already undercharging. They have known it for months. Possibly years. They have had the thought while looking at a bank statement, while doing the math on an hourly rate, while watching a colleague charge twice as much for comparable work. The knowledge is not the problem. The delay is.

The delay comes from a specific fear: that raising rates will cost them clients, and losing clients will cost them income, and losing income will confirm some deeper fear about whether they are actually worth more.

That fear is understandable. It is also consistently wrong.

The economics of rate increases for solopreneurs are not intuitive. When you raise your rates by 30%, you do not need to lose 30% of your clients for your income to stay the same. You can lose 23% of your clients and earn exactly the same amount. You can lose 30% of your clients and earn 10% more because the clients who leave are almost always the highest-maintenance, lowest-satisfaction clients in your portfolio.

The most common outcome when solopreneurs raise their rates: one or two clients decline to continue, the relationships that end are the ones that were already most strained, and the solopreneur ends up with fewer clients, less stress, and the same or higher monthly income.

This guide is not motivational. It is operational. By the end of it you will know exactly when to raise your rates, how to communicate the increase, what to say to clients who push back, and how to price new clients from the start.

## How to Know When You Are Ready

There are four indicators that tell you a rate increase is overdue.

**Indicator 1: You are fully booked more than two months out.**

If you cannot take a new client without waiting list or turning work away, demand exceeds supply. That imbalance has one correct response: raise the price until supply and demand rebalance. You are subsidizing your clients' access to you every month you stay fully booked at current rates.

**Indicator 2: You have not raised rates in more than 12 months.**

Inflation alone justifies annual rate increases. The cost of your time, your tools, your professional development, and your living expenses increases every year. Rates that do not keep pace with these increases represent a real-terms pay cut. Annual rate reviews are standard practice in any sustainable freelance or consulting business.

**Indicator 3: You feel resentment toward certain clients.**

Resentment in client relationships is almost always a pricing signal. When you feel that the effort you are putting in is not reflected in the compensation, and that feeling persists, you are undercharging. The resentment is your subconscious doing the math.

The honest question to ask yourself: if this client doubled my rate today, would I feel better about this relationship? If the answer is yes, the rate is the problem.

**Indicator 4: Your best clients came without resistance to your current rate.**

If the clients who are easiest to work with, most appreciative of your results, and most aligned with how you work never pushed back on your price, you have evidence that your current rate is below their willingness to pay. The easiest clients often represent the ceiling you have not yet tested.

## The 3 Types of Rate Increases

Not all rate increases are the same. Understanding the type you are executing determines the approach.

### Type 1: The Incremental Increase

An incremental increase is a modest, scheduled adjustment communicated as standard practice. Typically 10 to 20%. This is the annual review increase, the kind a professional services firm would implement without apology.

Incremental increases should be framed as exactly what they are: a standard adjustment for your practice. The language is matter-of-fact. No lengthy justification, no apology, no negotiation invited. You are not asking for permission to charge more for the same work. You are notifying a client of your updated rate.

Incremental increases work well with stable, long-term clients who are getting consistent value. The relationship is strong enough to absorb a 15% increase without friction.

### Type 2: The Value-Recalibration Increase

A value-recalibration increase is larger, typically 25 to 50%, and is triggered by a change in the scope, results, or market position of your work.

This type of increase is appropriate when you have developed new skills, when the results you generate for clients have become substantially more valuable, when you have moved from generalist to specialist, or when market rates in your field have moved significantly above your current pricing.

A value-recalibration increase requires more communication than an incremental one. You are making a case, explicitly or implicitly, that what you deliver is worth more than what you currently charge. The case is most convincing when it is grounded in specific outcomes: revenue generated, time saved, problems solved. Abstract claims about improved quality or expertise do not justify price increases. Concrete results do.

### Type 3: The Restructure Increase

A restructure increase changes not just the number but the model. Moving from hourly to project-based pricing. Moving from project-based to retainer. Adding a performance component. Restructuring the deliverables to align price with value instead of effort.

This is the highest-impact type of rate increase because it changes the underlying economics of the relationship. An hourly model caps your income at your available hours. A project-based model prices outcomes, which means as you become more efficient, your effective hourly rate increases. A retainer model creates predictable recurring revenue that does not require reselling.

Restructure increases often require more negotiation than the other types but they tend to produce the most durable improvements in income and working conditions.

## The Exact Communication Sequence

Here is the communication sequence for raising rates with existing clients.

**Step 1: Give 30 to 60 days notice.**

Raise your rates with enough notice that the client can adjust their budget, not enough notice that they feel ambushed. 30 days is the minimum. 60 days is courteous and demonstrates that you value the relationship.

**Step 2: Communicate in writing.**

Even if you tell a client in a call, follow up in writing. The written record protects both parties and gives the client something to reference when discussing the change internally with their team or finance department.

**Step 3: Use direct language with no apology.**

Word-for-word script for an incremental increase:

"Hi [Client Name], I want to let you know that beginning [date], my rate for [service description] will be [new rate]. This is a standard annual adjustment. I value our work together and am committed to continuing to deliver [specific value you provide]. Please let me know if you have any questions."

That is the entire message. No lengthy explanation. No apology. No "I hope this is okay." The confident delivery signals that this is a decision, not a negotiation.

Word-for-word script for a value-recalibration increase:

"Hi [Client Name], I'm reaching out about something I want to communicate with ample lead time. Beginning [date], my rate for [service description] will be [new rate]. Over the past [time period], I've [specific results: helped generate X in revenue, reduced Y from weekly to monthly, shipped Z deliverables]. My rate is adjusting to reflect the level of work and results I'm delivering. I wanted to give you [30/60 days] to work this into your plans. Happy to talk through it if that's helpful."

**Step 4: Do not negotiate with yourself.**

When you communicate a rate increase, the instinct is to immediately offer concessions: "but I'm happy to discuss if that doesn't work for you," or "we can revisit the scope to keep the cost similar." These hedges signal that the rate is not firm. Wait for the client to respond before offering any accommodation.

## Handling the Client Who Pushes Back

Pushback comes in three forms, and each has a different appropriate response.

**Form 1: The Budget Objection**

"That's outside our budget."

This is the most common pushback. The correct response depends on whether you want to retain this client.

If you want to retain them: "I understand. One option would be to adjust the scope. If we narrowed the engagement to [specific component], we could keep the rate closer to what you're used to. Would that work for your goals?"

If you are comfortable losing them: "I understand that's a constraint. My rate is firm, but I'm happy to refer you to someone who might be a better fit at your budget."

Do not reduce your rate in response to a budget objection without a corresponding reduction in scope. Doing so trains the client that your rates are negotiable when pushed. It also confirms in your own mind that your stated rate was not real.

**Form 2: The Comparison Objection**

"Other [freelancers/agencies/consultants] charge less."

"That's true. My rate reflects [specific differentiator: your 8 years of experience in this niche, your track record of X outcome, your availability and responsiveness]. If the primary factor is cost, there are good options at lower price points. If the primary factor is [the specific outcome you deliver], my rate is where it is."

This response neither apologizes nor argues. It acknowledges the market reality and returns the conversation to value.

**Form 3: The Silence**

Some clients do not respond to a rate increase notice. They simply stop responding or let the contract lapse.

This is information, not failure. A client who leaves without communicating was either already on their way out or was only staying for the price. Either way, the departure was not caused by the rate increase. It was revealed by it.

## The New-Client Rate vs. Existing-Client Rate Strategy

Raise your rates with new clients immediately. Do not wait for the perfect moment or a portfolio update or the right credentials. Start charging the rate you have decided you are worth with every new client who contacts you from this point forward.

This strategy works because new clients have no anchor to your old rate. They evaluate your rate against the value they expect and the alternatives they have considered. A new client who pays $150 per hour has no idea you were charging $100 per hour six months ago. That history is irrelevant to them.

The divergence this creates, where new clients pay higher rates than long-term clients, is actually a useful tool. Long-term clients who eventually ask for a rate review can be told, accurately, that your current rate for new clients is [higher rate] and that you have been maintaining their existing rate as a courtesy to the relationship. The comparison to new-client rates removes the perception that you are asking for something arbitrary.

**The runway approach.** If you want to transition your entire client base to a new rate over time, use a runway. New clients go immediately to the new rate. Existing clients get a six-month notice and transition to the new rate at the next contract renewal. Within two contract cycles, your entire base is at the new rate.

## What Happens When You Finally Charge What You Are Worth

The practical outcome is rarely what the fear predicted.

The clients who leave are almost always the ones who took the most time, paid the slowest, pushed back on every deliverable, and extracted the most mental energy for the least financial return. Their departure is a reduction in your revenue and an increase in your capacity, your clarity, and your energy.

The clients who stay at the higher rate tend to be the ones who valued the work more than the rate. They were already paying you less than you were worth in their minds. The increase simply realigns the transaction.

The new clients you attract at the higher rate tend to be different. Not always better people, but better aligned. Clients who pay premium rates for professional services generally have more resources, move faster, and have clearer goals. The problems they bring are often more interesting. The engagements are more satisfying.

There is also a psychological shift that happens when you consistently charge what you believe your work is worth. The resentment reduces. The quality of your work often improves because you are no longer half-committed to clients you feel undervalue you. You show up differently to the work.

None of this is guaranteed. Some rate increases do create friction. Some good clients leave. But the solopreneurs who have run the experiment consistently report the same thing: the fear of raising rates was larger than the reality.

The number is not the hard part. The decision to believe the number is. Make the decision. Send the email. Let the market respond.

Your rate is a message to the market about what you believe your work is worth. Make it an honest message.
