# The Solopreneur Service Packaging Guide

**By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin**

*Transform your skills into clearly priced service packages that sell without custom proposals.*

---

## Introduction: The Custom Proposal Trap

Every solopreneur who sells their services on a custom basis knows this cycle: the inquiry comes in, you spend two hours understanding the client's situation, you write a detailed proposal, you wait five days, you get a counter-offer, you negotiate, and by the time a project starts you have already spent half a day working without pay.

Do this ten times and you have spent a week of unpaid labor on proposals. Some of those clients converted. Some did not. You have no way to predict which, and no way to make the process faster without cutting corners that show.

The custom proposal model was designed for agencies with teams who specialize in closing. It does not translate well to a single-person service business where your time is the entire inventory.

Service packaging is the alternative. Instead of designing a custom engagement for every inquiry, you build three to four clearly defined packages, each with a fixed scope, a fixed price, and a fixed deliverable set. When a potential client reaches out, the conversation is not "tell me everything about what you need so I can build you a proposal." It is "here are my packages. Which one fits where you are?"

This guide builds that system from scratch.

## Part One: The Packaging Philosophy

Before you design any tiers, there is a philosophical shift required.

Most solopreneurs think of their work as inherently custom because every client situation is different. This is true in detail and false at the structural level. The specific deliverables vary. The underlying problem being solved, the process you use to solve it, and the outcome the client wants are almost always one of three or four things.

**The pattern recognition exercise.** Look at your last ten clients. Group them by the core problem they hired you to solve. Not the surface-level request. The underlying problem. Clients who hired you to write website copy were solving the problem of an ineffective online presence. Clients who hired you for brand identity were solving the problem of looking unestablished. Within each group, the work you did was actually quite similar in structure, even when the details differed.

**From custom to configurable.** The goal of packaging is not to eliminate customization entirely. It is to move customization from the proposal phase to the scope phase. The package defines the boundaries. Within those boundaries, you have room to adapt. The client gets a clear price and clear deliverables before they ever sign a contract. You get a clear starting point that protects your time.

**The scarcity of your time is the core argument.** Custom proposals make sense when the thing being priced is infinitely scalable. Your services are not. You have a limited number of hours and a limited number of clients you can serve well simultaneously. Packaged services let you serve more clients at sustainable margins because you eliminate the discovery and proposal overhead for every engagement.

**The trust transfer.** A clear package communicates competence before the client reads a single word of your bio. An organized, clearly priced menu signals that you have done this before, you know what works, and you are not improvising. Clients who are deciding between you and an agency with a polished pitch deck will often prefer the solopreneur with clear packages because the packages signal the same organizational maturity.

## Part Two: Tier Design

Three tiers is the right architecture for most solo service businesses. Two is too limiting. Four or more creates decision paralysis.

**The entry tier (Starter or Foundation).** This tier is designed to solve one specific problem with a defined scope and a price that makes the decision easy. It should not be everything you can do. It should be the most common first problem your clients need solved. Keep the scope narrow, the price accessible, and the deliverable list short and concrete. This tier also serves as a trial engagement: clients who are uncertain about working with you can start here and upgrade.

**The core tier (Standard or Core).** This is where most of your clients should land. It represents your most complete solution to the primary problem you solve. It should include the deliverables that make up a full engagement, a timeline that reflects realistic production, and a price that reflects the actual value delivered. Price this tier so that it is the obvious choice for clients who are serious about solving the problem.

**The premium tier (Pro or Complete).** This tier is for clients who want more done for them, faster, or with additional services adjacent to the core deliverable. The premium tier should not just add more of the same thing. It should add a different category of value: ongoing access to you, a deliverable that requires significantly more time or skill, or a guarantee that changes the risk profile for the client. Not every client will buy it. It should exist to capture clients who will.

**The anchoring function.** The premium tier serves a secondary purpose beyond its own sales. It makes the core tier look like the rational middle choice. The presence of a higher-priced option with more scope makes the core price feel reasonable by comparison. This is not manipulation. It is giving clients a reference point that helps them make a faster decision.

## Part Three: Naming and Framing

The names of your packages and how you describe them are part of the sale.

Most solopreneurs name their packages with generic labels: Basic, Standard, Premium. These names convey price tier. They do not convey value, identity, or outcome.

**Name to the outcome, not the tier.** Instead of "Basic," name the tier after what the client achieves: "Launch" or "Foundation" or "Audit." Instead of "Premium," name it after the highest-value state: "Done-For-You" or "Accelerator." The name is the first framing the client encounters. Make it do work.

**The description structure.** Each package description should follow the same structure: who it is for, what it includes, and what the client will have at the end. The who-it-is-for statement is particularly important. It lets clients self-select into the right tier without requiring a conversation. "This is for founders who are launching their first website and need a clear, professional presence in 10 business days" is more useful than a bullet list of deliverables.

**What to avoid.** Do not list features without explaining why they matter. "Five pages of website copy" means nothing without context. "Five pages of website copy written to convert first-time visitors into inquiry calls" explains the value. Translate every feature into the outcome it produces.

**The psychological frame.** The way you talk about your packages sets the frame for how clients think about the purchase. If you talk about "what I will do for you," you are positioning yourself as labor. If you talk about "what you will have after we work together," you are positioning yourself as an outcome provider. Outcome framing commands higher prices and attracts clients who are paying for results, not time.

## Part Four: Pricing Psychology

Pricing is not just about covering your costs plus a margin. It is about communicating value and positioning.

**The anchoring sequence.** Always present your packages in descending order: premium first, core second, entry third. The first number a client sees sets the anchor. If they see $4,800 first and then $2,400, the core price feels like a reasonable discount from the premium. If they see $600 first and then $2,400, the core feels expensive. The sequence matters.

**Round numbers and what they signal.** Round numbers like $500 or $1,000 signal that the price was calculated quickly. Specific numbers like $1,475 or $2,800 signal that the price reflects a precise calculation of value and effort. For most professional services, specific pricing communicates more care and credibility than round numbers.

**The price confidence principle.** If you feel uncomfortable saying your price out loud without following it with an explanation, your price is too high for where you currently position yourself, or you do not believe your price reflects the value you deliver. Both are solvable. Positioning is a marketing problem. Belief is an evidence problem. Gather evidence that your work produces results. Use it to raise your confidence and your price simultaneously.

**The comparison-to-alternatives frame.** Your price looks different when compared to the alternative. "Six hours of your time trying to write this yourself, or $1,200 for a copywriter who does it in 24 hours" is a different calculation than $1,200 in isolation. Whenever possible, ground your pricing in what it would cost the client in time, stress, or risk to do it themselves or hire an inferior alternative.

**Annual versus project revenue math.** Price your packages so that taking on a specific number of projects per month produces a sustainable annual revenue. If your target is $8,000 per month and your core package is $2,000, you need four clients per month. If your core package is $800, you need ten. Ten clients requires significantly more management overhead than four. Pricing low does not create a more sustainable business. It creates a higher-volume one.

## Part Five: Proposal Elimination

The goal of packaged services is to reach a point where the only document you send before a project starts is a one-page agreement, not a custom proposal.

**The sales page as proposal replacement.** A clear service sales page with package descriptions, deliverable lists, timelines, and prices answers every question a custom proposal answers. When a potential client visits your service page and understands exactly what they get at each price point, the sales conversation moves directly to "which tier fits your situation" instead of "let me understand your situation and get back to you."

**The qualification call structure.** When a potential client reaches out, the qualification call should take 20 minutes, not 60. The purpose is not discovery. It is matching: does this client's situation fit one of your packages? If yes, move to agreement. If no, refer them to someone who is a better fit. A 20-minute call that ends in a clear next step is better for everyone than a 90-minute call that ends in a custom proposal process.

**The decision timeline.** Include a clear decision deadline in your package conversation. "These packages have limited availability and I typically have a two to three week intake window. If you want to work together this month, I need to hear from you by Friday." This is not artificial urgency. You have limited capacity, and communicating that protects your calendar from clients who need months to decide.

**The objection-handling structure.** Most objections to packaged pricing come from clients who want something outside the defined scope. "Can you include X?" is answered with "X is not included in this package, but it is included in the Pro tier at Y price, or we can scope it as a separate project." This response protects the package boundaries while keeping the conversation productive.

## Part Six: Upsell Architecture

After a client completes a project, the most valuable next step is not going out to find a new client. It is extending the relationship with the client you just built.

**The natural next problem.** Every project solves one problem. Solving it typically surfaces the next one. A client who hired you to write their website copy now has a website with no traffic. A client who hired you for brand identity now needs brand guidelines applied across new marketing materials. Map the natural sequence of problems your clients face and design your service packages to address each stage.

**The maintenance or retainer offer.** After a project closes, some clients need ongoing support at a lower intensity than a full project engagement. A monthly retainer for maintenance, light updates, or strategic advisory is a natural post-project offer for clients who want continuity. Price this as a monthly agreement with a defined scope and a clear cancellation policy.

**The expansion package.** Design one explicit expansion package for each of your core services. This package is offered at project close to clients who completed the core or entry tier. It is scoped to the next logical problem and priced to feel like a continuation rather than a new sale. Clients who just had a positive experience with you are your highest-probability buyers. Make it easy for them to continue.

**The referral architecture.** Clients who got strong results become your best acquisition channel. Make it easy for them to refer by giving them a simple description of who you serve best and what you offer. "I work with founders launching their first marketing asset, three packages from $800 to $3,200." A sentence that specific is easy to pass on. A vague description of your general capabilities is not.

The final principle: a well-designed service packaging system turns one client engagement into multiple engagements, and multiple engagements into a referral network that reduces your dependence on outbound sales entirely.

## Summary: The Service Packaging System

1. Stop solving packaging at the proposal stage. Solve it once, then sell the system.
2. Pattern-match your past work into three to five recurring problem types.
3. Build three tiers: entry, core, and premium. Each solves a clearly defined problem.
4. Name packages by outcome, not by tier level.
5. Write every description to a specific type of client in a specific situation.
6. Present packages in descending order to anchor at the premium price.
7. Use specific pricing numbers to signal precision and confidence.
8. Replace the custom proposal with a service page and a 20-minute qualification call.
9. Design the natural next step from every project into a retainer or expansion offer.
10. Build the referral architecture into the close of every engagement.

---

## 10-Item Launch Checklist

- [ ] List your last 10 client engagements and identify the three to four recurring problem types
- [ ] Draft the three-tier package structure: entry, core, premium
- [ ] Write outcome-based names for each tier
- [ ] Write a who-it-is-for description for each tier
- [ ] Define the exact deliverable list and timeline for each tier
- [ ] Set prices in descending anchor order and verify annual revenue math
- [ ] Build or update your service page with all three packages
- [ ] Write a 20-minute qualification call script based on package matching
- [ ] Design one expansion or retainer offer for each core service
- [ ] Add a referral sentence to your project close email template
