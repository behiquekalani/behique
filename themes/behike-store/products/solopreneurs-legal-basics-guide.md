## Introduction: Why Legal Basics Matter Before You Make Your First Dollar

Most solopreneurs start the same way. They build something, sell it, and figure out the legal side later. Sometimes that works out. More often, it creates problems that cost far more to fix than they would have cost to prevent.

This is not a guide designed to make you afraid. Legal knowledge is not a burden. It is infrastructure. The same way you need a reliable payment processor before you can collect money, you need a basic legal framework before you can operate sustainably.

The problem is that legal information tends to be presented in one of two ways: either so simplified it is useless, or so technical it requires a law degree to parse. This guide takes a different approach. It treats you like the intelligent adult you are and gives you the foundational understanding you need to make better decisions, ask better questions when you do consult a professional, and avoid the most common and costly mistakes digital solopreneurs make.

A few things this guide covers: the real difference between operating as a sole proprietor versus forming an LLC, how to write contracts that actually protect your freelance work, intellectual property basics for digital product creators, the components of a terms of service that holds up, privacy policy requirements that are not optional, and how to protect your brand from being copied or stolen.

By the end, you will not be a lawyer. You will be a solopreneur who understands enough to move with confidence, knows when to get professional help, and has built a business that is harder to attack.

That is the point.

## Chapter 1: Understanding Business Structures. Sole Proprietor vs. LLC

### The Default You Did Not Choose

When you started selling things or offering services without formally registering a business entity, you became a sole proprietor. This happened automatically. No filing required. No fees. No ceremony. You just started doing business and the law classified you accordingly.

Sole proprietorship is the most common business structure in the United States because it requires nothing to start. Most freelancers, consultants, and digital product sellers operate this way, often without knowing it.

The question is not whether sole proprietorship is legitimate. It is. The question is whether it is the right structure for where you are and where you are going.

### What Sole Proprietorship Actually Means

As a sole proprietor, you and your business are legally the same entity. There is no separation. When your business earns money, you earn money. When your business owes money, you owe money. When someone sues your business, they are suing you personally.

That last point matters more than most people realize. Personal liability means your personal assets, bank accounts, car, savings, and in some cases your home, are potentially at risk if your business is sued and loses. For many solopreneurs, especially those just starting out, this risk is theoretical and remote. But it is not zero.

The tax treatment under sole proprietorship is straightforward. All business income flows directly to your personal tax return on Schedule C. You pay self-employment tax on net profit in addition to income tax. The self-employment tax covers Social Security and Medicare contributions that would otherwise be split between you and an employer. As of 2024, self-employment tax is 15.3% on the first $168,600 of net earnings.

### What an LLC Actually Means

A Limited Liability Company is a legal entity separate from you. It exists on paper as its own thing. You own it. You run it. But legally, it is not you.

The primary benefit is the liability shield. If your LLC is sued, the lawsuit targets the LLC's assets, not yours personally. Your personal bank account is generally protected. There are exceptions, which attorneys call "piercing the corporate veil," but those exceptions generally require you to have done something fraudulent or failed to maintain the LLC properly.

A single-member LLC, which is the most common structure for solopreneurs, is taxed as a "disregarded entity" by default. That means the IRS treats it the same as a sole proprietorship for tax purposes. Your business income and expenses still flow to Schedule C. The liability protection exists without a major tax change.

You can also elect to have your LLC taxed as an S-Corporation, which at a certain income level can reduce self-employment tax. This is worth discussing with a CPA when your net profit exceeds roughly $50,000 to $80,000 per year.

### The Real Cost of an LLC

Forming an LLC costs money upfront and annually. Filing fees vary by state from around $50 in Kentucky to $500 in Massachusetts. Some states charge annual fees regardless of revenue. California, for example, charges an $800 minimum franchise tax per year.

Beyond formation, you need to maintain the LLC properly. That means keeping business and personal finances completely separate, filing any required annual reports, having a registered agent (a person or service that receives legal documents on behalf of the LLC), and in some states, publishing a notice in a newspaper.

If you commingle funds, use the business account for personal expenses, or treat the LLC as an extension of yourself rather than a separate entity, a court may disregard the LLC and hold you personally liable anyway. This is called piercing the corporate veil. It happens when the separation between you and the business is not real in practice.

### When to Stay a Sole Proprietor

Sole proprietorship makes sense when:
- You are just testing an idea and not yet generating revenue
- Your business activity carries minimal liability risk (writing, education, templates)
- Your income is low enough that the formation and maintenance costs of an LLC are not justified
- You operate in a state where LLC maintenance costs are high

### When to Form an LLC

An LLC makes sense when:
- You are earning consistent revenue from your business
- You work with clients under contracts and face potential disputes
- You sell physical products that could cause harm or defects
- You want credibility with clients and platforms
- You are building something you intend to grow or eventually sell

### The Hybrid Approach Many Solopreneurs Take

Many digital solopreneurs start as sole proprietors, test their model, and form an LLC once they are generating $2,000 or more per month. This is a reasonable approach. It avoids unnecessary cost while you are validating, and adds protection once the business is real enough to protect.

The key is making the decision intentionally rather than defaulting to whatever is easiest.

### Practical Steps If You Want to Form an LLC

1. Choose your state. Most people form in their home state because they physically operate there. Delaware and Wyoming are popular for legal features but often create complications for solopreneurs who are not planning complex structures.

2. Choose a name. It must be unique in your state and cannot be identical or confusingly similar to existing businesses. Search your state's business name database before committing.

3. File Articles of Organization with your state's Secretary of State office.

4. Pay the filing fee. Varies by state.

5. Get an EIN (Employer Identification Number) from the IRS. Free. Takes minutes at irs.gov. This is your business's tax ID number. Use it instead of your Social Security number for business purposes.

6. Open a separate business bank account. Non-negotiable. Commingling funds is one of the fastest ways to lose your liability protection.

7. Create an Operating Agreement. Even if your state does not require it, write down how the LLC operates. For a single-member LLC this is simple, but it documents that you are treating the business as a real entity.

## Chapter 2: Contracts for Freelancers. What Protects You and What Does Not

### Why Most Freelancers Get Burned

The freelance contract dispute is one of the most predictable problems in online business. Client does not pay. Client demands endless revisions. Client disappears after receiving the work. Client claims ownership of something they did not pay for.

These situations are frustrating. They are also largely preventable with a contract that is clear, specific, and signed before any work begins.

The contract is not just legal protection. It is a communication tool. A good contract forces both parties to get explicit about what is being purchased, what is being delivered, and under what terms. That clarity prevents most disputes before they start.

### The Components of a Contract That Works

**Identification of Parties**
Every contract should clearly name who is entering into the agreement. Full legal names, or if applicable, the legal name of the business entity. Addresses are good to include. Using your LLC name rather than your personal name when you have an LLC keeps the contract attached to the business, not to you personally.

**Scope of Work**
This is the most important section. It describes exactly what you are delivering. Vague scope of work is the source of most freelance disputes. "A website" is not a scope of work. "A 5-page WordPress website including homepage, about page, services page, contact page, and a blog page, built using the provided theme, with mobile responsiveness and a functioning contact form" is a scope of work.

Be specific about what is included. Be equally specific about what is not included. Revisions, additional pages, copywriting, stock photography, and integrations not listed are out of scope. You can offer them at an additional rate, but the contract should make clear they are not included in the base price.

**Deliverables and Timeline**
What exactly will you deliver, and when. Use specific dates or timeframes rather than vague language like "within a reasonable time." Define what constitutes delivery. Is it a file drop in a shared folder? A published live website? An email with attachments?

**Payment Terms**
The amount, the due date, how payment is accepted, and what happens if payment is late. For projects over a few hundred dollars, requiring a deposit upfront (typically 25-50%) before work begins is standard practice. It filters out non-serious clients and gives you partial payment security.

Include language about late payment. A common approach is a late fee of 1.5% per month on unpaid balances. Whether you enforce it is your choice, but having it in the contract gives you leverage.

**Revision Policy**
Define how many rounds of revisions are included and what constitutes a revision versus a new request. "Two rounds of minor revisions" is still vague. Better: "Two rounds of revisions, where a round is defined as a consolidated list of changes submitted within five business days of delivery. Changes that alter the original scope are not considered revisions and will be quoted separately."

**Intellectual Property and Ownership**
Who owns the work? This is critical for digital creators. By default, the creator of a work owns the copyright unless they explicitly transfer it. For work-for-hire arrangements where the client intends to own the final product, that transfer needs to be stated in writing.

You need to decide what you are selling. A license to use the work? Full ownership transfer? If you are selling a license, specify whether it is exclusive or non-exclusive, and what the client can and cannot do with the work.

Many freelancers retain copyright and grant clients a perpetual, non-exclusive license to use the deliverables. The client gets what they need. You retain the right to use the work in your portfolio and potentially other contexts.

**Confidentiality**
If the client is sharing sensitive business information with you, a mutual confidentiality clause is appropriate. It protects them and signals professionalism on your part.

**Termination**
What happens if either party wants to end the project early. A common structure: the client can terminate with X days notice and owes payment for all work completed to that point. You can terminate if payment is not received within X days of a missed due date.

**Limitation of Liability**
This clause limits your financial exposure if something goes wrong with your deliverables. A common approach is to cap your liability at the total amount paid under the contract. Without this, a client could theoretically claim damages far exceeding your fee.

**Governing Law and Dispute Resolution**
Which state's laws govern the contract. This matters if you are in Texas and the client is in New York. Typically you specify your state. Also consider adding a clause requiring mediation or arbitration before litigation. This is cheaper and faster than court for most disputes.

**Signatures**
Both parties sign and date. Digital signatures via tools like DocuSign or HelloSign are legally binding in the United States under the E-SIGN Act.

### The Free Contract Mistake

The internet is full of free contract templates. Some are fine starting points. Many are poorly written, jurisdiction-specific, or missing key clauses.

The minimum investment for a solid freelance contract template is a one-time consultation with a business attorney. Many attorneys offer flat-fee services for reviewing or drafting contracts. The cost ranges from $150 to $500 depending on complexity. Compared to the cost of one bad client dispute, this is cheap.

### What to Do When a Client Refuses to Sign

Some clients push back on contracts. They say it is too formal, or that they do not need all that, or that they trust you. This is a red flag. Professional clients expect contracts. Clients who resist them are often the same ones who later dispute what was agreed upon.

You are allowed to require a signed contract before beginning any work. This is not demanding. It is professional. A simple response: "I work exclusively under signed agreements to protect both parties. Happy to answer any questions about the terms before we proceed."

## Chapter 3: Intellectual Property Protection for Digital Products

### What IP Actually Covers

Intellectual property law protects creations of the mind. For digital solopreneurs, the relevant categories are copyright, trademark, and to a lesser extent, patents and trade secrets.

Understanding which type of IP applies to what you create determines how you can protect it.

### Copyright: What You Automatically Own

Copyright protection attaches to original works of authorship at the moment of creation and fixation. When you write a guide, create a template, record a course video, or design a logo, you own the copyright in that work automatically. No registration required.

The "original" standard is low. It does not mean novel or creative in the aesthetic sense. It means the work originated with you and contains some minimal degree of creativity. A list of numbers has no copyright protection. A written guide does.

Copyright gives you the exclusive right to reproduce the work, distribute it, create derivative works, and display or perform it publicly. When someone copies your digital product and sells it without permission, they are infringing your copyright.

**What copyright does not protect:** ideas, facts, procedures, concepts, principles, or methods of operation. Copyright protects the specific expression of ideas, not the ideas themselves. If you write a guide about productivity systems, someone else can write their own guide about productivity systems. They cannot copy your specific text.

**Copyright registration:** While you own copyright automatically, registering your work with the U.S. Copyright Office provides important benefits. Registration is required before you can file an infringement lawsuit. Registered works are eligible for statutory damages (up to $150,000 per infringement for willful violations) and attorney's fees, which are far more valuable than actual damages in most cases. Registration costs $45 to $65 per work for online applications.

For digital products you sell, registration is worth considering for your most valuable products. It converts a theoretical right into one you can actually enforce in court.

### Trademark: Protecting Your Brand

Trademark protects your brand identifiers: your business name, product names, logos, slogans, and in some cases colors and sounds. Unlike copyright, which protects original works, trademark protects marks that distinguish your goods or services from others in commerce.

You gain common law trademark rights by actually using a mark in commerce in your geographic area. These rights exist without registration, but they are limited.

Federal trademark registration with the USPTO gives you:
- Nationwide priority in your trademark class
- The right to use the registered trademark symbol (R)
- The ability to block imports of infringing goods
- A public record of your ownership
- Stronger grounds for enforcement

The application process takes 12-18 months and costs $250 to $350 per class of goods or services. A trademark attorney can search for conflicts and prepare a stronger application, typically for $500 to $1,500 in fees beyond the government filing fee.

Before you invest significantly in a brand name, search the USPTO trademark database at tess.uspto.gov, do a Google search, check social media handles, and run a domain search. Building a brand on a name that someone else already owns is a costly mistake.

**Trade dress** is an extension of trademark law that protects the overall commercial image of your product or service, including its look and feel. For digital products, this could cover distinctive visual design elements that are non-functional and serve as source identifiers. This is more nuanced and less commonly used by solopreneurs.

### Patents: Generally Not Relevant for Most Solopreneurs

Patents protect inventions: useful, novel, and non-obvious processes, machines, articles of manufacture, or compositions of matter. They are expensive ($10,000 to $50,000 with attorney fees), take years to obtain, and are generally irrelevant for digital product creators and content-focused businesses.

Software patents exist but are difficult to obtain and even more difficult to enforce without significant resources. If you believe you have invented a genuinely novel technical process, consult a patent attorney. For most digital solopreneurs, this is not a practical path.

### Trade Secrets: What You Can Protect Without Registration

A trade secret is business information that derives economic value from not being generally known, and that you take reasonable steps to keep secret. Formulas, processes, customer lists, pricing strategies, and proprietary systems can all be trade secrets.

Unlike patents, trade secrets have no duration limit. They are protected for as long as they remain secret.

The "reasonable steps" requirement is important. You need to actually treat the information as confidential: use non-disclosure agreements with anyone who has access, store the information securely, and limit access to those who need it. If you do not protect it, you cannot claim it as a trade secret.

### Practical IP Strategy for Digital Solopreneurs

1. Write everything down. The moment your work is created and in a fixed form, you have copyright. Create a simple log of what you created and when.

2. Include copyright notices on your products. The format is: Copyright [year] [your name or business name]. All rights reserved. This is not legally required but makes infringement less defensible.

3. Register your most valuable products with the Copyright Office. Especially if you have invested significant time and if the product generates meaningful revenue.

4. Trademark your business name and product names once you are generating consistent revenue. This is a signal of real business, not just a money-spending exercise.

5. Use NDAs when sharing proprietary information. Especially with contractors, collaborators, or anyone who needs access to your processes.

6. Monitor your work. Set up Google Alerts for your product names and brand name. Check for unauthorized copies on Gumroad, Etsy, and similar platforms periodically.

7. When you find infringement, document it (screenshots with timestamps). Then send a DMCA takedown notice to the platform hosting the infringing content. Most platforms have streamlined processes for this.

## Chapter 4: Terms of Service for Digital Products. What to Include and Why

### Why Your ToS Is Not Just Boilerplate

Most digital product sellers either skip the terms of service entirely or copy one from another site without reading it. Both approaches leave you exposed.

A terms of service (also called terms and conditions) is a contract between you and your customers. It governs the relationship from the moment someone uses your product or visits your site. When someone purchases from you and clicks "I agree," they are entering into a legally binding agreement.

A well-written ToS clarifies expectations, limits your liability, defines what customers can and cannot do with your products, establishes how disputes will be handled, and gives you grounds to take action when someone violates the terms.

### Core Elements of a Digital Product ToS

**Acceptance of Terms**
State clearly that by purchasing or using the product, the customer agrees to the terms. Include a mechanism for acceptance, whether that is a checkbox at checkout or an acknowledgment in the purchase flow.

**Description of Product and License**
Describe what the customer is purchasing. For digital products, this almost always means they are buying a license to use the product, not ownership of the underlying intellectual property.

Specify the license terms: Personal use only. Non-transferable. Cannot be resold or redistributed. Cannot be used to create competing products. These terms define the scope of what customers are allowed to do.

**Prohibited Uses**
Be explicit about what customers cannot do:
- Resell, redistribute, or share the product with others
- Claim the work as their own
- Use the product to create derivative products for sale
- Remove copyright notices
- Use the product in illegal ways

**Refund Policy**
Because digital products are delivered instantly and cannot be "returned" in the traditional sense, many sellers have strict no-refund policies. This is legal, but it must be clearly stated before purchase.

If you do offer refunds, specify the conditions and time window. A common approach is a 7 or 14-day satisfaction guarantee with a valid reason required.

**Disclaimer of Warranties**
State that the product is provided "as is" and that you make no guarantees about specific results. For educational products, clarify that results vary based on individual effort and circumstances. This is especially important for business, finance, or health-related content.

**Limitation of Liability**
Cap your liability at the amount the customer paid for the product. This prevents a situation where a dissatisfied customer claims consequential damages (lost profits, lost opportunities) far exceeding your product price.

**Intellectual Property Ownership**
Explicitly state that you own all intellectual property in the product and that purchase does not transfer any IP rights.

**Governing Law**
Specify which state's laws govern the agreement.

**Changes to Terms**
Reserve the right to update your terms and describe how you will notify customers of changes.

**Contact Information**
Provide an email address for questions or concerns related to the terms.

### Where to Display Your ToS

Your terms of service should be accessible from every page of your site, linked in the footer. At checkout, there should be a clear reference to the ToS and an indication that purchase constitutes acceptance. For higher-priced products, a checkbox "I agree to the Terms of Service" is worth adding.

### Getting Your ToS Right

Generic ToS generators exist and provide a starting point. Services like Termly, iubenda, and similar tools generate basic terms for a monthly fee. These are better than nothing.

For a ToS you can rely on, a flat-fee review by a business attorney is worth the cost. Your terms will be reviewed in light of your specific business model and the laws applicable to you.

## Chapter 5: Privacy Policy Requirements. What the Law Actually Requires

### The Laws That Govern Your Privacy Policy

Privacy law is one of the areas where "I did not know" provides no protection. If you collect personal data from users or customers, you have legal obligations that depend on where you operate and where your customers are located.

**GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation):** European Union regulation that applies to any business that processes personal data of EU residents, regardless of where the business is located. If you have customers in Europe, GDPR applies to you.

**CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act):** California law that applies to businesses meeting certain thresholds (generally, annual revenue over $25 million, data on over 100,000 consumers, or deriving over 50% of revenue from selling personal data). Most solopreneurs do not meet these thresholds, but the CCPA has influenced best practices across the industry.

**CAN-SPAM Act:** U.S. law governing commercial email. Requires an opt-out mechanism, your physical address in every commercial email, accurate header information, and prohibition on deceptive subject lines.

**COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act):** If your site collects data from children under 13, specific requirements apply. Most solopreneurs avoid this by explicitly stating their products are for adults 18 and over.

### What Your Privacy Policy Must Include

**What Data You Collect**
Be specific. "We collect your name, email address, and payment information when you make a purchase" is better than "we collect information." List every type of data you collect: contact information, usage data, cookies, payment data, device information.

**How You Collect It**
Forms, checkout pages, cookies, analytics tools, email sign-up forms. If your payment processor collects data, mention that.

**Why You Collect It**
Legal bases for collection under GDPR include consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, and legitimate interests. For most digital solopreneurs, the basis is typically contract (you need the email to deliver the product) and consent (for marketing).

**How You Use the Data**
To fulfill orders, send purchase receipts, send marketing emails if opted in, improve your products, provide customer support.

**Who You Share It With**
Payment processors (Stripe, PayPal), email service providers (ConvertKit, Mailchimp), analytics services (Google Analytics), and any other third parties who receive customer data. Name them. GDPR requires this.

**Data Retention**
How long you keep data. A common policy is to retain customer data for the duration of the business relationship plus any legally required period.

**User Rights**
Under GDPR, EU residents have the right to access their data, correct inaccurate data, delete their data (the "right to be forgotten"), restrict processing, and data portability. Include information about how users can exercise these rights.

**Cookies**
If your site uses cookies, your privacy policy must describe this. A separate cookie banner with opt-in/opt-out functionality is required for GDPR compliance. Many site platforms handle this automatically with a plugin.

**Contact Information**
How users can reach you with privacy-related questions or requests.

**Effective Date and Updates**
Date the policy and describe your process for notifying users of changes.

### Practical Implementation

1. Audit what data you actually collect before writing your policy. Review every form, checkout page, analytics tool, and email system you use.

2. Use a privacy policy generator as a starting point. These tools ask you structured questions and generate compliant language. They are not perfect, but they cover most standard scenarios.

3. If you have EU customers, GDPR compliance is not optional. A consent management platform handles cookie consent. Many WordPress plugins and website builders include this.

4. Review your email marketing setup. Every list should have a clear opt-in. Every email should have an unsubscribe link. Your physical address (or a P.O. box) should be in the footer.

5. Review your policy annually. Laws change. Your data practices change. Keep your policy current.

## Chapter 6: Protecting Your Brand

### Your Brand Is an Asset

Most solopreneurs treat their brand as aesthetic. The logo, the colors, the vibe. These things matter for attraction. But from a legal standpoint, your brand is a business asset that can appreciate in value, be licensed, and if not protected, be taken.

Brand protection is not just about preventing copycats. It is about building something with real ownership. A brand that is properly protected is more valuable, more transferable, and more defensible than one that exists only in practice.

### Start With the Name

Before you commit to a name, search. This takes 30 minutes and can save years of rebuilding.

Search the USPTO trademark database. Search Google for the exact name and related variations. Search social media platforms. Check domain availability. Search your state's business name database.

You are looking for conflicts: existing trademarks in similar categories, established businesses using the same or similar name, and domains that could cause confusion.

If the name is clear, document when you started using it. Your first use date establishes your common law trademark rights even before federal registration.

### Trademark Your Core Brand Elements

Federal trademark registration is the strongest brand protection available. Priority is given to the first to register, not the first to use (with some exceptions based on common law use).

Register your brand name. Register your logo. If you have a distinctive tagline that you use consistently, consider registering that too.

Choose the right trademark classes. The USPTO uses 45 classes of goods and services. You register in the class that covers your business activity. For digital products and educational materials, Class 16 (printed matter) and Class 41 (education and entertainment) are commonly used. Consulting with a trademark attorney on class selection prevents errors that can invalidate your application.

### Domain and Social Media Protection

Register your primary domain and relevant variations (.com, .co, .net at minimum). Register your business name on all major social media platforms, even ones you do not currently use. This prevents others from claiming your name and potentially confusing your audience.

Use your trademark symbol appropriately. TM can be used for any trademark you claim, whether registered or not. The R symbol (in a circle) is reserved for federally registered trademarks. Using the R symbol on an unregistered mark is a federal offense.

### Monitor and Enforce

Trademark rights are use-it-or-lose-it in a very literal sense. If you allow others to use your mark without objection, you can lose the right to enforce it. This is called trademark abandonment through acquiescence.

Monitor for infringement:
- Google Alerts for your brand name
- Trademark monitoring services (some paid, some free at basic tiers)
- Periodic searches on major platforms and marketplaces

When you find infringement, you have options in escalating order of formality: contact the infringer directly, send a cease and desist letter, file a DMCA or trademark infringement complaint with the platform, and if necessary, pursue legal action.

Most small-scale infringers respond to direct contact or a cease and desist. Reserve legal action for situations where the infringement is causing real damage and the infringer is not responding to lesser measures.

### Copyright Your Content

Everything you create and publish should carry a copyright notice. Your product files, your website content, your social media posts. The notice does not have to be prominent, but it should be present.

For high-value content, register with the Copyright Office. This is the step that converts your theoretical rights into enforceable ones.

### Protecting Digital Products From Piracy

Piracy of digital products is common. PDFs get shared. Courses get uploaded to file sharing sites. Templates get redistributed.

Technical protection measures help but are not foolproof. Watermarking PDFs with the customer's purchase information creates a trail. Digital rights management (DRM) systems for video content add friction. Some creators embed unique identifiers in each copy sold.

The legal avenue is the DMCA. File takedown notices when you find your content distributed without permission. Platforms are required to respond to valid DMCA notices promptly. Keep a record of every notice you file.

## Chapter 7: Contracts for Digital Product Sales

### The Agreement Behind Every Purchase

When someone buys your digital product, a contract is formed. The terms of that contract are determined by your terms of service, your product description, your pricing page, and any communications you had with the customer. If you have not defined those terms clearly, they default to what is "reasonable" under the law, which may not align with your expectations.

### Licensing Agreements for Digital Products

The most important contract for a digital product seller is the license agreement. This is the document that defines what the buyer can do with the product.

**Personal Use License:** The buyer can use the product for their own personal purposes. They cannot resell it, share it, or use it to create other products for sale. This is the most restrictive common license.

**Commercial Use License:** The buyer can use the product to produce work for clients or generate revenue. They still cannot resell the product itself or redistribute it.

**Extended Commercial License:** May allow use in larger-scale commercial applications. Often sold at a premium.

**PLR (Private Label Rights):** The buyer can modify and resell the product under their own name. This is a deliberate business decision, not a default. PLR licenses are worth more and sold at higher prices.

Be clear in your product listing and in your terms which license applies. Confusion about licenses is one of the top sources of customer disputes in the digital product market.

### Affiliate Agreement Basics

If you run an affiliate program for your products, you need an affiliate agreement. This document covers:
- Commission rates and payment terms
- What affiliates can and cannot do in their promotion
- Disclosure requirements (FTC requires affiliates to disclose their relationship)
- Prohibited traffic sources
- Grounds for termination from the program

FTC disclosure requirements are federal law. Affiliates must clearly disclose when they receive compensation for promoting a product. Make sure your affiliate agreement requires this and that you educate your affiliates on how to comply.

### Collaboration Agreements

If you collaborate with other creators on a product, get the agreement in writing before creating anything. Address:
- Who owns the final product
- How revenue is split
- Who handles customer service
- What happens if one party wants to end the collaboration
- Rights to individual contributions if the collaboration ends

Handshake deals with people you trust work until they do not. A simple written agreement prevents ambiguity and preserves the relationship if disagreements arise.

## Chapter 8: Tax Basics for Digital Solopreneurs

### You Are a Business, Tax Accordingly

This chapter is not comprehensive tax advice. Tax situations are individual and complex. Work with a CPA who has experience with self-employed individuals and digital businesses. The information here gives you a foundation for that conversation.

### Self-Employment Tax

When you work for an employer, Social Security and Medicare taxes are split between you and the employer. As a self-employed person, you pay both halves. That is the self-employment tax of 15.3% on net earnings.

You can deduct half of the self-employment tax from your income when calculating your income tax. This does not eliminate the self-employment tax, but it reduces your taxable income.

### Quarterly Estimated Taxes

Self-employed individuals generally must pay estimated taxes quarterly. The IRS expects you to pay taxes as you earn income, not just at year end. Missing estimated tax payments can result in underpayment penalties.

The due dates are typically: April 15 (for Q1), June 15 (for Q2), September 15 (for Q3), and January 15 of the following year (for Q4).

Calculate your estimated taxes based on prior year liability or current year expected liability. A simple approach is to set aside 25-30% of every payment you receive and pay it quarterly.

### Deductible Business Expenses

As a solopreneur, many business expenses are tax deductible. Common deductions:
- Home office (if you use a portion of your home exclusively and regularly for business)
- Business software and subscriptions
- Domain names and hosting
- Professional development (courses, books relevant to your business)
- Equipment used for business (computers, cameras, microphones)
- Business portions of phone and internet
- Health insurance premiums (if self-employed)
- Retirement contributions (SEP-IRA contributions are deductible)
- Professional services (accounting, legal fees)
- Marketing and advertising

Keep receipts for all business expenses. Use a dedicated business account and credit card so your records are clean.

### Sales Tax on Digital Products

Sales tax on digital products is a complex and evolving area. Requirements vary by state. Some states tax digital downloads. Some do not. Some have economic nexus thresholds that require out-of-state sellers to collect sales tax once they exceed a certain volume of sales in that state.

If you sell through a marketplace like Gumroad or Etsy, these platforms typically handle sales tax collection and remittance for you. If you sell directly through your own website, you may need to collect and remit sales tax depending on your sales volume in various states.

This is an area where professional advice is worth the cost. A CPA or tax attorney can assess your specific situation and help you comply without over-collecting.

## Conclusion: Legal Infrastructure Is Business Infrastructure

You built something. That is the hard part. The legal layer is not optional but it does not have to be overwhelming.

Start where you are. If you are early, operating as a sole proprietor and selling with a clear terms of service and a privacy policy is a defensible starting point. As you grow, the infrastructure grows with you: forming an LLC, registering your trademark, getting contracts reviewed, registering your most valuable content with the Copyright Office.

The cost of building legal infrastructure incrementally as your business grows is manageable. The cost of rebuilding after a dispute, a copycat, or a regulatory problem is not.

The main principles from this guide:

- Understand your business structure and what it does and does not protect.
- Use contracts for every client engagement. Scope, payment, IP, and liability matter in writing.
- Own your intellectual property. Register what is worth registering. Monitor and enforce.
- Write honest, specific terms of service and privacy policies. Vague is not safe.
- Protect your brand before someone else claims what you built.
- Work with professionals for your specific situation. This guide gives you a foundation, not a substitute.

You built something worth protecting. Protect it.
