# Solopreneur's Proposal Writing System

**The proposals most solopreneurs send are losing before they're read. Here is what changes the outcome.**

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike

---

## Introduction

Most solopreneurs treat proposal writing as a documentation task. The client asks for a proposal. You write down what you will do, what it will cost, and when you will finish. You send it. You wait.

The waiting is the problem.

A proposal is not documentation. It is the final piece of a sales conversation, and it succeeds or fails based on whether it does one thing: make the potential client feel understood and confident that you are the right person for this specific problem. Length, credential lists, and portfolio links are secondary to that. Most proposals get both of them wrong: they are too long, and they lead with the wrong things.

This guide is built around the specific failure modes that kill proposals. Not the abstract ones that consultants talk about in LinkedIn posts, but the structural problems that cause real proposals to get filed away without a response. Each chapter addresses one failure mode with a concrete system.

By the end, you will have a 1-page proposal framework that takes less than an hour to produce, a follow-up sequence that keeps deals alive without feeling desperate, a pricing presentation approach that communicates value instead of inviting objection, and a retrospective system that makes every lost proposal a data point.

The goal is not a perfect proposal. The goal is a consistent, repeatable process that wins at a higher rate and improves over time.

# Part One: What Kills Proposals Before They're Read

---

## Chapter 1: The Length Problem

There is a direct negative correlation between proposal length and conversion rate for most solopreneur services.

The reason is not that clients are lazy readers. It is that length signals something specific: the proposal writer is not confident enough to distill the work into its essential elements, so they included everything and let the reader figure it out. That signals uncertainty. And clients who sense uncertainty about scope and process opt out.

A proposal that runs four pages tells the client: "I need to explain a lot to justify what I'm about to charge." A proposal that runs one page tells the client: "I understand this problem well enough to describe the solution concisely."

**Why proposals get long.** The most common reason solopreneurs write long proposals is that they are trying to preempt every possible objection. They anticipate the client asking "what does this include?" so they list everything. They anticipate the client asking about process, so they add three paragraphs about methodology. They anticipate pricing objections, so they add a value justification section.

The problem is that none of those additions resolve objections. They create new ones. The more a proposal tries to cover, the more surface area there is for the client to find something that does not fit their expectation, misses the mark, or introduces doubt.

**The 1-page standard.** A single-page proposal forces you to identify what matters and omit what does not. It forces a thesis: this is the problem, this is the solution, this is what success looks like, this is the investment. When you can write that in one page, you have demonstrated understanding. When you cannot, more words will not fix it.

---

## Chapter 2: The 1-Page Proposal Framework

The 1-page proposal has five sections. Each serves a specific function. Nothing else is needed at this stage.

**Section 1: The problem statement.** Two to three sentences describing the specific problem the client has. Not your interpretation of the category of problems they might have. The specific problem they told you about in the discovery conversation.

This section exists for one reason: the client should read it and think "yes, that is exactly the situation." If they read it and think "that is close but not quite right," you have already lost confidence. If they read it and think "this person understands the problem better than I described it," you have already won it.

Do not use generic language in this section. "Your business needs help with marketing" is not a problem statement. "You are getting inbound leads from referrals but have no system for converting them to paying clients, which means each month's revenue is unpredictable" is a problem statement.

**Section 2: The proposed solution.** Three to five bullet points describing what you will do. Not every task. The significant deliverables and the approach. Enough specificity that the client can picture the outcome, not enough that you are writing a project brief.

This section should be specific to this client's problem, not copied from a previous proposal. The client can tell when solution language is generic. It signals that you did not think about their situation specifically, which undermines the problem statement you just wrote.

**Section 3: Success criteria.** One to two sentences describing what the outcome looks like when the work is done. "By the end of this engagement, you will have X, which will allow you to Y." This section is often omitted. Do not omit it. It creates a shared definition of done, which protects you from scope creep and protects the client from ambiguity.

**Section 4: Investment.** One number. Not a range. Not an itemized list of tasks with hourly rates attached. One number, presented clearly with payment terms.

If the scope can vary, present two tiers: Tier A (core scope) and Tier B (expanded scope), each with a clear deliverable difference and a single price.

**Section 5: Next steps.** Two sentences. What happens if they want to proceed, and what the first action is. "If you'd like to move forward, reply to this message and I'll send a contract and invoice. Work begins the week we receive the deposit."

That is the full proposal. One page. All five sections. Nothing more.

## Chapter 3: Leading With the Problem, Not Your Credentials

The second most common proposal failure is leading with credentials.

A typical solopreneur proposal opens with: "I'm a freelance designer with seven years of experience working with brands in the health and wellness space. My clients include X, Y, and Z. I specialize in brand identity and digital design."

The client knows you have experience. That is why they agreed to a discovery call. They do not need to be re-convinced of your qualifications in the first paragraph of the proposal. They need to know that you understand their specific situation well enough to help.

**Why credentials first signals the wrong thing.** Leading with your credentials frames the proposal around you. The client is trying to evaluate whether you can solve their problem, and you are talking about yourself. That misalignment creates friction. The client has to translate your credential list into an answer to their question: "Can this person fix my problem?"

Leading with the problem statement does the opposite. It tells the client immediately: this proposal is about you and your situation, not about me and my background. That alignment earns attention.

**Where credentials belong.** Credentials and relevant experience belong in section two of the proposal, embedded in the solution description. "I'll build the identity system using the brand architecture method I developed working with [relevant context]" is more persuasive than a standalone credential paragraph. It connects experience to the specific solution rather than using it as a preamble.

For most service categories, one or two relevant proof points is enough. The portfolio or case studies should be linked, not embedded. The proposal is not the place to walk the client through your entire body of work. That conversation should have happened on the discovery call.

# Part Two: Scope, Pricing, and Proof

---

## Chapter 4: Writing the Scope Section

The scope section is where most proposals either protect the solopreneur or expose them to scope creep.

The failure mode is twofold: scope that is so vague that the client can interpret it broadly, or scope that is so detailed that the client treats it as a task list they can modify.

**Specific deliverables, not tasks.** Scope should be written in terms of deliverables, not hours or tasks. "Design and deliver a five-screen mobile app prototype" is a deliverable. "Design the home screen, settings screen, onboarding flow, profile page, and dashboard" is a task list. The deliverable-based scope protects you because the client knows what they are getting, and they cannot expand the engagement by adding tasks to a list they now own.

**What is not included.** One of the most effective scope tools is an explicit "not included" clause. Two to three sentences describing what falls outside the scope of this engagement. "This engagement does not include copywriting, photography, or printing coordination." That sentence takes ten seconds to write and prevents three hours of scope negotiation after the contract is signed.

**Round numbers of revisions.** If your work involves revisions (design, writing, development), state the number of revisions included. "Two rounds of revisions on each deliverable." Not "unlimited revisions until satisfied." That clause sounds client-friendly when you write it and turns into an extended engagement you never priced for.

**Flexibility within structure.** Being specific about scope does not mean being rigid in the work. Clients appreciate a solopreneur who is responsive and collaborative within the scope. The scope document exists to prevent scope expansion, not to prevent doing good work. Distinguish between these two things in your own mind, and your client relationship will be better for it.

---

## Chapter 5: Presenting Pricing Without Apology

Pricing is the section where most solopreneurs lose confidence in their own proposals.

The symptoms are specific: using hedging language around the number ("I was thinking around"), burying the price at the bottom of a long deliverables list, presenting a range instead of a number, or writing a paragraph of value justification before revealing the price.

Each of these behaviors signals that the solopreneur is not confident in the number. And when the person selling the service is not confident in the price, the buyer has no basis for confidence either.

**Present the number first.** State the investment number at the beginning of the investment section, not at the end. "The investment for this engagement is $4,800." Then provide the context: what is included, how payment works, when work begins.

This structure treats the client as an adult who can evaluate a number in context. It does not hide the number or delay its revelation to soften the impact. Clients who are price-sensitive will find out the number regardless. Presenting it directly moves the conversation past the number and into the evaluation of value.

**Neutral framing.** Use the word "investment" rather than "cost" or "fee." This is not spin. It is accurate framing. The client is investing in an outcome. Use that word consistently.

**Do not justify the price in the proposal.** The value justification for your price should have happened on the discovery call, not in the proposal document. If the client knows the problem you are solving costs them $15,000 per month in lost revenue, they know a $4,800 investment is rational. If they do not know that, the discovery conversation did not do its job. The proposal is not the place to remediate a weak discovery call.

**Present two tiers when scope genuinely varies.** If there is a real scope decision to be made, offer two clear tiers with one number each. "Option A: $3,200 / Option B: $5,400." The tiers should differ meaningfully in deliverables, not in quality. Both options should represent your best work. You are not offering a "budget option." You are offering different scopes at different prices.

## Chapter 6: Social Proof Placement in Proposals

Social proof in a proposal has one job: reduce the perception of risk.

The client is evaluating whether you can deliver on a specific promise. Social proof evidence that directly supports that promise is valuable. Generic testimonials that say "Kalani was great to work with" are not.

**Relevant proof only.** Include one or two pieces of social proof that are specifically relevant to the scope of work you are proposing. A testimonial from a client whose problem was similar to this client's problem. A case study result that demonstrates the outcome you are promising. "After this engagement, [client type] saw [specific result] within [timeframe]." Specificity in social proof is what makes it credible.

**Where to place it.** Social proof belongs after the solution section and before the pricing section. It functions as the bridge: "here is what I'll do, here is proof I've done it, here is the investment." This placement means the client evaluates the price with the proof already in mind.

**What counts as proof.** Results with numbers convert better than sentiment. "Email open rate went from 12% to 34% in the first campaign" is more persuasive than "working with [creator] transformed our email strategy." If you have numbers, use them. If you do not have numbers yet, use specific before-and-after descriptions. "Their onboarding process went from a five-email sequence that averaged 40% drop-off to a three-step system with 89% completion."

**Limit.** Two proof points is enough. Three is the maximum. More than that and the proposal stops feeling like a tailored document and starts feeling like a portfolio dump. Send the portfolio separately, on request.

# Part Three: After You Send the Proposal

---

## Chapter 7: The Follow-Up Sequence

Most solopreneurs either never follow up on sent proposals or follow up once with "just checking in."

Both are mistakes.

Not following up is a mistake because proposals frequently stall for reasons that have nothing to do with the client's interest level. The client had a team emergency. The proposal landed in a thread with thirty other emails. They meant to reply and did not. A follow-up surfaces the proposal again without requiring the client to take initiative.

"Just checking in" is a mistake because it gives the client nothing to respond to. It is asking the client to initiate a conversation. Most will not.

**The three-message sequence.** Send three follow-up messages after the proposal, spaced three to five business days apart.

Message one arrives three days after the proposal. It adds value. "Following up on the proposal. One thing I forgot to mention: I've done this specific thing that's relevant to your situation. Happy to walk you through it on a quick call." Give them something new, not a repeat of the proposal.

Message two arrives five business days after message one. It answers the most common objection preemptively. "A few clients have asked me about [the most common objection you receive], so I wanted to address it directly. [Clear answer to that objection]." This message shows that you have done this before and understand the evaluation process.

Message three arrives five business days after message two. It creates a decision point. "I'm finalizing my schedule for the next quarter. If you'd like to move forward with this engagement, now would be a good time to confirm. If the timing isn't right, I completely understand. Either way, let me know where you land." This message gives the client permission to say no, which removes the social friction of not responding. A "no" is better than silence because it lets you move on.

**What to do when all three messages go unanswered.** Mark the lead as dormant and move on. Do not send a fourth message. Do not call unannounced. Add the contact to a low-frequency nurture list (a newsletter or occasional update) that keeps you in their awareness without pressure. Clients who were not ready in March sometimes come back in September.

## Chapter 8: Proposal Tools

The format you use to deliver a proposal is less important than the quality of the content. But format does matter, and the decision involves real trade-offs.

**PDF proposals.** A PDF is the default for most solopreneurs and has clear advantages: it is professional in appearance, not editable by the client, and works everywhere. The disadvantage is that you get no visibility into whether the client opened it, and it requires the client to reply separately to indicate intent. For simple engagements at lower price points, PDF works well.

**Web proposals.** Services like Proposify and Better Proposals deliver proposals as URLs. The significant advantage is read tracking: you know when the client opened the proposal, how long they spent on each section, and whether they shared it internally. That information is actionable. If the client spent four minutes on the pricing section and two seconds on the scope section, you know something. Web proposals also typically have an integrated e-sign function, which removes a step from the conversion process. The disadvantage is cost (most tools charge $50 to $100 per month) and the occasional client who is skeptical of clicking a link rather than receiving a document.

**Google Docs or Notion.** Some solopreneurs use collaborative documents for proposals. The advantage is flexibility and speed. The disadvantage is that clients can edit the document, which creates ambiguity about the agreed scope. Collaborative documents work fine for internal planning but not as final proposal documents.

**The recommendation.** Use PDF for engagements under $2,500. Use web proposal software for engagements over $2,500 where read tracking and integrated signatures justify the tool cost. The visibility into client behavior at higher price points is worth the subscription.

**E-signature integration.** Regardless of the proposal format, the acceptance path should be as frictionless as possible. A client who decides to proceed should be able to do so without printing, signing, scanning, or setting up an account. DocuSign, HelloSign, or the e-sign function built into proposal software reduces the gap between "yes" and the signed contract.

## Chapter 9: The Proposal Retrospective

Most solopreneurs analyze won deals. Few analyze lost ones with enough rigor to learn from them.

A lost proposal is data. The question is whether you extract it.

**The retrospective log.** Keep a simple document or spreadsheet where you log each proposal. Columns: client name, service type, price, status (won/lost/pending), and notes on why it was lost if known. This does not need to be elaborate. Fifteen minutes per proposal.

After ten to fifteen proposals, patterns emerge. If you are losing most proposals in a specific service category, that is information about either your pricing in that category or the quality of leads you are attracting. If you are losing most proposals above a certain price threshold, that is information about your value communication or market positioning. If you are losing proposals from a specific lead source, that is information about audience fit.

**Ask for feedback.** When a proposal is declined, reply with one question: "I'm always working to improve my proposals. Would you be willing to share what made you decide not to move forward?" Most clients will not respond. The ones who do provide more useful information than any amount of internal analysis.

**Common patterns in lost proposals.** Across many solopreneur engagements, the three most common reasons proposals lose are: price without context (the number surprised the client because the discovery conversation did not establish the cost of the problem), scope ambiguity (the client was not confident about what was included), and timing (the client was not ready to proceed when the proposal arrived). The first two are proposal problems. The third is a lead quality problem. Distinguishing between them is what makes the retrospective useful.

**What to improve after each cycle.** The proposal should improve incrementally after every five to ten proposals. Not wholesale rewrites, but targeted improvements to the sections where you are losing. If the price section is generating the most friction, rework the framing. If clients keep asking scope clarification questions, tighten the deliverables language. If the problem statement is landing flat, practice extracting more specific pain language in your discovery calls.

The proposal retrospective closes the feedback loop between what you write and what wins. Without it, you are guessing at improvements. With it, every lost proposal becomes the foundation for the next one.

---

## Conclusion

A good proposal does not win by impressing the client. It wins by making the client feel clearly understood and confident in the path forward.

That requires a short document with a clear problem statement, specific deliverables, transparent pricing, and a follow-up process that keeps the conversation moving. None of that requires unusual skill. It requires a system applied consistently.

The solopreneurs who win proposals at higher rates are not more talented than the ones who struggle. They have a better process. This guide is the process.

Apply it to the next proposal you write. Log the result. Improve from there.
