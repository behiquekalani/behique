# The Content Repurposing System

**Create once. Publish everywhere. Stop starting from scratch.**

*By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike*

## Introduction: The Wrong Ratio

Most creators have it backwards.

They spend 80% of their time creating new content and 20% of their time distributing it. The result is a treadmill. New video, new post, new thread, new newsletter, all starting from zero every single time. The work is constant. The growth is slow. The burnout is real.

The creators who build real audiences, real businesses, real distribution, have flipped that ratio. They spend 20% of their time creating and 80% of their time distributing, adapting, and publishing the same core ideas across every platform that matters.

This is not about recycling. It is not about posting the same thing in five places. It is a system. A deliberate architecture where one piece of content, built correctly, branches into ten or fifteen pieces of content across platforms you do not have to think about every day.

The thesis: **you do not need more content. You need a better system for the content you already have.**

This guide builds that system from the ground up. It covers the mindset, the formats, the platforms, the weekly workflow, and the tools that make the whole thing run. By the end, you will have a repeatable process that produces a week of content from a single anchor piece, every week, without starting from scratch.

This is not theory. Every method in this guide is drawn from how working creators actually operate at scale. The ones who seem to be everywhere, posting constantly on every platform, are not working harder than you. They built a better system.

Let us build yours.

# Chapter 1: The Repurposing Mindset

## Why Original Content Is the Seed, Not the Harvest

The biggest mistake creators make is treating a piece of content as a destination.

You write a newsletter. It goes out. That is the end of it. You record a video. It gets uploaded. That is the end of it. The post gets published, the thread gets written, the podcast gets released, and then you move on to the next thing.

This is the wrong mental model. Every piece of content you create is not the harvest. It is the seed.

A seed contains everything the plant will become. The leaves, the branches, the fruit, all of it is encoded in the seed. Your anchor content works the same way. A well-written 2,000-word essay contains a Twitter thread. It contains a newsletter section. It contains a YouTube script. It contains three Instagram captions. It contains a short-form video hook. All of that is already in the essay. You just have to extract it.

The repurposing mindset requires one core belief: **ideas are more valuable than formats.**

The format is just the container. The idea is the thing that matters. When you have a strong idea, your job is to put it in every container that reaches your audience. Some of them prefer video. Some prefer email. Some prefer scrolling through short-form text on Twitter. The idea serves all of them, but only if you do the work of packaging it correctly for each format.

## The Hierarchy of Content

Not all content is equal in its repurposing potential. You need to understand the difference between anchor content and derivative content before you build the system.

**Anchor content** is long-form, high-effort, and idea-dense. It is the source material. A 2,000-word essay, a 20-minute YouTube video, a 10-episode podcast series, a long Twitter thread. Anchor content takes 80% of your creative effort and produces 100% of your raw material.

**Derivative content** is everything extracted from anchor content. A clip from the podcast. Three tweets from the essay. An Instagram caption from the newsletter section. A short video from the YouTube chapter. Derivative content takes 20% of your creative effort because the thinking is already done. You are packaging, not creating.

Most creators only produce derivative content, meaning they skip the anchor phase and just post raw reactions, short observations, and surface-level takes. This works for distribution but not for depth. You cannot repurpose what does not exist. The anchor has to come first.

The mindset shift is this: when you sit down to create something, you are not writing a post. You are building source material. You are generating raw intellectual ore that will get refined into a dozen different formats over the next week.

## Thinking in Systems, Not Sessions

A creation session that produces one piece of content is inefficient.

A creation session that produces one anchor piece, which then generates ten derivatives, is efficient.

The goal is to never sit down and ask "what should I post today?" That question is the sign that you have no system. The correct question is "which anchor am I processing this week?" and the answer should always be known in advance.

When you think in systems, your content calendar fills itself. Monday is anchor day. Tuesday through Friday is derivative day. The week does not start with "what do I create?" It starts with "which ideas from my last anchor need to be extracted and packaged?"

This is the mindset. Now let us build the mechanics.

# Chapter 2: The Core Content Formats

## Long-Form Anchor Content and Its Five Branches

Every anchor piece branches into five derivative formats. Learn these five, and you have a complete distribution system.

### The Anchor Formats

There are three primary anchor formats worth building around:

**1. The Long-Form Essay or Newsletter**
2,000 to 5,000 words. Thesis-driven. One central argument, fully developed. This is the highest-leverage anchor format because it contains the most extractable material. Every section becomes a derivative. Every argument becomes a thread. Every example becomes a caption.

**2. The Long-Form Video**
15 to 45 minutes. Either a structured tutorial, a deep-dive exploration, or a long-form conversation. Video is the richest anchor format because it produces both visual and audio derivatives. One video produces clips, shorts, transcripts, and audio.

**3. The Podcast Episode**
30 to 90 minutes. Either a solo episode or a guest interview. The guest interview is particularly powerful because you can repurpose the guest's ideas as well as your own. A single conversation with a knowledgeable person can produce two weeks of derivative content.

### The Five Derivative Formats

Every anchor produces derivatives in these five categories:

**1. Short-Form Written Content (Twitter/X, Threads, LinkedIn posts)**
The core ideas of your anchor, stripped to their sharpest form. One argument. One observation. One contrarian take. Standalone, self-contained, designed to be shared.

**2. Short-Form Video (Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts)**
The most compelling 60 seconds of your anchor. A clip from the video. A voiced-over text post. A talking head re-recording of the most shareable insight. Short-form video travels furthest and fastest.

**3. Newsletter or Email Content**
A curated section adapted for your email list. Not a copy-paste of the anchor, but a lens on it. "Here is what I wrote this week, here is why I think it matters, here is the one thing I want you to take away."

**4. Image or Carousel Content (Instagram, LinkedIn)**
The anchor's key frameworks, visuals, and lists turned into slide-by-slide breakdowns. The numbered list from your essay becomes a five-slide carousel. The comparison table becomes an infographic.

**5. Community or Forum Content (Reddit, niche communities, Discord)**
The question at the heart of your anchor, reformulated as a community discussion prompt. "I have been thinking about X. Here is my take. What do you think?" This drives engagement and often generates ideas for future anchors.

### The Rule: Every Anchor Gets All Five

The temptation is to pick your favorite format and stay there. Do not do this. The compounding happens when you are present across platforms, because algorithms on different platforms reach different people at different times.

A newsletter reader who sees you quote-tweeting your own essay and then sees a Reel expanding on the same idea is getting reinforced. They are more likely to remember the idea, share it, and associate it with you. Cross-platform presence is not about vanity metrics. It is about how many times a single person encounters your thinking before they decide to follow, buy, or share.

# Chapter 3: The YouTube-First Strategy

## Video as the Highest-ROI Starting Point

YouTube is the only platform where a video with zero views still has long-term value.

On every other platform, an Instagram post from six months ago is effectively invisible. A tweet from last week is buried. A newsletter from a year ago is unread. But a YouTube video from three years ago can get a thousand views today from a search that did not exist when you posted it. The searchability of YouTube means the shelf life of your content is measured in years, not days.

This is why YouTube-first makes sense as a repurposing strategy even if your channel is small. You are not optimizing for views on YouTube. You are creating a searchable archive of your ideas that compounds over time, while simultaneously generating derivative content for every platform that does care about recency.

## Why It Works Even Without Views

Most creators abandon YouTube because the feedback loop is slow. You post a video, it gets forty views, nobody comments, the algorithm ignores it, and you feel like you wasted three hours.

The problem is the goal. If your goal is views, YouTube is discouraging early on. If your goal is derivative content plus a long-term SEO asset, the forty-view video already paid for itself.

From a single twenty-minute YouTube video you can extract:

- Three to five short-form clips for Reels, TikTok, and Shorts
- A full newsletter issue covering the core argument
- A Twitter thread hitting the five main points
- A LinkedIn post expanding on the most counterintuitive idea
- A carousel breaking down the framework you presented
- A transcript that becomes a blog post
- A podcast version if you strip the audio

That is eight to ten pieces of content from one recording session. The twenty-minute video takes two to three hours to produce. Divide that across ten pieces of content and you are producing content at a cost of fifteen to twenty minutes per piece. No other format produces derivatives at that rate.

## The YouTube Script Architecture

To maximize repurposing potential, your YouTube video needs to be structured for extraction, not just for watching.

Structure every video in modules:

**Hook (0-90 seconds):** The reason to watch, the problem being solved, the counterintuitive claim. This becomes your short-form video hook.

**Context (90 seconds - 3 minutes):** Why this matters, what most people get wrong, the stakes. This becomes your newsletter intro.

**Core argument broken into 3-5 sections:** Each section is a standalone point. Each section becomes a derivative. If you have five sections, you have five potential threads, five potential carousels, five potential Reels.

**Conclusion + call to action:** Tie the argument together. This becomes your newsletter sign-off.

When you script a video with this structure in mind, the derivatives write themselves. You are not reverse-engineering the video after the fact. You are building the extraction points into the original.

## The Minimum Viable YouTube Channel

You do not need to be a full-time YouTuber to use this strategy. One video per week or one video every two weeks is enough. The content production from that one video fills your other platforms. The channel grows slowly, but it grows, and it is compounding. Every video is a permanent asset.

The creators who use YouTube as a repurposing engine rather than as their primary platform consistently outpace the ones who try to optimize for every platform from scratch. They do less, produce more.

# Chapter 4: Newsletter to Everything

## How One Email Becomes 10 Pieces of Content Across 5 Platforms

The newsletter is the most undervalued distribution channel for repurposing.

Most creators treat their newsletter as a summary of what they posted that week. This is backwards. The newsletter should be the anchor, or at minimum, a secondary anchor that runs in parallel with your video content. A well-written newsletter issue is dense with ideas, arguments, and examples. It is a goldmine for derivatives.

## The Newsletter as Anchor

A strong newsletter issue has this structure:

**One central thesis.** Not a roundup of five things. One idea, argued thoroughly.

**Three to five supporting points.** Each one could stand alone as a tweet, a caption, or a Reel talking point.

**One concrete example or case study.** This becomes your most shareable derivative because it is specific and illustrative.

**One call to action or implication.** What should the reader do with this information? This becomes the hook for your short-form video.

When you write with this structure, the derivatives map directly onto the sections:

- The thesis becomes a Twitter thread opener
- Each supporting point becomes a standalone tweet or LinkedIn observation
- The example becomes an Instagram caption or a Reel
- The call to action becomes a short-form video script

## The 10-Derivative Newsletter Process

After publishing each newsletter issue, run this process:

**1. Extract the thesis.** Turn it into a one-sentence claim. Post it as a standalone tweet with a link to the full issue.

**2. Pull the three best lines.** These are quote-worthy observations. Post each one as a separate tweet over three days. No link needed. Just the idea.

**3. Summarize the argument in five bullet points.** This becomes a LinkedIn post or a Twitter thread. Lead with the strongest point.

**4. Find the best example from the issue.** Write a standalone paragraph about it. This is your Instagram caption or LinkedIn article intro.

**5. Record a 60-second video talking about the main idea.** No script, just speaking from the thesis. This is your Reel or TikTok.

**6. Create a carousel.** One slide per supporting point. Simple design. Heavy text. This works on both Instagram and LinkedIn.

**7. Post the core argument in a relevant Reddit community.** Frame it as a question or observation, not a promotion.

**8. Save the full issue as a blog post.** Minor edits for SEO. This takes thirty minutes and creates a permanent searchable asset.

**9. Send a shorter version to a secondary email segment.** If you have different lists for different topics, adapt the issue for the relevant one.

**10. Screenshot the most shared tweet and post it as an image.** Social proof loops back into the newsletter. The most engaged piece of derivative content becomes a signal for what to anchor next.

That is ten pieces of content from one newsletter issue. And the newsletter itself took you two to three hours to write. You are now producing content at a rate of twelve to eighteen minutes per piece.

# Chapter 5: The Twitter Thread Engine

## Extracting Thread Ideas from Long-Form Content

Twitter threads are the most efficient short-form content format for idea-dense creators.

A thread allows you to make a full argument without the constraints of a single tweet. You can build a case across eight to twelve tweets, introduce complexity, and still keep the friction low for the reader. Threads get shared more than single tweets because they reward the reader for reading further.

The best threads do not start with "here is a thread about X." They start with a claim that demands an explanation.

## The Thread Formula

Every thread follows this structure:

**Tweet 1: The hook claim.** One sentence. Counterintuitive, specific, or directly useful. "Most creators produce 80% of their content without a repurposing system. Here is why that is the slowest way to build an audience."

**Tweets 2-3: The context.** Why does this matter? What is the problem being solved? What do most people get wrong?

**Tweets 4-8: The argument.** One point per tweet. Each tweet could stand alone. Each one is a claim + brief evidence or example.

**Tweet 9-10: The synthesis.** Bring it together. The implication. The takeaway.

**Final tweet: The CTA.** Follow for more. Link to the full piece. Ask a question to drive replies.

## The 3-Tweet Minimum Rule

Before you write a thread, apply the 3-tweet minimum rule: can you make this argument in three tweets or fewer?

If yes, write three tweets instead of a thread. Threads are for complex arguments that genuinely require space. If you are padding a thread to hit a certain length, you are wasting the reader's time and yours.

The minimum is not a maximum. Some ideas need ten tweets to land correctly. But forcing yourself to check the three-tweet minimum before writing saves you from writing threads that should have been single observations.

## Extracting Threads from Long-Form

For every anchor piece you publish, run this extraction process:

**Step 1: List every argument in the anchor.** Not the full argument, just the claim. If your essay makes seven claims, you have seven potential thread starters.

**Step 2: Rank by shareability.** Which claim would make someone stop scrolling? Which one would a reader want to send to a friend? Rank from most to least shareable.

**Step 3: Write the top two.** Do not try to thread every idea. Pick the two most shareable claims and build threads around them. Write them on the same day as the anchor for maximum momentum.

**Step 4: Schedule one for today, one for three days later.** You now have two threads from one anchor. You have not created anything new. You have repackaged.

## Thread Timing

Threads perform best when they are posted at times your audience is active and when you have momentum from other content. Post a thread the day after your YouTube video or newsletter drops. The audience is already thinking about the topic. The thread reinforces the idea and drives people back to the full piece.

# Chapter 6: Short-Form Video from Long-Form

## Clips, Shorts, Reels — The 60-Second Extraction Method

Short-form video is the highest-reach format in the current content landscape. Reels, TikToks, and YouTube Shorts are algorithmically favored on every platform because they keep users engaged without requiring a large time investment.

The problem most creators face is that short-form video feels like a separate creation effort. You have to think of a new idea, write a new script, shoot a new video, edit a new clip. Multiplied across multiple platforms, this is unsustainable.

The 60-second extraction method solves this by treating short-form video as a derivative of long-form, not as its own creation category.

## The Extraction Process

Every long-form video contains multiple short-form videos. Here is how to find them:

**Step 1: Watch your own content critically.** After publishing a long-form video or recording a podcast, watch or listen through it once with this question in mind: "Where did I say something that could stand alone in 60 seconds?"

**Step 2: Mark the timestamps.** Note every section where you made a clear point, told a compelling story, or asked a provocative question. These are your extraction candidates.

**Step 3: Apply the standalone test.** For each candidate, ask: "Would this make sense to someone who has never seen my channel and has no context?" If yes, it is a viable short-form clip. If no, it needs a brief intro to set context.

**Step 4: Cut and caption.** Export the clip. Add captions. This is the most important editing step for short-form, because most short-form video is watched without sound.

**Step 5: Add a hook in the first 3 seconds.** If the clip does not hook within the first three seconds, add a title card or a text overlay that creates the hook before the video starts. "This one habit tripled my output" over the first frame is what makes someone pause the scroll.

## The 60-Second Rule

Not every clip needs to be exactly 60 seconds. But 60 seconds is the practical upper bound for most short-form formats without requiring a richer narrative. If your extraction runs longer than 90 seconds, consider whether you can tighten the point.

The clips that travel furthest are:

**The counterintuitive claim with a rapid explanation.** 30-45 seconds. One idea, one reversal, one takeaway.

**The story clip.** 45-90 seconds. One specific moment, person, or event that illustrates a larger point. Stories retain attention longer than arguments.

**The "how I do X" clip.** 30-60 seconds. Concrete, procedural, specific. These are saved and revisited because they are reference material.

**The opinion clip.** 20-40 seconds. A direct take on something people disagree about. These generate comments and shares.

## Cross-Platform Adaptation

The same clip can go on Instagram Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts, and LinkedIn Video, but it should not be identical on all of them.

Instagram: Captions are more refined, aesthetic matters slightly more, hashtags are used but not stuffed.

TikTok: The hook has to be faster. The tone is more casual. Trending audio can help, but is not required for educational content.

YouTube Shorts: The thumbnail matters less than the first frame. Longer shorts (up to 60 seconds) perform better here than on TikTok.

LinkedIn: Strip the casual tone, lead with a more professional framing. The same clip rewritten with slightly more formal language performs better in a professional context.

The adaptation is ten minutes of work per platform. It is not re-creation. It is re-packaging.

# Chapter 7: The Weekly Repurposing Workflow

## Monday Anchor, Tuesday-Friday Distribution, the 2-Hour System

The most common reason creators do not repurpose is that the process feels undefined. They know they should do it. They just do not know when, or in what order, or how long it should take.

The weekly repurposing workflow makes the process concrete. Every day of the week has a defined role. Every platform gets served. The total time investment is approximately ten hours per week across creation and distribution, with eight of those hours spent in focused sessions rather than scattered throughout the day.

## The Weekly Structure

### Monday: Anchor Creation (3-4 hours)

Monday is for creating the anchor. This is the deep work block of the week.

Pick the idea you are working with this week. This should be decided Friday, not Monday morning. When you sit down Monday, the only question is "what is the best version of this idea?" not "what should I write about?"

Write the essay, record the video, or record the podcast. Do not think about derivatives during creation. The only goal on Monday is producing the best anchor possible.Derivatives from a weak anchor will also be weak.

At the end of Monday's session, do one thing: list every extractable idea from the anchor. Write them down in a simple document. "Thesis: X. Supporting points: A, B, C, D. Best example: Y. Most counterintuitive claim: Z." This list is your extraction map for the rest of the week.

### Tuesday: Thread and Short-Form Text (1.5-2 hours)

Tuesday is for written derivatives. Using your extraction map from Monday:

- Write two Twitter threads (use the process from Chapter 5)
- Write one LinkedIn post (the most professional angle on the main idea)
- Write two to three standalone tweets (single observations, no thread needed)
- Draft your newsletter section based on the anchor (if your newsletter is not the anchor itself)

Schedule these across Tuesday through Thursday using a scheduler. Buffer, Hypefury, or Typefully all work for this.

### Wednesday: Email and Newsletter (1-1.5 hours)

Wednesday is for your newsletter. If the anchor was your newsletter, this block is for the follow-up content. If the anchor was video, this block is for writing the newsletter issue that connects to it.

A newsletter issue written from a video anchor takes less time than writing from scratch because the argument is already formed. You are adapting, not originating. The goal is a 600-1,000 word issue that stands alone but rewards readers who watch the video.

### Thursday: Short-Form Video (1.5-2 hours)

Thursday is for video derivatives. Using your extraction map:

- Cut two to three short-form clips from your long-form video (if video was the anchor)
- Or record two to three short-form videos based on points from your essay anchor (no long-form to cut from, so you record fresh)
- Add captions to each
- Adapt for each platform (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube Shorts, LinkedIn)

The recording-fresh approach takes slightly more time, but it is still faster than creating short-form from scratch because the argument is fully formed. You are just presenting it directly to camera instead of extracting a clip.

### Friday: Images and Final Distribution (1-1.5 hours)

Friday is for visual content and cleanup:

- Design one to two carousels based on the anchor's numbered lists or frameworks
- Schedule your weekend posts (Twitter, LinkedIn, Instagram all perform well Saturday morning)
- Review the week's performance: which derivative got the most engagement? This signals your best idea and informs next week's anchor

### Saturday and Sunday: Passive Distribution

Nothing new. The scheduled content runs. You reply to comments. You engage with your community. The system does the work.

## The 2-Hour Daily Cap

On any given day, the target is two hours or less on content work. Creation days (Monday) go three to four hours because anchor content requires depth. Distribution days (Tuesday through Friday) stay at one to two hours because you are executing against a plan, not making creative decisions.

When the process feels like it is taking more than two hours on a distribution day, the problem is usually one of two things: you did not complete your extraction map on Monday, or you are re-thinking the ideas instead of packaging them. The ideas are already formed. Trust the map. Package and ship.

# Chapter 8: Tools and Automation

## Free Stack, Paid Stack, What Is Actually Worth Paying For

You do not need expensive tools to run this system. The free stack handles ninety percent of the workflow. The paid stack saves time at the margins. Knowing the difference matters.

## The Free Stack

**Notion or Obsidian (content calendar + extraction map)**
Free. Use one of these to maintain your weekly extraction map, track which derivatives have been published, and hold your content calendar. You want one document per anchor, with checkboxes for every derivative. When everything is checked, the anchor is fully repurposed.

**Buffer (free plan, up to 3 channels)**
Schedules posts to Twitter, LinkedIn, and Instagram. The free plan is limited but sufficient if you are running a lean operation. Upgrade only if you need more than three channels or want analytics.

**CapCut (short-form video editing)**
Free. The most capable free tool for creating short-form video with captions, effects, and formatting. Available on mobile and desktop. The auto-caption feature alone saves an hour per week.

**Canva (free plan, carousel and image creation)**
The free plan gives you templates for carousels, quote graphics, and cover images. Sufficient for everything in this system. The paid plan is worth it if you are producing daily visual content and need access to premium templates.

**Substack or Beehiiv (email newsletter, free to start)**
Both are free until you start charging for subscriptions. Beehiiv has a slightly more robust free tier. Either works for building your list and publishing issues.

**Descript (one hour of transcription per month for free)**
Transcribes your audio and video, which then becomes your blog post and thread source material. If you are doing more than one video per month, you will hit the limit, but one hour covers a lot.

## The Paid Stack

**Hypefury ($19/month, Twitter scheduling and analytics)**
Worth it if Twitter is a primary growth channel. The analytics show which tweets and threads got the most engagement, which informs your anchor selection. The auto-retweet and thread scheduling features save time.

**Opus Clip ($29/month, AI short-form clip extraction)**
Takes your long-form video and automatically extracts the most shareable clips. Not perfect, but good enough to cut your short-form video production time by sixty percent. Worth paying for if you are producing video weekly.

**ConvertKit or Beehiiv Pro ($49-99/month, advanced email)**
Only worth paying for when your list has more than 1,000 subscribers and you want automation sequences, segmentation, or monetization features. Before that point, the free plan is enough.

**Notion AI ($8/month)**
Useful for the extraction step. You can paste your anchor into Notion and ask it to pull the five strongest ideas, generate thread hooks, or summarize sections. Not required, but it accelerates the extraction map step.

## What Is Actually Worth Paying For

Based on where the system has the most friction:

**Pay for clip extraction first.** Short-form video is the highest-reach format, and extracting clips is the most time-consuming step in the workflow. Opus Clip or a comparable tool pays for itself in hours saved within the first month.

**Pay for scheduling second.** Posting manually across five platforms every day breaks the two-hour daily cap. Buffer or Hypefury removes the manual step and protects your focus time.

**Do not pay for design early.** Canva free is sufficient. The marginal improvement from Canva Pro does not move the needle on audience growth. Spend that money on distribution instead.

**Do not pay for AI writing tools to replace your voice.** AI writing assistants are useful for extraction, summarization, and draft generation. But your voice is your differentiation. If AI is writing your threads, your newsletter, and your captions, you lose the thing that makes people follow you instead of any other account covering the same topics. Use AI to accelerate packaging. Do not use it to replace thinking.

## Automation Opportunities

A few automation flows worth building as the system matures:

**Newsletter to Twitter thread:** Write your newsletter in a shared doc. A simple Zapier or n8n workflow can format the key sections into a thread draft automatically.

**YouTube upload to Transcript:** Connect YouTube to Descript or Whisper (free, open source). Every upload triggers an automatic transcription that appears in your content folder.

**Clip export to platform queues:** If you are using Opus Clip, connect its output to your Buffer or Hypefury queue automatically. New clips route directly to the scheduling queue without manual upload.

None of these automations are necessary on day one. Build them when you have proven the manual workflow and you know where the friction is. Automating a broken process just makes the broken process run faster.

# Appendix A: Platform-Specific Format Guide

## Twitter/X
- **Thread:** 8-12 tweets, hook claim first, one point per tweet, CTA last
- **Standalone tweet:** Under 280 characters, no link in the first tweet if possible (links reduce reach)
- **Best posting times:** 7-9am and 5-7pm in your audience's primary timezone
- **Cadence:** 1-2 threads per week, 3-5 standalone tweets per week

## Instagram
- **Carousel:** 5-10 slides, bold claim on slide 1, one point per slide, CTA on last slide
- **Reel:** Under 60 seconds, captions on, hook in first 3 seconds
- **Caption length:** 150-300 words performs well for educational content. Lead with the hook, not the context
- **Best posting times:** Tuesday-Friday, 9am-12pm and 6-9pm
- **Hashtags:** 5-10, mix of niche and broader tags

## LinkedIn
- **Long post:** 300-600 words, no images needed, conversational but professional tone
- **Carousel:** Same structure as Instagram, slightly more formal design
- **Native video:** Same clips as Reels, but with a professional framing in the caption
- **Best posting times:** Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10am
- **Formatting:** Use line breaks aggressively. Three lines max per block

## YouTube Shorts
- **Duration:** 15-60 seconds, vertical format (9:16)
- **Captions:** Required for reach
- **Thumbnail:** First frame matters more than a custom thumbnail since Shorts play automatically
- **Cadence:** 2-3 per week if you are posting one long-form per week

## TikTok
- **Duration:** 15-60 seconds for educational content
- **Hook:** Faster than any other platform. You have 1-2 seconds before swipe
- **Captions:** Use TikTok's auto-captions, they are accurate and native
- **Trend audio:** Use trending audio for cooking or lifestyle content. For educational, original audio is fine
- **Posting frequency:** 1 per day is the platform's recommendation for growth. 3-5 per week is sustainable

## Anchor Planning Backlog

Maintain a running list of 8-10 anchor ideas at all times. When you finish repurposing one anchor, the next one is already chosen. This removes the Monday morning decision paralysis.

Rate each idea on two dimensions:
- **Relevance score (1-5):** How relevant is this to my audience right now?
