# Solopreneur's Retainer Business Guide

**How one-person service businesses build predictable recurring income without losing control of their time**

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin

---

## The Core Argument

Project work is the default mode for most solopreneurs. A client approaches you with a defined scope. You propose a price. They accept or negotiate. You deliver. You invoice. You wait. You repeat this process with a new client or a follow-up project.

This model has a fundamental problem: it requires constant client acquisition. Every completed project creates a gap that must be filled. The income is lumpy, the pipeline is unpredictable, and the stress is structural, meaning it does not go away as you get better. It gets worse, because better work commands higher prices, which means fewer potential clients, which means more anxiety about each gap between projects.

Retainers solve this. A retainer is a recurring engagement where a client pays a fixed monthly fee for defined ongoing access to your work. The client gets predictability, ongoing support, and a dedicated resource. You get predictable income, a smaller number of client relationships to manage, and the ability to plan your time.

This guide is about building a retainer-based service business from scratch, or converting an existing project-based practice into one.

## Part One: The Economics of Retainers vs. Project Work

### The project work math problem

A freelance developer charges $150 per hour. They work 40 hours per week. In theory, they should earn $6,000 per week, $24,000 per month, $288,000 per year.

In practice, no solo service provider bills 40 hours per week consistently. Client acquisition takes time. Proposal writing takes time. Revisions and back-and-forth take time. Waiting for approvals and feedback takes time. Administrative work takes time.

Realistic utilization for a solo practitioner is often 50-60% of working hours. At 60%, that developer bills 24 hours per week. That is $3,600 per week, $14,400 per month. The gap between theoretical capacity and actual billing is where the anxiety lives.

Add the income variability: a great month might be $20,000. A slow month might be $6,000. The average is somewhere in between, but the business cannot be run on the average. It has to be able to survive the slow months, which means keeping expenses low relative to the worst-case month, which limits investment in growth.

### The retainer math

The same developer structures four monthly retainers at $5,000 per month each. That is $20,000 per month in baseline income, every month, regardless of whether the month has holidays or whether they spent some days sick.

Now the business math changes. You know your minimum income. You can plan expenses. You can invest in tools, learning, and business development. The anxiety floor drops dramatically.

At four retainer clients, you have roughly 40-45 hours per client per month at $5,000. That is $110-125 per hour at the same hourly rate, less than the $150 project rate, but with zero acquisition cost for those four clients and predictable scheduling. The effective hourly rate, when you account for acquisition time saved, is often higher.

More importantly, when a retainer client stays for two years, the economic relationship compounds. You have delivered $120,000 in fees from one client acquisition event. The project client might have delivered $15,000 total from that same acquisition event.

### Why clients pay retainers

The assumption many solopreneurs make is that clients want to minimize their fees. This is not accurate. Clients want to minimize their uncertainty and maximize the reliability of outcomes.

A retainer offers the client: predictable monthly costs they can budget around, guaranteed access to your capacity and attention, reduced friction for new requests (no new proposal required), and continuity of context (you know their business, their preferences, their systems).

These are valuable. Clients who have been burned by project-based engagements where the agency moved on after delivery, where institutional knowledge was lost, and where every new request required a new sales cycle, understand the value of a committed ongoing relationship. Your job is to articulate that value clearly.

## Part Two: Positioning for Recurring Work

### The specific problem that demands ongoing attention

Retainer positioning starts with identifying a problem that never goes away. Project work solves a defined problem that, once solved, is done. Retainer work addresses an ongoing need that the client has every month.

Common ongoing needs that support retainer structures:

Content production: writing, video, social media. The need is monthly because the publication is monthly.

Technical maintenance: websites, applications, databases, security. Systems require ongoing attention.

Strategic advisory: business strategy, marketing strategy, financial guidance. Decisions come up continuously.

Compliance and monitoring: accounting, legal, HR. The requirements are ongoing.

Customer communications: email marketing, community management, customer support. The audience needs to be served continuously.

If you are trying to build retainers, the first question is: does the service I provide address a recurring need, or a one-time problem? If one-time, can I expand my service scope to include the ongoing component?

A designer who builds websites (one-time) can expand to maintaining and improving those websites (ongoing). A copywriter who writes launch campaigns (one-time) can expand to running ongoing email marketing (recurring). The expansion does not have to be dramatically different from your core skill, but it needs to address something the client needs every month.

### Positioning language that attracts retainer clients

Most service providers describe themselves in terms of what they do: "I'm a freelance graphic designer" or "I'm a web developer." This framing positions them as a commodity, because the client's next question is "how much do you charge," and then they find someone who charges less.

Retainer-oriented positioning describes the problem you solve and the ongoing nature of your involvement. "I help SaaS companies maintain a steady pipeline of qualified case studies and customer stories, month over month, without their internal team having to manage it." This framing immediately communicates recurring value, not a one-time deliverable.

The positioning test: can the client hear your description and immediately understand why they would need you every month, not just once? If not, your positioning is still too project-oriented.

### Niching for recurring need

The narrower your niche, the more recognizable your positioning and the easier it is to justify a recurring engagement.

"I'm a copywriter" is broad. There is no immediate retainer rationale.

"I'm a copywriter who manages the weekly email campaigns for e-commerce brands in the outdoor gear category" has a clear recurring structure embedded in the description.

Niching by industry, by business size, by specific function, and by the frequency of the work all help create retainer-compatible positioning. The goal is to be the obvious choice for a specific client profile who needs your type of work consistently.

## Part Three: Retainer Structures

### The three main retainer architectures

**Capacity retainer.** The client buys a defined number of hours per month. They can use those hours for any work within your defined scope. Unused hours typically do not roll over. This is the simplest structure and the easiest to explain, but it can create anxiety for clients who worry about "using up" their hours.

**Deliverable-based retainer.** The client pays a monthly fee for a defined set of deliverables. "Eight social media graphics, one email campaign, and one blog post per month for $4,000." Both parties know exactly what they are getting. This is easier to value from the client's perspective and easier to manage from yours.

**Relationship retainer.** The client pays for priority access and ongoing advisory support. This works best for consultants, strategists, and advisors who provide thinking and guidance rather than specific outputs. The client can contact you within defined parameters, attend a monthly strategy call, and get same-day responses on questions. The fee is for access and reliability, not for deliverables.

Each structure has advantages. Early in a retainer relationship, deliverable-based is often easiest because it reduces ambiguity. As the relationship matures and trust deepens, moving to a relationship retainer is often better for both parties.

### Designing retainer scope

The most common retainer mistake is under-scoping. You define what is included vaguely, a client expands requests over time, and you end up delivering far more than the retainer fee justifies. This is scope creep, and it will eventually make you resent the engagement.

Effective retainer scope documents include:

What is explicitly included: specific deliverables, specific services, specific response time commitments, specific number of revision rounds.

What is explicitly excluded: types of work outside the retainer scope, number of revision rounds beyond which additional fees apply, response time outside business hours, project work that falls outside the defined parameters.

How additional work is handled: project work that falls outside the retainer scope can be handled at your project rate, or declined, or added to the retainer through a scope expansion amendment.

The scope document is not a legal document in the traditional sense. It is a clarity document. Its purpose is to ensure both parties have the same expectations. Misaligned expectations are the source of most retainer breakdowns.

### Pricing retainers

The project-to-retainer pricing conversion follows a general principle: retainers are priced for the relationship value, not just for the hours.

A useful starting calculation: estimate the monthly hours you will invest in the engagement. Multiply by your project hourly rate. Add 20-30% for the value of guaranteed capacity, context retention, and priority access. This is your minimum retainer price.

In practice, retainer pricing is often lower per hour than project pricing because of the offset benefits: no acquisition cost, predictable scheduling, lower administrative overhead. But the client should be paying more total than they would for equivalent one-off projects because the ongoing relationship delivers compounding value beyond individual deliverables.

Pricing tiers work well for retainer offers. Three tiers allow clients to self-select based on their budget and need:

Tier one (foundational): lower price, defined scope, monthly. Suitable for clients who need consistent support on a specific, narrow function.

Tier two (operational): mid-range, broader scope, potentially weekly check-ins. Suitable for clients who need you as an ongoing operational partner.

Tier three (strategic): premium price, highest access, includes advisory and strategy beyond execution. Suitable for clients who want you embedded in their decision-making.

This structure also makes it easy for clients to upgrade as the relationship deepens and value is demonstrated.

## Part Four: Client Selection for Long-Term Relationships

### The client characteristics that predict retention

Not every client is a retainer client. Some clients have seasonal needs, episodic requirements, or a one-time problem. Trying to force a retainer on them creates a bad relationship.

The client profile that predicts long retainer relationships:

They have a continuous need for your type of work. Their business requires ongoing content, ongoing development, ongoing strategy. This is a business characteristic, not a preference.

They have budget stability. Clients whose businesses are early-stage or highly seasonal may not have the consistent budget for a monthly commitment.

They are outcome-oriented rather than process-obsessed. Clients who are comfortable trusting your judgment on how work gets done retain longer than clients who want to approve every micro-decision. The latter is exhausting and eventually untenable.

They communicate proactively. Clients who surface issues before they become crises are easier to retain well. Clients who go silent for weeks and then panic when a deadline approaches are structurally difficult.

They view you as a partner, not a vendor. The language a client uses reveals their orientation. "We need someone to execute our briefs" is vendor language. "We need someone who can help us think through our content strategy and execute" is partner language. Partners retain. Vendors are replaced when a lower bid appears.

### The discovery process as client qualification

The proposal stage, specifically the discovery conversation, is your best tool for assessing whether a client is retainer-compatible.

Questions that reveal client orientation:

"What does ongoing success look like for you in this area?" A client who can answer this with specificity and continuity is thinking long-term. A client who answers with a one-time milestone may not be thinking beyond that milestone.

"How have you worked with external partners before?" This reveals their experience and expectations. "We've had great relationships with our previous agency" is a good sign. "We've been burned by every agency we've worked with" requires deeper investigation.

"How do you prefer to communicate and make decisions?" This reveals process compatibility. If they require daily check-ins and approval on every output, and you work best with weekly communications and high autonomy, you are not compatible regardless of how good the work is.

"What is your budget structure for this type of support?" A client who cannot answer this question or becomes evasive has not done the internal work to support a retainer engagement. You can educate them, but you cannot force budget clarity.

The discovery process is mutual qualification. You are evaluating them as much as they are evaluating you.

### Red flags that predict retainer failure

Certain client behaviors during the proposal phase predict short retainer relationships:

Aggressive price negotiation on first contact. Clients who open by asking you to cut your rate are signaling that they are primarily price-sensitive. They will look for a lower alternative.

Vague or shifting scope. If the client cannot describe what they need with reasonable specificity after two discovery conversations, the scope will expand indefinitely once the retainer starts.

Multiple decision-makers with no clear owner. When you do not know who is actually approving your work and who you are ultimately serving, delays and conflicts are inevitable.

Comparisons to cheaper alternatives. "The last person I worked with charged half of this." This signals they do not understand differentiation. They are price-shopping, not value-shopping.

Urgency pressure combined with low budget. "We need this immediately and we can't pay much" is two incompatible requests simultaneously. This client will generate high stress and low revenue.

### Building for long tenure from day one

The first three months of a retainer relationship determine whether it lasts three years or terminates at the three-month mark. This is when trust is established, when expectations are calibrated, and when patterns are set.

Over-deliver in the first 90 days. Not in scope, but in quality, responsiveness, and proactivity. If you see an opportunity that is adjacent to your scope, mention it. If you notice something is off with their current approach, raise it. Be the partner they did not know they needed.

Document everything. Send a brief monthly summary of what was delivered, what the results were, and what is planned for the next month. This creates a visible record of value and forces a regular review of whether the engagement is delivering.

Communicate about challenges before they become crises. If something is not working, bring it up early. Clients do not terminate retainers because of problems. They terminate because problems were hidden or minimized.

## Part Five: Pricing Retainers vs. Project Work

### The positioning premium

A retainer price should reflect the full value of the relationship, not just the cost of the hours. The value of guaranteed access, context continuity, and priority scheduling is real and should be priced.

The practical way to establish this premium: calculate what equivalent project work would cost if the client engaged you one project at a time. Add the cost of context loss between projects, the cost of re-establishing the relationship for each engagement, and the cost of the client's time managing that process. The retainer should be priced below the full-project equivalent but above the hours-times-rate calculation.

This framing is also useful in client conversations. "If we structured this as individual projects, you would likely pay X per engagement, and you would have to re-brief me each time and manage multiple project cycles. The retainer gives you everything that would cover at a lower monthly cost, with none of the project management overhead."

### Annual retainer discounts

Many service providers offer discounts for clients who pay annually upfront. This is rational from a cash flow perspective, a 10-15% discount for guaranteed annual payment provides meaningful predictability.

Be cautious about annual discounts for new relationships. If you discover in month three that the client is difficult and the engagement is not sustainable, being locked into an annual contract is painful. Annual structures make more sense for established relationships where you have evidence the engagement works for both parties.

### Raising retainer rates

Retainer rates should be reviewed annually. The standard business rationale for rate increases applies: your costs increase, your expertise deepens, and the market rate for your services evolves.

The process for rate increases: give at least 60 days notice. Frame the increase as a reflection of evolved scope and demonstrated value, not as an arbitrary business decision. "I'm increasing our monthly retainer from $4,500 to $5,200 as of January 1. This reflects the expanded scope we've taken on over the past year and aligns with current market rates for this level of engagement."

Expect some pushback. Have a conversation. If the client cannot accommodate the new rate and you cannot justify the current rate, it may be time to transition the engagement.

Clients who push back very aggressively on modest rate increases, in the 10-15% range annually, are often signaling that the relationship is not as strong as you thought. A partner who values your work will accept reasonable rate increases. A vendor relationship will resist any increase.

## Part Six: Keeping Clients Without Scope Creep

### The scope creep anatomy

Scope creep begins innocuously. A client asks for "one quick thing" outside the retainer scope. You say yes because the relationship is new, the request is small, and you do not want to seem rigid. This happens again next month. And the month after.

Within six months, you are delivering 30% more work than the retainer covers, and the client's expectation has shifted to include that extra 30% as baseline. When you raise it, they are confused and mildly offended, because from their perspective, you have always done this work.

Scope creep is not malicious. It is the predictable result of unclear boundaries and insufficient enforcement of those boundaries.

### The language of scope management

Managing scope does not require being adversarial. It requires being clear and consistent.

When a request falls outside the retainer scope: "That sounds like a great project. It falls outside our current retainer scope, so I can either add it as a separate project at [rate], or we can discuss adding it to the retainer as a scope expansion. Which direction makes more sense for you?"

This language does three things: it acknowledges the request as legitimate, it offers a path forward, and it makes clear that the extra work has a cost. Most clients will either opt for the project rate or accept that the scope expansion should be priced into an updated retainer.

The key is consistency. If you handle this language for every out-of-scope request from day one, it never feels like a surprise. If you let things slide for months and then suddenly enforce, it feels arbitrary and creates tension.

### Monthly scope reviews

Build a brief scope review into your monthly communication. "Here is what we delivered this month. I want to flag that the X work we did in week three is outside our defined scope. I am happy to include it, but I want to make sure we both recognize it as additional and decide how to handle it going forward."

This is not a confrontation. It is good account management. Clients who are professionally managed stay longer and pay more because they trust that the relationship is clearly defined and fairly handled.

The monthly scope review also creates natural opportunities to propose scope expansions that benefit both parties. If you have been delivering adjacent value that is not covered, you can propose adding it formally at an appropriate fee increase.

### The five signals that a client relationship is worth protecting

Not every client relationship deserves maximum retention effort. Your time and emotional energy are finite. Identify which clients are worth going above and beyond to keep, and manage the others more efficiently.

Signals of a high-value retainer relationship:

They pay on time, every time. This sounds basic, but it is a strong signal of respect and operational health.

They implement your recommendations. Clients who take your advice seriously get results, and clients who get results stay.

They refer other clients. Referral behavior indicates deep satisfaction and trust.

They are honest about challenges. Clients who tell you when something is not working give you the information you need to serve them better. Clients who hide problems until they have already decided to terminate are protecting themselves at your expense.

They grow. A client whose business is growing has more budget, more scope, and more need over time. Growing clients are your best long-term accounts.

Clients who exhibit these signals deserve extraordinary service. Clients who consistently exhibit the opposite signals, slow payment, ignored recommendations, no referrals, hidden problems, and stagnation, should be actively managed out when a better client becomes available.

## Part Seven: Building a Full Retainer Portfolio

### The capacity math for a solo retainer business

A solo service provider has a fixed number of working hours. Building a retainer portfolio requires understanding how many retainer clients you can serve well.

The calculation depends on your service type. A consultant who provides strategic advisory can manage more retainer clients than a developer who produces code. Advisory work scales with documentation and systems. Execution work scales less.

A general framework: identify how many hours per week you can dedicate to client work. Divide that by the minimum hours needed to serve each retainer client well per week. That is your client capacity.

Most solo service providers can serve three to seven retainer clients at a time, depending on retainer depth. Depth is determined by the scope, the communication volume, and the complexity of the work.

Start with three retainer clients. Learn how they collectively consume your time. Adjust your estimate. Then add a fourth only when you have evidence of unused capacity.

### Stacking retainer tiers strategically

Your retainer portfolio should not be uniform. Having clients at different price points creates stability and flexibility.

A healthy portfolio might include: one to two premium retainers at high monthly fees with deep scope, two to three mid-range retainers with defined deliverable scope, and one to two foundational retainers at lower fees that are largely systems-driven and require minimal active work.

The premium retainers generate most of your income and require most of your attention. The foundational retainers are easy cash flow. If you lose a premium retainer, your income drops but does not collapse. If you add a new premium retainer while foundational ones remain, your income grows significantly.

### Converting project clients to retainers

Existing project clients are the best source of first retainers because you have already established trust and demonstrated value.

The conversion conversation should happen naturally after successful project delivery: "The project went well. I've been thinking about what ongoing support would look like for your team. Can we talk about what a monthly engagement might look like?"

The client's natural hesitation is commitment. They know how to buy a project. They understand what they are paying for and what they are getting. A retainer feels open-ended.

Your job is to make the retainer feel as defined as a project. Present a scope document. Present a deliverable list. Present a clear monthly commitment. The client should be able to see exactly what they are buying.

Offering a trial retainer of two or three months removes the commitment objection. "Let's try this for 90 days. If it is not working for both of us, we part ways. If it is, we continue." Most clients who complete a 90-day trial renew because the value is demonstrable.

## Part Eight: Operations and Administration

### The systems that make retainers run

A retainer business requires different operational infrastructure than a project business. Projects have clear start and end points. Retainers are ongoing, which means the administrative overhead can compound over time without systems.

Essential systems for a solo retainer business:

Client communication protocol. Define how clients communicate with you, when you respond, and on what channels. If every client communicates on a different platform, the context switching is expensive. Standardize where possible.

Monthly delivery workflow. Create a repeatable workflow for each retainer. What happens in week one, week two, week three, week four of each month? The workflow should be documented so that if you are sick or overloaded, you can identify exactly where each client stands.

Invoicing automation. Monthly retainer invoicing should be automated. Stripe, HoneyBook, and HubSpot all support recurring invoice automation. You should never be manually sending recurring invoices.

Time tracking for capacity awareness. Even on deliverable-based retainers, tracking time tells you which clients are consuming disproportionate capacity. This information is critical for retainer pricing reviews and for understanding when to propose scope adjustments.

Scope documentation per client. Each client should have a current scope document that both parties have acknowledged. When it changes, create a new version with a date.

### Contract structure for retainers

A retainer contract needs to address several elements that project contracts typically do not:

Termination notice requirements. Both parties should be required to give 30-60 days notice to terminate. This protects you from losing a third of your income with no notice, and protects the client from being suddenly without service.

Scope adjustment protocol. How do scope changes get proposed, priced, and approved? Documenting this process prevents disputes.

Deliverable review and approval. Who is responsible for reviewing deliverables and providing approval? What happens if approval is not received within a defined timeframe?

Payment terms. Monthly in advance is standard for retainers. This is non-negotiable for a healthy cash flow. Clients who insist on paying after delivery are project-thinking and may not be ready for a retainer structure.

IP ownership. When does the client own the work? Usually on payment. Clarifying this prevents disputes about work done before a late payment.

## Part Nine: Growing a Retainer Business

### The referral engine

Satisfied retainer clients refer new clients. This is the primary growth engine for a solo retainer business, and it is more reliable and lower cost than any marketing activity.

The referral engine works when: you deliver exceptional work consistently, the client feels like the relationship is a genuine partnership, and you make it easy and natural for them to refer.

Making referrals natural: mention occasionally that you have capacity for one new client. Ask happy clients if they know anyone in a similar position who might benefit from the same support. Provide referral clients with a brief document explaining your services that they can share.

Do not incentivize referrals with commissions unless it fits naturally in your business context. The best referrals come from clients who are genuinely excited to share, not from a transactional incentive.

### Content and positioning as pipeline development

Creating content that demonstrates your expertise and approach serves two functions: it attracts inbound inquiries from potential retainer clients, and it reinforces your value with existing clients who see the content.

The content does not have to be high volume. One well-written piece per month, published on LinkedIn or your website, is sufficient to maintain visibility in your niche. The key is specificity. Generic content about broad topics does not differentiate. Specific content about niche problems demonstrates expertise.

Retainer clients who see your content are reminded of why they engaged you. This is a passive retention mechanism that costs almost nothing.

### When to raise prices across the board

The signal to raise prices across your entire retainer portfolio: your inquiry volume exceeds your capacity. If you are consistently turning away potential retainer clients because you are full, your prices are too low. The market is telling you that you are underpriced.

The second signal: your oldest clients are paying significantly less than your newest clients, because you have been raising prices for new engagements but not adjusting existing ones. This creates an internal imbalance where your most loyal clients are often your least profitable.

Price increases are not disrespectful. They are a normal part of a functioning business relationship. Clients who have worked with you for two or three years have watched your expertise deepen and your systems improve. They should expect that your pricing reflects that growth.

## Part Ten: The Philosophical Case for Retainer Business

### Predictability as the foundation of creative work

The creative, strategic, and executional quality of your work is highest when you are not anxious. Anxiety about income disrupts the thinking that produces excellent work. A business structure that eliminates income anxiety is not just financially preferable, it is quality-enabling.

The solopreneur who has four stable retainer clients and $20,000 in predictable monthly income makes better decisions about which projects to take, which clients to work with, and what their work is actually worth. The solopreneur who is constantly hunting new projects makes decisions under scarcity, which means making concessions that compound over time.

Retainer income is psychological infrastructure. It enables the quality of work that justifies retainer income. The two reinforce each other.

### Client relationships as a career asset

Each long-term retainer relationship is a career asset in the same way that a portfolio or a case study is. A client who has worked with you for three years and can speak to the specific outcomes you delivered is more valuable as a reference than a dozen one-time project clients.

Deep client relationships also create knowledge that cannot be bought. You understand their industry, their business model, their team dynamics, and their competitive position at a level of depth that no project work creates. That depth makes you more effective, more confident, and more valuable. It compounds.

### The long game in service business

The most successful solo practitioners are not the ones who had the most clients or completed the most projects. They are the ones who built a small number of deep, high-value relationships that lasted years.

Three clients at $8,000 per month who each stay for three years is $864,000 in revenue from three client acquisition events. That is what a thoughtful retainer business can produce.

The three clients know you. They trust you. They refer their peers. They tell you things they would not tell a new vendor. They give you work that is interesting and challenging. They pay on time. They respect your time.

This is what you are building toward. Not the next project, not the next client, but the architecture of a business that rewards the quality of your relationships as much as the quality of your work.

## Appendix: Templates and Frameworks

### Retainer scope document outline

**Engagement overview.** A brief description of the retainer's purpose and the primary outcome being supported.

**Included services.** A specific, enumerated list of what the monthly fee covers. Be as specific as possible.

**Excluded services.** Services explicitly not covered by the retainer.

**Deliverables and timelines.** Specific outputs expected each month and when they are due.

**Communication protocol.** How requests are submitted, expected response times, preferred channels, and escalation path.

**Revision policy.** Number of revision rounds included per deliverable and how additional revisions are handled.

**Additional work process.** How work outside the scope is quoted and approved.

**Termination terms.** Required notice period and final payment obligations.

**Review schedule.** When the scope and pricing will be formally reviewed. Recommend quarterly for the first year, annually thereafter.

### Monthly retainer report template

A brief document sent to the client at the end of each month:

Summary of work delivered this month, with specific deliverables listed and any results metrics available.

Any scope items that fell outside the defined scope and how they were handled.

Recommendations or observations from the month. One or two items that would improve results or address a risk you observed.

Plan for next month. What the primary focus will be and any items requiring client input.

This document takes 20-30 minutes to produce and is one of the most powerful retention tools available. Clients who receive regular summaries understand the value they are receiving and are far less likely to question the retainer fee.

### Retainer pricing worksheet

Step 1: Estimate monthly hours. How many hours per month do you expect to invest in this engagement, including communication, planning, and execution?

Step 2: Multiply by your project hourly rate. This is your floor, the minimum fee that covers your time at your standard rate.

Step 3: Add 20-30% for relationship premium. This accounts for guaranteed availability, context retention, priority scheduling, and reduced acquisition cost.

Step 4: Compare to deliverable value. If the client were to hire someone else to do this work as individual projects, what would it cost them? Your retainer should be below that number but above your floor calculation.

Step 5: Review against market rates. What do others with your experience and specialization charge for similar scope? Your pricing should be within a reasonable range of market rates.

Step 6: Select a round number. Retainer prices work best as clean numbers, $3,000, $4,500, $6,000 rather than calculated to the dollar. Round up.
