## Introduction: The Price You Set Is a Statement About What You Believe

Every time you set a price, you are making a claim.

A low price says: this is not worth much, and neither, by extension, am I. A price you set with confidence says: I know what this is worth, and I trust the people who understand that to find me.

Most creators undercharge. Not because their work is low quality. Not because the market will not pay more. But because charging more feels presumptuous. It feels like you are claiming to be better than you have proven. It feels like you are risking rejection.

That fear is understandable. It is also costing you money every day.

This guide is not about tricking people into paying more. It is about understanding why your current prices are probably wrong, what drives buyer decisions at different price points, how to build a pricing structure that converts better and earns more, and how to raise your prices without losing the clients who actually matter.

Pricing is a skill. It can be learned.

The creators who build sustainable businesses do not do so by working harder than everyone else or by building the largest audiences. They do it by understanding the relationship between price, value, and buyer psychology, and then building their product catalog and marketing accordingly.

The creator who earns $10,000 per month from 200 buyers at the right prices lives a different life than the creator chasing 2,000 buyers at discounted rates. The second path requires more marketing, more support, more energy, and produces more burnout. The first path requires better pricing.

What follows is the curriculum.

## Chapter 1: Why Creators Default to Low Prices

**The instinct to underprice is not random. It comes from specific, identifiable beliefs. Most of them are wrong.**

Before changing your prices, you have to understand why they are where they are.

### Belief 1: Cheap means accessible means more buyers

This is the most common pricing logic among new creators. The thinking goes: if I charge less, more people will buy. Therefore, lower price equals more revenue.

The reality is almost exactly the opposite. In most creator markets, lowering your price does not proportionally increase your volume. It signals low value, attracts price-sensitive buyers who demand the most support and complain the most, and makes it harder to raise prices later because you have trained your audience to expect cheap.

A $7 product and a $47 product can occupy the same market position. The $47 product will usually sell to buyers who are more committed, consume the product more thoroughly, and generate fewer refund requests.

Volume is not the goal. Profit is the goal. These are not the same thing.

### Belief 2: I have not earned the right to charge more

This is a confidence problem disguised as a market assessment. The creator thinks: when I have more experience, a bigger following, more testimonials, then I can charge more.

This creates a permanent deferral. There is always another threshold to hit before you feel like you have earned it. Meanwhile, creators with less expertise but more confidence are charging more and converting fine.

Your price is not a reward for past performance. It is a statement about current value. If your product solves a real problem for a real buyer, the price is set by the outcome it creates, not by your resume.

### Belief 3: Competition means I have to price low

Looking at what others charge and anchoring to the low end of the market is a positioning mistake. You are choosing to compete on price rather than on value, specificity, or positioning. In commoditized markets, the cheapest option wins. In expertise markets, the most credible option wins.

If your product is specific, well-made, and solves a clear problem, you do not need to be the cheapest. You need to be the most credible option at a fair price.

What your competitors charge is information. It is not instruction. Let it inform your understanding of the market without letting it anchor your price to the bottom.

### Belief 4: High prices will scare people away

Some buyers, yes. The right buyers, no.

The goal is not to convert every visitor. The goal is to convert the right visitors at a price that reflects the value of what you have built. Scaring away price-sensitive buyers who were going to be high-maintenance and low-commitment is not a loss. It is a filter.

Every buyer you turn away with a premium price is a buyer who was unlikely to get results from your product, unlikely to leave a good review, and likely to request a refund. You did not lose that buyer. You filtered them.

### Belief 5: I should see what sells before I commit to a price

This backwards logic leads creators to launch at low prices "to test the market" and then struggle to raise them once the product has an established price history.

You are not testing whether people will pay for your product. You are testing whether you have communicated its value clearly. If it does not convert at your target price, the problem is almost never the price. It is the positioning, the copy, or the distribution.

**Action step:** Write down the three beliefs that most influence your current pricing. For each one, write the counter-argument. This is not affirmations. It is a genuine audit of whether your pricing logic holds up.

## Chapter 2: Pricing Psychology

**Buyers do not process prices rationally. They process them through a set of cognitive filters. Understanding those filters is how you set prices that convert.**

The discipline of pricing psychology is deep. What follows is the practical subset that applies directly to creator products.

### Anchoring

The first price a buyer sees shapes how they evaluate every price that follows. This is the anchoring effect.

If you show a buyer a $197 product before showing them a $47 product, the $47 feels reasonable. If the first price they see is $47, the same $47 product feels like a normal purchase. If the first price they see is $27, the $47 feels expensive.

You control the anchor. Use it deliberately. In your pricing page or product listing, show the full value or a higher tier before you present the option you want most buyers to choose. The comparison does the work.

This is why tiered pricing converts better than single-price products even when most buyers choose the base tier. The higher tiers anchor the base tier as the reasonable choice.

### The $10 Rule

Products under $10 are in an impulse category. Buyers do not think carefully about them. They buy or they do not. If your goal is volume, this price point can work, but it attracts buyers who are least invested in getting results.

Products in the $20 to $50 range require a small but real commitment. Buyers feel the spend. They are more likely to consume the product and more likely to consider the purchase a real investment.

Products above $100 require trust and specificity. The buyer needs to believe you are qualified and that the product will solve a specific, valuable problem. Generic products at this price point do not convert. Specific, credible ones do.

Price your product for the buyer behavior you want, not just for the conversion rate you want.

### The 9 Effect

Prices ending in 9 ($19, $47, $97) consistently outperform round numbers in most creator markets. This is well-documented. The psychological effect is that $19 registers closer to $10 than to $20 in most buyers' mental processing.

This does not mean you should always use 9-endings. Premium positioning sometimes benefits from round numbers ($100, $150, $200) because they signal confidence and simplicity. Test both in your market.

A useful heuristic: use 9-endings for products under $100 in competitive markets. Use round numbers for premium products where you want to signal authority and confidence.

### Loss Aversion

Buyers feel losses more acutely than gains. "What you will miss without this" is more psychologically powerful than "what you will gain."

This is not about fear-mongering. It is about framing. "Most creators leave $3,000 to $5,000 per year on the table by pricing their first product wrong" lands differently than "learn to price your products better." Both say the same thing. One activates loss aversion.

### The Decoy Effect

When you offer three pricing options and want buyers to choose the middle one, make the highest tier only slightly better than the middle tier for significantly more money. The comparison makes the middle tier feel like the obvious choice.

This is called the decoy effect. It works because buyers do not evaluate prices in isolation. They compare. When the comparison is designed intelligently, you guide the decision without pressure.

### Scarcity and Urgency

Price psychology is amplified by scarcity and urgency. A limited quantity at a given price triggers loss aversion. A deadline on a promotional price triggers action.

The key is that scarcity and urgency must be real. False countdowns and manufactured scarcity are visible to buyers and damage trust. Real limits (only 20 spots in a cohort, price increases on a specific date) create legitimate urgency that motivates purchase without deception.

**Action step:** Rewrite one of your product descriptions using loss-framing for the problem section. Compare conversion rates over 30 days.

## Chapter 3: Value-Based Pricing

**The right price for your product is not what it cost you to make. It is what it is worth to the buyer who needs it most.**

Cost-based pricing is the default for creators because it feels objective. You spent forty hours making this course, so you calculate an hourly rate and price accordingly. The problem is that cost has almost no relationship to value.

A framework that took you two hours to develop because you have ten years of experience might be worth $500 to a buyer who would otherwise spend six months and $10,000 figuring it out alone. A course that took you 100 hours to produce might be worth $29 because the market is saturated and the specific problem it solves is not urgent.

Value-based pricing asks: what is this worth to the buyer, not what did it cost me to produce?

### How to Calculate Buyer Value

Ask yourself three questions about your ideal buyer:

**1. What is the cost of the problem this product solves?**
In time, money, or missed opportunity. A creator who is underpricing their services and leaving $2,000 per month on the table has a $24,000/year problem. A $97 product that fixes that problem is a 247x ROI in year one. That is not expensive. That is a bargain.

**2. How long would it take them to figure this out without you?**
Your experience collapsed a learning curve. That has real value. A buyer who would spend three months testing pricing models before finding what works will pay for a shortcut.

**3. What are their alternatives?**
A coaching session on this topic, a marketing course that covers it tangentially, hiring a consultant. If your alternatives are $500 to $2,000, a $97 guide is positioned well below the market rate for the same information.

When you run this analysis on your current products, most creators find their prices are set at 20% to 30% of what value-based pricing would suggest.

### The Value Statement

Before setting a price, write a value statement: "This product helps [specific buyer] solve [specific problem] which is worth [specific outcome] to them. Therefore the price range is [range]."

If you cannot complete that sentence specifically, your product is either too generic or you do not know your buyer well enough to price correctly.

### Value Versus Perceived Value

There is a gap between the actual value your product delivers and the perceived value at the point of purchase. Buyers cannot verify the actual value before they buy. They can only assess perceived value.

Perceived value is shaped by your sales page, your credibility signals, your testimonials, your positioning, and your price itself. A higher price, presented with confidence and backed by evidence, increases perceived value.

This is not manipulation. It is communication. If your product actually delivers $500 in value and you are pricing it at $47, you are not being humble. You are communicating that it is worth $47. That is misleading in the other direction.

**Action step:** Run the three-question value analysis on your top-selling product. What is the value-based price? Compare it to your current price. The gap is your undercharging tax.

## Chapter 4: The Cost of Undercharging

**Undercharging is not safe. It is expensive in ways that compound over time.**

Most creators think of low prices as the conservative choice. The low-risk option. Charge less, sell more, avoid rejection.

The actual cost of undercharging is significant and underexamined.

### Direct Revenue Loss

If you sell 50 copies per month of a $27 product and the correct price is $47, you are leaving $1,000 per month on the table. Over a year, that is $12,000 in foregone revenue from products you are already selling. You do not need more traffic. You need better prices.

That $12,000 compounds. At a correct price, you might invest in better distribution, a designer, or a product upgrade that further increases conversion. Undercharging prevents reinvestment.

### The Quality Buyer Problem

Low prices attract price-sensitive buyers. Price-sensitive buyers are not bad people. But they are statistically more likely to:

- Request refunds at higher rates
- Consume the product less thoroughly
- Leave lower ratings because their expectations were misaligned
- Generate more support burden
- Be harder to upsell

Premium buyers, meaning people who chose your product at a higher price point, have made a larger commitment. They show up differently. They implement. They get results. They become testimonials and referrals.

### The Positioning Signal Problem

Your price is a positioning signal. A $19 product says: this is introductory, low stakes, probably limited depth. A $97 product says: this is substantive, worth a real investment, produced by someone who takes their work seriously.

Buyers do not buy your self-assessment. They buy the signals you send. Price is one of the loudest signals in the creator economy.

When you underprice, you undermine all your other positioning signals. A well-designed landing page, strong testimonials, and a compelling case study can all be canceled by a price that signals low value.

### The Raise Problem

If you have an established audience at $19, raising to $47 feels like a betrayal. Existing buyers who paid $19 may feel cheated. New buyers will question what changed. The social friction of raising prices on an established product is real.

The right time to price correctly is at launch. The cost of underpricing at launch compounds forever.

### The Burnout Equation

When prices are too low, volume has to be high to hit revenue targets. High volume means high support burden, high refund processing, high content output to drive enough traffic to convert at scale.

The creator who charges correctly does not need to sell to everyone. They can build a focused, manageable business. The creator who undercharges runs on a treadmill that accelerates as they try to grow.

**Action step:** Calculate your direct revenue loss from underpricing. Use this formula: (correct price - current price) x monthly sales = monthly undercharging tax. Annualize it. Let that number sit with you.

## Chapter 5: How to Raise Prices Without Losing the Right Buyers

**Raising prices is uncomfortable. It is also necessary. Here is how to do it without destroying what you have built.**

The fear around raising prices comes from imagining that every existing buyer will revolt and that new buyers will disappear. Neither of these things typically happens when you raise prices correctly.

### The Grandfather Strategy

Existing subscribers or past buyers do not need to pay the new price. Their relationship with your work was established at the old price, and honoring that is both good business and the right thing to do.

When raising prices, announce the increase in advance to your list. Give them a window (seven to fourteen days) to purchase at the current price. Frame it as a reward for their loyalty: "Before I raise the price, I want you to have access at the current rate."

This announcement typically generates a sales spike before the price change and converts fence-sitters who have been considering the purchase.

### The Addition Method

If raising the price of an existing product feels too abrupt, add value before raising the price. A new chapter, a bonus template, an additional resource, a companion checklist. The product is genuinely better now. The price reflects that.

This gives you a story: "The expanded version now includes X, Y, and Z. The price is going up to reflect the added depth."

### The Tiered Introduction

Launch your new pricing with a tiered structure. The previous single price becomes the base tier. A new tier at a higher price includes additions. This preserves access at the old price point while introducing buyers to the higher-value option.

Many buyers will choose the higher tier. The ones who choose the base tier were the right buyers for that tier.

### How Much to Raise

Do not raise prices incrementally if the gap between your current price and the correct price is large. Going from $19 to $47 is better done as one change than as three $9 increases over eighteen months. Each increase resets buyer expectations and creates friction.

If your current price is $27 and the correct price is $97, move to $67 immediately and $97 in six months. This normalizes the trajectory rather than creating annual friction.

### Communicating the Price Change

How you communicate a price change matters as much as when you do it.

Wrong approach: "Prices are going up. Buy now before the increase."

Better approach: "I have added [specific content] to [product name] and updated the framework based on working with [number of clients/students] over the past year. The updated version launches at [new price] on [date]. If you have been considering it, this is the last chance at the current price."

The second version gives a reason, demonstrates activity and growth, and frames the deadline as a benefit to the buyer rather than a threat.

**Action step:** Pick one underpriced product. Choose one of these three methods. Set a date for the price increase. Put it on your calendar. The price is not changing until the date arrives, which gives you time to execute the strategy correctly.

## Chapter 6: Tiered Pricing Models

**One price for one product is leaving money on the table. Most creator products are suitable for tiered pricing, and tiered pricing almost always increases revenue.**

The principle behind tiered pricing is simple: different buyers have different levels of urgency, budget, and desire for support. A single price cannot serve all three segments optimally.

### The Three-Tier Model

**Tier 1: Core Product (base price)**
The product itself, complete and standalone. No extras. This tier serves buyers who want the information and are self-directed. Price it at your standard rate.

**Tier 2: Product Plus Support (1.5x to 2x core price)**
The core product plus a defined level of access. This might be a Q&A session, a community forum, a 30-minute review call, a set of templates. Buyers who are less confident or want faster results pay for this access. This tier often accounts for 30% to 40% of total revenue.

**Tier 3: Done-With-You (3x to 5x core price)**
The highest tier includes direct involvement from you. An audit, a strategy session, implementation support. This tier serves buyers with high urgency and budget. It should be limited (only X spots available) and it should be real, meaning you actually deliver personalized attention.

### Tier Ratio Guidance

Based on typical creator product markets:

- 60% of buyers choose Tier 1
- 30% choose Tier 2
- 10% choose Tier 3

If you move from a single $47 price to a three-tier model at $47/$97/$197, your revenue per sale goes from $47 to approximately $78 on average, assuming these ratios hold. That is a 66% revenue increase from the same audience without additional traffic.

### Naming Your Tiers

Avoid naming tiers by feature list. Name them by buyer identity or outcome.

Instead of: Basic / Pro / Premium
Use: The Guide / The Accelerator / The Implementation

Buyers self-select into tiers partly based on identity. "I am someone who implements this seriously" is a more powerful selection driver than "I want more features."

### When Not to Use Tiers

Tiered pricing adds complexity. It is worth it when your product is substantive enough to support meaningful differentiation between tiers and when your audience is large enough to convert meaningfully across multiple options.

For a creator just launching, a single well-priced product is simpler and more effective than a tier structure you do not have the support capacity to deliver. Build the base product first. Add tiers once you have validated demand and understand where buyers want more.

### Designing the Upgrade Path

Your tier structure should create a natural upgrade path as buyers progress. A buyer who starts at Tier 1 and gets results should see a clear path to Tier 2 that makes sense given where they are now.

This means your Tier 1 should deliver genuine value and create a clear next problem that Tier 2 solves. Not an artificially withholded result, but a genuine next step in the buyer's journey.

**Action step:** Design a three-tier structure for your best-selling product. You do not need to implement it immediately. Just build the architecture and price it. See if the math justifies the added complexity of offering multiple tiers.

## Chapter 7: Why Cheap Products Attract Difficult Buyers

**This chapter will sound harsh. It is also true.**

Price acts as a commitment signal. When someone pays $9 for a product, they are making a minimal commitment. The cost of abandoning it, not finishing it, or requesting a refund is negligible. There is nothing at stake.

When someone pays $97 for a product, they have made a real commitment. There is something at stake. They are more likely to consume it fully, implement what they learn, and attribute value to the experience.

This is not a moral judgment about buyers at different price points. It is a behavioral observation. Higher commitment produces better engagement produces better outcomes produces better testimonials and referrals.

### The Support Problem

Low-priced products generate disproportionate support requests. Buyers who paid almost nothing feel less obligated to invest effort in solving their own problems before reaching out. The result is a support burden that scales with volume rather than with revenue.

Premium buyers expect high-quality products, read the documentation, and attempt implementation before asking for help. They are also more forgiving of minor imperfections because they have committed to making the investment work.

A practical rule: for every dollar reduction in price, expect a proportional increase in support burden per unit. The product that costs $9 instead of $47 will generate roughly five times the support requests per copy sold.

### The Refund Rate Curve

Refund rates are generally inversely correlated with price in the creator economy, up to a point. Products priced below $15 refund at higher rates than products priced between $30 and $100. This seems counterintuitive. The explanation is buyer intent.

A buyer who paid $9 has low stakes and low commitment. If the product is anything other than immediately transformative, they refund. A buyer who paid $67 has enough at stake to actually try to implement before deciding it did not work.

### The Ecosystem Effect

Who you attract at your lowest price point sets the tone for your community, your testimonials, and your brand perception. If 80% of your buyers are deeply price-sensitive, your reviews will reflect that demographic's expectations. If 80% of your buyers chose your product deliberately at a fair price, your reviews reflect a different standard.

This is a long-term positioning question, not just a pricing question. Who do you want to attract? Price accordingly.

### The Comparison Trap Within Your Own Catalog

When you have products at multiple price points, buyers naturally compare them. If your $19 product is "almost as good" as your $97 product, the $97 product will not convert. The buyer will buy the $19 product and feel smart for doing so.

Ensure your price points reflect genuine differences in depth, access, or outcome. The $19 product should be complete at its level. The $97 product should be unmistakably more valuable, not just padded with extras that do not move the needle.

**Action step:** Review your refund data and support ticket volume by product price point. If lower-priced products are generating a disproportionate burden, this is data, not theory. Let it inform your next pricing decision.

## Chapter 8: The Premium Positioning Mindset

**Pricing is not just strategy. It is self-concept.**

Everything in this guide can be understood intellectually without being acted on. The reason most creators do not implement better pricing is not that they lack the information. It is that charging more requires a belief about their own worth that feels presumptuous before it feels natural.

This chapter is about that belief.

### The Permission Problem

Creators wait for permission to charge premium prices. They want the market to tell them they are worth it by validating each incremental price increase. What they do not realize is that the market follows their lead, not the other way around.

When you price confidently, buyers interpret the confidence as a signal of quality. When you price tentatively or apologetically (and buyers can sense this in how you write your sales pages), they mirror that tentativeness back in their decision to convert.

You do not get permission to charge premium prices. You give yourself permission. The market responds to that confidence.

### Confidence in Copy

Pricing confidence shows up in the language you use before buyers even see the price.

Tentative copy: "This guide might help you think about how to approach your pricing. It covers some concepts that could be useful if you are new to pricing strategy."

Confident copy: "This guide will show you exactly how to find the correct price for your digital products, how to communicate that price with confidence, and how to raise your prices without losing buyers who matter. The framework has been applied by creators across multiple markets with consistent results."

The first version signals uncertainty. The second signals conviction. Both can describe the same product. One converts. The other undercuts itself before the buyer reaches the price.

### The Comparison Trap

Most creators undercharge because they are comparing themselves to peers at the same career stage. You see someone similar charging $19, and it feels out of reach to charge $67. But you are not competing with people at your career stage. You are competing for buyers who need what you offer.

The question is not "what do my peers charge?" The question is "what do buyers at my level of specificity and credibility pay?" And the answer, when you look at established creators in your space, is usually significantly higher than where you have anchored yourself.

### What Premium Pricing Requires

Premium pricing is sustainable when it is backed by four things:

**1. Real specificity.** Your product solves a defined problem for a defined buyer. Not "improve your content" but "a framework for booking three qualified consulting calls per month through LinkedIn."

**2. Visible credibility.** Case studies, testimonials, demonstrated expertise. Not a list of credentials but evidence of outcomes.

**3. Confident presentation.** Your sales page, your pricing page, your product name all communicate that you believe in what you made. Hedging language undermines premium positioning.

**4. Consistent delivery.** Premium buyers expect quality. Your product needs to match the promise. If it does not, the price will be the thing they point to in negative reviews. If it does, the price becomes evidence that they made a smart investment.

### The Long-Term Brand Effect

Creators who establish premium positioning early build a brand that compounds differently than creators who compete on price. A premium brand attracts premium collaborations, premium speaking invitations, premium clients. A budget brand attracts budget opportunities.

This is not about status. It is about trajectory. Where you price today shapes the opportunities that find you in two years.

**Action step:** Write the version of your next product launch as if you had complete confidence in charging 50% more than you are currently planning. What would the sales page say? What would you include? What would you stop apologizing for? That is your pricing north star.

## Chapter 9: Building Your Pricing System

**A pricing system turns what you know about pricing psychology and positioning into a repeatable process that improves over time.**

Without a system, pricing decisions are made under pressure, with incomplete data, and based on whatever feels defensible in the moment. A system removes the emotion and replaces it with a process.

### The Pricing Audit (Run Every Quarter)

**Step 1: Map your current products and prices.**
List every product, its current price, its monthly sales volume, its refund rate, and its support burden.

**Step 2: Apply value-based pricing to each.**
Using the three-question framework from Chapter 3, calculate the value-based price range for each product. Compare to current prices.

**Step 3: Identify underpriced and correctly priced products.**
Products priced below 50% of value-based range are underpriced. Products priced above 80% of value-based range are well-positioned.

**Step 4: Create a pricing roadmap.**
For underpriced products, identify the target price and the method to get there (direct increase, addition method, or tiered introduction). Set a date.

**Step 5: Track conversion rate by price point.**
After each price change, track conversion rate for 60 days. You will likely find that modest price increases either maintain or slightly improve conversion rate because of the quality signal effect.

### Launch Pricing Strategy

New products should be priced at the correct price from launch, not at a promotional price that becomes the expected standard. If you want a launch promotion, frame it as a limited-time discount with a clear end date, then raise to the permanent price.

This trains your audience that your prices are real and that acting quickly has value. It also means your default price is the premium price, not the promotional one.

The alternative, launching at a low price to drive initial sales volume, has a hidden cost: every buyer at the low price becomes a social anchor for what the product "should" cost. When you raise the price, these buyers often become vocal critics even if the product is objectively worth the new price.

### Bundle Pricing

Bundles work best when they group complementary products and price at 60% to 70% of the individual total. A $47 product and a $37 product bundled at $67 is a 19% discount on total value. That is enough to make the bundle feel like a deal without devaluing the individual products significantly.

Avoid mega-bundles that include everything you have made. They dilute your brand, create support obligations that are hard to manage, and signal "I could not sell these individually."

The best bundles solve a sequence of related problems. Buyer completes product A, has a new problem that product B solves, and the bundle makes it easy to continue the journey at a slightly lower total cost.

### The Annual Price Review

Once per year, systematically review your entire catalog against these criteria:

- Is this product still relevant to your current positioning?
- Is the price consistent with your current authority level?
- Are there products that should be retired, merged, or repriced upward?

Your pricing should evolve as your authority and audience grow. A price that was right twelve months ago may be leaving significant money on the table today.

As your brand matures, your early products become legacy items. Do not let them anchor your brand at an old price point. Either retire them, significantly update them, or reprice them to reflect their position in a more developed catalog.

### Testing and Iteration

Pricing is not permanent. Test different price points on new products, track results, and iterate. The goal is to find the price that maximizes revenue per unit while attracting the buyer quality you want.

A price test requires a meaningful sample. Running a product at a new price for two weeks with 20 visitors is not a test. Run it for 60 days with enough traffic to see a statistically meaningful difference. Only then does the data tell you something reliable.

**Action step:** Run a complete pricing audit on your current catalog using the five-step process above. You do not need to change anything today. You need to know what the data says.

## Conclusion: The Price Is the Product

When a buyer encounters your product, the price is part of the product. It shapes expectations, attracts a specific buyer persona, signals the level of quality they will receive, and determines whether they approach the purchase seriously.

Setting the right price is not about extracting maximum value from buyers. It is about being honest about what your work is worth and attracting the buyers who will get the most from it.

Low prices attract high volume and low commitment. Premium prices attract lower volume and high commitment. The creator who builds a catalog of well-priced, specific products for committed buyers builds a more durable and more profitable business than the creator chasing volume at the bottom of the market.

Raise your prices. Not because you need the money, though you do. Because your work deserves it. And because the buyers who will change their results because of your work are waiting for you at the right price.

The confidence to charge correctly is not something you find. It is something you build, one price increase at a time, until the market's response teaches you what you could not convince yourself of when you started.

Start that process now.
