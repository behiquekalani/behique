# Invoice System for Solopreneurs

## Get Paid Faster and Stop Chasing Late Payments

---

## The Real Reason You Are Not Getting Paid On Time

Before you blame the client, look at the system.

Most solopreneurs who deal with late payments have one thing in common: their invoicing is an afterthought. They finish the work, feel awkward about asking for money, send an invoice three days later with vague terms, and then wait. When the payment is late, they feel uncomfortable following up because the whole process has no structure to stand behind.

The client is not always the problem. The system is.

Late payment is almost always a systemic failure, not a personal one. Clients pay late because nothing in your process created urgency, clarity, or consequence. When the invoice lands with no due date context, no late fee policy, and no follow-up sequence, the client's accounting stack moves it to "whenever." Your invoice competes with every other payable on their desk.

This guide builds the system that removes that ambiguity. When your invoicing process is clear, professional, and automatic, payment behavior changes. Not because clients suddenly become more ethical, but because the path of least resistance becomes paying you.

## Part 1: Invoice Timing Best Practices

### When You Send Matters as Much as What You Send

Most solopreneurs invoice too late. They wait until the project is fully complete, wait a few more days to "make sure the client is happy," and send the invoice as a formality. By then, the budget period may have closed, the internal approver may be traveling, and the invoice is landing in a context where it was not expected.

**For project-based work:** Send the invoice the same day you deliver the final work. Not the next day, the same day. The client is in receipt mode. They have just received something from you. An invoice alongside delivery is a natural pairing. An invoice four days later is an interruption.

**For retainer work:** Invoice on the same calendar date each month, preferably the first or the last business day of the month. Predictable billing dates allow clients to anticipate and plan. Unpredictable billing creates friction even when the client intends to pay on time.

**For milestone work:** Invoice immediately when each milestone is approved, not when the full project ends. This keeps cash flow consistent and ties payment to a defined event that both parties acknowledged.

**The exception: invoicing before work begins.** For new clients, a deposit invoice sent before you start is the clearest signal that your work has professional standards. This is covered in Part 7.

### The Cost of Slow Invoicing

If you wait five days to send an invoice, you have effectively given yourself a five-day late payment. Over the course of a year, slow invoicing on three to five clients per month adds up to weeks of delayed cash flow.

Treat invoice sending as the final deliverable of every project. It is not separate from the work. It is the last step.

## Part 2: What to Include on Every Invoice

### The Fields That Prevent Disputes and Delays

A professional invoice is not complicated, but it must be complete. Missing fields create questions. Questions delay payment.

**Every invoice must include:**

1. **Your business name and contact information.** Full name or business name, email address, and phone number. The client needs to know who to contact if there is a question.

2. **Invoice number.** Sequential numbering (INV-001, INV-002) makes tracking easy and signals that this is a professional operation, not a one-off transaction.

3. **Invoice date.** The date you sent it.

4. **Due date.** Explicit. Not "net 30" as a vague convention. "Payment due by March 15, 2026." Write the actual date.

5. **Client name and billing address.** Especially important if the client is a company with a separate accounts payable department. The invoice needs to reach the right person.

6. **Itemized list of services.** Each deliverable listed separately with a description and the amount. Do not lump everything into one line. Itemization prevents disputes about what was included.

7. **Total amount due.** Clear, prominent, in the currency you agreed to.

8. **Payment methods accepted.** List exactly how you accept payment: bank transfer (with routing and account numbers or IBAN), PayPal, Stripe link, or whatever you use. Do not make the client ask.

9. **Late fee policy.** A single line: "Invoices unpaid after the due date accrue a [X]% monthly late fee." This is covered in more detail in Part 5.

10. **Project or purchase order reference.** If the client gave you a PO number or project code, include it. This eliminates accounts payable processing delays.

### What Not to Include

Do not include a long message about how much you enjoyed working with the client. Save that for a separate email. Invoice text should be professional and functional. The goodwill message belongs elsewhere.

Do not include vague line items like "miscellaneous work" or "additional tasks." These create questions and slow payment. If there was additional work, describe it specifically.

## Part 3: Net-30 vs. On-Receipt vs. Milestone Payments

### Choosing the Right Payment Structure

The payment term you set determines how long the average gap between delivery and payment will be. Most solopreneurs default to net-30 because it sounds professional. For many, it is the wrong choice.

**Net-30**

The client has 30 days from invoice date to pay. This is standard in corporate procurement, and large companies often require it. For small business clients, net-30 is a courtesy they will sometimes use to the fullest extent, meaning they pay on day 29 or day 31.

Net-30 is appropriate when: the client is a large organization with formal procurement, when you are billing for recurring work with predictable amounts, or when you have an established relationship with a reliable payment history.

**On-Receipt (Due Upon Receipt or Net-7)**

The invoice is due when it is received, or within seven days. This is the correct default for most independent solopreneurs working with small businesses and individual clients.

On-receipt is appropriate when: the work is project-based, the client is a small business or individual, or the relationship is new.

The reason most solopreneurs avoid this term is that it feels aggressive. It is not. It is the professional standard for independent contractors. A plumber who fixes your sink does not give you 30 days to pay. Neither should you, by default.

**Milestone Payments**

For longer projects, milestone payment structures align payment with delivery. A typical structure for a three-month project: 50 percent upfront, 25 percent at the midpoint deliverable, 25 percent on final delivery.

Milestone payments protect you from project scope drift and ensure that work completed is work paid for. They also reduce the risk of the final invoice dispute, since only 25 percent of the total is at risk if the relationship deteriorates late in the project.

**The Practical Default**

For new clients: require a deposit (40 to 50 percent) before starting, with the balance due on delivery. This is on-receipt for the balance.

For established clients with reliable history: net-14 or net-30 is reasonable.

For corporate clients with procurement departments: accept net-30 and track carefully.

## Part 4: The Payment Reminder Sequence

### Exact Wording, Three Touchpoints

The most important thing about a reminder sequence is that it exists before you need it. The solopreneur who writes the first reminder email the day after a missed due date, while feeling anxious and resentful, writes a worse email than the one who has a template ready to send.

Write these templates now, when the relationship with your current clients is good. Save them. Send them without modification.

**Reminder 1: Three Days Before Due Date**

This is a neutral, professional courtesy reminder, not a warning.

Subject: Invoice [INV-00X] due on [date]

Hi [Name],

Just a quick note that invoice [INV-00X] for [project name] is due on [date]. The total is [$X].

Payment details are included in the original invoice. If you have any questions or need a copy resent, let me know.

[Your name]

---

**Reminder 2: On the Due Date (If Not Yet Paid)**

Subject: Invoice [INV-00X] due today

Hi [Name],

Invoice [INV-00X] for [$X] is due today. If payment has already been sent, please disregard this message.

If you need any information to process payment, reply here and I'll get it to you right away.

[Your name]

---

**Reminder 3: Seven Days After Due Date**

This is the escalation. The tone shifts slightly, but stays professional. No anger, no guilt, no apologies for sending it.

Subject: Invoice [INV-00X] — 7 days past due

Hi [Name],

Invoice [INV-00X] for [$X] is now 7 days past the due date of [date].

Per the terms on the invoice, a [X]% late fee will be applied if payment is not received by [date 7 days from now]. I want to make sure this gets resolved before that happens.

If there is an issue with the invoice or the payment process, please reply and we can sort it out quickly.

[Your name]

---

**After Reminder 3:**

If payment is not received after Reminder 3, escalate to a phone call or a more direct written communication. If the amount justifies it and the client is unresponsive, consider a collections service or small claims court. Document every communication in writing.

The sequence above handles 90 percent of late payment situations. Most late payments are genuinely forgotten, not intentional. The reminder sequence catches those before they become problematic.

## Part 5: Late Fee Policies

### Having the Policy Matters More Than Enforcing It

The function of a late fee policy is not primarily to collect late fees. It is to change payment behavior before a payment is late.

When a client sees a late fee clause on your invoice, they know the due date is real. It is not a suggestion. The policy creates incentive to pay on time without any confrontation required.

**Standard late fee language:**

"Invoices not paid by the due date are subject to a 1.5% monthly service charge (18% annually) on the unpaid balance."

This is the language used by most professional service providers and is recognized as reasonable in most jurisdictions.

**Where to put it:**

Include it in your invoice template (in small print below the total), in your contract or engagement agreement, and in your initial project kickoff email when you summarize terms.

**How to enforce it:**

When an invoice is late, note in Reminder 3 that the late fee will apply. If payment comes in after the late fee date and the amount is significant, issue a revised invoice that includes the late fee amount.

If the client has an otherwise strong relationship with you, you can waive the late fee as a professional courtesy once. Do not make this a habit, and do not waive it before it is applied. Apply it, then waive it if you choose. Waiving before applying removes the credibility of the policy.

## Part 6: Tools for Invoicing Under $20/Month

### What You Actually Need

You do not need expensive software to run a professional invoicing system. You need software that creates professional invoices, tracks payment status, and sends automatic reminders.

**Wave (Free)**
Wave is genuinely free for invoicing and accounting. It handles recurring invoices, payment tracking, and basic reporting. The free tier is sufficient for most solopreneurs with under 20 active clients.

**Invoice Ninja (Free, with paid tiers from $10/month)**
Open-source invoicing software with a hosted version. More features than Wave for the same price range. Supports multiple payment gateways, automated reminders, and client portals.

**Bonsai ($17/month)**
Bonsai combines contracts, proposals, and invoicing in one tool. If you are currently using separate tools for contracts and invoices, Bonsai simplifies the workflow significantly. The contract + invoice combination is valuable for establishing payment terms legally before work begins.

**FreshBooks ($15/month for the first six months)**
FreshBooks is purpose-built for freelancers and solopreneurs. Cleaner interface than Wave, better client experience on the receiving end of invoices. Worth considering if client experience is a priority.

**What you do not need:**
QuickBooks, Xero, or any enterprise accounting software. These are designed for businesses with multiple employees and complex payroll. The overhead is disproportionate to the needs of a solo operation.

**Payment processors:**
Whatever tool you use for invoicing, set up direct bank transfer (ACH or wire) as the primary payment method. It is the cheapest option for both parties. Stripe is the best alternative for card payments, with a 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction fee that is reasonable for project amounts.

## Part 7: The Upfront Payment Model for New Clients

### The Single Change That Ends Most Cash Flow Problems

If you could make one change to your invoicing system that would have the greatest impact on your cash flow and your client quality, it is this: require a deposit from every new client before you start work.

The deposit model does three things simultaneously.

First, it protects you from non-paying clients. A client who refuses to pay a 40 percent deposit is telling you something important before you invest hours into their project. Walk away.

Second, it creates shared investment. Clients who have paid a deposit are more engaged, more communicative, and more likely to provide timely feedback. The financial stake changes their behavior.

Third, it filters for serious clients. The prospect who "wants to start right away but can we invoice at the end" is almost never the prospect who pays easily. The prospect who says "send me the deposit invoice and I'll get it processed today" is the client who values your work correctly.

**The standard deposit structure:**

- New clients, projects under $5,000: 50% deposit, 50% on delivery
- New clients, projects $5,000 to $15,000: 40% deposit, 30% at midpoint, 30% on delivery
- Ongoing retainer work: first month's payment upfront, then monthly on the same date

**The conversation:**

Do not apologize for the deposit requirement. State it as policy, not preference.

"My standard terms for new projects include a 50 percent deposit before work begins. Here is the deposit invoice. Once that is received, I will confirm the start date."

Most legitimate clients accept this without question. The ones who push back hard on a deposit are the ones most likely to cause payment problems later.

## Final Note

An invoicing system is not just a way to get paid. It is a signal.

Every interaction a client has with your invoicing process tells them something about how you operate. An invoice that arrives immediately, is itemized clearly, states its terms explicitly, and follows up professionally communicates that you run a real business. It builds confidence in you as a professional.

A vague, late invoice with no terms sends the opposite signal.

Build the system once. Set up the templates, the payment reminders, and the deposit policy as defaults. Then let the system handle the administrative awkwardness so you can put your attention where it belongs: doing excellent work.
