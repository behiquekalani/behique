# The Retainer Model for Solopreneurs

## Build Predictable Monthly Revenue Without Constant Selling

---

## What Makes Retainers Work vs Fail

The retainer model is either the best business structure a solopreneur can build or a trap that slowly drains their energy. The difference is almost entirely determined by whether the retainer was designed correctly from the start.

A retainer is a monthly recurring agreement where a client pays you a fixed fee for ongoing access to your work, thinking, or output. That is the clean definition. In practice, a retainer is a bet on clarity. If both sides know exactly what the retainer includes, how much it costs, and what the expected outcomes are, retainers work well. If any of those three things are ambiguous, retainers fail in specific and predictable ways.

The most common retainer failure mode: the solopreneur prices the retainer based on what feels fair at the start of the relationship, without accounting for what the work actually requires over time. At month 1, a $2,000/month retainer for social media management feels fine. By month 4, the client has expanded their expectations, the solopreneur is working 50% more hours than originally scoped, and the relationship is strained because neither side wants to have the difficult conversation about repricing.

The second failure mode: retainers designed around time rather than outcomes. "20 hours per month" is not a retainer. It is an hourly agreement dressed up as a retainer. Time-based retainers do not build the leverage that makes the model valuable. They just guarantee the solopreneur will feel resentful if the client "uses all their hours" and feel anxious if they do not.

The third failure mode: retaining clients who do not need ongoing work. Not every engagement should be a retainer. Some projects are genuinely finite. Trying to convert a finite project into a retainer creates an artificial relationship that both sides eventually recognize as hollow.

This guide is honest about when retainers work and when they do not. The goal is not to convince you that every client should be on a retainer. The goal is to help you build a retainer book that is genuinely sustainable.

## The 4 Types of Retainer Structures

Most solopreneurs run one type of retainer without realizing there are four distinct structures, each with different use cases and different requirements.

**Type 1: The Deliverable Retainer**

The client pays a monthly fee for a specific set of deliverables per month. Four blog posts. Eight social media graphics. Two email campaigns. The scope is defined by output, not time.

This is the simplest retainer structure and often the most appropriate for service providers who produce discrete outputs. The client knows exactly what they are getting. The solopreneur knows exactly what they owe.

Works best for: copywriters, designers, content creators, developers building recurring features.

The risk: scope creep on individual deliverables. "Can you make this just a bit longer" applied to four blog posts per month accumulates quickly. Define the deliverable tightly: word count range, number of revisions included, turnaround time per piece.

**Type 2: The Capacity Retainer**

The client pays for guaranteed access to your capacity and expertise for a defined period each month. Not a set number of deliverables. A defined block of your focus.

This structure works for more strategic or consultative roles where the work is variable and judgment-dependent. A fractional CMO working 2 days per week for a startup is on a capacity retainer. A consultant who is available for calls, reviews, and strategic input within a defined monthly boundary is on a capacity retainer.

Works best for: consultants, strategists, coaches, and advisors.

The risk: "available" expanding to mean "always available." A capacity retainer requires clear definition of what availability means: response time, meeting hours per month, whether weekends are included.

**Type 3: The Outcome Retainer**

The client pays a monthly fee tied to the achievement of ongoing outcomes rather than specific deliverables or time blocks. "I manage your SEO and you pay $1,500/month. The outcome is consistent ranking maintenance and monthly reporting."

This is the highest-leverage retainer for the solopreneur because it decouples payment from time. If you get the same result in 10 hours that used to take 20, you earn the same amount. Your efficiency directly improves your effective hourly rate.

Works best for: SEO specialists, paid media managers, email marketers, anyone managing an ongoing system.

The risk: outcomes can be influenced by external factors you do not control. An SEO retainer can look bad when an algorithm update hits. Build outcome definitions that reflect what you actually control.

**Type 4: The Access Retainer**

The client pays monthly for the right to access your expertise on demand, within a defined limit. This is sometimes called a "fractional" model when it includes executive-level thinking.

Access retainers are common for lawyers, accountants, and senior consultants. A startup pays a $1,000/month retainer to have a fractional CFO available for questions, reviews, and monthly strategy calls. The value is in the access, not in a specific output.

Works best for: experienced professionals whose expertise reduces risk or cost for clients.

The risk: this model requires a strong track record and clear positioning. An access retainer is hard to sell when the client cannot point to specific past results that justify paying for ongoing access.

## Pricing a Retainer Correctly

The most common pricing mistake in retainers: taking your hourly rate, estimating how many hours the retainer will require, and multiplying. This is the worst way to price a retainer.

Here is why. This approach anchors your price to your labor rather than to the value the client receives. It also guarantees that as you get more efficient, you feel increasingly underpaid. And it invites the client to evaluate the retainer by counting hours rather than by assessing results.

A better approach: price the retainer based on three factors.

**Factor 1: The value of the outcome to the client**

What is the result of your work worth to the client on a monthly basis? If your SEO work generates $15,000 per month in organic traffic revenue for a client, a $1,500/month retainer is obviously underpriced. The client is getting a 10x return. You have significant room to increase price.

Most solopreneurs do not know the downstream value of their work because they have never asked. Ask. "How much do you expect this work to be worth to your business?" is a legitimate question to ask before pricing a retainer. The answer changes how you price.

**Factor 2: Your replacement cost**

What would the client pay to hire someone full-time to do what you do for them? A full-time social media manager in the US costs $55,000 to $75,000 per year in salary plus benefits. That is $5,000 to $7,500 per month. If you are delivering comparable output and strategic thinking, a $2,500/month retainer is still a 50% discount versus the in-house alternative.

This framing shifts the conversation from "is your rate fair" to "is this a good business decision for us." It is.

**Factor 3: Your actual cost to deliver**

This is the floor, not the ceiling. Calculate how many hours the retainer realistically requires per month, including communication overhead, not just execution time. Multiply by the minimum hourly rate you can sustainably work at. That number is your floor.

Your price should be at or above this floor, ideally significantly above it. If the floor calculation shows you need $800/month to break even on a retainer and the market will support $2,000/month, price at $2,000/month.

**The retainer premium:**

Retainers command a premium over project-based work for three legitimate reasons: the client is paying for guaranteed priority access to your schedule, you are absorbing the risk of variable monthly demand, and you are providing predictability that has business value. Price that premium in.

A rule of thumb: your retainer monthly fee should equal roughly 1.5x to 2x what you would charge if the same scope were a standalone project billed monthly. The premium reflects guaranteed availability and relationship depth.

## Scoping to Prevent Scope Creep in Retainers

Scope creep in retainers is more damaging than scope creep in projects because it compounds monthly. An extra 3 hours of work per month in a project ends when the project ends. An extra 3 hours per month in a retainer that runs for 18 months is 54 hours of uncompensated work.

The solution is not to be rigid with clients. It is to define scope clearly enough that both sides can recognize when something is inside or outside the agreement.

**The retainer scope document:**

Every retainer should have a scope document that is separate from the contract but referenced by it. The scope document defines:

What is included: specific deliverables, specific services, specific access, specific response times.

What is not included: name exactly what you will not do under this retainer. This is not adversarial. It is professional. "Strategy calls are included; implementation of the strategies discussed is not" is a clear boundary that prevents a common conflict.

The change request process: what happens when the client wants something outside scope. A simple process: "Additional work outside this scope is available at $[rate]/hour, invoiced separately and confirmed in writing before starting."

Revision limits: how many rounds of revision are included per deliverable, and what constitutes a revision versus a new deliverable.

**The scope review:**

Every 3 to 6 months, review the retainer scope against what you are actually delivering. If the actual work has expanded beyond the defined scope, you have two options: renegotiate the retainer price to reflect the actual scope, or reduce the scope back to the contracted level.

Both options are legitimate. The worst option is continuing to deliver expanded scope without addressing it. This breeds resentment on your side and entitlement on the client's side.

## The Retainer Proposal Structure

A retainer proposal is different from a project proposal. A project proposal justifies a one-time investment. A retainer proposal justifies an ongoing relationship.

The structure that converts:

**Section 1: The Situation**

Two to three paragraphs that demonstrate you understand the client's ongoing need. Not their history. Not their industry background. The specific recurring challenge that requires an ongoing engagement.

"Your content output requires consistent strategic oversight. Without ongoing guidance on content performance, distribution, and iteration, you will continue to produce content that does not compound into audience growth over time."

**Section 2: The Engagement**

What the retainer includes, stated clearly. Use the deliverable or capacity framing depending on which type of retainer you are proposing. Be specific.

**Section 3: What This Costs to Not Have**

Not your price. The cost of the alternative. "Hiring a full-time senior content strategist costs $85,000 per year. Doing this without strategic oversight produces content that has a documented 70% lower distribution reach."

This section reframes the buying decision. The client is not deciding whether to spend $2,500/month on you. They are deciding whether to spend $2,500/month versus a much more expensive alternative.

**Section 4: Investment**

Your monthly fee, clearly stated. If you offer different tiers, show no more than three. More than three options creates decision paralysis.

Include a note on the minimum commitment period. Three months is a reasonable minimum for most retainers. It gives both sides enough time to assess whether the relationship is working.

**Section 5: The Process**

How the engagement starts, what the first 30 days look like, and how you will measure whether the retainer is delivering value.

## Transitioning Existing Clients to Retainers

The easiest retainer clients to get are the ones you already have. A client who has completed a project with you and had a good experience is the ideal candidate for a retainer conversation.

The transition pitch is not a pitch. It is an observation.

"Over the last three months, we have had to restart the conversation from scratch at the beginning of each project. I think there is a more efficient way to work together. Are you open to a conversation about a monthly arrangement?"

This frames the retainer as a solution to a real pain point the client has already experienced, not as an upsell for your benefit.

Before making the transition pitch, evaluate whether the client is a good retainer candidate:

Good retainer candidates: clients who have ongoing needs in your area, clients who find the project-start-and-stop cycle inefficient, clients who trust your judgment and act on your recommendations, clients who pay on time and communicate clearly.

Poor retainer candidates: clients who micromanage deliverables, clients who frequently change direction, clients who negotiate price at every renewal, clients who have seasonal or truly finite needs.

Not every good project client becomes a good retainer client. The ongoing nature of the relationship amplifies whatever dynamic exists in the project relationship. A mildly difficult project client becomes a genuinely difficult retainer client.

**The transition conversation:**

Have the conversation after a successful project completion, not mid-project. "Now that we have finished [project], I wanted to share an idea about how we might work together going forward." Present the retainer as a natural evolution, not a new product.

Offer a transition rate for the first three months. Not a discount. A rate that reflects the shorter ramp-up time you need with a client you already know. "Because we have already established how we work together, I can offer a slightly lower rate for the first quarter while we calibrate the ongoing scope."

## Managing a Full Retainer Book as a Solo Operator

A full retainer book for most solopreneurs is 3 to 6 clients. At that number, the revenue is substantial and the management complexity is still manageable. Above 6 retainer clients, most solopreneurs find that communication overhead alone begins to erode the efficiency advantage of the model.

**The operational system:**

Each retainer client needs a clear monthly rhythm. This means a fixed date for deliverables, a fixed date for the monthly check-in call, and a fixed date for invoice delivery.

When all 5 of your retainer clients have different rhythms, you spend significant cognitive energy remembering who needs what when. Batch where possible. If you can schedule all monthly check-in calls in the same week, your schedule becomes predictable and mentally manageable.

**The monthly rhythm for each retainer client:**

Week 1: Execution on primary deliverables
Week 2: Check-in call, review of results from the previous month, direction for the next
Week 3: Secondary deliverables or additional work
Week 4: Invoice delivery and internal review of whether the retainer is healthy

**The health check:**

Every 60 days, evaluate each retainer against three criteria:

Is the client satisfied with the results? You should know this from the monthly calls, but ask directly if you are unsure.

Are you satisfied with the rate relative to what you are actually delivering? If the scope has expanded informally, this is where you notice it.

Is the relationship positive or deteriorating? A retainer where both sides are glad to work together is worth keeping. A retainer that produces anxiety at the mention of the client's name needs to be restructured or ended.

**Ending a retainer:**

Not every retainer should continue indefinitely. Some clients rotate out, some needs become truly finite, and some relationships run their course.

End retainers on 30-day notice, with the notice in writing. Be direct about the reason if the reason is business-related: "The scope of this engagement has shifted beyond what this retainer was designed to cover." If the reason is relationship-based, you can be general: "I am restructuring my client book to focus on a narrower niche."

End well. The client you transition out professionally will refer others. The client you ghost or handle poorly will not.

## The Honest Tradeoffs

The retainer model is not the right choice for every solopreneur. Here are the tradeoffs as they actually exist.

**Retainers reduce revenue ceiling but increase revenue floor.** If you price your retainers conservatively, a month with unusually high client demand produces no extra revenue because the fee is fixed. The same is true in reverse: a slow month still pays the retainer fee. If your tolerance for variable income is low, retainers solve that problem. If you want to capture upside from exceptional performance, retainers limit that.

**Retainers require ongoing client management.** A project-based business ends when the project ends. A retainer-based business requires continuous relationship maintenance. For solopreneurs who prefer the clean finish of a completed project, the ongoing nature of retainers can feel draining.

**Retainers become stale if not actively maintained.** A retainer that made sense 12 months ago may not make sense today. Markets change, client needs change, and your own positioning evolves. The solopreneur who set up retainers and never revisited them often finds they are working for rates that no longer reflect the value they provide.

**The best retainers are built on trust, not contracts.** A retainer contract protects both parties. But the thing that makes retainers work long-term is the client's genuine belief that you are adding value and your genuine belief that the relationship is worth maintaining. No contract creates that. You build it through consistent execution, honest communication, and proactive thinking about the client's situation.

When the model is right and the relationships are right, a full retainer book is the closest thing to financial stability that exists in solo business. You know your revenue on the first of the month. You know who you are working for. You know what they need. That predictability is genuinely valuable, and it is worth building deliberately.

Build the model correctly and it compounds over years, not quarters.
