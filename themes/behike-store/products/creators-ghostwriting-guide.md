## Get Paid to Write for Others While Building Your Own Brand

## Why Ghostwriting Is One of the Best Early-Stage Income Streams

Most writers spend months building an audience before seeing a dollar. They post consistently, grow slowly, and wait for monetization to catch up. Meanwhile, ghostwriters get paid from day one.

Ghostwriting is the practice of writing content that someone else publishes under their name. Tweets, LinkedIn posts, newsletters, blog articles, books, course scripts, podcast outlines. The market for this work is large, it pays well, and almost no one talks about it seriously.

Here is the reality of the current creator economy: there are thousands of business owners, executives, consultants, and public figures who have ideas worth sharing but no time or ability to write them down. They are not looking for a marketing agency. They want one person who can understand how they think and translate that into words that sound like them.

That person can be you.

The argument for starting with ghostwriting is simple. You do not need an audience to make money. You need one client. You do not need a product. You need a skill. You do not need a platform. You need a process.

And here is the part that most ghostwriters do not tell you: ghostwriting builds your own brand at the same time. You are practicing writing every single day. You are studying how different experts communicate. You are learning what resonates with different audiences. You are getting paid to train.

Writers who spend a year ghostwriting come out the other side with sharper instincts, better speed, and a clearer sense of their own voice. That is not a coincidence. It is the mechanism.

## How to Find Ghostwriting Clients

The first mistake new ghostwriters make is going to job boards. Sites that list ghostwriting gigs are flooded with competition and the rates are terrible. You are not competing on a platform. You are positioning yourself as a specialist.

The better approach is identifying who needs a ghostwriter and approaching them before they start looking.

**Who needs ghostwriters right now:**

- LinkedIn thought leaders who post inconsistently or whose writing does not match their actual intelligence
- Founders and executives who have strong ideas but weak written communication
- Course creators who have audio recordings but struggle to produce written content
- Podcast hosts who want their episodes converted to newsletters or LinkedIn posts
- Consultants and coaches who know what they want to say but spend hours staring at a blank page

**Where to find them:**

Start with LinkedIn. Search for founders, consultants, or executives in a niche you understand. Look at their content. If they post rarely, or if their writing feels generic compared to their credentials, they are a candidate. If their replies are sharper than their posts, they are definitely a candidate.

Twitter and X are also productive. Look for people with credibility in an industry who engage well in comments and threads but rarely produce original long-form content.

**How to approach:**

Do not send a generic pitch. Do not start with your rates or your portfolio. Start with a piece of free work.

Find a person whose content could be stronger. Write one sample post or article in their voice based on what you can already observe about how they communicate. Then reach out with something like:

"I read your post about [topic]. I wrote a version that takes the same point further. Sharing in case it's useful."

Attach the sample. No ask. No pitch. Just demonstrate.

If they like it, they will ask. If they ask, you have a warm lead. From there, you have a real conversation about whether they want ongoing help.

This approach works because it removes the risk for the client. They do not have to imagine whether you can do the work. They see it.

## Pricing Your Ghostwriting Services

The range is wide. Entry-level ghostwriters charge $25 to $50 per post. Experienced writers working with executives charge $500 to $2,000 per post. Book ghostwriters charge $20,000 to $150,000 per project. The difference is not years of experience alone. It is positioning, specialization, and the ability to match someone's voice at a high level.

**Starting range: $200 to $500 per month**

When you are starting with no track record in ghostwriting specifically, your goal is proof of concept, not maximum income. Get two or three clients at a low rate, deliver excellent work, and build testimonials.

A retainer at this stage might look like: four LinkedIn posts per week plus one email newsletter for $400 per month. That is reasonable for someone who can demonstrate they understand the format.

**Mid-range: $1,000 to $3,000 per month per client**

Once you have results, case studies, or a client who has grown their audience with your writing, you can charge at this level. At this range, you are typically handling strategy as well as execution. You are not just writing posts. You are advising on topics, angles, and cadence.

**High-end: $5,000 per month and above**

This range is for writers who specialize in high-stakes clients: investors, executives, authors with real platforms, or companies with significant content budgets. You are not a generalist here. You are the person who understands their industry, their tone, and their audience deeply.

**How to move up the range:**

The path from low to high rates is not time. It is evidence. Every client should produce a result you can point to. Audience growth. Engagement increase. A post that went semi-viral. A newsletter that generated inbound leads. Collect these outcomes. They are your pricing leverage.

Raise your rates when you are at capacity. If you have four clients and cannot take on more, double your rate for the next inquiry. Some will say no. Some will not. The ones who do not will fund the same income with half the clients.

## Capturing Another Person's Voice

This is the skill that separates functional ghostwriters from great ones. Writing in someone else's voice is not about imitating their quirks. It is about understanding their thinking pattern.

Every person has a consistent way of constructing an argument. Some people build from personal experience outward to principle. Others start with a contrarian claim and then prove it. Some use lists. Some use narrative. Some are verbose. Some are ruthlessly concise.

Your job is to identify the pattern before you write a single word.

**The voice capture process:**

Step one: read everything they have already published. Not to take notes. To feel the rhythm. Pay attention to sentence length, vocabulary range, the kinds of examples they use, how they handle complexity.

Step two: listen to them talk. If they have podcasts, interviews, or videos, watch at least two hours. The way people speak naturally is closer to their authentic voice than anything they have written, because writing involves self-editing that often strips the personality out.

Step three: ask them direct questions. What words do you never use? What phrases do you hate? Is there a writer or speaker whose style you admire? What do you want people to feel when they read your content?

Step four: write a sample and get specific feedback. Not "does this sound like me?" That question produces vague answers. Instead: "Which sentences feel off? Which words would you change? Is there a paragraph where I lost the voice?"

The feedback from that first round is the most valuable input you will get. People cannot always describe their voice in the abstract, but they can point to specific sentences that feel wrong. Those moments teach you more about how they think than any briefing call.

**The rule of invisible writing:**

Your goal as a ghostwriter is to write so well in someone else's voice that they never want to edit it. The measure of success is not "this is good writing." It is "this sounds exactly like me." Those are different targets. The second is harder.

## Deliverables and Process

A professional ghostwriting relationship needs structure. Clients who have never worked with a ghostwriter often do not know what to give you or what to expect. Your job is to make the process feel effortless for them.

**What a typical monthly retainer looks like:**

- One monthly strategy call (30 to 45 minutes). You discuss topics, current thoughts, recent experiences, and upcoming projects. This is your input session.
- One weekly check-in (15 minutes) or async voice memo from the client. This captures anything that happened during the week worth writing about.
- Drafts delivered on a set day each week. Never when they feel ready. Always on schedule.
- One round of revisions included per piece. More than that is a scope issue, not a writing issue.

**The briefing document:**

Create a one-page document for each new client that covers: their core audience, the three topics they want to be known for, five to ten words or phrases they use constantly, five words or phrases they never use, three pieces of content they love, and their goal for each platform.

This document takes 30 minutes to create and saves hours of revision on every project after.

**Turnaround time:**

Be clear about your timelines upfront. Most ghostwriting clients want drafts within 48 to 72 hours of receiving input. If you need more time, say so. Nothing breaks a ghostwriting relationship faster than a client who needed to post on Tuesday and your draft arrived Thursday.

## Moving from Hourly to Per-Project Pricing

Hourly pricing is a trap. It penalizes you for getting better. The faster and more efficient you become, the less you earn for the same output.

Per-project and retainer pricing aligns your incentives correctly. You are paid for the outcome, not the hours. If you get faster over time, your effective hourly rate increases without asking for a raise.

**How to transition:**

If you currently charge hourly, start tracking how long each deliverable type takes you. A single LinkedIn post might take you 45 minutes on average. A newsletter might take two hours. A blog article might take three.

Once you have those baselines, multiply by the hourly rate you want to be at. That becomes your per-piece price. Then bundle those pieces into monthly retainers with a slight discount for commitment.

The conversation with clients is simple: "I'm moving to project-based pricing because it makes planning easier for both of us. Here's what the same scope looks like as a monthly retainer."

Most clients prefer retainers anyway. They get predictable cost. You get predictable income. The negotiation is just about the total number.

## The Ethics Conversation Most Ghostwriters Avoid

Is ghostwriting dishonest?

This question comes up constantly and most ghostwriting guides skip it or dismiss it with a wave. The honest answer is: it depends on context, and you should decide your position before you start.

**The case for it being fine:**

Ghostwriting is as old as publishing. Most speeches given by politicians and executives were not written by the person who delivered them. Many business books were written with a ghostwriter. The practice is legal, accepted, and common across every professional field.

When a consultant posts a LinkedIn article about a methodology they genuinely developed and use, and a ghostwriter translates their ideas into readable prose, no one is being deceived. The ideas are real. The expertise is real. The writing is professional help, same as having an editor or a designer.

**The line that matters:**

The ethical concern is not whether someone helped with the writing. The concern is whether the person stands behind the ideas as their own. A thought leader who has opinions and knowledge they genuinely hold is not deceiving anyone by having a writer articulate those ideas clearly.

A person who manufactures expertise they do not have and uses ghostwritten content to pretend otherwise is doing something different. That is not a ghostwriting problem. That is an honesty problem that predates the writing.

**Where you set your line:**

Some ghostwriters only work with clients whose expertise they believe is real. Others do not make that judgment. That is a personal decision and there is no universal answer.

What matters is that you make the decision consciously. Know where your line is before you take on a client whose content might conflict with it. Walking away from a paying client because of a values conflict is easier when you have already decided what the conflict looks like.

## The Writer Who Gets Paid First

Most advice for writers assumes the end goal is an audience. Build followers, then monetize. Ghostwriting flips that model. You get paid before you have an audience, and the work itself builds the skills you will need when you eventually write under your own name.

The writers who will do well in the next five years are not the ones who waited to be discovered. They are the ones who went to work on day one, got paid for the skill they already had, and used that income to buy themselves the time and freedom to build something larger.

Ghostwriting is not a fallback plan. For a writer who knows how to execute, it is the fastest path to paid.
