**Stop selling your time. Start building a system that sells while you sleep.**

## Introduction: The Trap Nobody Warned You About

Freelancing feels like freedom. You chose your clients, set your rates, work from anywhere. Compared to a 9-to-5, it looks like the answer.

Then six months in, you realize the problem. You are still trading time for money. The client who pays the most requires the most hours. When you stop working, you stop earning. Vacation is not rest. It is lost revenue. Sick days are not benefits. They are financial penalties.

This is the freelance trap, and it catches almost everyone who enters this world with the wrong mental model. The trap is not about effort. Most freelancers work harder than any employee. The trap is structural. You built a job for yourself, not a business.

The difference between a freelancer and a founder is not scale. It is not funding, a team, or a brand. The difference is where your income comes from. A freelancer's income comes from their time. A founder's income comes from assets: products, systems, audiences, and processes that generate value independent of whether they showed up today.

This playbook is about making that transition. Not quitting freelance overnight. Not gambling your rent on a product idea. A methodical, six-month process of building income streams that compound while your current freelance work keeps the lights on.

The thesis is simple: knowledge you already have is worth more than the hourly rate you currently charge for it. The question is how to package and distribute that knowledge in a form the market will pay for repeatedly, without requiring your direct involvement each time.

You will not find abstract advice here. Every chapter contains specific numbers, specific timelines, and specific decisions. By the end of this playbook, you will have a product concept, a launch plan, and a roadmap that fits around your existing freelance work without blowing it up.

# Chapter 1: The Mental Shift

## Why Most Freelancers Never Escape the Time Trap

The problem starts with how freelancers think about their business.

Ask a freelancer what they sell. Most will say a service: design, writing, development, consulting, photography. Ask an employee what their job is and you get the same answer. This is not an accident. Freelancing inherits its mental model from employment.

In the employment model, you are compensated for your presence and your labor. Hours worked equals value delivered. When you become a freelancer, most people carry this model forward. They charge by the hour or by the project, but the underlying assumption is the same: your income is a function of your personal output.

The founder model is different. A founder asks: what systems can I build that generate value without requiring my personal output every time? This might be software. It might be a team. For most solopreneurs starting out, the fastest entry point is knowledge assets.

A knowledge asset is anything that captures your expertise in a form that can be replicated infinitely at near-zero marginal cost. A book, a course, a template, a framework, a swipe file, a checklist, a process guide. You create it once. It sells while you sleep.

### The Leverage Gap

Consider two people with the same skill set. One charges $100 per hour for consulting. The other packages that same knowledge into a $97 guide and sells 50 copies per month. The consultant makes money only when they work. The guide creator makes $4,850 per month whether they are at their desk or on a beach.

The same underlying knowledge is present in both scenarios. The difference is leverage. The consultant has none. The guide creator has maximum leverage: time spent creating is decoupled from income earned.

Most freelancers intellectually understand this but resist making the shift. The resistance is usually one of three things.

The first is imposter syndrome. "Who am I to teach this? There are people who know more than me." This misunderstands how knowledge products work. Buyers do not want the world's foremost expert. They want someone slightly ahead of them who can explain things clearly. If you have two years of freelance experience in your niche, you know things that someone just starting out would pay to know.

The second is the audience myth. "I don't have followers. I can't launch a product." This is backwards. You build the product first. You find the first buyers through channels that require zero audience. The audience comes after proof of concept, not before.

The third is perfectionism. "I need to build something comprehensive before I can sell it." This guarantees you never ship. The Minimum Viable Product principle exists precisely because perfectionism is expensive and unnecessary. A focused, actionable 40-page guide beats a bloated 200-page one that takes six months to write.

### From Employee to Owner: The Four-Stage Model

Most successful transitions from freelancer to founder follow a pattern.

Stage 1 is the pure freelancer. All income from client work. No leverage. No assets. Income ceiling determined by available hours.

Stage 2 is the hybrid. Still doing client work, but building a parallel track. First product created. Initial sales made. Proof of concept established. This stage usually lasts 6 to 18 months.

Stage 3 is the productized service. Client work has been standardized into packages. Products contribute 20 to 40 percent of income. The freelancer starts saying no to custom work that falls outside their packages.

Stage 4 is the founder. Products, courses, or productized services generate the majority of income. Client work, if it exists, is selective and high-margin. Time is mostly spent creating, not delivering.

This playbook focuses on getting you from Stage 1 to Stage 2 with enough momentum to reach Stage 3. The full journey takes one to two years for most people, but the first product can go live in 60 days.

# Chapter 2: Audit Your Existing Skills

## What You Already Know That the Market Pays For

Before you build anything, you need a clear inventory of what you know.

This sounds obvious. Most people skip it or do it superficially, then spend three months building a product nobody wanted. The skill audit done properly takes two hours and determines everything downstream.

### The Three-Column Audit

Draw three columns on paper or in a document. Label them:

Column 1: Things I know how to do that took me real time to learn.
Column 2: Things people regularly ask me about or come to me for help with.
Column 3: Things I have helped clients or others with that produced measurable results.

Fill each column completely before moving on. You want at least 15 entries per column.

Column 1 captures your expertise. What did you spend 50, 100, or 500 hours learning? What do you do for clients that most people in your industry do not do well? What shortcuts, frameworks, or processes did you develop through experience?

Column 2 captures market signal. If people keep asking you the same questions, that is demand surfacing. What do your clients consistently want explained? What do friends in adjacent careers ask you? What topics come up in your field where people look at you for answers?

Column 3 captures proven value. The market pays for results, not knowledge. If you helped a client increase their conversion rate, reduce their overhead, finish a project faster, or avoid a costly mistake, that outcome is the product. The knowledge that produced the outcome is what you are packaging.

### Finding the Overlap

After filling all three columns, look for skills that appear in at least two of them. Ideally all three. This overlap is your highest-probability product territory.

A freelance copywriter might list email marketing, A/B testing frameworks, and objection handling in Column 1. Clients ask them about subject lines and open rates in Column 2. They have helped three clients double email revenue in Column 3. The overlap is clear: a guide or course on email copywriting for conversions.

A freelance developer might list automation scripts, API integrations, and no-code tools in Column 1. Non-technical founders constantly ask them how to automate repetitive business tasks in Column 2. They have saved clients 10 to 15 hours per week by setting up simple workflows in Column 3. Product direction: a guide to automating small business operations without writing code.

### Validating Market Demand

Before committing to a product direction, spend 30 minutes validating that people pay for this topic.

Go to Gumroad and search for products in your category. If others are selling similar products, that is good news, not bad. Competition confirms there is a paying market. Look at price points. Look at how many sales indicators you can find.

Check Amazon Kindle for ebooks in your niche. Note which ones have reviews. A book with 200 reviews on a specific topic means hundreds of people paid for that knowledge.

Search Google for "[your skill] course" and "[your skill] guide." Note what comes up. Look at the prices. Look at how much content exists versus how much paid content exists.

If you find five to ten competitors selling something related to what you want to build, proceed. If you find nothing, the market either does not exist or you are searching with the wrong terms. Do more research before committing.

### The Niching Decision

The most common mistake in product creation is scope that is too broad.

A guide called "How to Succeed in Digital Marketing" will not sell. A guide called "How to Write Facebook Ad Copy That Converts for E-commerce Brands Spending Under $1,000 Per Month" will sell very well to a specific audience.

The narrower your focus, the easier the product is to write, the easier it is to market, and the easier it is for a buyer to decide it is right for them.

The mental model: your product should be something a specific person in a specific situation would buy at a specific moment. Not "useful for anyone interested in topic X." Useful for this person, in this situation, with this specific problem right now.

Choose a niche before you write a single word of the product.

# Chapter 3: Productize Your Knowledge

## Turn a Service into a Digital Product

There are many ways to package knowledge. Most people overthink this. Three formats account for the majority of digital product sales in the solopreneur market.

### Format 1: The Guide (PDF or ebook)

A written guide is the fastest to produce, requires no tools beyond a text editor, and has the lowest buyer resistance. For a $9 to $49 product, a well-structured 30 to 80 page guide is the right format.

Guides work when the buyer's primary need is information and a framework. They want to understand a process, have a step-by-step approach, or reference something repeatedly. A "how to" with depth and specificity.

Production time for a focused guide: 20 to 40 hours if you know your material. That is two to four weeks at 10 hours per week, built around existing freelance work.

Price ceiling for guides: around $49 to $97 for detailed, actionable content in a business or professional niche. Consumer niches may be lower.

The key to a guide that sells is structure, not volume. Buyers pay for clarity. A 35-page guide that takes someone from confused to confident on a specific topic is worth more than a 150-page dump of everything you know.

### Format 2: The Template or System

Templates solve a different problem than guides. The buyer does not want to learn. They want to execute. They want to take your system and apply it immediately.

Templates might be spreadsheets, Notion databases, swipe files, prompt libraries, design assets, workflow templates, financial models, or any other artifact a buyer can apply directly to their situation.

Templates typically sell for $9 to $39. They are faster to create than guides, require no writing ability, and can be extremely compelling because the value is immediately obvious.

A financial template that took you 10 hours to build can sell for $19 and require zero explanation if the use case is clear. A set of 50 copywriting formulas you have personally used in client work can sell for $29 as a swipe file.

The advantage of templates: almost no writing. If writing is not your strength, templates are a natural starting point.

The limitation: buyers may not implement them without guidance. Consider pairing a template with a short guide explaining how to use it.

### Format 3: The Cohort or Workshop (for later)

This is mentioned for completeness, but it is not the starting point. Live workshops and cohort-based courses require marketing infrastructure, scheduling, and a warm audience. They are the right move after you have proven demand with a guide or template.

When you eventually reach Stage 3, workshops offer high revenue per event. A 3-hour workshop at $197 with 20 attendees is $3,940 in a single session. But you need to have sold your $27 guide to 100 people first, so you have an audience to invite.

### The Product Stack

The most effective knowledge product businesses do not sell a single product. They sell a stack.

The entry product is priced at $7 to $27. Low-friction, fast to consume. Its job is to convert a stranger into a buyer. Once someone has bought from you, they are much more likely to buy again.

The core product is priced at $29 to $97. This is the main guide or course. It delivers the primary transformation you promise.

The premium product is priced at $99 to $497. A comprehensive course, a bundle, a workshop, or a done-with-you program. Only a fraction of buyers will reach this, but a small number of premium sales can equal many entry product sales.

For the purpose of this playbook, you are building the core product. The entry and premium products come after you have proven the concept.

# Chapter 4: Build the Minimum Viable Product

## The 2-Week Product Creation Sprint

The goal of the first product is not perfection. The goal is proof of concept.

Proof of concept means: at least one person who does not know you personally paid real money for your product. That is it. One sale to a stranger validates the model. Zero sales is data that something needs to change.

### Pre-Sprint Setup (Day 0 to Day 2)

Before you write anything, lock in four things.

First, the title. Your title is your promise. It tells the buyer exactly what they will be able to do after reading. Good format: "[Specific Outcome] for [Specific Person] in [Specific Timeframe or Condition]". Test two or three options and choose the one that most clearly states the outcome.

Second, the table of contents. Write the chapter structure before writing any content. Each chapter title should be a problem solved or an outcome delivered. Your table of contents is your product outline and also your sales page structure later.

Third, the price. Decide the price before you write. This affects scope. A $19 product does not need to be comprehensive. A $79 product needs to be more robust. Decide what transformation you are delivering and price accordingly. Do not underprice out of insecurity. A $19 guide is easy to dismiss. A $49 guide feels like an investment.

Fourth, the platform. You will sell on Gumroad. It takes 20 minutes to set up, charges no monthly fee, and handles payment processing. You can set up a product page before the product is finished. The URL exists. The price is set. The landing page is live. This creates commitment.

### The Writing Sprint (Day 3 to Day 12)

You have 10 days. Here is the breakdown that produces a complete guide without overwhelming you.

Days 3 to 4: Write the introduction and first two chapters. Do not edit while writing. Get ideas out. Aim for 800 to 1,200 words per session, one session per day.

Days 5 to 6: Write chapters 3 and 4. By now you have a rhythm. The first two days are the hardest because you are establishing your voice. After that it flows.

Days 7 to 8: Write chapters 5 and 6. You are over halfway.

Day 9: Write the conclusion and any appendix material: checklists, worksheets, resource lists.

Day 10: Read through the full draft. Fix obvious issues. You are not polishing. You are checking for gaps and incoherence.

Days 11 to 12: One round of editing. Run it through a spell checker. Fix structure. The goal is clarity, not literary quality. This is a business document. Direct, clear, actionable.

### Format and Delivery

Export your guide as a PDF. A properly formatted PDF looks professional and cannot be easily edited by buyers. Use a tool like Notion to PDF, Google Docs to PDF, or any word processor.

Design matters more than most people think, but a simple design beats a complex one. Black text on white background, readable font, consistent heading hierarchy, no walls of text. Use short paragraphs. Use bullet lists where appropriate. Add a table of contents with page numbers.

For the cover, a clean typographic cover is better than a cluttered one. Dark background, title in large type, author name below. Tools like Canva can produce a professional cover in 20 minutes.

Total file size should be under 5MB. Buyers do not want to wait for a large download.

### The Feedback Loop Before Launch

Send the completed guide to three to five people who match your target buyer profile. Ask them three specific questions.

One: What is the most useful part of this guide?

Two: Where did you get confused or feel like something was missing?

Three: What would you pay for this?

The answers to these questions are more valuable than any amount of solo editing. Revise based on the patterns you hear. Not every suggestion, just the patterns.

If multiple people get confused at the same point, fix that section. If nobody can identify the most useful part, the guide needs more structure. If the price they name is lower than what you planned, either reconsider the price or clarify the value more aggressively.

This feedback loop adds three to five days to your timeline but dramatically increases the quality of the final product and your confidence going into the launch.

# Chapter 5: Distribution Without an Audience

## How to Get Your First 100 Buyers Without Followers

The audience myth stops more potential founders than any other factor. The assumption is that you need a large following before you can sell a product. This is false. The first 100 buyers come from channels that require zero follower count.

### Channel 1: Community Distribution

Online communities are full of people experiencing the exact problem your product solves. Your job is to participate genuinely and make your product visible at the right moment.

Find three to five communities where your target buyer congregates. These might be subreddits, Discord servers, Facebook groups, Slack communities, or forums. Lurk for one week before posting anything. Understand what questions people ask, what frustrations they express, and what language they use.

After your observation period, start contributing genuinely useful answers to questions in your area of expertise. Do not mention your product. Just be useful. Do this for one to two weeks.

Then, when the opportunity arises naturally, mention your product. "I actually wrote a guide on this. Here is the link if you want to go deeper." Or, if the community allows promotional posts, create a value-first post that shares three to five insights from your guide, then offers the full guide at the bottom.

The key is genuine participation first. Communities detect promotional accounts immediately and reject them. Authentic contributors get trust, and trust converts.

### Channel 2: Direct Outreach

Identify 20 to 30 people who would benefit from your product. These might be connections on LinkedIn, people you have talked to at virtual events, former clients who are not in a buying relationship with you, or people who have engaged with content you have shared in the past.

Send a personal, direct message. Not a mass email blast. A personal note.

Example: "Hey [name], I know you have been working on [problem area]. I just put together a guide specifically about [specific topic] that I think would be useful to you. I am offering it to a small group of people at [price] before opening it up more broadly. Interested?"

This approach has three advantages. It requires no marketing infrastructure. It produces direct feedback from real people in your network. And it generates your first sales and testimonials, which you need for everything that comes next.

Send 30 messages. Expect 3 to 8 responses. Expect 1 to 4 purchases. That is your first real social proof.

### Channel 3: SEO-Adjacent Content

You do not need a big blog or YouTube channel to benefit from content distribution. You need a few well-placed pieces of content that rank for the specific terms your buyers search.

The approach: write one to two detailed, genuinely useful articles or posts directly related to your product topic. Post them where they can be indexed: your own site, Medium, LinkedIn articles, or a relevant publication that accepts contributor posts.

Use the same keywords your buyers would search. If your product is about freelance pricing strategy, write something titled "How to Price Your Freelance Services Without Undercharging" and include a link to your guide in the body or at the bottom.

This takes time to produce results. Three months is a reasonable expectation for initial traffic. But it works while you sleep and compounds over time without requiring any additional effort from you.

### Channel 4: Partnerships

Find three to five creators, newsletter writers, or community managers who serve your exact audience. Not competitors. Adjacent creators whose audience overlaps with yours.

Reach out with a specific proposition: "I have a guide on [topic] that I think your audience would find valuable. Would you be open to sharing it with them in exchange for [50% of sales / a free copy / a mutual mention]?"

Affiliate partnerships can produce significant early sales if the partner has even a modest audience of 500 to 2,000 engaged people. A newsletter mention to 1,500 targeted subscribers can produce 10 to 20 sales in a day. That is more than most solo marketing efforts produce in a month.

### Channel 5: Product Hunt and Similar Platforms

For digital products, Product Hunt, AppSumo Marketplace, and similar launch platforms can drive a meaningful spike of traffic and sales. These platforms exist specifically to feature new digital products.

A successful Product Hunt launch requires some advance preparation: a clear product description, a compelling tagline, screenshots or a demo, and ideally a small group of supporters who can upvote on launch day. But even a modest launch with no pre-existing network can produce 50 to 100 page views and 5 to 15 sales.

The principle across all five channels is the same. You are finding concentrations of your target buyer and putting your product in front of them in a way that is relevant and non-intrusive. You are not broadcasting to an indifferent mass audience. You are having specific, targeted conversations with the right people.

# Chapter 6: The Transition Plan

## Keep Freelance Income While Building Product Income

The most dangerous mistake in this transition is burning your primary income source before the alternative is established.

Freelance work is your runway. It pays your rent, funds your tools, and buys you time. Do not resent it. Use it. Every month of solid freelance income is another month you can build without financial pressure forcing bad decisions.

The six-month roadmap below assumes you are working full-time on freelance projects and have 10 to 15 hours per week available for building. If you have more time, accelerate. If you have less, extend the timeline but do not skip steps.

### Month 1: Research and Foundation

Week 1 to 2: Complete the skill audit from Chapter 2. Identify your product direction. Validate demand. Choose a platform.

Week 3 to 4: Set up your infrastructure. Create a Gumroad account. Create a minimal landing page for your product (even if the product is not done). Buy your domain if you do not have one. Set up a simple email capture for people who are interested before launch.

Milestone: Product direction validated, basic infrastructure live, writing started.

### Month 2: Build and Pre-Launch

Week 5 to 8: Complete the product writing sprint from Chapter 4. Get feedback from 5 people. Revise. Create the PDF. Write the Gumroad product page. Write the landing page copy.

Begin community distribution activities: find three communities, start participating. Send 20 direct outreach messages to your network.

Milestone: Product complete, on Gumroad, first 3 to 5 sales made through outreach.

### Month 3: Launch and Iterate

Week 9 to 10: Full launch. Post in communities. Launch on Product Hunt or equivalent. Contact potential affiliate partners. Share on LinkedIn.

Week 11 to 12: Review early sales data. Read every piece of customer feedback. Identify the most common entry point that produced sales and double down on it. Identify what is not working and stop doing it.

Milestone: 15 to 30 total sales. First testimonials collected and added to your product page.

### Month 4: Optimize the Sales Funnel

By month 4, you have proof of concept. Now you optimize.

The primary optimization targets are: conversion rate on your landing page, the channel that is generating the most sales, and your product page copy.

Install a free analytics tool on your landing page. Track where buyers are coming from. The channel that converts best gets more of your energy. The ones that produce nothing get less or none.

Write two to three new pieces of SEO content related to your product. Start capturing email addresses from anyone who downloads your product. Email is your most valuable long-term asset. A list of 300 people who bought your product once is worth more than 3,000 random followers on any social platform.

Milestone: Consistent 5 to 10 sales per month. Email list growing. One SEO article published.

### Month 5: Stack Products

Once you have sold your first product 30 or more times, you understand your buyer. You know what questions they ask after they finish the guide. You know what they need next.

Build the adjacent product. If your first product was about finding freelance clients, the second product might be about pricing or contracts or client communication. The second product should sell naturally to the same buyer who bought the first.

Use your existing buyer list to pre-sell. Email the 30 to 50 people who bought your first product. Tell them you are building a follow-up guide and offer it at a 40 percent discount to the first people who commit. This funds the next product before you write it and tells you instantly whether there is demand.

Milestone: Second product concept validated. Pre-sales made. Writing started.

### Month 6: Evaluate and Plan the Full Transition

At month 6, you have two products, a small email list, proof of what channels work, and a clearer picture of what this business can become.

Now you run the honest numbers. How much did the products earn in month 6? What is the trend? At the current growth rate, when does product income match your freelance baseline?

Most people reach $500 to $1,500 per month in product income by month 6 if they executed the steps above. Some reach more. This is not enough to quit freelancing, but it is enough to see the trajectory clearly.

The decision point is not "when can I quit freelancing" but "when is product income stable enough that I can take fewer freelance projects and invest that time back into the product business?" For most people, that decision comes at month 9 to 12.

# Chapter 7: Scaling to $5,000 per Month

## What the Math Actually Looks Like

$5,000 per month in digital product income is achievable for most people who commit to this path. It is not guaranteed and it is not fast. But the math is not complicated.

### The Revenue Model

There are essentially three ways to reach $5,000 per month in product income.

Option A: Volume at low price. 500 units at $10 per month. This requires either a large audience or very strong SEO. Difficult without an existing platform.

Option B: Mid-price with moderate volume. 100 units at $50 per month. Achievable with consistent community distribution, one partnership per month, and 2 to 3 SEO articles indexed. This is the realistic path for most people in the first year.

Option C: Low volume at higher price. 20 units at $250 per month. Requires a premium offering and a warmer audience. Achievable in year two after establishing credibility.

The most realistic path to $5,000 per month in year one is Option B: a combination of a $39 to $59 core guide, a $97 to $197 bundle or course, and a free lead magnet that feeds both. With 60 sales per month across the catalog at an average order value of $55, you reach $3,300. Add a small number of premium sales and you approach $5,000.

### Pricing Strategy

Most solopreneurs underprice. The fear is that nobody will pay. The reality is that a $47 product faces less friction than a $17 product in many business niches, because buyers associate price with seriousness. A $17 product reads as a casual tip. A $47 product reads as a real investment.

Test prices. Gumroad lets you change prices at any time. Start at what feels slightly uncomfortable. If conversion rates are strong, you have room to raise. If traffic is high but conversion is low, try dropping.

The $24 to $49 range is the sweet spot for most first-time guides in business niches. If your guide delivers a transformation that saves the buyer 10 hours or $500, pricing it at $29 is a reasonable trade.

As you build a catalog, bundle your products. Three guides at $29 each become a bundle at $67. Bundles increase average order value without requiring new content. A customer who found you through your first guide and sees a bundle that includes three related products is likely to upgrade.

### Building the Email List as the Scaling Asset

The email list is the most important asset you will build. Social platforms change algorithms and throttle reach. The email list is yours.

Every product you sell should result in an email subscriber. Use a tool like MailerLite or ConvertKit. Both have free tiers that cover your needs until you have a few hundred subscribers.

Your list allows you to generate revenue on demand. When you launch a new product, your first move is emailing the list. A list of 500 engaged buyers who purchased from you will convert at 3 to 8 percent on a well-crafted launch email. That is 15 to 40 sales before you do any external marketing.

At 1,000 subscribers with a 5 percent conversion rate on a $47 product, a single launch email generates $2,350. This is why the email list is worth more than any social following.

### The Multiple Products Math

By the end of year one, aim to have:

Product 1: Entry guide at $19 to $29. Volume driver. Easy to buy. Converts browsers to buyers.

Product 2: Core guide at $39 to $59. Main revenue product. Full depth on your primary topic.

Product 3: Bundle at $69 to $97. Products 1 and 2 plus a bonus template or worksheet. Higher AOV from the same traffic.

Product 4: Premium option at $147 to $297. Could be a more comprehensive course, a done-with-you session, or a bundle with a live call. Bought by 5 to 10 percent of your audience.

With this stack and 300 monthly visitors to your landing pages, converting at 4 percent, you have 12 transactions per month. At an average order value of $55, that is $660. Modest, but real.

Scale the traffic to 1,500 monthly visitors through SEO, partnerships, and ongoing community contribution, and the same math produces $3,300 per month. Add the email list launch campaigns and you approach $5,000.

The timeline is 12 to 18 months for most people who execute consistently. It requires patience and consistency more than any particular insight or tactic.

# Appendix B: Pricing Calculator Worksheet

Use this worksheet to find the right price for your product.

**Step 1: Define the transformation**
What can the buyer do after using your product that they could not do before?

Write it in one sentence: _______________________________

**Step 2: Calculate the value to the buyer**
What is the outcome worth in time or money?

Estimated hours saved: _______ x buyer's hourly rate ($______) = $________

Estimated money earned or saved from outcome: $________

**Step 3: Price relative to value**
A standard digital product priced at 5 to 15 percent of the value it delivers converts well.

If your product saves $500: price range is $25 to $75.
If your product saves $1,000: price range is $50 to $150.
If your product earns $2,000: price range is $100 to $300.

**Step 4: Check against market**
Find 5 competing products. Average their prices: $________

Your target price: $_________

**Step 5: Reality check**
At your target price, how many sales per month would you need to reach $500? ________
Does that number feel achievable given your planned distribution channels?

If yes: set the price. If no: either raise the price or reconsider the scope.
