# The Creator's Sponsorship Media Kit Guide

**By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin**

*Build a media kit that lands brand deals even with a small audience.*

---

## Introduction: Brands Do Not Pay for Followers

Somewhere along the way, creators got a bad idea lodged in their heads. That sponsorships are a reward for large audiences. That brand deals are what happen after you hit 100K, or 500K, or a million.

That idea is wrong. Brands pay for access to specific, engaged audiences who buy things. Not for raw follower counts. Not for numbers on a dashboard. For buyers.

A creator with 8,000 highly targeted followers who buys a specific type of product is more valuable to the right brand than a creator with 200,000 general lifestyle followers. The creator with 8,000 can charge more, deliver better results, and build a longer relationship because their audience actually trusts them.

Your media kit is how you communicate that value clearly, quickly, and credibly. It is a sales document. Not a portfolio. Not a resume. A concise argument for why partnering with you is a good business decision for a specific type of brand.

This guide shows you how to build that document and how to use it.

---

## Chapter One: What a Media Kit Actually Is

A media kit is a one to three page document that tells a brand who you are, who your audience is, what you have done, and what you offer. It is designed to be read in under three minutes by a marketing director who is reviewing a hundred creator pitches.

It is not a scroll through all your metrics. It is not a biography. It is a targeted argument built for one kind of reader: a brand that has a budget and needs to decide whether you are worth part of it.

### The Four Functions of a Media Kit

**First impression.** Before any call, before any negotiation, the media kit creates the initial impression of you as a professional. Creators who send a polished kit get calls. Creators who send no kit or a messy one do not.

**Credibility anchor.** The kit gives the brand something to reference. Numbers, past partners, testimonials. It converts your credibility from abstract to concrete.

**Efficiency tool.** A well-built kit answers the three questions every brand has before booking a creator. Who is their audience? What kind of engagement do they get? What do they charge? If your kit answers all three clearly, the conversation moves immediately to deal terms instead of basic qualification.

**Negotiation anchor.** When you send a rate card as part of your kit, you set the starting point of any negotiation. Creators who do not send rates default to whatever the brand offers, which is almost always the lowest number in their budget. Your rate card shifts the conversation.

### What Brands Are Actually Looking For

When a brand reviews a creator's media kit, they ask three questions. Is this person's audience the same person we are trying to reach? Does their audience actually trust them? What results have they produced?

Every element of your media kit should answer one of those three questions. If an element does not answer one of them, cut it.

Brand decision-makers are not looking for creative portfolios. They are looking for evidence. Evidence that your audience is real, that your audience pays attention, and that when you recommend something, your audience responds.

## Chapter Two: The Core Elements of a Media Kit

### Your Headline and Bio

The first thing a brand sees should tell them in one sentence who you are, who you serve, and what you do. Not a creative bio. Not your creator origin story. A positioning statement.

"I help [specific audience] do [specific thing] through [format]."

Then one paragraph of supporting context. How long you have been creating. What platforms you are on. Why you are credible on this topic. Keep it under 75 words.

Do not lead with your follower count. Lead with your audience identity. "I create content for first-generation wealth builders in the US" is more compelling than "I have 12,000 Instagram followers."

### Audience Demographics

This section is critical and most creators underdeliver on it. Do not just list follower counts. Give the brand a picture of the actual human being who follows you.

Include: age range (primary and secondary), gender split, top geographic locations (at least top 3 countries or cities), income level if you know it, and the one sentence that describes your audience's worldview or primary interest.

If you have audience survey data, include highlights. "72 percent of my audience has purchased an online course in the last 12 months" is worth more than any demographic chart.

You can collect this data from your platform analytics. Instagram Insights, YouTube Analytics, and Twitter Analytics all provide demographic breakdowns. Export what you have. Present the data that best matches the brands you want to work with.

### Platform Statistics

List your metrics for each active platform. For each platform include: total followers or subscribers, average reach per post, engagement rate (likes plus comments plus shares divided by followers, expressed as a percentage), and monthly impressions or views.

Present your best numbers, but do not fabricate. Brands verify. A single discrepancy kills the relationship and your reputation.

Context helps. If your engagement rate is 6 percent on a 10,000 follower Instagram, note that the platform average is 1 to 3 percent. This transforms "6 percent" from a number into evidence.

If you have an email list, include it here. Email typically converts at 3 to 5 times the rate of social media. A 2,000 person email list is worth more to many brands than 10,000 Instagram followers.

### Past Partnerships

If you have done brand deals before, list them. The brand name, the type of deliverable, and if possible, one result (clicks generated, promo codes redeemed, products sold).

If you have not done brand deals yet, this section becomes "featured in" or "mentioned by." Press mentions, podcast appearances, collaborations with other creators, or any affiliation that signals credibility.

Even one small brand partnership, executed well, becomes the anchor that makes future brands comfortable saying yes. Your first deal is the hardest. The section does not need to be long. A few logos and a one-line result from each is enough.

### Rate Card

Most creators fear putting prices in their kit. They think keeping rates vague gives them negotiating flexibility.

It does not. It gives brands an excuse to offer whatever they want.

A rate card is a professional document that sets the floor of negotiations. It signals that you know your value, you have thought about it, and you are not going to pretend you have not.

Structure your rate card by deliverable type. One Instagram feed post. One Instagram Story set (3 to 5 slides). One YouTube integration (15 to 60 seconds in video). One dedicated email to your list. One TikTok video. Set a price for each.

Your initial rates should be defensible based on your engagement data. A simple formula: $100 per 1,000 engaged audience members per deliverable. This is a starting point, not a rule. Adjust based on the specificity of your niche and the premium value of your audience.

## Chapter Three: Designing a Kit That Gets Read

A media kit that is hard to look at does not get read. Design is not decoration. It is communication.

### The One-Page vs Three-Page Decision

For most creators under 50,000 followers, a one-page media kit is the right call. It forces you to be selective. Brands skim. One clear page beats three cluttered pages every time.

Once you have 3 or more strong past partnerships, a second page becomes worth adding. Once you have distinct audience segments across platforms, a third page for platform details makes sense.

Start with one page. Expand as your credibility grows.

### Visual Design Principles

Dark background or white background, both work. What does not work: inconsistent fonts, clip art, colors that do not relate to your brand, walls of text.

Use one font family. One brand color. Clean alignment. White space is your friend. A media kit that breathes is easier to process than one that is packed.

Include a professional photo. Brands are entering a relationship. They want to see who you are.

Your kit should look like your content. If you create clean, minimal content, your kit should be clean and minimal. If your brand is warm and colorful, your kit should reflect that. Brand alignment builds trust even in a document.

### Tools for Building Your Kit

Canva has dozens of media kit templates that require only content substitution. For most creators, Canva is the right choice. Fast to build, easy to update, exports to PDF cleanly.

If you use Notion, there are media kit templates there as well. A Notion kit has the advantage of being live, meaning you can update your numbers and the link you share always shows current data.

PowerPoint or Google Slides with a single slide per section also works. Export as PDF before sending.

Whatever tool you use, export as PDF. Not a link to a Google Doc. Not a Word file. A PDF. It preserves formatting, it is professional, and it opens in every browser without friction.

## Chapter Four: Finding Brands to Pitch

Having a media kit means nothing until it is in front of brands who might buy. Finding those brands is an active process, not a passive one.

### Mapping the Brands in Your Ecosystem

Start with the products and services you already use and recommend organically. Your audience sees you use them. You have genuine affinity for them. Pitching brands you already believe in is faster, more authentic, and easier to execute.

Make a list. Every brand whose product you have mentioned in content, in casual conversation, or in your own life. These are your tier-one prospects.

Next, make a list of brands who advertise to your audience through other channels. The brands buying Instagram ads targeting your demographic. The brands sponsoring newsletters your audience reads. These brands are already spending. You are pitching a different channel, not convincing them sponsorships work.

### Finding the Right Contact

Do not send your media kit to info@ or through a brand's general contact form. Find the specific person who handles influencer or creator partnerships.

LinkedIn is the fastest path. Search the company name plus "influencer marketing," "brand partnerships," or "social media." The specific role title varies by company size. At small companies, it is often a marketing manager or founder. At large companies, it is a dedicated partnerships or influencer team.

If you cannot find the exact person, a marketing director or head of social is the correct escalation point. They will route you or respond directly.

Email is better than DM for initial outreach. It signals professionalism and gives the brand a document trail for their internal approval process.

### The Pitch Email Formula

Your outreach email should be four components, nothing more.

One sentence establishing who you are and why you are relevant to them. One sentence of audience credibility (the specific number or fact that makes them lean forward). One sentence stating the proposal. One sentence asking for a next step.

Attach the media kit as a PDF. Do not paste it into the email body.

Subject lines that work: "[Your Name] x [Brand Name] Partnership" or "Media Kit: [Your Platform] for [Brand's Target Audience]." Direct is better than clever.

Do not write five paragraphs of flattery before getting to the point. Decision-makers at brands get dozens of these emails. Respect their time by getting to the ask in the first two sentences.

## Chapter Five: Negotiating and Closing Brand Deals

Getting interest from a brand is not the end. It is the start of a conversation that requires skill to navigate well.

### Anchor High, Concede Strategically

Your first number should be above what you expect to accept. Not offensively high. Professionally high. A price that is ambitious but defensible.

When a brand counters, do not immediately cut your rate. Counter with a reduction in deliverables instead. "I can do that for $500, or for your $350 budget I can deliver one feed post instead of two." This preserves the value of your full offer while finding a middle ground.

Never reduce your rate without reducing your deliverables. Discounting without removing scope teaches brands that your prices are negotiable down, which becomes the pattern in every future deal.

### Package Deals

Brands prefer predictability. A one-off post is a transaction. A 3-month partnership is a relationship. Three to six month packages with multiple deliverables per month are easier to budget, create more consistent results for the brand, and pay you more total.

Offer your package rate at a 10 to 15 percent discount versus per-post pricing to incentivize commitment. Require a contract for anything over one month.

### What to Include in Your Contract

You do not need a lawyer for basic creator contracts. But you do need one. Key terms to include: deliverable description (platform, format, length, posting date), usage rights (how long the brand can use your content in their own ads), exclusivity clause (if you are agreeing not to promote competitors, charge more for this), revision policy (how many rounds of edits are included), and payment terms (deposit required, balance due upon delivery or by a specific date).

Exclusivity and usage rights are where most creators give away money. Exclusivity in your niche for 30 days is worth an extra 25 to 50 percent. Allowing a brand to run your content as paid ads is worth an additional 20 to 30 percent. These are not arbitrary numbers. They reflect real value you are providing.

### Getting Paid

Require a 50 percent deposit before you create anything. This protects you from brands who ghost after delivery and filters out brands who are not serious.

Net 30 payment terms are standard, meaning the remaining balance is due 30 days after delivery. Net 15 is better if you can negotiate it. Payment upon delivery is ideal for smaller deals.

If a brand is slow to pay, a polite but firm follow-up on day 31 is appropriate. Three follow-up emails with 7-day intervals, then hand it to a collections service or small claims if the amount is significant. Do not create content for a brand that has not paid for previous work.

## Chapter Six: Building a Sustainable Sponsorship Pipeline

One brand deal is a transaction. A pipeline is a business.

### The Outreach Cadence

Sponsorship income becomes reliable when you treat outreach like a sales job. Set a weekly goal for new brand contacts. Ten new pitches per week is a reasonable pace for a creator building toward $2,000 to $5,000 per month in sponsorship income.

Track every outreach in a simple spreadsheet. Brand name, contact, date of first outreach, last follow-up date, status, and deal value if closed. This system prevents leads from falling through, shows you where deals are stalling, and forces you to follow up systematically.

Most deals require three to five follow-up touches over 30 to 90 days. The creator who follows up twice is not as persistent as the brand's inbox. The creator who follows up seven times in 10 days is annoying. The middle path, one follow-up every 10 to 14 days with a brief update or new piece of social proof, converts the most.

### Raising Your Rates Over Time

Update your media kit every quarter. Every update is an opportunity to adjust pricing. When your engagement rate rises or your audience grows, your rates rise with it. When you have 3 strong past partnerships, your rates rise again because you have social proof to back the ask.

Your rates should always feel slightly ahead of where your audience is. Brands budget in cycles. They need time to approve spending. Your current rate card will often be evaluated against next quarter's budget, not this week's.

### Turning One Deal Into Many

Every brand deal you close is a case study. Document the results. Screenshot the analytics. Ask the brand contact for a quote about their experience working with you.

A good result creates two assets. First, a result to add to your media kit. Second, an introduction to other brands in the same category. Ask every brand contact you work well with: "Are there other brands in your network who might benefit from working together?" A warm referral from an existing brand partner skips the entire cold outreach process.

The most successful creator sponsorship programs are built primarily on referrals and repeat clients. Get the first deal right and it compounds.

## Conclusion: Start Before the Kit Is Perfect

The perfect media kit is the one a brand actually receives.

Most creators spend weeks polishing a kit instead of sending it. They want one more month of growth, one more partnership, one more improvement before it is ready. This is avoidance dressed up as preparation.

Build your first version in a weekend. Send it to 10 brands in week two. Improve it based on what you learn from the responses. The real education happens in the pitching, not the polishing.

Your audience is already valuable. Your content is already working. The media kit is the document that converts that value into income. Build it. Send it.

## Media Kit Launch Checklist

- [ ] Write your creator positioning statement (one sentence)
- [ ] Collect demographics from all active platforms
- [ ] Calculate engagement rate for each platform
- [ ] Document your top geographic markets
- [ ] List every past brand partnership (or press mention if none yet)
- [ ] Set rates for each deliverable type
- [ ] Write your email newsletter statistics if applicable
- [ ] Choose your media kit design tool (Canva recommended)
- [ ] Add a professional photo
- [ ] Export as PDF and test that it opens correctly
- [ ] Build your tier-one brand prospect list (10 brands you already use)
- [ ] Find specific contacts on LinkedIn for each brand
- [ ] Write your template pitch email
- [ ] Set up your outreach tracking spreadsheet
- [ ] Send your first 5 pitches before the end of the week
- [ ] Schedule quarterly media kit review dates
