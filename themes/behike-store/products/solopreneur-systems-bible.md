## Introduction: Systems Are the Only Advantage You Actually Have

Most people building one-person businesses are running on talent, luck, and adrenaline. The talent works until they get busy. The luck runs out. The adrenaline crashes at 2am when they realize they have three client deadlines, no content scheduled, and zero idea what their revenue looks like this month.

The problem is not effort. Everyone building something is putting in effort. The problem is that effort without structure produces chaos that scales. The more you grow on chaos, the worse the chaos gets.

Here is what nobody tells you at the beginning: a solopreneur does not have the advantages of a team. No specialist to hand things off to. No manager to catch dropped balls. No system administrator keeping everything running while you sleep. You are the CEO, the content creator, the sales team, the customer support, and the finance department simultaneously.

The only thing that makes that sustainable is systems.

A system is not a productivity hack. It is not a Notion template you fill out once and forget. A system is a documented, repeatable process that produces consistent output regardless of your energy level that day. On a great day, the system runs fast. On a bad day, the system runs anyway.

This book contains eight core systems for running a one-person business. Each chapter covers one system completely: the logic behind it, the exact steps, the tools that run it, and the decisions you need to make once so you never make them again under pressure.

There is also a bonus section with twelve templates for Notion and Obsidian that match the workflows in every chapter.

These are not theoretical frameworks. Every system in this book came from the actual work of building a content-driven solopreneur business from scratch. The content creation system was built because I was spending four hours writing posts I never scheduled. The product creation system was built because I had ideas I kept not shipping. The finance system was built after realizing I had no idea what my real take-home was after tools, subscriptions, and taxes.

Build the systems first. Everything else becomes easier after that.

## Chapter 1: The Content Creation System

### Idea to Published in 90 Minutes

The average creator spends more time thinking about what to post than actually posting. They open a blank document, stare at it, open Twitter for "inspiration," watch 40 minutes of content, feel guilty, and close the laptop. The next day they repeat this.

The content creation system solves this by separating the three phases of content work that should never happen at the same time: capture, creation, and distribution.

When these three phases are collapsed into a single sitting, the brain is asked to do three incompatible things simultaneously. Divergent thinking (coming up with ideas), convergent thinking (writing and editing), and execution (formatting and posting). These use different cognitive modes. Running all three at once is the reason a "quick post" takes three hours.

**Phase 1: The Capture System (runs continuously)**

Ideas do not come when you sit down to write. They come in the shower, during walks, in the middle of a conversation. The capture system catches them before they disappear.

The tool does not matter. What matters is that it is always accessible and frictionless. One tap to open, one tap to record. Voice memo, quick note, text file. The format is irrelevant. The discipline is: capture now, process later.

Every captured idea goes into a single inbox. Not organized. Not formatted. Raw.

Process the inbox once a day, not throughout the day. Move ideas from raw captures into one of three buckets:
- Ready: strong enough to write from today
- Develop: needs more thinking, add one line of elaboration
- Archive: no longer relevant, delete without guilt

The goal of Phase 1 is a never-empty queue of Ready ideas.

**Phase 2: The Creation Block (90 minutes, three times per week)**

The creation block is sacred. It is not a "when I have time" slot. It is a scheduled, protected block on the calendar that runs regardless of how inspired you feel.

The creation block structure:
- 0-10 min: Pick one idea from the Ready queue. Write the thesis sentence. The thesis is the one claim the post makes. Not the topic, the claim.
- 10-40 min: Write the first draft without editing. The internal editor is turned off. Bad sentences are better than no sentences.
- 40-60 min: Edit. Cut everything that does not serve the thesis. The rule is ruthless: if removing it does not weaken the main argument, remove it.
- 60-80 min: Format for the platform. Platform formatting is not aesthetic preference, it is reading psychology. What reads well on LinkedIn reads terribly on Twitter.
- 80-90 min: Schedule or publish.

Three 90-minute creation blocks per week produce 12 pieces of content per month. That is enough for a serious presence on any platform.

**Phase 3: The Distribution System**

Covered in Chapter 4. The creation system hands off to the distribution system at the moment of publishing.

**The Content Operating Rhythm**

Monday, Wednesday, Friday: creation blocks.
Tuesday, Thursday: engagement blocks (30 minutes, respond to comments, reply to DMs, leave genuine comments on relevant accounts).
Saturday: content audit (what performed, what did not, update the queue accordingly).
Sunday: week ahead prep (confirm next week's ideas are in the Ready queue).

This is not a content calendar. It is a content operating rhythm. The difference is that a content calendar tells you what to post on Tuesday March 14. An operating rhythm tells you what type of thinking happens on which day of every week. The rhythm survives weeks when you cannot plan in advance.

**Tools for the Content System**

- Capture: Apple Notes or Telegram saved messages for frictionless capture
- Queue management: Notion database or Obsidian vault with three status labels
- Writing: plain text editor, not the publishing platform
- Scheduling: Buffer, Typefully, or native platform scheduling depending on channel

The quality of your content system is measured by one thing: the average time between idea and published post. If that number is more than two days, the system has a bottleneck. Find it, fix it.

## Chapter 2: The Product Creation System

### Research to Launch in 7 Days

Most solopreneurs either never launch a product or spend six months building one. The first group is afraid of rejection. The second group is afraid of shipping before it is perfect. Both are the same fear wearing different costumes.

The seven-day product creation system forces both problems into the open and solves them structurally.

**Day 1: The Problem Statement**

A product does not start with a topic. It starts with a problem. The problem must be specific, painful, and underserved by existing solutions.

The research process for Day 1:
- Spend 30 minutes reading comments on content in your niche. Look for recurring complaints, questions, frustrations. These are unmet needs wearing the clothing of comments.
- Write three candidate problem statements. Each one follows this format: "[Specific person] struggles with [specific situation] because [root cause], and existing solutions fail because [gap]."
- Pick the problem with the strongest combination of: frequency (how often people mention it), pain level (emotional intensity in how they describe it), and gap (how poorly current solutions address it).

**Day 2: The Scope Decision**

A product's scope determines everything about its success. Too narrow and nobody buys it. Too broad and nobody trusts it does one thing well.

The scope decision tool: Can you solve the stated problem completely in under 100 pages, under 3 hours of video, or under one implemented system? If yes, the scope is right. If no, narrow it until the answer is yes.

Write the table of contents before writing any content. The table of contents is the product. If the table of contents does not solve the problem from chapter one, the content will not either.

**Day 3: The Outline and Research**

Expand each chapter heading into five bullet points. These are not section summaries. They are the five things a reader needs to know to fully understand that section. Every bullet either teaches something, demonstrates something, or removes an objection.

Research means: for every claim you plan to make, find one concrete example, one data point, or one personal experience that makes it real. Abstraction without specificity is the most common failure mode in digital products.

**Days 4-5: The First Draft**

Two days, two to three hours each. Write fast. The editing instinct will try to slow you down. Override it. Write ugly. Every section should have a draft by the end of Day 5.

The only rule during drafting: never write to fill space. If a section is complete in 400 words, it is complete. A shorter product that delivers fully is more valuable than a longer product that pads.

**Day 6: The Edit Pass**

One edit pass, top to bottom. The editing criteria:
- Does this section solve the stated problem from Day 1?
- Is every claim concrete and specific?
- Can anything be cut without weakening the argument?
- Does the voice stay consistent throughout?

Do not add content during the edit pass. Cut and sharpen only.

**Day 7: The Launch Infrastructure**

The landing page, the payment link, the delivery mechanism. In sequence:
- Write the landing page copy first (the template is in Chapter 3 of this book)
- Create the Gumroad or Lemon Squeezy product listing
- Test the purchase flow yourself before announcing it
- Send to three people who match the target buyer and ask for one line of feedback

Launch means: the product is live, the link works, and you have told at least 50 people it exists.

The seven-day system is not about producing a masterpiece in seven days. It is about proving the product exists in the market and getting real feedback before you spend months building something nobody wanted.

## Chapter 3: The Sales System

### Landing Page, Email Sequence, Follow-Up

Most solopreneurs lose sales not because their product is weak, but because their sales infrastructure is missing or broken. A strong product with weak sales infrastructure underperforms every time. A mediocre product with strong sales infrastructure will outsell it.

This chapter covers the three components of a complete sales system: the landing page, the email sequence, and the follow-up protocol.

**The Landing Page**

A landing page has one job: convert a curious visitor into a buyer. Everything on the page either moves toward that conversion or it does not belong there.

The structure that consistently converts:

*Section 1: The Hero Block*
One headline. One subheadline. One CTA button. The headline states what the product does for the reader, not what it is. "The operating system for your one-person business" outperforms "A business planning workbook" every time. The subheadline handles the "for who" and the "by when."

*Section 2: The Problem Block*
Three to five short paragraphs describing the exact situation the buyer is in right now. Not the solution, the problem. Read this section and the buyer should feel precisely understood. The instinct is to rush to the solution. Resist it. The longer the buyer feels understood, the more they trust that your solution is real.

*Section 3: The Solution Introduction*
What the product is and what it does. One paragraph. Not a feature list yet. This paragraph answers: "How does this solve what we just described?" in one specific, concrete sentence.

*Section 4: The Chapter / Feature Overview*
Show what is inside. Use specific chapter titles or module names, not vague benefit statements. "Chapter 4: The distribution system (one post becomes 10 assets)" is better than "Learn how to maximize your content reach."

*Section 5: The Who It Is For Block*
Three to five bullet points, each starting with "This is for you if..." The specificity here is what builds qualified buyers and filters out buyers who will ask for refunds. Both serve you.

*Section 6: Social Proof*
Testimonials, if you have them. If you do not yet have them, use specific descriptions of the outcome instead. "After using this system, posts that used to take four hours took ninety minutes" is a verifiable outcome description. It functions similarly to a testimonial while being honest.

*Section 7: The Price Block*
State the price clearly. No anchoring tricks. No artificial crossed-out prices unless you are running a genuine limited sale. Explain what is included in one short list. One CTA button.

*Section 8: The FAQ*
Five questions. Not fake questions that are really just more marketing. The questions that the actual buyer is actually asking before they decide. Address objections directly and honestly.

**The Email Sequence**

Every buyer should enter an email sequence. The purpose of the sequence is not to sell immediately. It is to deliver value, build trust, and make the next offer feel earned.

A five-email product welcome sequence:
- Email 1 (Day 0, immediate): Delivery + one quick win. The product link plus a single action they can take in the next ten minutes that will show them the product works.
- Email 2 (Day 2): Context. Why you built this. The story behind the problem, not a pitch.
- Email 3 (Day 5): Depth. Go deeper on one concept from the product. Show that you think about this more than the book contains.
- Email 4 (Day 8): Social proof or case study. A result from using the system. Specific numbers where possible.
- Email 5 (Day 12): The next step. What do they do after they finish this product? This is where the next offer, or an invitation to reply, lives.

**The Follow-Up Protocol**

People who visit the sales page and do not buy are not lost. They need follow-up. The protocol:

- If using email capture on the page: send a three-email sequence over ten days with increasing specificity about the problem and solution.
- If not capturing emails: retarget via content. Post about the exact problems the product solves. The people who were on the fence will see it.
- Price objections are handled by demonstrating value density, not lowering the price. Show the depth per dollar.

The entire sales system runs on its own once it is built. Build it once for each product, then let it run.

## Chapter 4: The Distribution System

### One Post Becomes Ten Assets

Creating content is expensive. It costs time, cognitive energy, and creative capacity. Publishing it to one platform and moving on is the most common and most expensive mistake in content marketing.

The distribution system does not create more content. It maximizes the yield from content that already exists.

**The Hub and Spoke Model**

One piece of long-form content sits at the center. Everything else is a derivative. The long-form content can be a newsletter issue, a YouTube video, a blog post, or a podcast episode. The format matters less than the depth.

From one long-form piece, you extract:
- 3 short-form posts (the key insights, stated differently for Twitter/Instagram/LinkedIn)
- 1 carousel post (the step-by-step structure of the piece, if it has one)
- 1 quote graphic (the strongest single sentence from the piece)
- 1 short-form video script (the core argument condensed to 60-90 seconds)
- 1 email newsletter version (the long-form with an intro and a CTA added)
- 1 repurposed thread (the argument broken into tweet-sized chunks)
- 1 Pinterest pin with the title and a key visual

That is nine assets from one piece of long-form work. The total additional creation time is 45-60 minutes if the system is running correctly.

**The Distribution Queue**

Not all assets go out the same day. The distribution queue spreads them over seven to ten days, which creates the impression of consistent daily activity without requiring daily content creation.

Queue structure for one long-form piece:
- Day 1: Long-form publishes on primary platform
- Day 2: Short-form post 1 (strongest insight)
- Day 3: Email newsletter version
- Day 4: Short-form post 2 (contrarian angle or counterintuitive point)
- Day 5: Carousel or thread
- Day 6: Short-form post 3 (practical application)
- Day 7-10: Quote graphic, video, Pinterest

**Platform-Specific Adaptation**

Repurposing is not copy-pasting. A LinkedIn post and a Twitter post about the same idea should feel native to each platform.

LinkedIn: Professional framing, personal story opening, numbered or bulleted takeaways, a question at the end to drive comments.
Twitter: Compressed. The thesis in tweet one. The evidence in tweets two through five. The implication in the last tweet.
Instagram: Visual-first. The caption supports the image or carousel, not the other way around.
Email: Conversational. Write to one person. No formal headers. One idea, one CTA.

**Measuring Distribution Effectiveness**

The metric is not reach. The metric is "click-through to something that moves the business." That means email signups from short-form posts, landing page visits from email, product sales from all channels.

Review these numbers weekly (the framework for this is in Chapter 7). The content that drives traffic and the content that drives engagement are often different pieces. Both matter, but they serve different functions in the system.

## Chapter 5: The Client Acquisition System

### For Service-Based Income

Products are recurring. Services are higher-margin but require a different acquisition system. Many solopreneurs who build a product business also run a service component, either by design or because clients find them through their content.

The client acquisition system makes that inbound flow predictable.

**The Authority Content Stack**

Service clients come from trust. Trust comes from demonstrated expertise in public. The authority content stack is the specific content mix that converts content consumers into service buyers.

The stack:
- 20% of content demonstrates your process (not just your results, but how you think and work)
- 30% demonstrates your worldview (what you believe about the problem your clients face)
- 30% delivers direct value without any pitch (the content that gets shared)
- 20% shows your personality and reality (the human behind the expertise)

This ratio is not arbitrary. The process content speaks to buyers who are already almost decided. The worldview content speaks to buyers who are evaluating whether they agree with your approach. The value content speaks to everyone and brings new people in. The personality content creates the parasocial trust that converts.

**The Client Offer Structure**

Before acquiring clients, the offer needs to be clear. Vague offers attract vague inquiries that waste time.

The offer document covers:
- Who it is for (specific enough that the wrong person opts out themselves)
- What the deliverable is (concrete output, not "I will help you with your strategy")
- What the timeline is (specific, not "we will work together until it is done")
- What it costs (stated upfront, not after a discovery call for routine work)
- How to start (one clear next step)

**The Inbound System**

Inbound means clients find you. This happens through:
- Content that demonstrates expertise (the authority stack above)
- A clear "work with me" page that converts interest into inquiry
- Past client results in public (case studies, testimonials, or described outcomes)

A simple inbound system: all content links to one place (a landing page, a Linktree, a bio link). That place links to the work-with-me page. The work-with-me page has an application form or a booking link. That is the entire funnel.

**The Outbound System**

Outbound means you initiate contact. This gets a bad reputation because most outbound is poorly targeted, generic, and transactional.

Effective outbound for solopreneurs:
- Target by observable signal, not by broad category. The signal is: someone just posted about the exact problem you solve. They are actively thinking about it. This is the right time to reach out.
- The outreach message adds value before making any pitch. A reaction to what they posted that shows you understood the nuance of their situation. Not a compliment, an observation.
- The ask is small: a fifteen-minute conversation, not a commitment.

**Qualification and Closing**

Not every inquiry becomes a client. The qualification conversation covers four things:
- What specifically is not working right now
- What they have already tried
- What success looks like at the end of the engagement
- What their timeline and budget are

If the answers align with what you do and what you charge, the next step is a proposal. If they do not, refer them elsewhere or decline cleanly. Bad client fits cost more than they pay.

The close is simple: a written proposal that recaps what you heard on the call, states the deliverable and price, and asks one question: "Does this capture what you are looking for?"

## Chapter 6: The Finance System

### Revenue Tracking, Reinvestment Rules

Most solopreneurs have a general sense of whether money is coming in but no real understanding of their actual financial position. They know their gross revenue. They do not know their net income after tools, subscriptions, tax withholding, and operating costs.

Running a business without financial clarity is the equivalent of driving without a dashboard. You might make it. But you will not know you are in trouble until the engine stops.

**The Monthly Finance Audit**

Once a month, not once a quarter, not when tax season forces it. Monthly. It takes 30 minutes.

The finance audit checklist:
- Total gross revenue this month (all sources, all platforms)
- Total operating costs this month (every recurring tool, subscription, and service)
- Net operating income (gross minus costs)
- Tax withholding (set aside 25-30% of net income for taxes if operating as self-employed)
- Actual take-home (net income after tax withholding)
- Month-over-month revenue trend (is the number growing, flat, or declining)
- Highest-revenue product or service (what is actually driving the business)
- Lowest-revenue product or service (what is consuming attention without returning it)

This data goes into one spreadsheet or Notion table. The format is not important. The habit is.

**The Reinvestment Framework**

When revenue grows, the instinct is to buy tools and upgrade subscriptions. This instinct usually wrong. Most tools do not create leverage. They create the feeling of leverage while adding operational complexity.

The reinvestment framework uses three categories:

*Revenue-generating reinvestment:* Tools, ads, or contractors that have a clear, measurable line to revenue. An email marketing platform, if you are actively running email campaigns, is revenue-generating. A project management tool you opened twice is not.

*Time-saving reinvestment:* Things that genuinely buy back hours you would otherwise spend on low-value work. Virtual assistant, automation tools, template creation. The test: would I pay this cost if my revenue halved? If no, it is optional.

*Learning reinvestment:* Courses, books, events, coaching. This is legitimate reinvestment in your most important asset. The rule here is: one major learning investment per quarter, not one per month. Accumulating courses without completing them is consumption, not investment.

**Revenue Diversification Rules**

A single-revenue-stream solopreneur business is fragile. One platform change, one algorithm shift, one client leaving can cut revenue in half overnight.

The target diversification structure:
- At least two product revenue streams (different products targeting the same audience, or similar products targeting different audiences)
- At least one service or consulting revenue stream
- At least one recurring revenue source (membership, retainer, or subscription product)

You do not need all three on day one. You build toward them in that order: products first (they have no marginal cost), services second (higher margin, but your time-capped), recurring third (compounds over time).

**Cash Flow vs. Profit**

Cash flow and profit are not the same thing. Profit is revenue minus expenses on paper. Cash flow is actual money available right now. You can be profitable and cash-flow negative if clients pay late or if revenue is lumpy.

Track both. The practical tool: a 90-day cash flow projection. What revenue do you realistically expect in the next 90 days? What costs are coming? Is there a gap? Build a buffer before you need it.

## Chapter 7: The Weekly Review System

### What to Measure, What to Cut

Most productivity advice focuses on the daily level. Wake up early, plan your morning, use a time-blocking system. These things matter, but they are tactical. The weekly level is where strategic decisions actually happen.

The weekly review is the single highest-leverage habit in the solopreneur's operating system. It is the moment where everything else gets calibrated.

**The Weekly Review Structure**

Set a recurring two-hour block at the end of your work week. Friday afternoon or Sunday morning both work. The time matters less than the consistency.

The review covers six questions in sequence:

*1. What got done this week?*
List everything that shipped, finished, or moved forward. Not the activity, the output. "Wrote three posts" is activity. "Three posts published and scheduled" is output. This question grounds the review in reality, not in how the week felt.

*2. What did not get done, and why?*
Not a guilt exercise. A diagnostic. Did tasks not get done because they were blocked by something external? Because they were unclear? Because you avoided them? Each reason has a different fix. Blocked tasks need the blocker removed. Unclear tasks need to be defined before next week. Avoided tasks need to be examined for whether they belong on the list at all.

*3. What are the numbers?*
The financial snapshot from Chapter 6. Content performance metrics (reach, engagement, email signups). Product revenue. Client work status. You cannot improve what you do not measure.

*4. What is the one project that matters most next week?*
This is a forcing function. You have multiple projects. All of them seem important. But they are not equally important right now. The weekly review forces you to rank them and give the most important project the first two hours of your best work days next week.

*5. What should be cut?*
The most underused question. Every week, something on the project list has either stopped being valuable or should never have been started. The instinct is to keep it "just in case." The cost of keeping dead projects is not just the time spent on them. It is the mental overhead of their presence. Cut anything that has not moved in three weeks and does not have a concrete next action.

*6. What is the next week's operating plan?*
Three priorities, not ten. Each priority has one concrete next action. The rest goes in the backlog.

**The Quarterly Direction Review**

Four times a year, the weekly review expands into a two-hour quarterly review. This review asks one additional question: is the direction still right?

The quarterly review compares where you are now against where you intended to be. Not to judge, but to recalibrate. Markets change. Your interests change. What made sense in January might not make sense in April.

The output of the quarterly review is a updated one-page document that states: the primary focus for the next quarter, the metrics that will tell you if it is working, and the projects you are officially pausing to maintain focus.

## Chapter 8: The AI Integration Layer

### Which Tools, When to Use Them

AI tools are not magic. They are also not irrelevant. The mistake most solopreneurs make is treating AI as either a solution to everything or a threat to ignore. Both positions are wrong.

AI is a layer you add to existing systems, not a replacement for having systems. A good system with AI is faster. A bad system with AI just fails faster and at scale.

This chapter covers the specific integration points where AI adds genuine leverage, and the points where it does not.

**Content Creation: Where AI Helps and Where It Does Not**

AI helps with:
- Generating the first draft of structured content (outlines, lists, frameworks)
- Rephrasing existing content for different platforms or audiences
- Expanding bullet points into full paragraphs when you have the ideas but not the words
- Generating variation in headlines, subject lines, and CTAs for testing

AI does not help with:
- Your original thesis. The insight that makes content worth reading must come from your own thinking and experience. AI trained on existing content produces the average of existing content. Your competitive advantage is the perspective you have that average content does not.
- Voice and tone. The longer AI writes on your behalf without editorial input, the more content sounds like every other AI-assisted content. Human editing is not optional if you want a recognizable voice.
- Strategic decisions. What to write about, who to target, what product to build next. These require judgment that comes from knowing your audience, your market, and your goals.

**The AI Workflow for Content**

The practical integration:
1. You write the thesis sentence and the three supporting points
2. AI expands the outline into a draft
3. You edit the draft for voice, accuracy, and specific examples from your own experience
4. AI handles platform adaptation (same core idea, reformatted for LinkedIn, Twitter, etc.)
5. You review and schedule

This workflow is faster than writing from scratch. It is not fully automated, and it should not be. The human input in steps 1 and 3 is what makes the content yours.

**Product Creation: Where AI Helps**

AI significantly accelerates:
- Market research synthesis (paste in 20 customer comments, ask AI to identify the recurring pain patterns)
- Table of contents generation from a problem statement
- First draft content expansion
- FAQ generation from a product description

The same rule applies: the structure, the insight, and the specific examples must be yours. AI fills in around them.

**Business Operations: The Real AI Leverage**

The highest-leverage AI integration for solopreneurs is not content. It is operations.

- Email triage: AI can draft replies to common inquiry types
- Document templates: AI generates first drafts of proposals, contracts, and onboarding documents faster than writing from scratch
- Research synthesis: given a set of inputs, AI summarizes and extracts key points faster than reading everything yourself
- Transcription and summarization: voice memos become searchable text, long content becomes short summaries

**The Tool Stack That Actually Works**

The principle: fewer tools that you use deeply are more valuable than many tools you use shallowly.

For content:
- Claude or GPT-4 for drafting and ideation
- Notion AI for in-database content operations
- Typefully or Buffer for scheduling and analytics

For operations:
- Claude or GPT-4 for email, proposals, research synthesis
- Zapier or n8n for workflow automation (AI-triggered actions based on inputs)
- Whisper or native voice transcription for idea capture

For product research:
- Perplexity for fast research with citations
- Claude for synthesis and framework generation
- Manual reading still matters. AI summarizes what exists. Your edge is connecting things that have not been connected.

**What to Automate and What to Keep Human**

The decision framework:
- Automate any task that is repetitive, rules-based, and does not require contextual judgment
- Keep human any task where the quality of the output depends on understanding nuance, relationship, or context that the AI does not have access to

Distribution, formatting, basic research, template generation: automate.
Original thinking, client relationships, strategic decisions, public voice: keep human.

The solopreneur who automates the right things and stays human in the right places outperforms both the person who automates everything and the person who automates nothing.

## Bonus: 12 Templates for Notion and Obsidian

The following templates are referenced throughout this book. Each one corresponds to a system chapter.

### Template 1: Content Idea Queue (Notion)

A database with the following properties:
- Title: the raw idea or thesis sentence
- Status: Capture / Develop / Ready / Published
- Platform: Primary channel for this piece
- Created: date captured
- Published: date published (empty until live)
- Notes: development notes, additional angles

View setup: Kanban view with Status as column groups. Default filter: Status is not Published.

### Template 2: Weekly Creation Tracker (Notion or Obsidian)

A simple table with:
- Piece title
- Platform
- Status (Drafted / Edited / Scheduled / Published)
- Publish date
- Performance score (updated after 48 hours)

The weekly creation tracker shows at a glance whether you are on pace for your content goals.

### Template 3: Distribution Checklist (Notion)

For each long-form piece, a checklist of every derivative asset:
- [ ] Short-form post 1
- [ ] Short-form post 2
- [ ] Short-form post 3
- [ ] Email version
- [ ] Carousel or thread
- [ ] Quote graphic
- [ ] Short video script
- [ ] Pinterest pin

Check each box when the asset is scheduled. The goal is 100% completion before moving to the next long-form piece.

### Template 4: Product Development Tracker (Notion)

Seven-day project view with:
- Day number
- Task for that day
- Status (Not Started / In Progress / Done)
- Notes

One row per day, one column per field. The view shows whether you are on track to launch on day seven.

### Template 5: Sales Page Copy Template (Obsidian or Notion)

The eight-section landing page structure as a document template with placeholder text for each section and word count targets per section.

### Template 6: Email Sequence Builder (Notion)

A database with one row per email:
- Email number
- Send day
- Subject line
- Body (text area)
- CTA
- Status

A one-page document template covering:
- Who this is for
- The deliverable
- The timeline
- The investment
- How to start

One file per service offering. Updated as the offer evolves.

A table with one row per month:

A rolling document with one section per week. Each section contains the six review questions as headers with space for answers. Linked to the Monthly Finance Audit.

A database of proven prompts organized by use case:
