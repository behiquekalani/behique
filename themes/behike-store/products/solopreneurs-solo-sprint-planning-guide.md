# Solo Sprint Planning: How One-Person Businesses Get Things Done

**By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin**

*A practical system for solopreneurs who need structure without bureaucracy.*

## The Planning Problem No One Talks About

There is a version of solopreneurship that looks like this: you wake up, open your laptop, check messages, respond to the most urgent thing, start three tasks without finishing any of them, feel productive because you were busy, and close the laptop at 11pm having made no meaningful progress on anything that actually moves the business.

That version is far more common than anyone admits.

The typical advice is to get a planner, use a productivity app, or build a morning routine. That advice misses the actual problem. The problem is not that solopreneurs lack discipline. The problem is that they are running a business designed for teams, using systems designed for employees, while operating as a single person with no management layer and no accountability structure.

The sprint model, borrowed from software development and adapted for one-person businesses, solves this. It gives you a short planning horizon, a commitment mechanism, a review process, and a feedback loop. All of it is sized for one person.

This guide is the complete system.

---

## Why Solopreneurs Resist Structured Planning

Before building the system, it is worth being honest about why solopreneurs avoid it.

**The identity problem.** Many people become solopreneurs precisely because they rejected the structure of traditional work. Sprints and planning sessions feel like bringing the corporate playbook into something they built to escape it. This is an understandable reaction to bad structure. The solution is not to abandon structure but to design structure that actually fits.

**The uncertainty problem.** A solopreneur's week is genuinely unpredictable in ways that a corporate employee's is not. Clients have urgent requests. Revenue opportunities appear unexpectedly. Personal circumstances intervene. Building a rigid plan into that environment feels pointless because the plan will break within 48 hours. The solution is not a rigid plan. It is a flexible container.

**The ambition problem.** Solopreneurs tend to be ambitious people with many ideas. Planning requires choosing, and choosing means not doing most things. The planning session that forces you to pick five tasks from a list of forty is uncomfortable. It requires a kind of clarity that takes practice.

**The accountability vacuum.** In a company, planning has social consequence. You present a plan, others depend on it, and your performance against it is visible. Alone, nothing forces you to take your own plan seriously. Without accountability structure, planning becomes optional, and optional things stop happening.

The 2-week sprint model addresses all four of these. It is short enough to tolerate uncertainty, specific enough to drive execution, and structured enough to create a real feedback loop.

## The 2-Week Sprint Model Adapted for One-Person Businesses

A sprint is a fixed period of time during which you commit to completing a specific set of work. The period is short enough that you can predict it reasonably well and long enough that you can complete meaningful work.

Two weeks is the right horizon for a solopreneur because:

- It is long enough to complete multi-day projects without constant re-planning
- It is short enough that your predictions about what you can accomplish are usually accurate
- It creates 26 natural review points per year, which is enough feedback to improve your planning over time
- It matches well with typical client billing cycles and content publishing cadences

The core structure of a solo sprint looks like this:

**Day 1: Sprint planning session (90 minutes)**
You identify the 5 things you will complete in the next two weeks and make a specific plan for how you will do them.

**Days 1-10: Execution**
You work through your sprint tasks alongside client work and daily operations, using time blocks and focus sessions.

**Day 13: Sprint review (30 minutes)**
You assess what you completed, what you did not, and what that tells you.

**Day 14: Sprint retrospective (15 minutes)**
You answer one question that makes the next sprint better.

That is the full structure. It is deliberately minimal because complexity is the enemy of consistency.

## Sprint Planning Session: How to Pick the Right 5 Things

The planning session is the most important 90 minutes of your two-week sprint. A bad planning session produces 10 days of confused effort. A good planning session produces 10 days of directed work.

Before the session, you need two things: your list of everything you could do (your backlog) and a clear picture of what your business needs most right now.

**The backlog is not a to-do list.** A backlog is a prioritized inventory of potential work. Every project, idea, client deliverable, and improvement you could work on lives in the backlog. When you sit down to plan a sprint, you pull from the backlog. Nothing enters a sprint without passing through the backlog first.

**The 5-task rule is a constraint, not a suggestion.** Choosing five tasks for a two-week sprint will feel insufficient if you are accustomed to writing 30-item to-do lists. Stick to five. A two-week sprint contains roughly 80 available working hours, accounting for client work, admin, and daily operations. Five meaningful tasks is actually ambitious.

How to select the right five:

1. What is the one task that would most move revenue if completed this sprint?
2. What is the one task that has been in the backlog the longest and is still genuinely important?
3. What client deliverable is due within the sprint window?
4. What is the one task that removes a recurring friction point in your business?
5. What is one thing you have been avoiding that you know needs to get done?

That framework will give you five tasks that are spread across revenue, operations, clients, and personal accountability. They are not random. They are selected.

For each task, define a clear completion condition. Not "work on the landing page" but "landing page is published and has been reviewed by one outside person." Vague tasks produce vague outcomes. Specific completion conditions make the sprint review honest.

## Daily Execution Inside a Sprint

The sprint plan tells you what to do. Daily execution is how you actually do it.

The core unit of daily execution is the focus session. A focus session is a block of 60-120 minutes dedicated to a single sprint task with no interruptions. You are not multitasking. You are not checking messages. You are working on one thing until the session ends.

A solopreneur running a two-week sprint should aim for two focus sessions per day. One in the morning, one in the afternoon. Everything else (client communication, admin, social media, planning) happens in the time between and after focus sessions.

This sounds obvious. It is surprisingly hard to protect in practice because the nature of solo business work is that everything feels equally urgent. The emails feel urgent. The client Slack feels urgent. The competitor who just launched something feels urgent. Focus sessions are the mechanism that separates what feels urgent from what actually moves the sprint forward.

**Time block structure for a sprint day:**

- Morning block (2 hours): Sprint task focus session, no communications
- Mid-morning (1 hour): Client communication, email, admin
- Afternoon block (2 hours): Sprint task focus session or client delivery work
- Late afternoon (1 hour): Planning for next day, async communication, content
- Buffer (30 minutes): Unscheduled. This is where unexpected things go.

The buffer is not optional. If you schedule every minute, the first unexpected thing collapses the entire day. The buffer absorbs the unexpected without destroying your sprint progress.

**Daily sprint check-in (5 minutes at end of day):**
Ask one question: Did I move my sprint tasks forward today? Yes or no. If no, what is getting in the way? Write the answer down. Over 10 days, this creates a pattern. The pattern tells you exactly what to fix in the next sprint.

## Dealing with Interruptions and Unexpected Tasks

The most common reason solopreneurs abandon sprint planning is that life does not respect the plan. A client has an emergency. A revenue opportunity appears that requires immediate attention. A family situation intervenes. The sprint breaks, and because it broke, the whole system feels pointless.

This is the wrong conclusion. Sprints are not meant to be impervious to reality. They are meant to be a clear home base that you return to when the interruption is over.

**The interruption protocol:**

When something interrupts your sprint, do not abandon the sprint. Triage the interruption against three questions:

1. Is this genuinely urgent and important? If yes, handle it and return to the sprint as soon as possible.
2. Is this urgent but not truly important? If yes, schedule it for a specific future time and protect the current focus session.
3. Is this neither urgent nor important? If yes, it goes to the backlog and gets evaluated at the next sprint planning session.

The discipline is not in preventing interruptions. It is in returning to the sprint after them.

**For unexpected tasks that are genuinely important:**

A new client request that needs to be done this week is a legitimate sprint addition. When this happens, do not just add it to the list. Trade it for something. Every addition requires removing a sprint task of equivalent size. This keeps the sprint realistic and forces honest prioritization.

If more than two tasks get swapped in a sprint, that sprint's planning session was not accurate. This is data for the retrospective, not a personal failure. The system improves through honest review.

## Sprint Review: Measuring Completion vs. Measuring Impact

At the end of the sprint, before you plan the next one, you run a sprint review. The sprint review has two components.

**Completion review (15 minutes):**
Go through each of your five tasks. Mark each one as completed, partially completed, or not completed. For each incomplete task, write one sentence explaining why.

This should be factual, not self-critical. "Task 3 was not completed because the client approval process took longer than expected" is useful information. "Task 3 was not completed because I am bad at this" is not. You want data, not guilt.

**Impact review (15 minutes):**
This is the harder part. For the tasks you did complete, ask: did completing this task produce the outcome I expected? Did finishing the landing page bring in leads? Did publishing the content drive engagement?

Most solopreneurs measure their productivity by completion. They feel good when they finish tasks. But completion and impact are not the same thing. A sprint where you completed all five tasks but none of them moved the business forward is not a successful sprint. It is a signal that your selection process needs to improve.

Over time, the impact review teaches you what kinds of tasks actually move your business and what kinds feel productive but do not. This is one of the most valuable things the sprint system produces.

## The Sprint Retrospective: One Question That Makes the Next Sprint Better

The retrospective is short. It is the final step before you close the sprint and open a new one.

Ask yourself one question: "If I could change one thing about how I ran this sprint, what would it be?"

Write the answer down. Do not write a list of everything that went wrong. One thing. The most important one.

Then, in the next sprint planning session, act on that answer. If your answer is "I need to protect my morning focus sessions better," the first thing you put in next sprint's plan is a specific mechanism for doing that. If your answer is "I overestimated how fast I could write," the first thing you adjust is task sizing.

The power of the retrospective is compound. Each sprint produces one improvement. Over six months, you have made twenty-six adjustments to a system that was already working reasonably well. At that point, the system is calibrated to your specific work patterns, your specific interruption profile, and your specific business context in a way that no generic productivity system ever could be.

## Managing Client Work and Personal Projects in the Same Sprint

One of the hardest parts of solopreneur sprint planning is that two categories of work compete for the same time: client work (which pays immediately) and personal projects (which build long-term leverage). Both belong in the sprint. Both need attention. When client work urgency dominates, personal projects never progress. When personal projects dominate, client relationships suffer.

The solution is not to balance them in every session. It is to plan them in different time layers within the sprint.

**Client work gets time blocks, not sprint tasks.** If you have active client work, it belongs in your daily schedule as protected time, not as one of your five sprint tasks. Sprint tasks are for work that builds your own business. Client work is your current revenue stream and should be treated as a commitment, not a project.

**Personal projects get sprint tasks.** The five tasks you choose in your sprint planning session should be weighted toward your own business development. A typical healthy split is three personal project tasks and two tasks that are either hybrid (client work that also builds portfolio or skills) or business operations (things that make your business run better).

**The budget rule:** Allocate a percentage of your weekly hours to each category before the sprint starts. A common starting point is 60% client work, 30% personal projects, 10% admin and planning. If you track actual time against this budget at the end of each sprint, you will quickly see whether your intentions and your reality match.

When a client rush forces you to violate the budget in one sprint, you do not need to feel guilty about it. You do need to notice it, log it in the retrospective, and plan the next sprint with the constraint that personal project time needs to be recouped.

## Sprint Cadence Over Time: How the System Compounds

The first sprint will not go well. You will overestimate what you can accomplish, underestimate how long tasks take, and be interrupted by things you did not plan for. This is normal. It is not a reason to abandon the system.

The value of the sprint model is not in any individual sprint. It is in the cumulative effect of 26 sprints per year, each slightly more accurate than the last.

By sprint 4, your time estimates will be meaningfully more accurate.
By sprint 8, your backlog will be well-organized and you will have a clear sense of your highest-value work.
By sprint 13, you will have a pattern of what kinds of tasks you complete reliably and what kinds you consistently defer.
By sprint 26, you will have a tuned system that produces consistent forward progress on your business even during weeks when everything else is chaotic.

The sprint model also creates something that solo work rarely produces naturally: a record. When you look back at 12 months of completed sprint reviews, you can see exactly what you built, what decisions you made, and how your business evolved. That record is motivating in ways that looking at a vague sense of progress never is.

The compounding works in another direction too. As your execution improves, your planning improves. As your planning improves, your ambition adjusts to be realistic rather than wishful. Realistic ambition is the foundation of consistent output. Consistent output over time is how solopreneurs build real businesses.

## Starting Your First Sprint

The first sprint starts with one planning session. No setup required, no software needed, nothing to buy. You need:

- A list of everything you could work on right now (your backlog)
- A document or paper where you will write your five tasks
- A completion condition for each task
- A calendar where you will block your focus sessions

That is the entire starting requirement.

Start the sprint on a Monday. Run it for two weeks. At the end, run the review and retrospective. Start the next one.

The system is not complicated. What makes it work is the commitment to run it consistently, to review honestly, and to improve it incrementally.

Solopreneurs do not fail because they lack capability. They fail because they lack systems that match how they actually work. The solo sprint is a system that was designed for one person, operating with full autonomy, building something that did not exist before they started.

That is you. Start the sprint.
