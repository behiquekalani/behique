# The Niche Authority Blueprint
### How to become the go-to person in a specific niche without credentials, a degree, or years of experience.

---

## Introduction: Authority Is Not Assigned

There is a doctor who has practiced for 25 years and never written a word about what he knows. There is a 23-year-old who has been studying the same field for two years and publishes something every week. In most industries, the second person has more influence. More followers. More clients who reach out. More money.

This is not a statement about who is more competent. It is a statement about how authority actually works in 2024. Authority is not assigned by a credentialing body. It is not automatically granted by tenure. It is not proportional to how much you know in private.

Authority is demonstrated publicly, consistently, over time. The person who teaches what they know always beats the person who only knows it.

This is uncomfortable for a lot of smart, skilled people. They have spent years building real competence and feel that visibility is somehow beneath them. Or they are waiting until they know enough to speak. Or they believe credentials will open doors that credentials have never actually opened.

None of that is how it works anymore.

The barrier to sharing what you know is zero. The cost of publishing is zero. The number of people who can see your work, learn from it, and decide to pay you based on it is functionally unlimited. In this environment, the question is not whether you are qualified. The question is whether you are visible.

This guide is about becoming visible. It is about picking a specific territory, demonstrating competence inside it, and building the kind of authority that produces income. Not theoretical authority. Not authority that feels good but pays nothing. The kind of authority where people find your content, trust your judgment, and buy what you sell.

The guide is structured as seven chapters. Each one covers a specific piece of the system. By the end, you will have a niche selected, a content strategy mapped, a platform chosen, a positioning statement written, and a monetization path clear.

No credentials required. No permission needed. Let's build.

## Chapter 1: Why Niches Win

### The Expert Paradox

The first thing most people do when they decide to build an authority presence is go broad. They want to cover productivity, business, health, and technology. They want to be helpful to as many people as possible. They are afraid that if they narrow down, they will lose reach.

This logic is exactly backwards.

Here is the paradox: the more specific you are, the more people trust you. When someone is trying to solve a specific problem, they do not want a generalist. They want the person who clearly knows this problem better than anyone else. Generalists feel like a consolation prize. Specialists feel like the answer.

This plays out in revenue too. A consultant who works with "businesses of all sizes across multiple industries" charges $75 an hour. A consultant who works specifically with SaaS companies under 20 employees on their onboarding flow charges $300 an hour. The second person is more valuable not because they know more, but because they are more relevant. Relevance is a function of specificity.

The expert paradox is this: by trying to appeal to everyone, you become compelling to no one. By narrowing to a specific group with a specific problem, you become the obvious choice for that group.

### The Signal-to-Noise Problem in Broad Markets

There are millions of people talking about "marketing." There are thousands talking about "digital marketing." There are hundreds talking about "email marketing for e-commerce brands." There are dozens talking about "email marketing for Shopify stores selling home goods."

Each step narrower makes you easier to find, easier to remember, and easier to trust. In broad markets, you are noise. In narrow markets, you are signal.

The practical implication: if you are trying to grow an audience in a broad category, you are competing with people who have been publishing for years, have massive distribution, and have media companies backing their content. You will not win by fighting on their terrain.

The narrow niche has a different dynamic. Competition is lower. The audience is underserved. When someone who cares deeply about that specific topic finds you, they feel like they found the only person speaking their language. That feeling converts to follows, email subscribers, and purchases at a rate that broad content cannot match.

A creator who publishes about "personal finance" will spend years grinding for attention. A creator who publishes about "personal finance for freelancers in their 20s dealing with irregular income" will find their audience faster, retain them at higher rates, and convert them to buyers more easily. The specificity itself is the marketing.

### Why Narrow Positioning Feels Scary

Narrowing your niche feels like closing doors. It feels like you are cutting off potential customers, limiting your reach, making yourself smaller.

That feeling is real. The logic behind it is wrong.

What actually happens when you narrow is that you stop competing on volume and start competing on relevance. You are no longer fighting for the attention of everyone. You are becoming indispensable to someone.

The fear usually shows up as: "What if I get bored of this topic?" or "What if the market is too small?" or "What if I pick the wrong niche and get stuck?"

Chapter 2 addresses all three of those directly. For now, the important thing to understand is that positioning is not permanent. You can evolve. You can expand. But you cannot build authority from nowhere. You need a base, and the base is always specific.

The people who stay broad stay broke. The people who go narrow build assets.

## Chapter 2: Choosing Your Niche

### The 3-Axis Test

Choosing a niche is not about finding your passion. It is not about taking a personality quiz. It is not about picking what sounds cool. It is about finding the intersection of three things that actually matter.

The three axes are: what you know, what the market pays for, and what you can talk about for three years.

**Axis 1: What You Know**

This is your starting inventory. It includes formal education, but that is only a small part of it. It includes every job you have had, every problem you have solved, every skill you have built, every environment you have navigated. Most people dramatically underestimate what they know.

Write a list. Not a curated list. A brain dump. Every topic you could teach something about. Every question people ask you. Every time someone said "how did you do that?" and you had an answer. Every skill you have developed, even if it took you less time than you think it should have. That list is your raw material.

The standard is not mastery. The standard is ahead-of-someone. If you know more about something than a meaningful group of people who need that knowledge, you have something to teach.

**Axis 2: What the Market Pays For**

Not every topic has an audience that pays. Some topics have large audiences and no buyers. Some topics have small audiences and high-value buyers. You want the second category.

How to test this: look for existing products. Go to Gumroad, Amazon, Udemy, and Google. Search for the topic. If there are products and courses being sold at meaningful prices, the market pays. If there is nothing for sale, either the market does not pay or you have found an underserved space. Both are useful information.

Look at what people search for. Google's autocomplete and "People also ask" sections tell you what problems are active in people's minds. Active problems are problems people will pay to solve.

Look at what people hire for. If companies post job listings for skills related to your topic, those skills have market value. You can teach people to develop those skills faster.

**Axis 3: What You Can Talk About for Three Years**

This is the filter that most frameworks leave out.

Building authority takes time. Not forever, but longer than a sprint. You need to produce enough content over a long enough period that people start to associate you with this topic. That requires genuine interest, not forced interest.

The question is not "am I passionate about this?" Passion fluctuates. The question is: "Can I find something new to say about this every week for the next three years?" If the answer is yes, even imagining the hard weeks, you have a sustainable niche. If you already feel bored thinking about it, move on.

The intersection of all three axes is your niche. The topic you know, that the market pays for, and that you can sustain for years.

### Working the 3-Axis Test

Here is how to actually use it.

List five to ten candidate niches from your knowledge inventory. For each one, ask the three axis questions and score them simply: strong, moderate, or weak.

Example: Someone with a background in HR who has spent years hiring for startups.

- **Topic: corporate HR compliance** — Strong on knowledge. Market pays (companies need this). But weak on three-year sustainability — feels like work, not interest.
- **Topic: how to get hired at startups** — Strong on knowledge (hiring side perspective). Market pays (career coaching is a real industry). Can talk about this for years. Strong on all three axes.
- **Topic: building a recruiting process as a first-time founder** — Strong on knowledge. Market pays (founders will pay for this). Can sustain it. Strong on all three.

That person has two viable niches. They pick one to start. They go narrow within it. "Helping first-time founders build their first 10 hires" is better than "startup hiring tips."

### The Anti-Passion Trap

Following your passion is bad advice for niche selection. Here is why.

Passion is not a stable signal. People are passionate about things they are bad at. People are passionate about things that have no market. People's passions change. Passion alone does not tell you anything useful about whether a niche will work.

The better question is: what are you genuinely curious about? Curiosity is more durable than passion. You can be curious about something even when it frustrates you. Curiosity drives you to keep reading, keep testing, keep learning. That is the quality you need for the long game of authority building.

Combined with market demand, curiosity beats passion every time.

The other version of the passion trap is waiting until you are passionate enough. "I'll start when I feel really excited about this." That excitement is not coming before you start. It comes after. After you have published 50 things. After people have responded. After you have seen ideas land and refined your angle. The feeling follows the action.

Pick the niche that passes the three-axis test. Start there. Refine as you go.

## Chapter 3: The Authority Content Stack

### What Actually Builds Authority

Authority is built through one mechanism: demonstrating that you understand something better than the people you are trying to reach. Content is how you do this publicly.

Not all content builds authority equally. There are four types that do the work. Most people only use one or two. The creators who build authority fastest use all four in rotation.

### Content Type 1: Teaching

Teaching is the most direct form of authority building. You know something. You explain it. People understand it better because of you. They associate that clarity with your name.

Teaching content includes how-to posts, tutorials, explainers, frameworks, step-by-step guides, and any content where the primary goal is transferring knowledge.

The key principle for teaching content: go specific and go practical. "How to improve your email marketing" builds almost no authority. "How to write a subject line that gets opened when your list is cold" builds a lot of authority. The more specific and usable your teaching, the more it signals expertise.

Good teaching content answers: "What do you know that I don't, and how does it change what I do?"

Real example: Justin Welsh built a significant creator business largely by teaching extremely specific things about LinkedIn and solopreneurship. Not general productivity. Not generic business advice. Specific tactics, documented clearly, published consistently. His authority came from the specificity of the teaching.

### Content Type 2: Case Studies

Case studies are proof. They show that something you know actually works in the real world, not just in theory.

The power of case studies is that they answer the question before the reader asks it: "Does this actually work?" When you publish a case study that shows a real outcome, you preempt skepticism. You are not claiming expertise. You are showing results.

Case studies do not have to be client results. They can be your own experiments. "I tested three email subject line formats across 4 sends and here is what happened" is a case study. "I rebuilt my content calendar using this system and my output went from 2 posts a week to 7" is a case study. The format is: here is what I tried, here is what happened, here is what it means.

The standard is honesty. Show what worked and what did not. Authority built on real results is durable. Authority built on curated wins is fragile.

### Content Type 3: Opinions

Opinions are the content type most people avoid because they feel risky. They are also the most powerful for building authority.

Here is why. Teaching and case studies show you know things. Opinions show you have a perspective. Perspective is what separates an expert from a database. A database has information. An expert has a point of view.

When you take a clear position on a contested question in your niche, you do several things at once: you filter your audience toward people who respect your reasoning, you become memorable, and you signal confidence in your knowledge. People do not follow people who have no opinions. They follow people who think clearly and say things other people are thinking but not saying.

Opinion content is not contrarianism for its own sake. It is genuine perspective based on what you have seen and learned. "Here is the conventional wisdom in this field. Here is why I think it is wrong, and what I think instead." That is opinion content.

The fear is that people will disagree. Some will. That is the point. The people who agree will trust you more than they would trust someone who never takes a position.

### Content Type 4: Process Documentation

Process documentation is showing your work in real time. It is the "building in public" approach, but more structured.

Instead of just posting updates, you document how you do things. "Here is how I built my client onboarding system." "Here is the process I use to research new topics before writing about them." "Here is exactly how I set up my content calendar each week."

Process documentation works because it is inherently credible. You are not claiming a result. You are showing your method. Readers can see the logic. They can try it themselves. When it works for them, you have built real trust.

It also compounds. Each process you document becomes a reference piece. People return to it. They share it. It continues building authority long after it is published.

### How to Rotate the Stack

Most creators who struggle with authority content are stuck in one mode. They only teach, or they only share opinions, or they only post updates.

The most effective approach is rotation. In any given week or month, you should have a mix of all four types. Teaching to demonstrate knowledge. Case studies to show proof. Opinions to show perspective. Process documentation to show method.

A simple weekly structure: one teaching piece, one opinion piece, and occasional case studies and process docs when you have good material. This keeps your content varied, keeps your audience engaged, and builds authority on multiple dimensions at once.

## Chapter 4: The Demonstration Loop

### The Proof Problem

The most common objection to building authority is this: "I don't have clients yet, so I don't have case studies. I don't have case studies, so I can't get clients." This feels like a closed loop.

It is not a closed loop. The mistake is assuming that proof has to come from client work.

Proof is evidence that you know what you are doing. Client results are one form of evidence. They are not the only form. Before you have clients, you can generate proof through three other channels: side projects, public experiments, and documented learning.

### Side Projects as Proof

A side project is something you build or run for the purpose of generating results you can document and share. It is not a product, necessarily. It is a laboratory.

If you are positioning yourself as a social media authority, run a social media account in your niche and document the growth. Build it yourself using the methods you teach. Show the results. The account itself becomes proof.

If you are positioning as an email marketing expert, build a small email list and document every step of the process. Share your open rates. Share what worked and what did not. The transparency itself is proof of competence.

The goal is not to run a massive side project. The goal is to generate real data from a real experiment that you can document and share. Even small results, documented honestly, are more compelling than large claims without evidence.

Creator Dickie Bush used this approach when building Ship 30 for 30. He started writing publicly every day, documented the process and results, and used that documentation as proof of the methodology he was teaching. His side project became his most compelling sales tool before he had a single paying student.

### Public Experiments

A public experiment is when you test something explicitly and share the outcome publicly.

The format: "I am going to try X for Y days and share what happens." Then you do it and report honestly.

Public experiments build authority because they are credible by design. You are not claiming to know the answer in advance. You are testing something and sharing real data. Even negative results build trust, because they show intellectual honesty.

Public experiments also give you a reason to publish consistently. Instead of trying to generate teaching content from abstract knowledge, you are reporting on ongoing work. The experiment provides the content.

Good public experiment formats: "I cold emailed 100 prospects using this exact script. Here are the results." "I rewrote my LinkedIn bio four times in 30 days and tracked profile views. Here is what changed." "I published every day for 60 days. Here is what happened to my follower count, engagement, and income."

### Documented Learning

Documented learning is sharing your learning process in real time.

This is for people who feel like they do not know enough to teach. The answer is: you do not have to be ahead of everyone. You just have to be ahead of someone, and document the path for people who are where you were six months ago.

The format is simple: "Here is what I am learning right now. Here is what I am reading, who I am learning from, what questions I am trying to answer. Here is what I have figured out so far."

This is particularly powerful in fast-moving fields. If you are learning about a new technology, a new market, or a new methodology, documenting your learning gives people who are curious about the same thing a guide through the same territory. You become a trusted navigator even before you are an expert.

The key is to document with enough specificity to be useful. "I am learning about AI" is not documented learning. "I spent the last two weeks testing three different AI writing tools for newsletter writing. Here is what I learned about each one and which one I am sticking with" is documented learning.

### The Loop in Practice

The demonstration loop works like this: you run an experiment or build a side project, you document it in real time, and you use that documentation as content that builds authority. That content attracts an audience. The audience validates your positioning. The positioning makes it easier to attract clients or customers. The clients or customers produce results. The results become case studies. The case studies add to the authority content stack.

The loop starts before you have clients. You start it by doing the work yourself, in public.

## Chapter 5: Building Your Authority Platform

### The Distribution Problem

You can produce excellent content with no one seeing it. Distribution is the part of authority building that most people underestimate. They focus on quality of content and forget that content needs a delivery mechanism.

The distribution problem is not solved by being on every platform. It is solved by going deep on one platform before you spread.

### Choosing Your Primary Channel

Your primary channel is the platform where you will put 80 percent of your distribution effort for the first year. Not all your content, but most of your distribution effort.

There are several viable primary channels for building authority: Twitter/X (long-form threads and daily posts), LinkedIn (professional audiences, higher purchase intent), YouTube (video-first authority, high trust-building), Substack or email newsletter (the most durable distribution channel), and a blog or website (SEO-driven, slower but compounding).

How to choose: match the channel to where your target audience already is, and where your natural content format fits.

If your audience is professionals and buyers who make business decisions, LinkedIn is typically the highest leverage channel. If your audience is builders, creators, and tech-adjacent people, Twitter/X has the densest concentration. If you can teach through video and want to build deep trust quickly, YouTube. If you want the most durable relationship with your audience, email.

Most authority builders who are doing this right have a primary channel that drives discovery and an email list that captures the audience. The primary channel is the top of the funnel. Email is how you own the relationship.

### The Minimum Viable Distribution Stack

For year one, the minimum viable distribution stack is: one primary channel, plus an email list.

That is it. Everything else is optional.

The primary channel is for discovery. People find you there, engage with your content, and decide whether you are worth following. The email list is for depth. People who move from a social channel to your email list are signaling higher interest. They are closer to buying.

One common mistake: treating the social channel as the destination. It is not. The destination is the email list, because you own it. Social platforms change their algorithms. They can suspend accounts. They can collapse. The email list is yours regardless.

Every piece of content on your primary channel should have a pathway to your email list. Not in an aggressive way. In a natural way: "If you found this useful, I go deeper on this in my newsletter. Here is the link."

### Why You Need One Deep Channel Before Spreading

The temptation to spread across channels early is strong. More channels means more reach, right?

It does not work that way in practice. Here is why.

Authority on a platform is a function of consistency over time. If you publish on five platforms, you cannot produce five times the content. You produce roughly the same amount of content spread thinner. You are mediocre on all five platforms instead of good on one.

More importantly, algorithm and platform dynamics reward depth. On LinkedIn, the algorithm shows your content to your followers' networks when your content gets engagement. That requires consistent publication and a meaningful follower base. Building that takes time, and it requires showing up regularly enough that the platform learns your content is worth distributing.

This applies to every platform. You need a critical mass of activity and engagement on a platform before it starts working for you. That critical mass takes six to twelve months of consistent publication. If you are spreading across five platforms, none of them reaches critical mass.

The advice: pick one. Go deep. Build a meaningful presence. Then, when that channel is producing results, you can repurpose content to secondary channels. Not before.

### The Content Frequency Question

How often do you need to publish to build authority?

The honest answer is: more than you are probably doing now, but less than you probably think.

For most primary channels, the minimum effective dose is three to five times per week. On Twitter/X, once a day is better. On LinkedIn, three to five times per week. On YouTube, once a week is enough.

The key variable is consistency over volume. Publishing three times a week for two years builds far more authority than publishing daily for three months and then stopping. The platform algorithms reward consistency. Your audience rewards consistency. The compounding effect is a function of time, not just volume.

Build a system that lets you maintain your minimum effective dose even on hard weeks. If your content system requires perfect conditions to function, it will not function.

## Chapter 6: Positioning Statements That Work

### The Authority Bio Formula

Your positioning statement is the most leveraged sentence you will write. It determines whether someone who sees your content for the first time knows immediately whether you are relevant to them.

Most positioning statements are either too vague or too credentials-focused. "Marketing consultant" tells me nothing. "I help startup founders get their first 1,000 subscribers using organic content strategies" tells me who you serve, what you do for them, and how.

The formula: [I help / For] [specific audience] [achieve specific outcome] [through specific method or differentiation].

Examples:
- "I help solo consultants price their services so they can work 20 hours a week and earn what they made in 40."
- "I teach bootstrapped SaaS founders how to write email sequences that convert free trial users into paying customers."
- "For independent coaches and consultants who are stuck in hourly pricing, I show you how to move to packages that double your revenue without adding clients."

Notice what these have in common: a specific person (not everyone), a specific outcome (not general improvement), and either a method or a differentiator that implies your approach.

### How to Describe Yourself in One Sentence

The test for your positioning statement is the stranger test. If someone reads your bio for the first time and does not immediately know whether they are your target audience, your positioning needs work.

Apply the specificity filter: replace every vague word with a concrete one. "Business professional" becomes "SaaS founders." "Improve results" becomes "double your conversion rate." "Strategies" becomes "email sequences" or "cold outreach frameworks" or whatever specific method you actually use.

Then apply the "who is excluded" test. If your statement does not exclude anyone, it is too broad. Good positioning makes some people feel like you are exactly for them, and others feel like you are not for them. That second reaction is a feature, not a bug.

Here are iterations of the same positioning getting progressively better:

- "I help people with their business." (Useless. Excludes no one, compels no one.)
- "I help entrepreneurs grow their revenue." (Better. Still too broad.)
- "I help freelancers turn their skills into digital products." (Good. Specific audience, specific transformation.)
- "I help freelance writers create their first digital product in 30 days using content they have already written." (Excellent. Specific audience, specific outcome, specific timeframe, specific differentiator.)

The last version creates an immediate "that is for me" reaction in a specific person. That reaction is worth more than broad appeal.

### The Anti-Generalist Positioning

One of the most common positioning mistakes is framing expertise as versatility. "I work with businesses of all sizes" or "I help with all aspects of marketing" are phrases that signal generalist, not specialist.

Generalist positioning might feel safer because it does not exclude anyone. In practice, it fails to attract anyone, because it does not feel like it was made for anyone specific.

The anti-generalist positioning is about owning your specific angle and not apologizing for the people it excludes. If you help health coaches with their email marketing, you do not also need to serve e-commerce brands. Resist the expansion. The narrower your positioning, the more trust it creates in the specific person you are targeting.

When you are first starting, the fear is that the niche is too small. It almost never is. There are more health coaches with broken email marketing than you can serve in a decade. There are more freelance writers who want to create products than your content will ever reach. The niche is big enough. The broader positioning just makes you invisible inside it.

## Chapter 7: The Authority Monetization Ladder

### How Authority Converts to Income

Authority is not an end in itself. It is a mechanism that creates trust at scale, and trust converts to income through a predictable ladder of products and services.

The monetization ladder works like this: free content builds the audience, low-cost products convert the audience into buyers, mid-tier products serve buyers who want more depth, and services or consulting serve buyers who want implementation.

Each rung is built on the trust established by the ones below it.

### Rung 1: Free Content

Free content is not a product. It is the mechanism that builds the audience that everything else depends on.

The goal of free content is not to give away your best ideas and make money on what remains. The goal is to demonstrate that you think clearly about a specific topic, regularly enough that people follow you and look forward to what you publish next.

Free content should be good enough that people wonder what your paid products are like. If your free content is weak, no one is going to pay for your products. If your free content solves real problems, people will assume your products go even deeper. They will want access to that depth.

The best free content strategy: teach the "what" and "why" for free. The "how" is in the products.

### Rung 2: Low-Cost Products ($9 to $29)

The first paid product is the most important conversion in the authority monetization system. It transforms someone from a free follower into a buyer. That transformation is psychologically significant. People who have bought from you are dramatically more likely to buy again.

Low-cost products do three things: they give buyers a win, they establish trust in your paid work, and they qualify buyers for higher-ticket products.

For an authority in a specific niche, the ideal low-cost product is a focused guide, template pack, or workbook that solves one specific problem very well. Not a comprehensive system. One problem, solved clearly, packaged cleanly.

This guide, The Niche Authority Blueprint, is an example of this tier. $18.99 for a specific guide on a specific problem. That is the right price and the right scope for a product at this rung.

Pricing principle: do not price too low. A $1 product is not more accessible. It signals low value and attracts buyers who are not serious. The $9 to $29 range creates enough of a commitment that buyers actually use the product, which means they get results, which means they trust you enough to climb the ladder.

### Rung 3: Mid-Tier Products ($49 to $197)

After a buyer has a positive experience with a low-cost product, they are ready for something with more depth. This is where courses, cohort programs, larger template systems, and comprehensive playbooks live.

The mid-tier product expands on what the low-cost product introduced. If your low-cost product is about niche selection, your mid-tier product might be about the complete authority-building system. If your low-cost product is about email subject lines, your mid-tier product might be about the full email marketing strategy for your specific niche audience.

The relationship between tiers matters. Each product should naturally lead to wanting the next one. Buyers of the $18.99 guide who got results will naturally ask: "What comes next? How do I go deeper?" The mid-tier product is the answer to that question.

At this tier, community can be added to increase value without dramatically increasing your workload. A private Discord or Slack with course buyers provides peer support and accountability, which increases completion rates and results, which increases the trust that drives referrals.

### Rung 4: Services and Consulting ($500 to $5,000+)

The top of the ladder is direct access to you. Not information about what you know. You, applying what you know to someone's specific situation.

Services and consulting command a premium because they are not scalable. Your time and attention are limited. The price reflects that scarcity.

The authority you build through content makes consulting possible at prices that would otherwise require years of reputation-building. When someone has read 50 of your posts, watched your case studies, and bought two of your products, they already trust you. The consulting conversation is not "convince me you know what you are doing." It is "let's talk about whether we are a good fit and what this looks like."

Services at this tier can include: one-on-one consulting sessions, done-for-you packages, productized services (same deliverable, repeatable process, fixed price), and retainers.

The practical path: start taking on a small number of service clients at the same time as you build the product ladder. Real client work generates case studies that strengthen your free content. Better free content attracts more clients and buyers. The two reinforce each other.

### The Timeline

The honest timeline for authority monetization: six months of consistent output to build an audience, twelve months to generate consistent income from low-cost products, eighteen to twenty-four months to have a functioning ladder from content to consulting.

This is not slow. The people who think it is slow are comparing it to luck or viral moments, not to traditional career timelines. Building an authority business in eighteen months is faster than getting promoted, faster than building a referral-based service business, and far faster than waiting for credentials to open doors.

The people who do not get there are not the ones who are too slow. They are the ones who stop at month three.

2. _______________
3. _______________
4. _______________
5. _______________

For each candidate, rate each axis as Strong / Moderate / Weak.

| Topic | What You Know | Market Pays | 3-Year Sustainability |
|-------|--------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Topic 1 | | | |
| Topic 2 | | | |
| Topic 3 | | | |
| Topic 4 | | | |
| Topic 5 | | | |

Topics with three Strong ratings are your strongest candidates.

**Step 3: Narrow the Leading Candidate**

Take your top candidate and apply specificity filters:

- Who specifically is your audience? (Profession, life stage, situation)
- What specific problem do they have in this area?
- What specific outcome do they want?
- What makes your angle on this different from others in the space?

Narrow niche statement: "I help [specific person] [achieve specific outcome] through [specific method/angle]."

**Step 4: Market Validation**

For your leading niche:
- [ ] Search for existing products (Gumroad, Amazon, Udemy)
- [ ] Check Google search volume for relevant keywords
- [ ] Look for active communities (Reddit, Facebook groups, Discord servers)
- [ ] Identify 3-5 existing creators in this space

If products exist and communities are active, the market is real.

### C. Positioning Statement Templates

Use these templates to draft your positioning statement. Fill in the brackets. Then edit for natural language.

**Template 1: Outcome-focused**
"I help [specific audience] [achieve specific outcome] without [common obstacle or sacrifice]."

Example: "I help freelance developers get their first three consulting clients without spending money on ads or cold calling."

**Template 2: Method-focused**
"I teach [specific audience] how to [specific skill or process] so they can [desired outcome]."

Example: "I teach solo consultants how to package their expertise into digital products so they can earn income that does not require trading time for money."

**Template 3: Problem-focused**
"For [specific audience] who are struggling with [specific problem], I [what you do] so that [outcome]."

Example: "For coaches and consultants who are stuck at $5K months because all their clients come from referrals, I show you how to build an inbound content system that brings clients to you."

**Template 4: Status-based**
"I work with [specific audience] who want to go from [current state] to [desired state] using [method]."

Example: "I work with mid-career professionals who want to go from employed expert to independent consultant using a documented expertise system."

**Testing Your Statement**

Read it out loud. Then ask these questions:
- Does it name a specific type of person?
- Does it name a specific outcome?
- Does a stranger immediately know if they are your audience?
- Does it exclude people who are not your audience?

If yes to all four, your positioning is working.
