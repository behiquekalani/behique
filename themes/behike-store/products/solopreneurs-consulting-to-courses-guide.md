## The Core Problem With Consulting

Consulting is the most direct way to monetize expertise. You know something valuable. Someone pays you to apply that knowledge to their situation. It works.

It also has a structural ceiling that will eventually trap every consultant who does not plan around it.

The ceiling is simple: you only have so many hours. You can raise your rates. You can work more hours. You can hire subcontractors. But at some point, your income flatlines because your time is fully allocated and there is no room to add more clients.

The consultants who escape this ceiling do it by extracting their knowledge from their brain and their calendar and putting it into a product that runs without them. That product is a course.

This guide is the complete system for making that transition, from how to identify what to teach, to how to structure and record your course, to how to sell it without a large audience.

## Part 1: The Knowledge Extraction Framework

### Why Your Consulting Work Is Already a Course

Every consulting engagement follows a pattern. You diagnose a problem. You apply a methodology. You execute or guide execution. The client gets a result.

That pattern, repeated across dozens of clients, is a course waiting to be packaged. You have already built the curriculum. You just have not documented it yet.

The question is not "do I have enough to teach?" The question is "which part of what I already do is worth systematizing?"

### The Three Types of Consulting Knowledge

Before you can package your knowledge, you need to classify it.

**Type 1: Process Knowledge**
This is the step-by-step methodology you follow to get a client from Point A to Point B. It is sequential, teachable, and the most valuable type for course packaging. Example: "My 6-step process for diagnosing why a business is losing customers and fixing it."

**Type 2: Judgment Knowledge**
This is the accumulated experience that tells you when to apply which process, what exceptions look like, and how to handle edge cases. This is harder to systematize but can be packaged as case studies, decision frameworks, and "when X, do Y" modules.

**Type 3: Network Knowledge**
This is knowing who to call, what tools work best, what shortcuts exist, and what mistakes are expensive. This is extremely valuable but often overlooked as a course asset. Toolkits, vendor recommendations, and resource libraries are monetizable.

Most consultants build courses exclusively around Type 1 and ignore Types 2 and 3. The consultants with the best courses build curriculum around all three.

### The Extraction Process

To pull your process out of your head and into a structure, run through this exercise for your last 10 client engagements.

For each engagement, write down:
1. What was the client's situation when they came to you?
2. What was the first thing you did?
3. What were the 3 to 7 major steps between start and result?
4. What was the specific result achieved?
5. What would have gone wrong without your involvement?

After completing this for 10 clients, look for the patterns. You will find that 70% to 80% of your engagements follow the same core sequence. That sequence is your course structure.

The remaining 20% to 30% are the exceptions and edge cases. Package those as bonus modules or advanced material.

## Part 2: Course Structure Design

### The Transformation Arc

Every course that sells and gets completed is built around a transformation. The student enters with a problem, works through the material, and exits with a specific result.

The transformation arc has three components.

**The Before State:** Who is your student when they arrive? What specific pain, problem, or frustration are they experiencing? Be precise. "Struggling with marketing" is not a before state. "A consultant who has four clients but cannot seem to close new ones because they do not have a repeatable sales process" is a before state.

**The After State:** Who is your student when they finish? What specific capability, result, or circumstance do they have? Again, be precise. "Makes more money" is not an after state. "Has a documented 7-step sales process they can run consistently to close 2 new clients per month" is an after state.

**The Bridge:** The bridge is your curriculum. Each module moves the student one step closer to the after state. If a module does not move them along the transformation arc, it does not belong in the course.

### The Module Structure

A well-built course module has four components.

**Component 1: The Concept (10 to 20 minutes)**
Explain the principle, framework, or process step. This is the "what" and the "why." Keep it tight. Students do not need your full professional history. They need the specific insight that changes how they think about this step.

**Component 2: The Application (10 to 20 minutes)**
Show exactly how to apply the concept. Walk through a real example from your consulting work (anonymized if needed). Demonstrate the process in action. Students learn by watching, not just by hearing.

**Component 3: The Exercise**
Give students a specific task to complete before moving to the next module. This is not optional. Courses that have assignments at every module see completion rates 3 to 5 times higher than courses that are purely passive viewing. The exercise forces application and creates accountability.

**Component 4: The Resource**
Every module should include one tangible asset: a template, a checklist, a worksheet, a script, a swipe file. These resources are often the most valued part of the course and are what students tell others about when recommending the program.

### The Ideal Course Length

Most course creators make courses too long. More content does not mean more value. Completion rates drop as course length increases. A student who completes a 4-hour course and gets results is more valuable to your business than a student who abandons an 18-hour course at hour 6.

For most consulting knowledge:

**Mini-Course (1 to 3 hours):** Solves one specific problem. Priced $47 to $197. Ideal for entry-level products and first-time course creators.

**Core Course (4 to 10 hours):** Covers a complete methodology from start to finish. Priced $197 to $997. This is the standard consulting-to-course format.

**Comprehensive Program (10 to 20 hours):** Deep curriculum with coaching support. Priced $997 to $3,000+. This is appropriate when your consulting engagements typically span months.

Start with the mini-course or core course format. You can always add to a course. You cannot force students through a 20-hour curriculum if the core transformation only requires 6 hours.

## Part 3: The Recording and Production System

### The Minimum Viable Setup

You do not need a professional studio. You need:

- A laptop or desktop with a decent processor
- A USB condenser microphone ($60 to $120, Blue Yeti or similar)
- Headphones
- A quiet room
- Loom, OBS, or Camtasia for screen recording

Audio quality is more important than video quality. Students will tolerate a slightly blurry video. They will not tolerate muffled, echoey, or distorted audio. Invest in a quality microphone before anything else.

For slides-based lessons: Keynote or Google Slides. Keep design minimal. White background, dark text, one concept per slide.

For demonstration lessons: screen recording software showing exactly what the student would see on their own screen while performing the task.

For face-to-camera lessons: a basic webcam or smartphone pointed at your face with a well-lit background. The iPhone camera is sufficient. Natural light from a window works better than most artificial lighting setups.

### The Recording Workflow

Batch your recording to preserve consistency and save time.

**Week 1:** Write all scripts or detailed outlines for all modules. Do not record yet. Review everything to ensure the transformation arc is intact and every module has a clear purpose.

**Week 2-3:** Record all video content in batches of 2 to 3 modules per session. Record each module in one take if possible. Mistakes are acceptable in online courses; re-recordings are time-consuming and create subtle inconsistencies. Keep the rough take unless the error would confuse a student.

**Week 4:** Edit if needed (basic cuts to remove long pauses, verbal mistakes, or off-topic tangents), upload to your course platform, and create all supporting materials (templates, worksheets).

Do not let production perfectionism delay launch. A finished course that is 80% polished is infinitely more valuable than a course you spend eight months perfecting before anyone buys it.

## Part 4: Course Platform Selection

### The Three Platform Models

**Hosted Platforms (Teachable, Thinkific, Kajabi)**
These platforms handle the technical infrastructure. You upload content, they manage hosting, student accounts, and payment processing. Their downside: fees (5% to 10% on the lower-tier plans) and branding constraints.

Best for: First-time course creators who want to get live quickly without technical setup.

**Self-Hosted Platforms (Podia, Gumroad, Lemon Squeezy)**
Lower fees, more direct customer relationships. Gumroad and Lemon Squeezy work particularly well for single-product launches and creators who want simple checkout flows.

Best for: Digital product businesses that prioritize low overhead and direct customer access.

**Custom Platforms (Circle + Course Plugin, WordPress + LearnDash)**
Full control over experience and branding. Requires technical setup but eliminates platform risk.

Best for: Established course businesses doing $50,000+ annually who want to own their infrastructure.

For most consultants launching their first course: Gumroad or Teachable. Both let you launch in under 48 hours with no technical knowledge.

### Pricing Your Course

Pricing a course is not about matching what others charge. It is about anchoring to the value of the outcome.

Ask this question: if a student fully implements what you teach, what is the minimum value they will generate or save?

If the answer is $5,000, your course can be priced at $500 to $1,500. If the answer is $50,000 in avoided mistakes or saved time, your course can be priced at $1,000 to $5,000.

The formula: price at 10% to 30% of the minimum value the student receives.

Common pricing tiers for consulting-based courses:

- $47 to $97: Quick-win, specific-skill mini-courses
- $197 to $497: Core methodology courses
- $497 to $997: Comprehensive transformation programs
- $997 to $2,997: High-touch programs with coaching support

Raise your price from where you start. The market will tell you when you have found the ceiling through declining conversion rates. Until then, assume you are underpriced.

## Part 5: Launch Strategy for Consultants

### The Warm Audience Launch

Your first course launch should go to your existing network: past clients, current clients, newsletter subscribers, and professional contacts. This is your warmest possible audience. They already trust you. They have already seen your work.

Your first launch does not need to be perfect. It needs to happen.

**The minimum viable launch sequence:**

Day 1: Email your list announcing the course with the premise and transformation arc. Include the price. Link to the sales page.

Day 3: Send a follow-up email with a specific case study or result from your consulting work that proves the methodology works.

Day 5: Send a final email with an early-bird price that expires in 48 hours.

That is it. Three emails over five days. If you have 200 people on your list or in your network and 5% to 10% buy, that is 10 to 20 sales. At $297, that is $2,970 to $5,940 from your first launch week.

### The Presell Launch

If you have not built the course yet, presell it before building it.

Announce the course to your audience. Offer a founding member price (20 to 30% below final price). Set a deadline for founding member pricing. Collect payment before the course is complete.

This approach validates demand before you invest 40 to 80 hours building the course. It also gives you the first month's income to fund production, and the students who buy early often provide feedback that improves the final product.

If your presell does not generate at least 5 sales, that is data. Either the price is wrong, the premise does not resonate, or your audience does not have this problem. Adjust accordingly.

### Selling to Prospects, Not Just Clients

Your existing clients know your work. New prospects are discovering you for the first time. The course itself is a trust-building tool for people who would never hire you for consulting but want access to your methodology.

A $297 course buyer often becomes a $2,000 consulting client six months later. The course is not a lesser product. It is often the entry point to a deeper business relationship.

Structure your offer stack with this in mind:
- Course: $297 (self-paced, no direct access to you)
- Course + Implementation Reviews: $997 (async feedback on their work)
- Course + Coaching: $1,997 (monthly calls included)
- Full Consulting Engagement: $5,000+ (done-with-you or done-for-you)

Every tier serves a different buyer. Someone who cannot afford your consulting rate can still access your methodology. Someone who starts with the course self-selects for the next tier when they want faster results.

## Part 6: Automating the Course Business

### The Evergreen Funnel

A launch is a sprint. An evergreen funnel is a marathon. The goal is to move from periodic launches to a system that sells the course continuously.

The evergreen funnel has five stages:

**Stage 1: Traffic Source**
Content marketing (blog, YouTube, podcast, social media) drives cold traffic to a landing page. Each piece of content addresses a problem your course solves.

**Stage 2: Lead Magnet**
A free resource related to the course topic. This is the exchange: the visitor gives you their email in exchange for the resource. The lead magnet should deliver genuine value AND naturally position the paid course as the logical next step.

**Stage 3: Email Nurture Sequence**
A 5 to 7 email sequence delivered over 7 to 14 days after someone downloads the lead magnet. Each email builds credibility, addresses objections, and moves the reader closer to a purchase decision. The final emails in the sequence include a direct offer.

**Stage 4: Sales Page**
A dedicated page that presents the course with full context: who it is for, what they will learn, what they will be able to do afterward, what is included, who created it, and what it costs.

**Stage 5: Delivery Platform**
The course itself. Completion leads to a request for a testimonial and an invitation to the next offer tier.

Once this funnel is live and generating consistent sales, it requires minimal maintenance. You optimize elements over time (test different subject lines, improve the sales page copy, add new content to the lead magnet), but the system runs without your active involvement.

### Scaling with Affiliates and Partners

After your first launch, identify potential partners who serve the same audience without competing directly with you.

Offer them a 30% to 50% commission for each course sale they drive through their own audience. A single partnership with someone who has 5,000 engaged followers in your niche can generate more sales than months of solo marketing.

The mechanics: set up an affiliate program through your platform (most course platforms offer this natively), provide your partners with email swipe copy, social media posts, and context about who the course serves, and pay commissions promptly.

## Part 7: The Long Game

### From Course to Curriculum Empire

A single course is a product. Multiple courses at different price points and addressing different stages of your customer's journey is a curriculum.

The curriculum model:
- Entry product ($47 to $97): Solves a narrow, specific problem. Creates trust.
- Core product ($297 to $997): Your main methodology course.
- Advanced product ($997 to $2,997): Deeper mastery, implementation-focused.
- Premium product ($2,997+): Group program or high-touch hybrid.

Build top to bottom. Start with the core product. Create the entry product to fill the top of the funnel. Add advanced products as demand emerges from students who complete the core course and want more.

### Protecting Your Methodology

As you build your course business, document your methodology formally. Give it a name. Use that name consistently in your marketing, your content, and your delivery.

A named methodology becomes a brand asset. "The 7-Step Client Acquisition System" is more memorable, more marketable, and more defensible than "how to get clients." It signals that you have developed and refined something specific, not just compiled general advice.

Named methodologies also create natural authority. When you consistently refer to your framework by name, it becomes associated with your brand. Other people begin referring to it as "your method." This compounds into positioning that no competitor can copy.

### The Hybrid Model

Most successful consultant-turned-course-creator businesses operate as hybrids. They continue taking consulting clients, but selectively. The course handles the lower-ticket, scalable demand. The consulting handles high-touch, premium clients who want direct access.

The course creates a pipeline of warm, pre-educated clients who arrive at consulting conversations already understanding your methodology. These clients close faster, argue less about price, and get better results because they have already internalized your framework.

The consulting improves the course. Every new client engagement surfaces new questions, new edge cases, and new applications that can be added to course material. The two products make each other better.

## Conclusion: The Leverage Shift

Consulting trades time for money. A course converts knowledge into an asset that generates income independent of your hours.

The shift does not happen overnight. The first course takes longer to build than you expect. The first launch rarely goes perfectly. The first evergreen funnel requires iteration.

But every hour you invest in building the course is an hour that compounds indefinitely. A well-built course can generate revenue for 10 years with periodic updates. No consulting engagement has that return on time invested.

Build the course. Package what you know. Put it in front of people who need it.

The work you have already done in consulting is more than enough. You just need to document it.
