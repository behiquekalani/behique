## How to Package Existing Digital Products Into High-Value Bundles That Sell for 3-5x More

## Introduction: The Packaging Problem Most Creators Never Solve

Most creators build products the wrong way.

They spend months crafting a single guide, price it at $19, and wonder why the math never adds up. To hit $5,000 a month at $19 per sale, you need 263 buyers. That is 263 separate people to find, attract, and convert. Every month.

There is a better model.

Bundling is not about selling more products. It is about restructuring what you already have into a format that commands a different price point, serves a different buyer psychology, and reduces the friction between someone discovering your work and actually paying for it.

A $22 guide sits in a specific mental category for buyers. They evaluate it as a single document. A $97 bundle sits in a different category entirely. They evaluate it as a system, a curriculum, a toolkit. The perceived value of a system is structurally higher than the perceived value of its parts added together.

This guide covers the complete playbook:

- Why bundles outperform individual products at nearly every price point
- The three bundle architectures and when to use each
- How to price bundles without guessing
- What your bundle page needs to convert cold traffic
- The launch sequence that works without an existing audience
- How to use bundles as an upsell engine that runs automatically

This is not theory. Every framework in here is built around how buyer psychology actually works, drawn from the economics of digital product markets, conversion research, and the practical mechanics of running a solopreneur product business.

Work through it in order. The frameworks build on each other.

## Chapter 1: Why Bundles Outperform Single Products

### The Arithmetic of Single Products

Selling individual products at $15-$30 is not a sustainable business model for most creators. The margin is reasonable but the volume requirement is brutal.

Consider the conversion math. A well-optimized sales page for a cold audience converts at around 1-3%. If your guide sells at $22, you need roughly 300 visitors to close 6-9 sales. To hit $3,000 a month from a $22 product at 2% conversion, you need 6,818 monthly visitors to your product page.

That is a significant distribution operation just to make $3,000. And it never compounds. Every month you are back at zero, hunting traffic.

Bundles change the math in two ways: they increase the revenue per transaction, and they attract a buyer who is already committed to solving a problem rather than browsing options.

### The Perception Gap

Buyers do not do math when they buy. They do comparative value assessment.

When someone lands on a $22 guide, they are thinking: "Is this guide worth $22?" That is a lonely question. The product has to carry all the weight by itself.

When someone lands on a $97 bundle with five components, they are thinking: "Is this collection of resources worth $97?" That question has a different answer structure. Five components spread the risk across multiple potential wins. Even if two components turn out to be irrelevant to the buyer, three might be exactly what they needed. A single guide has no such buffer.

This is why bundles command 3-5x the price of their individual components. It is not that they contain more information. It is that the risk/reward calculation shifts in the buyer's favor.

### The Authority Signal

Selling a bundle also signals something about you as a creator. A creator who has built five guides on a topic has clearly spent time inside that problem space. A bundle communicates depth of expertise in a way that no individual product can.

This matters especially if you are not yet well-known. Audience trust is a substitute for reputation, and a reputation is built from signals. A rich bundle is a much stronger signal than a lone PDF.

### The Real Cost of Volume Selling

One more calculation worth running: the cost of volume. Marketing, customer support, refund requests, and platform fees all scale with transaction count, not revenue. Selling 200 units at $15 and selling 50 units at $60 might net similar gross revenue, but the operational overhead of 200 transactions is substantially higher.

Bundles let you achieve similar or greater revenue from fewer transactions. That is the business model shift most creators miss entirely.

### The Compounding Catalog Effect

There is a fourth advantage to bundles that compounds over time. Every new product you build can either stand alone or join an existing bundle, strengthening that bundle's value without requiring a new launch.

A creator who builds 10 products and sells them individually earns the sum of 10 separate small markets. A creator who builds 10 products and organizes them into 2-3 bundles earns from those small markets plus the buyers who only engage at the bundle level. The catalog compounds in a way that individual products never do.

## Chapter 2: The Three Bundle Architectures

Not all bundles are the same. There are three fundamentally different structures, and each serves a different buyer, a different traffic source, and a different position in your product line.

### Architecture 1: The Depth Bundle

The Depth Bundle takes a single topic and goes wide within it. Every component in the bundle addresses the same core problem from a different angle.

Example: A "Complete Email Marketing Bundle" might include:
- The Email List Blueprint (building from zero)
- The Email Copywriting Playbook (writing sequences that convert)
- The Newsletter Revenue System (turning a list into recurring income)
- The Email Automation Guide (setting up flows that run without you)

The buyer who wants this bundle has a single clear outcome in mind: build a profitable email business. Each product addresses a different phase or dimension of that outcome.

Depth Bundles are ideal when:
- You have 3-5 products on the same topic
- Your audience has a well-defined outcome they are working toward
- You want to position yourself as the go-to authority on a specific subject

Price range: 2.5x to 4x the price of your most expensive individual component.

### Architecture 2: The Stage Bundle

The Stage Bundle follows a progression. It is a curriculum rather than a collection. Each component represents a phase in a journey from point A to point B.

Example: A "Zero to $5K Digital Creator" Stage Bundle might include:
- Phase 1: Finding Your Niche and Building an Audience
- Phase 2: Creating Your First Digital Product
- Phase 3: Writing a Sales Page That Converts
- Phase 4: Building Your Email List and Launch Strategy
- Phase 5: Scaling With Bundles and Upsells

The buyer who wants this bundle is not an expert. They are someone who does not know what order to do things in. The bundle provides the map, not just the tools.

Stage Bundles are ideal when:
- Your audience tends to be beginners or intermediate practitioners
- You can sequence your products into a logical journey
- You want to sell a transformation rather than a collection of tools

This is the most powerful bundle type for premium pricing. When you sell a clear before/after transformation with a structured path, you are no longer in the information product category. You are in the mentorship category. That justifies prices of $97, $147, and beyond.

Price range: 3x to 5x your most expensive individual component.

### Architecture 3: The Toolkit Bundle

The Toolkit Bundle is horizontal rather than vertical. It groups products by buyer type or use case rather than by topic depth or learning progression.

Example: A "Solopreneur Starter Kit" might include:
- The Productivity Stack for Creators
- The Minimal Tech Stack Guide
- The $0 to $1K Digital Product Roadmap
- The Content Repurposing System
- The Freelancer to Founder Playbook

Each product targets the same buyer persona (an early-stage solopreneur) but covers a different domain: time, tools, products, content, and career positioning.

Toolkit Bundles are ideal when:
- You have products that serve the same buyer type across multiple topics
- You want to create an entry point into your product ecosystem
- You are building a brand around a persona (the solopreneur, the creator, the freelancer) rather than a single topic

Price range: 2x to 3.5x your most expensive individual component.

### Choosing Your Architecture

The most common mistake is trying to bundle everything together without a unifying structure. An incoherent bundle is worse than a single product because it signals chaos rather than expertise.

Ask: what is the one outcome a buyer wants from this bundle? If you cannot answer that in one sentence, your bundle is not ready.

The architecture you choose should follow from the answer. Specific topic mastery points to a Depth Bundle. Clear step-by-step progression points to a Stage Bundle. Serving a well-defined persona across multiple domains points to a Toolkit Bundle.

### Naming Your Bundle

The name carries more weight than most creators realize. A strong bundle name does three things: it names the buyer, names the outcome, or names the method. Ideally two of the three.

Weak name: "Creator Resources Pack." It names nothing.

Strong name: "The Solopreneur Systems Bible." It names the buyer (solopreneur) and implies comprehensive mastery (systems, bible).

Stronger name: "Zero to $5K Digital Creator Stage Bundle." It names the buyer, the outcome, and the architecture in one phrase.

You do not need to use the architecture label in the public-facing name. But you do need to be able to answer: who is this for, and what will they be able to do after buying it.

## Chapter 3: Pricing Frameworks for Bundles

Pricing is where most creators leave money on the table or price themselves out of the market. Both mistakes come from using intuition instead of a framework.

### The Anchor Stack Method

Anchor Stack pricing works by building the value display around the sum of individual parts.

Here is the structure:

List every component with its individual price:
- Component A: $22
- Component B: $19
- Component C: $24
- Component D: $18
- Total individual value: $83

Then price the bundle at a 40-60% discount from that anchor: $47 or $57.

The buyer is not paying $47 for unknown value. They are paying $47 for something worth $83. That is a clear, legible deal. The anchor price does the psychological work.

Important rule: every component must have a real standalone price. If you cannot sell Component D on its own for $18, do not list it at $18 in the anchor stack. Buyers will research, and inflated anchors damage trust.

### The Outcome Valuation Method

This method prices the bundle based on the value of the outcome rather than the cost of the components.

Ask: if someone fully implements this bundle, what is the conservative financial result?

A bundle about building a digital product business might conservatively help someone earn an additional $500-$1,000 in their first 90 days. Price the bundle at 5-10% of the lowest conservative outcome. On $500, that is $25-$50. On $1,000, that is $50-$100.

This method works best for Stage Bundles where you can name a specific before/after transformation. "Take someone from zero to their first $1,000 in digital product revenue" is a clear outcome with a clear value.

The limitation of this method is that it requires a credible outcome claim. If you cannot make that claim with confidence, use the Anchor Stack Method instead.

### The Comparison Anchor Method

This method anchors the price against alternatives the buyer would otherwise pay.

A business coaching call costs $200-$500 per hour. A structured course on the same topic costs $300-$1,000. Your bundle, which contains the distilled frameworks from both, at $97-$147, is a fraction of what they would pay elsewhere.

Use this method in your sales copy, not on your pricing page. It is a justification mechanism, not a pricing mechanism. It explains why your price is reasonable rather than helping you decide what price to charge.

### Price Point Guidelines by Bundle Type

These are working ranges, not rules. Test your own numbers against your audience.

Depth Bundle (3-5 components): $47-$97
Stage Bundle (4-6 phases): $67-$147
Toolkit Bundle (5-8 components): $57-$97

Entry-level bundles (fewer than 3 components, newer creator): $27-$47
Premium bundles (7+ components, established creator): $97-$197

### Psychological Price Anchors

Certain price points have behavioral weight independent of the comparison math. $97 converts differently than $100, even though $3 is trivial. $47 converts differently than $50. This is not irrational buyer behavior. It is pattern recognition: rounded prices read as estimated, precise prices read as calculated.

Use odd prices that end in 7 or 9 for your bundles. Not because of folklore, but because buyers associate them with products that have been price-tested rather than arbitrarily rounded.

The exception is luxury positioning. If you are building a brand associated with premium quality, rounded prices ($100, $150, $200) signal confidence and reduce the "discount product" association. Match your price format to your brand position.

### What Kills Bundle Conversion at the Pricing Stage

Two mistakes:

Pricing too low signals low quality. If your 5-component bundle is $29, the buyer assumes the components are not worth much individually. The bundle price implies a floor on perceived quality.

Pricing too high without matching the signal of value. A $197 bundle from an unknown creator with no social proof will stall. The price requires trust infrastructure: testimonials, a track record, or a very strong Outcome Valuation argument.

Match your price to your trust level, not just your content volume.

## Chapter 4: Bundle Page Design That Converts

A bundle page fails for one of three reasons: the offer is unclear, the value is not visible, or the risk feels too high. Every design decision on your page should address one of these three problems.

### The Six-Section Bundle Page

**Section 1: The Thesis Headline**

Your headline should name the transformation, not the contents. "Five Guides That Will Teach You Email Marketing" is a content headline. "Build a Profitable Email Business From Zero in 90 Days" is a transformation headline.

The transformation headline does two things: it filters for buyers who want that specific outcome, and it frames everything that follows as steps toward that outcome rather than items in a list.

Keep it under 12 words. Use plain language. Avoid cleverness that requires decoding.

**Section 2: The Problem Frame**

Before introducing your solution, name the problem with precision. This is the section where the right buyer recognizes themselves.

Write two to four short paragraphs (2-3 sentences each) that describe:
- What they are currently doing that is not working
- Why the typical advice fails them
- What they actually need

Do not sell here. Diagnose. The reader who sees their problem named accurately will trust your solution more readily.

**Section 3: The Bundle Overview**

This is where you introduce the bundle as a system, not a list. Lead with the outcome it produces. Then walk through each component with a one-sentence description of what it does and why it is included.

Do not pad component descriptions. One sharp sentence beats three vague ones. If you cannot say why a component is included in one sentence, the component might not belong in the bundle.

**Section 4: What Is Inside (Detailed Breakdown)**

After the overview, go deeper on each component. Two to four bullet points per component covering:
- The core concept it teaches
- The specific result it produces
- One thing the buyer will be able to do after going through it

Keep each bullet under 20 words. The buyer is scanning, not reading.

**Section 5: Social Proof**

If you have testimonials, place them here, after the detail and before the price. Testimonials at the beginning read as advertising. Testimonials after the product detail read as confirmation.

If you do not have testimonials yet, use specificity as a substitute. "This framework helped me build a $3,000/month digital product business in six months" from your own experience carries more weight than vague claims about transformational results.

**Section 6: The Price Block**

Your price block should contain four elements:
- The anchor stack (individual prices adding to the total)
- The bundle price, large and clearly displayed
- A single, specific CTA button ("Get the Bundle for $97")
- A brief risk reversal statement (refund policy or satisfaction guarantee)

Do not put multiple CTA buttons with different prices here. One decision, one action.

### Visual Design Principles for Bundle Pages

The design should not compete with the copy. Use high-contrast text (white or near-white on dark, or near-black on light), minimal color (one accent, one CTA color), and no decorative elements that do not carry information.

Spacing is your primary design tool. Short paragraphs with real whitespace between them read faster and retain more information than dense blocks. Every paragraph should be able to stand alone as a unit of information.

Mobile is where most of your buyers will see the page first. Test on mobile before you test anything else. A page that works on mobile and breaks on desktop is better than the reverse.

**Typography hierarchy:** One clear size for headlines, one for subheads, one for body text. No more than two font families. The goal is that a reader's eye knows where to go without any effort.

**Color usage:** Your accent color belongs on key words in headlines and section headers. Your CTA color belongs only on buttons. Using them elsewhere dilutes their signal value.

**The scroll test:** Every section of your page should answer one question as the buyer scrolls down. Headline: what is this? Problem: is this for me? Overview: what do I get? Detail: why is it worth the price? Proof: does it work? Price: what do I pay? If any section does not answer a clear question, it is filler and should be removed.

## Chapter 5: The Launch Sequence for Solopreneurs

A launch is not an event. It is a sequence of reveals designed to build desire before the offer becomes available.

This sequence works with or without an existing audience. It is built around content, not email list size.

### Phase 1: The Problem Post (Days 1-3)

Before you mention the bundle, publish one piece of content that names the problem the bundle solves. Do not mention a product. Just describe the problem with precision and explain why it is harder to solve than most creators realize.

This post does two things: it attracts people who have the problem, and it positions you as someone who understands it deeply.

Distribution: publish it to whatever channel you are already active on. Twitter, Instagram, LinkedIn, a newsletter segment. Wherever your smallest genuine audience lives.

### Phase 2: The Framework Reveal (Days 4-7)

Publish one piece of content that introduces your core framework. Give it a name. Explain the structure at a high level without giving away the full detail.

The goal of this post is not to teach everything. It is to make the reader think: "There is clearly more to this. I want the full version."

End the post with a signal: "I am putting together a bundle that goes deep on this. I will share it in a few days." This seeds anticipation without a hard sell.

### Phase 3: The Case Study Post (Days 8-10)

Publish one piece of content that demonstrates the framework in practice. This can be your own example, a hypothetical example with real numbers, or an anonymized story from someone you know.

Concrete numbers earn trust. "I used the Anchor Stack Method on my three existing guides, set the bundle price at $67, and made $890 in the first week without running any ads" is more persuasive than any abstract promise.

If you do not have your own numbers yet, be honest about that and use projections with clear reasoning. Fake credibility is worse than no credibility.

### Phase 4: The Launch Post (Days 11-12)

Now you announce the bundle. Your launch post should include:
- One sentence on what the bundle is
- One sentence on who it is for
- The price and what it includes (as a list, not prose)
- The direct link
- A clear deadline or reason to act now (early access price, limited time, launch window)

Keep the launch post short. People who have been following the sequence are already warm. They do not need a 1,200-word sales pitch. They need a clear path to buy.

### Phase 5: The Reminder (Days 13-14)

Post once more, 24-48 hours before any deadline you set. This is not a repeat of the launch post. It is a different angle: address the most common objection or hesitation you anticipate.

"I know $97 feels like a lot for digital content. Here is how I think about it." Then make your case clearly and concisely. One brief paragraph.

### Running the Sequence Without an Audience

If your social following is small or new, the sequence still works. It just works more slowly.

Publish each phase on multiple platforms simultaneously. Pinterest, Reddit communities (where relevant and permitted), Twitter threads, Instagram carousels. Each piece of content is designed to attract people who have the problem, not just people who already follow you.

The sequence builds a mini-audience in the 14 days before launch. Some of those people will buy. More of them will return later when they see your next launch. The sequence is not just a launch tactic. It is an audience-building exercise disguised as a launch.

### After the Launch Window

Once your launch window closes, the bundle does not go away. It becomes part of your permanent product line. The launch sequence was a way to activate the people already paying attention. The permanent sales page is how you convert cold traffic over time.

This is why the bundle page quality matters as much as the launch execution. The launch gets you initial buyers. The page gets you all the buyers who come later.

## Chapter 6: Building Your Upsell Sequence

A bundle is not the end of the sales journey. For many buyers, it is the beginning.

The buyer who purchases a $97 bundle has already demonstrated something important: they trust you enough to pay for your work, and they care about the topic enough to invest in a substantial resource. That buyer is far more likely to buy from you again than a cold prospect who has never heard of you.

The upsell sequence captures that momentum.

### The Three Positions in Your Product Stack

**Entry Point (Free or Under $15):** A single focused resource that solves one specific problem. This is how cold traffic enters your world. The goal is not revenue, it is trust and email capture.

**Core Products ($15-$30 range):** Individual guides or playbooks. These serve buyers who want to address a single specific need. They also serve as components for your bundles.

**Bundle ($47-$147):** Your core revenue driver. Higher transaction value, lower transaction volume.

**Premium Offer ($97-$500+):** A done-for-you service, a consulting package, a deep-dive course, or a community. This is where the math changes dramatically. One sale at $300 equals the revenue of 12-15 bundle sales.

Most solopreneurs skip the premium offer because it requires more delivery. But the buyers who come through your bundle are the ones most likely to want it.

### The Immediate Post-Purchase Upsell

Immediately after purchase, before the buyer has closed the tab, present one focused upsell offer. Not three. One.

The rule for immediate upsells: it must be the logical next step from what they just bought.

If they bought a content creation bundle, the upsell might be your consultation session on content strategy, or your premium email course on audience growth. It should not be an unrelated product just because it exists.

Keep the upsell page short: what it is, who it is for, what it costs, one button. If they bought your $97 bundle, an immediate upsell at $47 (something they can add without major deliberation) will convert better than a $297 offer that requires a new level of commitment.

### The 7-Day Email Sequence

After purchase, every buyer should receive a 7-day email sequence. This sequence has two jobs: it helps them actually use what they bought (which reduces refund requests and builds loyalty), and it warms them for your next offer.

Email 1 (Day 0, immediate): Welcome and quick-start guide. Tell them exactly what to do first to get value quickly.

Email 2 (Day 2): The one thing. One high-leverage concept from the bundle, elaborated with a practical application. This teaches them something new even if they have already read the material.

Email 3 (Day 4): A result story. Your own or a customer's. Make it specific and brief. This reinforces that the bundle works.

Email 4 (Day 6): The next problem. Now that they are solving the problem your bundle addresses, what is the next problem they will face? Name it. Offer a resource (free or paid) that addresses it.

Email 5 (Day 7): Your premium offer. By Day 7, you have delivered value in every email. You have earned the right to make an ask. Present your highest-tier offer clearly and briefly.

### Building the Premium Offer From Your Bundle

If you do not yet have a premium offer, your bundle buyers can tell you what it should be. After the 7-day sequence, send one more email asking a single question: "What is the one thing you are still stuck on after going through the bundle?"

The answers cluster. Three people mention the same problem. That is your premium offer. Build it, price it at $200-$500, and offer it first to the people who told you they need it.

This is not a guessing exercise. It is market research that costs nothing and happens automatically once you have buyers.

### The Long-Term Sequence

Beyond 7 days, every buyer should receive your regular content. Not product pitches every week, but regular value that keeps you present in their attention. When you launch your next bundle or your next premium offer, they are already warm.

The long-term sequence is just your newsletter or content platform, consistently maintained. There is no trick here. Show up with value, over time, and your existing buyers become your best distribution channel through referrals and repeat purchases.

## Chapter 7: Common Bundle Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Knowing what not to do is as useful as knowing what to do. These are the most common failure patterns.

### Mistake 1: Bundling Without a Unifying Outcome

A bundle of your five best-selling products with no unifying thread is not a bundle. It is a discount pile.

Buyers do not purchase collections. They purchase outcomes. Before you name your bundle, name the outcome. If you cannot name it clearly, restructure or wait until you have the right components.

### Mistake 2: Pricing Relative to Effort, Not Value

"It took me 200 hours to make this, so it should sell for $200" is not a pricing framework. Buyers do not compensate you for your time. They compensate you for what they get from your work.

Price on value delivered to the buyer, not cost of production to you.

### Mistake 3: Underestimating the Sales Page

Creators who spend 80% of their time on product creation and 20% on the sales page consistently underperform relative to their quality.

A mediocre product with a great sales page will outsell a great product with a mediocre page. That is not an endorsement of mediocre products. It is a reminder that the page is part of the product. The buying experience is part of the experience.

### Mistake 4: Launching Without Warming the Audience

Posting "my new bundle is live, buy it now" to an audience that has never seen your work on this topic is asking strangers to trust you. The launch sequence in Chapter 5 exists specifically to address this.

If you have no existing audience, the warming sequence is even more important. Use content to earn attention before you ask for money.

### Mistake 5: Creating Bundles Before You Have the Components

Some creators plan bundles as their first product strategy, then build the components to fit. This often leads to rushed, shallow components that individually would not sell on their own.

Build your individual products first. Let the market tell you which ones resonate. Then bundle the resonant ones. The bundle should feel inevitable in retrospect, not forced in advance.

### Mistake 6: Ignoring the Post-Purchase Experience

The sale is not the finish line. The post-purchase experience determines whether the buyer becomes a repeat customer, a referrer, or a refund request.

A buyer who gets immediate value from their purchase tells other people about it. A buyer who feels confused or underwhelmed after purchase either requests a refund or disappears. A good onboarding email costs nothing to write and converts one-time buyers into returning customers.

### Mistake 7: Building Too Many Bundles Too Fast

More bundles does not mean more revenue. Each bundle requires its own sales page, its own launch sequence, and its own ongoing traffic. A creator with three well-promoted bundles will outperform a creator with twelve poorly-promoted ones every time.

Build one bundle at a time. Launch it fully. Optimize it based on real buyer feedback. Then build the next one. The compounding comes from depth, not breadth.

## Conclusion: The System That Compounds

The bundle strategy is not a single tactic. It is a system that compounds over time.

When you build individual products, you are building components. When you bundle components into coherent systems, you are building offers. When you launch those offers with proper sequences and follow-up, you are building a business.

Each component you create strengthens every bundle it belongs to. Each bundle you launch gives you social proof for the next one. Each buyer who goes through your post-purchase sequence becomes a more valuable relationship than a one-time transaction.

The math of digital products eventually favors everyone who builds systematically and does not quit. The problem is most creators quit before the math becomes favorable because they are grinding through single-product sales without ever restructuring their catalog.

Restructure now. You likely already have what you need.

The framework is simple enough to execute in a month. The principles are deep enough to build a business on for years. That combination is rare. Most tactics either require months of setup or stop working the moment the market shifts.

Bundling is structural. It works because it aligns with how buyers think, how trust is built, and how value is perceived. None of those things change.

Start with what you have. Build one bundle. Run one sequence. Learn from one launch. Then do it again with better information.

That is the whole system.

## Action Plan: Your 30-Day Bundle Launch

### Week 1: Audit and Design

Day 1-2: List every product you have (or are close to finishing). Write one sentence per product: what problem it solves and who has that problem.

Day 3: Identify which three to five products share a buyer persona or a progression path. That is your first bundle.

Day 4-5: Choose your bundle architecture (Depth, Stage, or Toolkit) and write the one-sentence outcome statement.

Day 6-7: Price the bundle using the Anchor Stack Method. Confirm every component has a real standalone price.

### Week 2: Build the Bundle Page

Day 8-9: Write the six sections of your bundle page in order. Start with the Problem Frame, not the headline. Write the headline last.

Day 10-11: Build the page on your platform of choice. Mobile-test before anything else.

Day 12: Add the price block, CTA button, and risk reversal statement.

Day 13-14: Review the page as a cold buyer. Read it aloud. Cut everything that does not advance toward a decision.

### Week 3: Pre-Launch Sequence

Day 15-16: Write and publish the Problem Post (Chapter 5, Phase 1).

Day 17-19: Write and publish the Framework Reveal (Chapter 5, Phase 2).

Day 20-21: Write and publish the Case Study Post (Chapter 5, Phase 3).

### Week 4: Launch and Follow-Through

Day 22: Launch. Publish your launch post across all active channels.

Day 23-24: Monitor. Answer questions. Do not tinker with the page unless something is clearly broken.

Day 25: Publish your Reminder post (Chapter 5, Phase 5).

Day 26-27: Set up your 7-day post-purchase email sequence (Chapter 6).

Day 28: Close your launch window if you set one. Send a final notice.

Day 29-30: Review your numbers. Sales, conversion rate if you can track it, refund requests. Note what to improve for the next launch.

Then begin your next bundle.
