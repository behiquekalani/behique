# The Solopreneur's Pricing Bible
### The complete guide to pricing your products, services, and knowledge as a one-person business.

---

## Introduction: The Single Highest-Leverage Decision in Your Business

Pricing is the most important decision you will make as a solopreneur. Not your niche. Not your content strategy. Not your product quality. Pricing.

Here is the thesis: two people with identical products, identical skills, and identical audiences can have a 10x revenue difference based on pricing alone. This is not a hypothetical. It happens every day. One creator sells an ebook for $9. Another sells essentially the same content as a $97 course. Same knowledge. Different framing, different positioning, different price. The second person earns ten times more per sale, works the same amount, and often attracts better clients.

Most solopreneurs systematically underprice. Not by a little. By a factor of 3 to 10. The reason is almost never market reality. The reason is psychology. They price based on what feels safe, what they think they deserve, what they imagine someone will pay rather than what the market will actually bear. They confuse their own discomfort with evidence.

This guide fixes that.

What you will find here is a complete pricing system for every part of a one-person business: digital products, services, consulting, bundles, and knowledge products. Each chapter is built around real numbers, real buyer psychology, and frameworks that you can apply immediately.

There is no fluff here. No motivational filler. Pricing is a craft, and this is the manual.

A few things this guide assumes: you are building or already running a one-person business. You have at least one product or service to sell, or you are designing one. You are willing to do the uncomfortable work of charging more.

That last point matters. The information in this guide is only useful if you act on it. Knowing that you should charge $500 instead of $50 for your consulting package is worth nothing if you revert to $50 the moment someone pushes back. Throughout this guide, you will find not just the numbers but the reasoning, so that when resistance comes, internal or external, you have something solid to stand on.

Let us begin.

## Chapter 1: The Psychology of Price

### What Price Actually Communicates

Price is not just a number. It is a signal. Before a buyer reads a single word of your sales page, before they understand what your product does, they look at the price and make a judgment about quality, legitimacy, and whether the product is for someone like them.

A $7 ebook signals: this is a commodity, a starting point, something the author did not believe in enough to charge real money for. A $97 course on the same topic signals: this is substantial, this is worth protecting with a real investment, this is for people who are serious.

Neither signal is inherently accurate. But both are real.

This is the foundation of pricing psychology: price creates expectation, and expectation shapes experience. Research in behavioral economics has consistently shown that people rate the same wine as better when they believe it cost more. The same medication works more effectively when patients are told it is expensive. The same coaching session is remembered as more valuable when the client paid $500 for it versus $50.

Your price is part of your product.

### Anchoring

Anchoring is one of the most reliable and well-documented phenomena in pricing psychology. The first number a person sees becomes their reference point for everything that follows.

In practice: if you show someone a $497 product first, then offer them a $97 product, the $97 feels affordable. If you show them the $97 first with no context, they may hesitate.

Applied to your business: lead with your highest-tier offer. Structure your pricing pages so the most expensive option appears first, or appears prominently enough to anchor the perception of value. Make everything else feel like a deal by comparison, not by discounting, but by contrast.

Example: A solopreneur selling business templates lists three tiers: $297 (premium), $97 (core), $27 (starter). A buyer who sees $297 first looks at $97 and thinks, "that is a much better deal." A buyer who sees only $27 thinks, "this is just another cheap template pack." Same product. Radically different buyer psychology.

### Perceived Value vs. Cost-Based Pricing

Cost-based pricing is the method most solopreneurs default to, and it is almost always wrong.

Cost-based pricing says: I spent 40 hours creating this course, my time is worth $25 per hour, therefore I should charge $1,000. Or more commonly: I spent 40 hours, I feel guilty charging more than $10 per hour for digital content, so I will charge $400 and feel okay about it.

The problem: buyers do not care what it cost you to create something. They care what it is worth to them.

Value-based pricing asks a different question: what outcome does this deliver, and what is that outcome worth to the buyer?

A guide that helps someone price their consulting at $200/hour instead of $80/hour delivers $120/hour in additional income. For a consultant billing 20 hours per week, that is $2,400 per week, or roughly $10,000 per month in additional revenue. What is a guide that delivers $10,000 per month in additional revenue worth? Certainly more than $49.

This does not mean you should charge $5,000 for a PDF. It means you should price relative to outcome, not relative to input cost. A $97 pricing guide that helps someone earn $10,000 more per month is a spectacular value. A $9 pricing guide priced on perceived "fairness" is a missed opportunity.

### The Dangerous Myth of the "Fair Price"

Solopreneurs often set prices based on an internal sense of fairness. They ask: what is a fair amount to charge for this? This question is a trap.

"Fairness" in pricing is a subjective, emotionally loaded concept that has almost no relationship to market value. When you ask what is fair, you are really asking: what price will not make people think I am greedy? What price will not make me feel guilty?

These are psychological questions, not economic ones. And they reliably produce underpricing.

The market does not reward fairness. It rewards positioning, perceived value, and the confidence to hold a price. Some of the most valuable products in the world are priced at multiples of their production cost. An iPhone does not cost $1,200 to make. A designer handbag does not cost $2,000 to produce. These prices are set based on brand, positioning, and what the market will pay, not on fairness.

You are allowed to do the same thing.

### Why Low Prices Attract Difficult Clients

This is the part most solopreneurs learn the hard way: cheap prices attract cheap clients.

When you price low, you attract buyers who are primarily motivated by the price. These are the clients who ask for refunds after consuming the entire product. These are the buyers who demand more for free because they "already paid so much." These are the consulting clients who nitpick every invoice, scope-creep constantly, and treat you like an employee rather than an expert.

Higher prices filter for buyers who have made a real commitment. Someone who pays $2,500 for a course shows up. They do the work. They get results and tell other people. Someone who pays $19 during a flash sale may never open the product.

This is not elitism. It is economics. Skin in the game matters. When someone has invested real money, their behavior changes. They protect their investment by taking action.

Charging more does not just increase your revenue. It improves the quality of every client interaction you have.

## Chapter 2: Pricing Digital Products

### The $9.99 Trap

The $9.99 digital product is the default price for creators who do not believe in their own work. It is the price that says: I am not sure this is worth real money, so I am making it cheap enough that no one can object.

There are exactly two situations where $9.99 is the right price for a digital product:

1. You are selling a loss leader specifically to build your email list, and you have a back-end product where you make real money.
2. You are selling to an audience with very limited purchasing power, and you have done the math on volume.

In every other case, $9.99 is a mistake. Here is why.

At $9.99, to earn $10,000 per month, you need to sell 1,001 copies per month. That is 33 sales per day. For a solo creator without a massive audience, that is nearly impossible to sustain.

At $97, you need 103 sales per month. About 3 per day. Achievable with a modest audience and solid marketing.

At $297, you need 34 sales per month. About 1 per day. Possible for even a small, engaged audience.

The volume trap is real. Low prices feel safer because each individual transaction seems more likely. But the total math almost always favors higher prices, because the total number of buyers required drops dramatically.

### The 3 Pricing Tiers and Their Buyer Psychology

Every digital product market has three natural pricing tiers. Understanding them changes how you position every product you create.

**Tier 1: $7 to $37 (Impulse/Entry)**

This is the "I will try it" price. Buyers at this tier are browsing. They make decisions in seconds. They are not deeply invested in the outcome. They buy on impulse, often without reading the sales page carefully. Refund rates are higher at this tier because commitment is lower.

Best for: simple checklists, short guides, templates designed for quick wins. Should be used strategically as lead magnets or entry points to a larger product ecosystem. If this is your only offer, you are leaving most of your revenue on the table.

**Tier 2: $47 to $197 (Considered Purchase)**

This is the core of the digital product market for solopreneurs. Buyers at this tier read the sales page. They consider the purchase for a few minutes or a few hours. They have a specific problem and they are looking for a specific solution. Refund rates are lower. Engagement is higher. These buyers are more likely to implement and get results.

Most well-executed ebooks, guides, frameworks, and short courses should live here. $97 is the sweet spot for a substantial standalone guide. $147 to $197 works well for something with multiple formats: PDF plus video walkthrough, plus templates.

**Tier 3: $297 to $997+ (Investment Decision)**

Buyers at this tier treat the purchase as a real investment. They read the sales page multiple times. They may research you. They consider the ROI. They are serious. They show up and do the work.

This tier is accessible for courses, comprehensive systems, and cohort-based learning. The key to pricing in this range is not adding more content. It is delivering a clearer outcome. Buyers paying $497 are not paying for more hours of video. They are paying for a transformation they believe is worth $497.

### Testing Your Price Ceiling

Most solopreneurs never test their price ceiling. They set a price based on intuition or competitor research and never find out what the market would actually pay.

Here is a simple testing method:

Launch your product at a price 30 to 50 percent higher than what feels comfortable. Not reckless, but genuinely higher. Measure conversion rate. If conversion rate is above 1 to 2 percent on cold traffic, your price has room to go higher. If conversion is well below 0.5 percent and traffic is warm, the price may be too high. Adjust and re-test.

For service providers running a discovery call to close deals: quote 20 percent higher than your current rate on your next three calls. Record the responses. If no one pushes back at all, you are underpriced. Light pushback with most clients still closing means you are near the ceiling. Significant resistance with most clients declining suggests the price is above market.

Price testing is not a one-time event. It is a regular practice.

A concrete example: a solopreneur selling a $49 freelancing course on cold traffic converts at 1.8 percent. She tests $97. Conversion drops to 0.9 percent. Revenue math: at $49, 1.8 percent conversion on 1,000 visitors = $882. At $97, 0.9 percent conversion on 1,000 visitors = $873. Nearly identical revenue with fewer support requests and higher-quality students. She then tests $127. Conversion drops to 0.6 percent. Revenue: $762. She settles on $97 as the optimal price point. She now knows her ceiling.

### Price Anchoring for Digital Products

On your sales page, show the value before the price. Build the perception of worth before the buyer sees the number.

A simple structure: list what the buyer will be able to do after purchasing (outcomes). Calculate the economic or time value of those outcomes. Then present your price in contrast. "This framework saved me 6 hours per week. At a modest $25/hour, that is $150 per week, $600 per month. The entire course is $97." The price feels small against that context.

## Chapter 3: Pricing Services and Consulting

### The Three Models: Hourly, Project, Retainer

Every service business lives in one of three pricing models, and each has a different ceiling and a different set of trade-offs.

**Hourly pricing** is the most common and the least scalable. You trade time for money in the most direct way possible. The ceiling is set by the number of hours in a day multiplied by your rate. Clients can scope-creep you because every extra hour is billable. The advantage: simplicity. The disadvantage: you are constantly accountable for your time, which creates friction and micromanagement, particularly with clients who question every hour.

Hourly pricing also creates a perverse incentive: the faster you get, the less you earn. An expert who completes a task in 2 hours earns half what a slower junior earns for the same task. This is a structural problem.

**Project-based pricing** charges a flat fee for a defined deliverable. Better than hourly for most solopreneurs because it rewards efficiency. If you complete a $3,000 project in 6 hours, you earn $500 per hour. The risk is scope creep if the project scope is not clearly defined at the outset. The solution is a detailed statement of work with explicit change order policies.

Project pricing works best for clearly defined, repeatable deliverables: brand identity design, website builds, copywriting packages, SEO audits.

**Retainer pricing** is recurring monthly income for ongoing access, maintenance, or a set number of deliverables per month. The most predictable model for solopreneurs. The trade-off: you commit to availability over a period of time. The upside: $3,000 per month from three retainer clients is more stable than hunting three $3,000 projects every month.

Most experienced solopreneurs move toward a mix of project and retainer work, with hourly reserved only for overflow or advisory work.

### Value-Based Pricing for Services

Value-based pricing for services is the same concept as for digital products: price to outcome, not to input.

The method:

1. Identify the specific outcome your service delivers. Not "social media management." "Consistent, high-quality content that builds your audience and drives inbound leads."
2. Quantify that outcome in client terms. If consistent content generates 3 additional clients per month for the client at $2,000 each, you are worth $6,000 per month in direct revenue to that client.
3. Price at a fraction of that value. Charging $1,500/month for a service worth $6,000/month in client revenue is excellent value for the client and excellent pricing for you. Charging $500/month for the same service is leaving $1,000 per month on the table.

This requires a discovery process. Before proposing a price, understand what the client currently earns, what problem you are solving, and what the solved problem is worth. That conversation is not a nice-to-have. It is the foundation of every value-based proposal.

### The Effective Hourly Rate Calculation

Even if you do not charge by the hour, you should know your effective hourly rate for every project and retainer you take on. It is a diagnostic tool, not a pricing method.

**Effective hourly rate = Total project revenue / Total hours spent**

Track your hours for one month. Include every email, every revision, every call, every hour of project work. Calculate your effective hourly rate for each client.

What it reveals:
- Clients with low effective hourly rates are candidates for repricing or offboarding.
- Projects with unexpectedly low effective rates reveal scope problems or underpricing.
- Over time, your effective rate should rise as you systematize your process and increase your stated rates.

Real example: a freelance copywriter charges $1,500 for a website copy project. She tracks her hours: 2 hours of calls, 3 hours of research, 6 hours of writing, 2 hours of revisions. Total: 13 hours. Effective rate: $115/hour. She decides her floor is $150/hour effective. Her next website project quote is $2,000. She now tracks whether she can complete it in under 13 hours. Her systems improve. Her effective rate rises. Her prices stay justified.

### The Anchor Client Problem

Many solopreneurs have a client they have been working with for years at a rate that made sense when they started but no longer reflects their current skill level. This is the anchor client problem.

Anchor clients feel safe. They provide predictable income. They are familiar. But they are often paying 40 to 60 percent below market rate because the relationship was established before the solopreneur developed their current value.

The solution is a structured repricing conversation, covered in detail in Chapter 4. But the recognition comes first: if you have been working with a client for more than 18 months without a rate increase, you almost certainly have an anchor client problem.

## Chapter 4: The Price Increase System

### When to Raise Prices

The question is not whether to raise prices. The question is how often and by how much.

Practical signals that it is time to raise prices:

- **You are fully booked more than 2 weeks in advance.** Supply and demand. Scarcity has value. Price to create breathing room.
- **Clients accept your price with no hesitation whatsoever.** Some resistance is healthy. Zero resistance means you are underpriced.
- **You have learned significantly since your last price setting.** Every major skill advancement justifies a pricing review.
- **12 months have passed without any increase.** Inflation alone justifies 3 to 5 percent annual increases. Skill development justifies more.
- **Your best clients tell you they would pay more.** This happens rarely, but when it does, believe them.

How much to raise: for services, 10 to 25 percent per increase is sustainable and defensible. For digital products, testing a higher price point is lower-risk since you can revert if conversion collapses.

### The Announcement Method

Do not simply raise prices without communicating the change. The announcement method turns a price increase into a marketing moment.

Structure:

1. **Announce the increase with a deadline.** "Effective [date 4 to 6 weeks from now], my consulting rate increases from $150 to $200 per hour."
2. **Give a reason.** Not an apology. A reason. "I have restructured my client intake process, added a new onboarding framework, and my availability is limited. This reflects the current value I deliver."
3. **Offer existing clients the opportunity to book or prepay at the old rate before the deadline.** This creates urgency and often generates a burst of revenue.
4. **Hold the price on the announced date.** This is non-negotiable. If you announce a price increase and then quietly keep the old price, you signal that your prices are negotiable.

For digital products: announce a price increase on your email list with a 72-hour window at the current price. "This guide increases to $97 on Thursday. Current price is $67." This is a legitimate urgency driver that rewards your existing audience.

### Grandfather Clauses

When raising prices for service retainers, a grandfather clause protects long-term clients for a defined period.

Example: "Current clients will remain at their current rate through the end of Q2. Starting Q3, all rates will reflect the new pricing."

Grandfather clauses are a courtesy, not an obligation. They work best for clients who have been consistently respectful, on-time paying, and low-drama. Difficult clients who happen to have legacy rates do not deserve grandfathering. This is a business decision, not a loyalty reward.

Do not grandfather indefinitely. Set a clear end date. Permanent exceptions create a two-tier system that becomes harder to manage over time and signals to the grandfathered client that your rates are always negotiable.

### Handling Pushback

Price increase pushback takes two forms: emotional and logical.

**Emotional pushback**: "I feel like the price has gotten really high." "This is hard for us to justify in our budget."

Response: "I understand. The new rate reflects the value we are delivering together, and I want to make sure we are both in a position where this works long-term. If the budget does not support this, we should talk about what scope makes sense at what you are able to invest."

This response is calm, does not discount, and opens a scope conversation rather than a price negotiation. Reducing scope is legitimate. Reducing rate is a capitulation that damages your positioning.

**Logical pushback**: "I can find someone else who does this for less."

Response: "That is true, and that might be the right decision for your situation. My rate reflects the experience level and the outcomes I deliver. If it does not fit your budget, I am happy to refer you to someone who might be a better fit at this time."

This response is confident and non-defensive. It also eliminates the bluff. Most clients who threaten to find someone cheaper are testing whether you will fold. When you do not fold, roughly 70 percent of them stay.

## Chapter 5: Bundles and Tiers

### Why Bundles Increase AOV Without Discounting

AOV is average order value. It is the average amount each buyer spends per transaction.

The simplest way to increase AOV is to sell more products at once. Bundles accomplish this without requiring you to lower the price of any individual product.

The psychology: when you offer a bundle, the buyer perceives combined value larger than the sum of individual parts. A bundle of three $47 products priced at $97 appears to be a deal even though you are technically selling all three products at a discount. The buyer focuses on the total value ($141) relative to the bundle price ($97) rather than on the individual product value.

The key to effective bundling: select products with complementary use cases. "The Content Creator Bundle" combining a writing system, a content calendar template, and a growth playbook makes sense because they serve the same buyer for the same purpose. A random collection of unrelated products sold as a bundle does not convert because the buyer cannot immediately see how all the pieces fit together.

Bundle pricing guidelines:
- Bundle price should be 30 to 50 percent below the sum of individual prices.
- At least one product in the bundle should be a known, desirable product that drives interest.
- The bundle should have a clear theme or use case narrative.

### The Good-Better-Best Tier Structure

Tiered pricing gives buyers the ability to self-select their level of investment based on their current situation and confidence level. It increases total revenue by capturing buyers at multiple price points who would not buy at a single fixed price.

The structure:

**Good (lowest tier):** Core product. The essential information or deliverable. No extras. Appeals to buyers who are price-conscious or just beginning.

**Better (mid tier):** Core product plus one or two meaningful additions. Templates, bonus modules, an implementation checklist, access to a community, one async review. Priced at 2 to 2.5x the lowest tier. This is typically your highest-converting tier because it feels like the best balance of value and price.

**Best (highest tier):** Everything in Better plus high-touch elements: a live session, direct access, personalized feedback, a done-with-you component. Priced at 3 to 5x the lowest tier.

Example for a content strategy course:
- Good: $97 (core course, PDF guide)
- Better: $197 (core course, PDF guide, 90-day content calendar, template library)
- Best: $497 (everything in Better, plus one 60-minute strategy session)

Typical conversion distribution: 40 to 50 percent Good, 30 to 40 percent Better, 10 to 20 percent Best. The Best tier exists partly to anchor perception and partly because a meaningful minority will always buy the highest tier when it includes direct access.

### The Anchor Tier

The anchor tier is a pricing concept from behavioral economics. Its purpose is not primarily to sell at that price. Its purpose is to make other prices look more reasonable by comparison.

If you are selling a course at $297, adding a $997 tier (even if very few people buy it) makes $297 feel accessible. The buyer's reference point shifts. They are no longer asking "is $297 reasonable?" They are asking "is $297 a good deal relative to $997?" The answer is yes, and the purchase becomes easier to justify.

To make an anchor tier legitimate: it needs a real offer, not a fake price. Include something genuinely valuable and inaccessible at lower tiers: personal mentorship, a custom strategy session, quarterly access, a done-for-you component. The anchor tier buyer (roughly 5 to 10 percent of buyers) pays for the highest-value experience you can provide and subsidizes the lower tiers for everyone else.

## Chapter 6: Discounting Without Destroying Value

### When Discounting Is Strategic

Discounting is not inherently bad. Used strategically, it drives urgency, rewards loyalty, and clears inventory. Used carelessly, it trains buyers to wait for discounts, conditions the market to view your standard price as inflated, and attracts buyers who are optimizing for the lowest price rather than for the outcome you deliver.

Strategic discounting scenarios:

**Launch pricing**: New product, no social proof yet, audience does not fully trust the format or delivery. A launch discount (20 to 30 percent below future standard price) rewards early adopters who are taking a risk on an unproven product and generates initial reviews and testimonials. The key: announce that this is launch pricing and it ends on a specific date.

**Email list rewards**: Occasional discounts exclusively for your email subscribers reward loyalty and give people a reason to stay on your list. Keep these infrequent (once or twice per year) to maintain value.

**Affiliate or partner discounts**: A specific discount code for a partner's audience is not a general discount. It is a targeted offer to a specific warm audience. Acceptable and often effective.

**Seasonal sales**: One major sale event per year (Black Friday, a business anniversary) is a common industry practice. Buyers expect it and plan around it. More than one or two sale events per year begins to condition your audience to wait.

### Launch Pricing vs. Permanent Discounts

The difference between launch pricing and a permanent discount is intent and communication.

Launch pricing says: "This product is new. As a thank-you for early support and feedback, I am offering this at $67 for the first 72 hours. It will be $97 after that."

A permanent discount says: "This product is $97, but I am always running some promotion so it is always available for less."

The first creates urgency based on a real, time-limited condition. The second destroys the perceived value of the standard price.

Once a product has been available at a discounted price for more than 30 days with no end in sight, the market begins to treat the discounted price as the real price. Do not allow this to happen.

### The Coupon Strategy

Coupons serve two purposes: tracking marketing effectiveness and targeting specific audiences.

Tracking: create a unique coupon code for each traffic source (podcast appearance, newsletter feature, social media bio). Track which codes are used most. Now you know which channels drive the most conversions. This is valuable data.

Targeting: offer a coupon exclusively to your email list for a 48-hour window. Do not make the same coupon available publicly. This rewards your subscribers and creates a two-tier system where being on your list has real, tangible value.

What coupons should not do: they should not be permanently available or easily searchable. If someone can Google "[your product name] coupon" and find a current 50 percent off code, your standard price is irrelevant.

## Chapter 7: Pricing for Different Markets

### The Purchasing Power Parity Problem

If you sell primarily to a US or Western European audience and you expand to LATAM, Southeast Asia, or Eastern Europe, you face a real pricing challenge. The income and purchasing power in these markets is substantially different.

A $97 product is an impulse purchase for a professional in the US. It is a significant investment for a professional in Colombia, the Philippines, or Romania. The same buyer, with the same need, faces a very different psychological and economic barrier.

Ignoring this either leaves those markets entirely (lost revenue) or prices the product out of reach for most of the potential audience.

### International Pricing Strategies

**Option 1: Single global price with no adjustment.** Simple to manage. Loses sales in lower-purchasing-power markets. Acceptable if your primary audience is in high-income markets and the administrative complexity of pricing adjustments is not worth it.

**Option 2: Regional pricing tiers.** Platforms like Gumroad and Lemon Squeezy allow you to set regional prices. You might price a $97 product at $47 for purchases originating from LATAM countries. The buyer pays in their local currency at a price calibrated to their market. Your per-sale revenue is lower, but your total volume is higher.

Purchasing power parity (PPP) indexes are publicly available (the Economist's Big Mac Index is a simple approximation). A rough rule: if per capita income in a target market is 40 to 50 percent of the US, pricing at 40 to 50 percent of your standard price is reasonable.

**Option 3: Create market-specific products.** Instead of adjusting prices, create different products for different markets. A lower-priced version with less content, or a version delivered in the local language, addresses both price sensitivity and relevance. More work, but more defensible positioning since you are not selling the same product for different prices.

### Currency Considerations

Price in the buyer's local currency whenever the platform allows. A buyer in Mexico who sees $97 USD has to do currency conversion math in their head. A buyer who sees $1,900 MXN sees a locally-framed price that does not trigger the same mental conversion friction.

Most modern payment platforms handle currency conversion automatically. The psychological benefit of local currency pricing is worth the minor setup investment.

### LATAM-Specific Notes

For a Behike audience with significant LATAM reach: the freelance economy in Latin America is substantial and growing. The appetite for business education content is high. Purchasing power varies significantly by country: Argentina and Chile are closer to US purchasing power than Bolivia or Paraguay.

A practical structure for LATAM digital products: standard price in USD, with a documented policy that buyers in LATAM can request the standard price in their local currency via email if auto-detection does not work. This creates a human touchpoint and filters for buyers who are genuinely interested rather than price shoppers.

## Chapter 8: The Pricing Audit

### The Annual Pricing Review

Your prices should not be static. The market changes, your skills develop, your positioning evolves, and what was accurate pricing 12 months ago may be significantly off today.

An annual pricing audit is a structured review of every product and service you sell. It should happen once per year at minimum, with smaller reviews at major milestones (new product launch, major skill acquisition, significant audience growth).

The audit process:

**Step 1: List every product and service with its current price.**

**Step 2: For each item, calculate or estimate:**
- Conversion rate (what percentage of people who see the offer buy it)
- Revenue per month or per quarter
- Support burden (how many questions, complaints, refunds per sale)
- Time to deliver (for services)

**Step 3: Compare to current market.**
What are comparable products and services selling for today? Not just in your immediate peer group, but one tier above where you currently position yourself. What are more experienced practitioners charging?

**Step 4: Identify underpriced items.**
Any product with a conversion rate above 3 percent on warm traffic is potentially underpriced. Any service where clients consistently accept the price with no hesitation is underpriced. Any product you are embarrassed to share the price of because it seems "too low" is underpriced.

**Step 5: Create an adjustment plan.**
Not every price needs to change at once. Identify the two or three highest-impact changes and execute those first.

### The Competitive Analysis Method

Competitive analysis for pricing is not about matching competitors. It is about understanding the landscape so you can position intelligently within it.

Process:

1. Identify 5 to 10 direct or adjacent competitors in your niche.
2. Document their pricing for comparable offers.
3. Look for patterns: what do entry-level products sell for? What do premium products sell for? Is there a gap in the market (nobody serving the $150 to $200 range, for example)?
4. Position yourself intentionally. If the market is crowded at $47 and $297, and the $150 range is empty, that could be an opportunity.

The goal is not to be the cheapest or even the most expensive. The goal is to be priced at the level where the perceived value of your specific positioning is highest.

### The Elasticity Test

Price elasticity measures how sensitive demand is to price changes. In practice: if you raise your price 20 percent and revenue stays the same or increases, demand is inelastic, meaning buyers will pay the higher price. If revenue drops significantly, demand is elastic.

A simple elasticity test: raise the price of one product by 20 percent. Keep it at the higher price for 30 days. Compare total revenue to the previous 30-day period. Adjust based on results.

Run this test during a period of comparable traffic. Do not run it during a launch spike or a slow month. Normalize for traffic volume.

Most solopreneurs discover that their products are more price-inelastic than they expected, meaning demand holds even as the price increases. This is the empirical evidence that breaks the "but what if people stop buying" fear loop.

## Appendix

### Pricing Worksheet

Use this worksheet when setting a price for a new product or service.

**Product/Service Name:**

**What specific outcome does this deliver?**
Write one clear sentence: "This [product/service] helps [buyer] achieve [specific outcome] in [timeframe]."

**What is the economic value of that outcome to the buyer?**
Estimate in dollars per month or per year.

**What is the emotional/status value of that outcome?**
Is this about making money, saving time, avoiding pain, or gaining status? Qualitative only.

**What are 3 to 5 comparable products/services, and what do they charge?**
List them with prices.

**Based on the above, what is the minimum price that reflects real value?**
(Not what feels comfortable. What the outcome is actually worth.)

### AOV Calculator

Average Order Value = Total Revenue / Number of Orders

To increase AOV, choose one:

1. **Add an upsell**: Offer a relevant add-on immediately after purchase. Even a 15 percent upsell uptake increases AOV significantly. Example: if 15 percent of $97 buyers take a $47 upsell, AOV increases from $97 to $104.
2. **Create a bundle**: Combine 2 to 3 products at a price 30 percent below their sum. Buyers who would not buy individually often buy as a bundle.
3. **Add a higher tier**: A $297 "premium" version above your standard $97 increases AOV because some buyers always choose the highest tier.
4. **Add a low-friction add-on at checkout**: A $17 or $27 checklist, template, or companion guide at checkout is easy to add and captures incremental revenue.

**Monthly AOV target calculation:**
Revenue target / Projected number of buyers = Target AOV

If your revenue target is $5,000/month and you project 50 buyers, your target AOV is $100. Design your product suite to hit that number.

### Value-Based Pricing Formula Sheet

**For Digital Products:**

Value delivered to buyer (in dollars or hours saved) = X
Price = X multiplied by 0.01 to 0.10

A product that saves 5 hours per week at $50/hour = $250/week in value. Price: $25 to $250 (depending on depth and format).

**For Services:**

Monthly value delivered to client = Y
Retainer price = Y multiplied by 0.15 to 0.30

A service that generates $5,000/month in client revenue. Price: $750 to $1,500/month retainer.

**For Consulting:**

Annual value of problem solved = Z
Consulting fee = Z multiplied by 0.10 to 0.20

Solving a hiring process problem that costs a client $40,000/year in turnover. Consulting fee: $4,000 to $8,000.
