# The Creator's Substack Growth Strategy

**How to build a profitable newsletter from zero, without burning out or publishing for free forever.**

*By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike*

---

## Introduction: Most Substack Advice Is Wrong

Most newsletter advice starts with the wrong premise. It tells you to grow first and monetize later. Build an audience, then ask them to pay. Get to 1,000 subscribers, then launch a paid tier. Earn trust for 12 months, then reveal you have a product.

This advice works if you are willing to publish for free for a year or more and hope that an audience converts to paying customers at the end. Most people are not willing to do that. Most people run out of energy around month three and the newsletter dies.

The better approach is positioning, not patience.

The newsletters that convert at high rates do not convert because they waited long enough for people to trust them. They convert because readers genuinely could not find the same information anywhere else, the format matched how that reader wanted to learn, and the price was obvious given the value.

That is a product design problem, not a patience problem.

This guide treats your Substack as a product. It will show you how to position it before you publish your first post, how to build a recommendation network that fills your list with people who are already primed to pay, how to structure free versus paid content so the upgrade decision is obvious, and how to run conversions without sounding like someone who just read a copywriting course.

By the end you will have a clear architecture for a Substack that earns money. Not eventually. Within the first 60 to 90 days if you execute the system correctly.

# Chapter 1: Positioning Before Publishing

## The Question That Determines Everything

Before you write a single post, answer this question: Who is this newsletter for that no other newsletter serves well?

Not "who is interested in this topic." There are hundreds of newsletters about productivity, marketing, investing, and fitness. You do not need more interested readers. You need a specific reader who will pay because your newsletter fits their situation in a way that generalist publications cannot.

The most profitable Substacks are not the most popular ones. They are the most specific ones.

A newsletter about investing advice serves millions of potential readers. A newsletter about real estate investing for people who work full-time and want to buy their first rental property in the next 12 months serves far fewer, but those readers will pay $15 to $25 per month without hesitation because the advice is directly applicable to their exact situation.

Specificity creates perceived value. Perceived value creates willingness to pay.

## How to Choose Your Angle

You have an existing knowledge base. It accumulated from your profession, your interests, your failures, and your unusual combinations of skills. The newsletter topic that will work best for you is not the most popular topic. It is the topic where your specific knowledge base produces insights that someone with a more conventional background would miss.

Ask yourself three questions:

What do I understand about a topic that most people in that space get wrong?

What combination of knowledge do I have that most experts lack? A software engineer who also studies behavioral psychology understands habit design differently than either a software engineer or a psychologist alone.

What decisions do people make in this space where better information would change the outcome? Newsletters that help readers make consequential decisions convert better than newsletters that entertain.

Your answers point toward your angle. The angle is not the topic. It is your specific thesis about how that topic should be understood and acted on.

## Writing Your Positioning Statement

Before publishing, write one sentence that completes this structure:

This newsletter is for [specific reader] who wants to [specific outcome] and currently struggles with [specific obstacle].

Do not publish until you can write this sentence clearly without hedging. If you hedge, your positioning is not clear enough yet.

The positioning statement does three things. It tells a potential subscriber whether this is for them in under ten seconds. It guides every editorial decision you make. It becomes the framing for your paid offer.

## Competitive Landscape Research

Spend three to four hours reading the top five newsletters in your category. You are looking for gaps, not inspiration. What do they consistently not cover? What format do they use that you find unsatisfying? What type of reader do they seem to write for versus what type of reader would benefit from that topic?

The gaps you find are your whitespace. Your positioning lives in the whitespace.

Document what you find. This becomes part of your welcome email later, where you tell new subscribers what makes this newsletter different and why that difference matters to them specifically.

# Chapter 2: Architecture — Structuring Free vs. Paid

## The Core Mistake

Most Substack creators make paid access too complicated and free content too valuable. They put their best work behind the paywall and publish filler on the free tier. Subscribers can tell. The free tier signals what the paid tier will be like. If your free posts are not worth paying for, readers assume the paid posts will be similar.

The correct structure is counterintuitive: publish genuinely good content for free, then make paid content feel like a natural extension of what free readers are already getting.

## The Tiered Value Stack

Think of your newsletter in three categories:

**Orientation content.** This is free and public. It establishes your thesis, introduces your framework, and demonstrates your angle. These posts should be shareable and should bring in new readers who were not previously aware of you. Write 20 to 30 of these posts per year.

**Depth content.** This is where paid earns its value. Take the same topics from orientation content and go three layers deeper. Not longer for the sake of length. Deeper in terms of specificity, implementation detail, edge cases, and nuance that generalist coverage misses. Paid readers who read your free content first should feel like they just got the professional version of what they sampled.

**Community content.** This is optional but powerful. Behind the paywall, you can offer Q and A threads, working documents, templates, spreadsheets, or direct access. This is not content in the traditional sense. It is participation. Readers who get value from depth content will upgrade. Readers who value access to you or to other serious practitioners will upgrade for community.

You do not need all three. You need orientation and depth at minimum.

## The Upgrade Trigger

The paid conversion happens when a free reader reaches a point in a post where they feel a genuine deficit. Not artificial scarcity. Not a teaser that ends right before the good part. A natural boundary where the free version covers the concept and the paid version covers the implementation.

A free post might explain why most people price their digital products wrong. It articulates the mental model. It gives readers a new way to think about pricing. The paid version of that post shows the actual pricing methodology, the calculation spreadsheet, and three specific case studies with numbers.

The free reader finishes the post understanding the problem better than they did before. The paid reader finishes the post knowing what to do.

That is the upgrade trigger. Concept free, implementation paid.

## How Many Posts Per Week

For most Substack writers, one post per week is the right cadence. Two is sustainable for some writers and unsustainable for most. Zero posts some weeks followed by four posts the next week is a pattern that kills subscriber retention.

The right answer is the cadence you can maintain without the quality dropping. Not the cadence that maximizes theoretical reach.

For a new Substack, publish weekly for the first three months before launching a paid tier. You need a body of work that demonstrates the value before you ask for payment. Twelve to sixteen free posts gives readers enough material to evaluate whether this newsletter is worth paying for.

# Chapter 3: The Import Strategy

## Understanding Substack's Import Feature

Substack allows you to import an existing email list. If you have subscribers on any other platform, Mailchimp, ConvertKit, Beehiiv, an old WordPress site, you can bring them over. This is one of the most underused tools in the Substack ecosystem.

The import feature does not automatically move people to your paid tier. It moves them to your free list, which is correct. You want existing subscribers to go through the orientation layer of your new Substack before you ask them to upgrade.

To prepare an import, export your existing list as a CSV with at minimum the subscriber's email and first name. Clean the list before importing: remove hard bounces, remove subscribers who have not opened an email in six months or more, and remove any email addresses that do not look like real personal addresses.

Importing a clean list of 500 engaged subscribers is worth more than importing a dirty list of 2,000.

## The Migration Email

When you import subscribers from another platform, send a migration email first from the old platform. This is not optional. You are moving people without their explicit permission and you owe them transparency.

The migration email should do three things: tell them who you are and why you are moving, explain what they will get on Substack that they were not getting before, and give them a simple opt-out if they prefer not to follow.

Keep this email short. Three to four paragraphs at most. The people who matter will follow. The people who do not want to follow should not be on your new list anyway. An imported list of readers who want to be there converts to paid at far higher rates than a larger list of people who were moved without warning.

## Migrating Content

Substack allows you to import your archive from a previous newsletter or blog. If you have existing posts that align with your new Substack's positioning, import them. This gives new subscribers immediate depth to explore and signals that you have been publishing consistently, not just getting started.

Only import content that matches your current positioning. Do not import old posts that belong to a different audience or topic. The archive shapes how new subscribers understand what your newsletter is about. A coherent archive makes the case for a paid subscription. A mixed archive creates confusion.

## Cross-Platform List Building Before Launch

If you do not have an existing list, build one before you launch your Substack. Create a simple landing page, Substack's own built-in referral page works, and drive traffic to it from every platform where you currently have an audience, even a small one.

Write a thread on Twitter or X about the problem your newsletter solves. Put the signup link at the end. Post a note on LinkedIn explaining your newsletter's angle. Tell people you are building something and invite them to follow from the beginning.

The goal is 100 to 200 subscribers before your first paid launch. Even 50 engaged subscribers who know why they signed up is better than 500 people who signed up for an unclear reason.

# Chapter 4: The Recommendation Network

## What the Recommendation Algorithm Rewards

Substack's recommendation system is the most powerful organic growth mechanism for newsletters in 2024 and 2025. When a new reader subscribes to a Substack, the platform recommends other newsletters. If your newsletter gets recommended by several Substacks with overlapping audiences, you can acquire hundreds of subscribers per month without paid advertising.

The algorithm favors newsletters that get recommended by multiple other publications. It is a network effect. The more newsletters recommend you, the more Substack surfaces you to new readers in that niche.

This means your growth strategy is not just about content quality. It is about building relationships with other Substack writers.

## How to Build Your Recommendation Stack

Start with 10 to 15 Substacks in your niche or adjacent niches. These should be newsletters with 1,000 to 20,000 subscribers. Too small and the recommendation value is low. Too large and the writer has no incentive to reciprocate.

Read these newsletters genuinely. Not as competitive research. As a reader who is curious about their work. Reply to their posts. Leave substantive comments. Reference their work in your own posts with attribution. Do this for four to eight weeks before you approach anyone for a mutual recommendation.

When you do approach someone, do it in email, not in a comment. Keep it brief. Mention a specific post of theirs that you found valuable and why. Explain what your newsletter covers. Ask if a mutual recommendation would make sense given the overlap in your audiences.

Most writers who share an audience with you and have been building their own Substack will say yes. The recommendation feature costs nothing. It is one of the few genuine win-win mechanisms in newsletter publishing.

## The Right Number of Outbound Recommendations

You want to recommend newsletters that your readers will find valuable. Do not recommend newsletters just because someone asked you to. Your readers trusted you when they subscribed. If you send them to low-quality publications, that trust erodes.

Recommend a maximum of five to eight newsletters from your profile. Keep that list current. Remove newsletters that stopped publishing or drifted away from the topics your readers care about.

The writers you recommend publicly are part of your brand. Curate accordingly.

## Network Cross-Promotion Beyond Recommendations

Beyond the built-in recommendation feature, you can grow through explicit cross-promotion with other writers. This means:

Newsletter swaps where you both feature the other's newsletter in a dedicated section of your next post.

Guest posts where you write for another newsletter and they write for yours.

Joint posts where two writers collaborate on a single piece that gets published on both newsletters.

Collaborative posts work especially well when the two writers have adjacent but non-competing angles. A newsletter about content strategy and a newsletter about audience building have enough overlap to interest both audiences and enough differentiation to add new value when combined.

# Chapter 5: Paid Conversion Tactics

## The Right Time to Launch Paid

Launch your paid tier at 300 to 500 free subscribers. Not before. Not much after.

Before 300 subscribers, you do not have enough social proof and your conversion rate will be low regardless of offer quality. After 500 subscribers, you have been providing value for free long enough that free readers have anchored to zero cost. The longer you wait to launch paid, the harder the conversion becomes.

The 300 to 500 subscriber range is when readers know you well enough to evaluate whether your work is worth $X per month but have not yet settled into a permanent free tier expectation.

## Pricing Your Substack

Most Substacks undercharge. The default Substack pricing of $5 per month or $50 per year is a starting point, not a ceiling.

If your newsletter serves professionals and helps them make better business decisions, price at $15 to $25 per month or $120 to $200 per year. Professionals spend more on information that has a clear return on investment.

If your newsletter serves consumers and helps them with lifestyle, wellness, or creative pursuits, $8 to $12 per month is a better range.

The test is simple: after you announce paid, do the first five paying subscribers convert without hesitation? If yes, you priced correctly or slightly low. If everyone pauses and asks questions about the value before subscribing, you priced too high or the value proposition is unclear.

Do not lower your price to get more conversions unless the value proposition is genuinely unclear. Lowering price is a short-term fix that permanently anchors readers to a lower price point.

## The Launch Email Sequence

When you launch your paid tier, send three emails over one week:

**Email 1: The announcement.** What you are offering, why you are launching paid now, and what paid subscribers will get that free subscribers will not. Keep this honest and direct. Do not write it like a sales email. Write it like you are explaining a decision you made.

**Email 2: The demonstration.** Publish your best work behind the paywall on day four or five. The post should be genuinely excellent, the kind of post that makes a free reader feel like they missed out on something real. This is not manufactured scarcity. This is just your best work first.

**Email 3: The final reminder.** On day seven, send a brief note reminding free readers that the paid tier is live. Include a short testimonial from an early paying subscriber if you have one. Do not beg. Do not add urgency that is not real. Simply remind.

The sequence works because it spaces out the ask. Not every free reader sees every email on day one. Multiple touchpoints over seven days catch readers at different moments in their week.

## Annual vs. Monthly Pricing

Offer both annual and monthly. Price the annual plan at a discount that amounts to two months free. At $15 per month, the annual plan should be $150, not $120.

Most newsletters make the mistake of making annual too cheap. A large annual discount trains readers to wait for annual pricing before subscribing. A smaller discount still incentivizes the annual commitment without training readers to delay.

Aim for 40 to 50 percent of your paid subscribers on annual plans. Annual subscribers have dramatically lower churn than monthly subscribers. One annual subscriber who stays for three years is worth more than six monthly subscribers who each stay for six months.

## The Founding Member Tier

For your launch week only, offer a Founding Member tier at a higher price, typically two to three times your regular annual rate. Founding Members get everything paid subscribers get plus a direct acknowledgment in the newsletter, access to an annual Q and A, and the knowledge that they supported the work at the beginning.

Founding Member tiers work because they give early adopters a way to express stronger support without you having to create meaningfully different content for them. The psychological value of being a Founding Member is real. Some readers will pay $150 per year for a newsletter they would also pay $50 for, simply because being a founding supporter matters to them.

# Chapter 6: Structuring Your Publishing Cadence

## The Three-Type Content Calendar

A sustainable Substack runs on three types of content rotating through the week or month:

**Cornerstone posts.** These are your signature pieces. They articulate your thesis, challenge a dominant assumption in your niche, or synthesize a framework that readers cannot find assembled this clearly anywhere else. These take more time to write and you publish fewer of them. Monthly for most newsletters.

**Current posts.** These respond to something happening in your niche right now. A trend, an announcement, a public conversation. They are timely and they show readers that you are paying attention. These can be shorter. Weekly or biweekly.

**Practical posts.** These give readers something they can do or use immediately. A checklist, a template, a step-by-step process, a resource list. These are often the highest-converting type of content because readers share them and return to them repeatedly. Every two to three weeks.

A good calendar for a weekly Substack alternates between current and practical posts on regular weeks, with a cornerstone post once per month.

## Batch Writing

Single-session writing for newsletters is unsustainable. The session takes too long, the quality varies too much based on your mental state that day, and missing one week creates pressure that can break a publishing streak.

Batch writing means writing two to four posts in a single extended session, then scheduling them to publish over the coming weeks. Most professional newsletter writers batch their content.

To batch write effectively, you need an idea pipeline. Keep a running document with potential topics. Add to it every time you read something interesting, have a conversation that reveals a gap in your niche's conventional wisdom, or notice something that does not add up. When you sit down to write, you are selecting from a backlog of developed ideas, not starting from scratch.

The pipeline should always have at least ten to fifteen ideas in it. When it drops below ten, spend thirty minutes replenishing before writing your next batch.

## The Reuse Protocol

Good newsletter posts have a lifecycle beyond the newsletter. A cornerstone post becomes a Twitter thread that drives new subscribers. A practical post becomes a standalone PDF that gets sold or offered as a lead magnet. A current post becomes a short video or podcast episode that reaches a different format preference.

Document your content reuse workflow before you publish your first post. Knowing that every piece will be reused changes how you write it. You write more precisely because precision works across formats. You write with clearer structure because clear structure converts to threads and outlines.

The newsletter is not the end point of your content. It is the source layer from which other formats are derived.

## Protecting the Cadence

The biggest threat to a newsletter is not quality. It is inconsistency. Readers are more forgiving of an imperfect post published on time than a perfect post published two weeks late.

When life intervenes and you cannot publish a full post, publish a shorter version. A brief note explaining what you have been thinking about, a few links with your commentary, or a question you are sitting with. These shorter posts maintain the cadence and often generate more replies than longer posts because they invite the reader into an open question rather than presenting a finished answer.

Never go silent for more than two consecutive weeks. After three weeks without a post, subscriber churn accelerates and new reader conversion drops.

# Chapter 7: Cross-Promotion and Distribution

## Where Your Readers Actually Come From

Substack readers come from a small number of sources: recommendations from other newsletters, your own social media presence, search (mostly Google for older posts), and word of mouth from existing subscribers.

Direct social media traffic is lower than most creators expect. A post with 10,000 impressions on Twitter typically drives 30 to 80 new subscribers. That is not nothing, but it is also not the primary growth lever.

The primary growth lever is the recommendation network combined with creating content that existing subscribers want to share.

## Building for Shareability

Most newsletter posts are not shared because they do not contain anything worth sharing. They contain good analysis and useful advice, but nothing that makes a reader think "I know exactly who needs to see this."

Posts that get shared fall into a small number of categories:

Posts that contradict a widely held belief in the niche with a clear and well-argued case. These get shared because readers want to show the contradiction to people who hold the conventional belief.

Posts that organize information that previously existed only in scattered form. These get shared because they save time for the recipient.

Posts that validate an experience that readers feel alone in having. These get shared because readers want peers to understand something about their situation.

Structure at least one post per month around one of these three shareability patterns.

## Guest Writing for Audience Building

Writing for other publications, other newsletters, podcasts, and YouTube channels is the fastest way to grow a newsletter audience outside of paid advertising.

When you write for another newsletter, you reach an audience that is already predisposed to read long-form content in your niche. They are exactly the kind of people who subscribe to newsletters. The conversion rate from guest posts to newsletter subscriptions is higher than from almost any other traffic source.

Pitch one guest post or podcast appearance per month. The pitch should lead with what you can offer the audience, not what you want. Explain the specific topic you would cover, why it serves their readers, and why you are the right person to cover it.

## Social Media as a Newsletter Driver

Your social media presence should exist primarily to drive newsletter subscriptions, not for its own sake.

This means every content format you use on social media should have a clear conversion path to your newsletter. Twitter threads end with a newsletter link. LinkedIn posts reference your newsletter for the deeper version. Short-form video content refers viewers to the newsletter for the written framework.

Do not build a social media audience independent of your newsletter unless you have a clear plan for how that audience eventually becomes newsletter subscribers or paying customers. Social media reach is rented. Newsletter subscribers are owned.

# Chapter 8: Monetization Beyond the Subscription

## Paid Newsletters Are Not the Only Revenue Model

Many successful Substack writers earn more from adjacent products than from subscriptions. The newsletter is the audience development mechanism. The products are where revenue scales.

Understand this architecture early: the newsletter builds trust, the product generates revenue, and the combination creates a business that is more durable than either component alone.

## Digital Products for Newsletter Audiences

Newsletter readers are high-intent buyers. They opted into your content, they read it regularly, and they trust your judgment enough to pay for the deeper version. They will also buy digital products that extend the value of your writing.

The digital products that convert best for newsletter audiences are:

Templates and frameworks that operationalize your newsletter's core ideas. If your newsletter is about pricing strategy, a pricing calculation template is a natural extension.

Deep-dive guides that go further than any single post. A guide covering a full system that your newsletter addresses one piece at a time. This is what you are reading now.

Courses that teach the methodology you write about. For newsletters that cover process-heavy topics, a self-paced course gives readers who want to go faster than the weekly cadence allows a way to do so.

Consulting or coaching sessions for readers who want your direct input on their specific situation. Priced per session, not as a subscription.

## Sponsored Content

Sponsorships become available once you have a list of 2,000 to 5,000 engaged subscribers. The rate you can charge depends on your niche, your open rate, and the specificity of your audience.

A newsletter with 3,000 subscribers and a 45 percent open rate in the B2B software space commands $400 to $800 per dedicated sponsorship post. A newsletter with 10,000 subscribers and a 25 percent open rate in a general consumer niche might command less.

The key metric advertisers care about is not list size. It is the number of people who actually open and read the email. Promote your open rate, not your subscriber count.

Only accept sponsorships from products you have used or would genuinely recommend. One bad sponsorship can permanently damage the trust that took months to build.

## Affiliate Partnerships

Affiliate revenue from products you recommend honestly inside your newsletter is the lowest-friction monetization available. You link to something you already use or recommend, and you earn a commission when readers purchase through your link.

The ethical requirement is disclosure. Always tell readers when a link is an affiliate link. Readers who trust you will not resent the disclosure. They will appreciate the honesty.

Build a short list of three to five affiliate partnerships in products directly relevant to your newsletter's topic. These become permanent links in your resource recommendations rather than one-off mentions.

# Chapter 9: Analytics and Iteration

## The Only Metrics That Matter

Substack provides open rate, click rate, and subscriber growth. These are the three numbers worth tracking regularly.

**Open rate.** Anything above 35 percent is good. Above 45 percent is excellent. If your open rate drops consistently below 30 percent, you are either attracting the wrong subscribers or the content is not meeting the expectation you set at signup.

**Click rate.** This tells you which posts drove readers to take action. If your click rate is consistently low, your calls to action are either absent or not compelling. If one post type consistently drives higher click rates, write more of that type.

**Subscriber growth rate.** Track new subscribers per week, not total count. Growth rate tells you whether your distribution channels are working. A plateau in new subscribers before 1,000 total subscribers means you need to work the recommendation network harder.

Do not track open rate on individual posts obsessively. One low-performing post tells you very little. Track three-month averages to see actual trends.

## When to Pivot

A pivot is warranted when you have published 20 or more posts and you cannot identify a pattern of what resonates. If after 20 posts you cannot answer the question "what kind of post does my audience respond to most," your positioning is not clear enough.

The solution is not more posting. The solution is revisiting your positioning statement, identifying whether the audience you have is the audience you intended to attract, and making one specific change to how you describe the newsletter to new readers.

Small positioning adjustments compounded over time move a newsletter from average to excellent. Large pivots usually confuse existing subscribers and reset growth. Make small, deliberate adjustments based on what the analytics reveal.

## The Annual Review

Once per year, do a full audit of your Substack. Read your ten highest-performing posts and your ten lowest-performing posts. Identify the pattern. Ask what the high performers share that the low performers lack.

Review your paid conversion rate. If it is below three percent of free subscribers, something in your free-to-paid architecture needs adjustment.

Review your churn rate. If more than five percent of paid subscribers cancel each month, either the content quality dropped, the cadence dropped, or the value proposition was never as clear as you thought it was.

The annual review is also when you decide whether to raise prices. If your newsletter has been consistently excellent for a year and your subscriber base has grown, a five to ten dollar per year increase is appropriate and most engaged readers will not leave over it.

# Chapter 10: The Long Game

## What a Substack Looks Like at Year Two

If you execute the system in this guide for 24 months, here is what is realistically achievable:

A free list of 3,000 to 10,000 subscribers depending on how aggressively you work the recommendation network and guest posting.

A paid conversion rate of three to seven percent, which puts paid subscribers between 90 and 700.

Monthly recurring revenue of $1,000 to $8,000 from subscriptions alone, plus additional revenue from digital products and sponsorships.

An archive of 50 to 100 posts that covers your topic comprehensively enough that new readers can spend hours exploring it.

These numbers assume one post per week, consistent recommendation network building, one guest post or podcast appearance per month, and a paid product launched in the first six months.

They do not require luck. They require the system executed consistently.

## The Compounding Effect

The most important thing to understand about newsletter growth is that it compounds nonlinearly.

Your first 100 subscribers take the most effort per subscriber to acquire. Every subsequent 100 are slightly easier because your back catalog does work, your recommendation relationships are active, and your reputation in the niche is building.

This means the worst time to give up is between month three and month six. The effort-to-result ratio looks terrible at that stage. It improves substantially between month nine and month twelve. And by month 18, a newsletter that has been running consistently feels almost self-sustaining because the recommendation network, the back catalog, and the word of mouth are all working simultaneously.

The writers who build profitable newsletters are not more talented than the ones who quit. They simply stayed in the game long enough for compounding to work.

## Your First Action After Reading This

The single most important next step is not to build a better landing page, not to research the right Substack configuration, not to draft the perfect positioning statement.

The single most important next step is to write your first post and publish it.

Everything in this guide becomes relevant after you have started. Nothing in this guide is useful before you start. The analysis, the research, the positioning work, all of it should take no more than five to seven days before your first post is live.

Build the newsletter the way you would build anything real. By doing the work before the work is perfect.

That is the system. Now execute it.

## Quick Reference: The Substack Growth Checklist

**Before Publishing:**
- Positioning statement written and clear
- Top five competitive newsletters reviewed with gap analysis documented
- First 12 posts outlined covering orientation, depth, and practical categories
- Import strategy ready if you have an existing list

**Months 1-3:**
- Publish weekly without exception
- Identify five to ten newsletters for recommendation partnerships
- Send migration email if importing from another platform
- Build idea pipeline to 20 or more concepts

**Month 3 Milestone:**
- Recommendation conversations initiated with at least five writers
- At least 100 free subscribers acquired
- Clear understanding of your two or three highest-resonating post types

**Month 4-5:**
- Launch paid tier at 300 to 500 free subscribers
- Send three-email launch sequence over one week
- Founding Member tier offered during launch week only

**Month 6+:**
- Guest post or podcast appearance booked monthly
- First digital product in development if subscription revenue is established
- Annual review scheduled
