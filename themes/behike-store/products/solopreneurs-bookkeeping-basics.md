# The Solopreneur's Bookkeeping Basics

**A simple bookkeeping system for solopreneurs who hate accounting**

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike

---

## The Thesis

Most solopreneurs avoid bookkeeping until it becomes a crisis. A tax deadline arrives, a client asks for an invoice history, or you look at your bank account and genuinely cannot explain where the last three months of revenue went. The avoidance is not laziness. It is a rational response to a system that was designed for accountants, not for one-person businesses.

This guide gives you a different system. One built around the reality of how solopreneurs actually operate. No accounting degree required. No expensive software required for most people in their first two years. Just a clear understanding of what you actually need to track, why it matters, and a workflow you can run in under two hours per month.

By the end of this guide, you will have a working bookkeeping system set up, a monthly maintenance routine you can actually stick to, and enough financial clarity to make decisions about your business with confidence.

## Part 1: What Bookkeeping Actually Is (And What It Is Not)

Bookkeeping is the practice of recording every financial transaction in your business. Money that came in. Money that went out. That is it. Everything else in finance and accounting is built on top of this foundation.

Bookkeeping is not:
- Filing your taxes (that is tax preparation, which uses your bookkeeping records)
- Analyzing your business performance (that is financial analysis, which also uses your records)
- Making investment decisions (that is financial planning)

When solopreneurs confuse bookkeeping with the more complex practices built on top of it, they avoid everything because it feels overwhelming. Start here: bookkeeping is just recording. Your records become the raw material for everything else.

### Why It Matters More Than You Think

Three reasons bookkeeping matters for a solopreneur specifically:

**Taxes.** Every legitimate business expense you fail to record is money you pay in taxes that you did not have to pay. A freelancer who earns $60,000 and fails to track $8,000 in legitimate deductions pays taxes on money they spent running their business. That is a meaningful amount. Bookkeeping pays for itself.

**Cash flow reality.** Revenue is not the same as cash. You can have a great revenue month and a terrible cash flow month because invoices are unpaid, subscriptions renewed at a bad time, or you bought equipment. Without records, you are flying blind on the thing that actually determines whether your business survives a slow month.

**Decision-making.** When you are deciding whether to raise your rates, take on a specific client, or invest in a tool, the answer is in your financial records. How much did you actually make last quarter? What was your effective hourly rate on that project? What percentage of your revenue went to tools and software? Without records, these decisions are gut feelings. With records, they are informed choices.

## Part 2: The Four Accounts Every Solopreneur Needs

Most bookkeeping guides start with the full chart of accounts from traditional double-entry bookkeeping. That is overkill for a one-person business under $200,000 in annual revenue. You need four categories.

### 1. Business Checking Account

Your primary account for receiving client payments and paying business expenses. This account should be separate from your personal checking. The separation is the single most important structural decision you can make as a solopreneur.

Why separation matters: every transaction in your business account is a business transaction until proven otherwise. Every transaction in your personal account is a personal transaction. This makes categorization trivial instead of requiring judgment calls on every single transaction.

If you are currently mixing personal and business finances, open a business checking account this week. It does not have to be a "business" account at a bank with fees. A free checking account at a credit union or online bank works. The separation is what matters, not the account type.

### 2. Business Savings Account

One account for taxes. You will transfer a percentage of every payment you receive directly to this account. It sits there. You do not touch it except to pay taxes.

The percentage to set aside depends on your tax situation, but for most self-employed people in the US, 25-30% of net profit is a reasonable starting point. If you are outside the US, research your country's self-employment tax rate and set aside accordingly.

The savings account removes the most painful part of being self-employed: the tax bill you did not save for. Pay yourself first on taxes, and it stops being a crisis.

### 3. Income Tracker

A simple record of every payment received. This can be a spreadsheet, a note, or a simple invoicing tool. What matters is that every payment is recorded with three pieces of information: the date, the amount, and the client or source.

Nothing else is required for the income tracker to be functional. You can add project codes, payment method, and invoice numbers later as your volume grows. Start with three columns.

### 4. Expense Tracker

A simple record of every business expense. Same principle as income: date, amount, and what it was for.

For expenses, you will also want a category column eventually. Common expense categories for solopreneurs:
- Software and subscriptions
- Equipment and hardware
- Contractor payments
- Marketing and advertising
- Education and training
- Travel (if applicable)
- Bank fees and payment processing fees
- Professional services (accountant, lawyer)
- Home office (if you work from home)

Start without categories if the setup friction is stopping you from starting at all. Add categories in month two.

## Part 3: The Monthly Bookkeeping Workflow

The goal of this workflow is to take your total bookkeeping time from "overwhelming and avoided for months" to under two hours per month. This is achievable if you run the workflow consistently. The work that takes two hours per month takes twelve hours when you have avoided it for six months.

### Step 1: Download Your Bank Statements (10 minutes)

At the start of each month, download the prior month's bank statement from your business checking account. Most banks let you export as CSV or PDF. CSV is preferable because you can import it into a spreadsheet.

Also download statements from any other accounts used for business: a business credit card, PayPal, Stripe, or any other payment processor.

### Step 2: Record All Income (15 minutes)

Go through your income sources for the month and confirm every payment is recorded in your income tracker. Cross-reference your bank statement with your invoicing tool or client list. Every payment should have a matching invoice or record.

Flag any payments you do not recognize. This is rare but happens, especially with platform payouts.

Total your income for the month. Write it down.

### Step 3: Categorize Expenses (30 minutes)

Go through every expense on your bank statement and categorize it. Mark each transaction as either:
- **Business expense:** Record it in your expense tracker with category
- **Personal expense:** Note it as personal (these are owner draws if you paid from business account)
- **Transfer:** If you moved money between accounts, note it as a transfer, not income or expense

This step takes the most time initially and gets faster every month as you recognize your recurring expenses and their categories become obvious.

Total your expenses for the month by category. Write it down.

### Step 4: Calculate Your Net and Tax Transfer (10 minutes)

Net for the month = Total income minus total expenses.

Take 25-30% of your net and transfer it to your tax savings account now, before you do anything else with the money. Not at the end of the quarter when you remember. Now.

If your expenses exceeded your income this month, there is no tax transfer. Note that the loss may be deductible and discuss with a tax professional during your annual review.

### Step 5: Reconcile (15 minutes)

Reconciliation means confirming that your records match your actual bank balance. Add up all the income you recorded. Subtract all the expenses you recorded. The result should match your current bank balance (adjusted for the opening balance and any transfers).

If it does not match, find the discrepancy. Common causes:
- A transaction you recorded twice
- A transaction you missed
- A bank fee you did not record
- A pending transaction that cleared later than expected

Do not skip reconciliation. It is the quality check that makes your records trustworthy.

### Step 6: File and Move On (5 minutes)

Save your completed records. Add the month to a simple P&L summary sheet (income, expenses, net, for each month in a year). This running summary is all you need to understand your business's financial health at a glance.

Total time: approximately 85 minutes for most solopreneurs. Under two hours. Every month.

## Part 4: Tools

### The Minimum Viable Setup (Free)

A Google Sheets document with three tabs:
- Tab 1: Monthly Income (date, client, amount, payment method)
- Tab 2: Monthly Expenses (date, vendor, amount, category)
- Tab 3: Annual Summary (month, total income, total expenses, net)

This is all you need for the first year of a solo business. It is not glamorous. It works, it is free, it is searchable, and you own your data.

A template for this structure takes about 20 minutes to build or you can find free templates by searching "solopreneur bookkeeping spreadsheet Google Sheets."

### When to Move to Paid Software

Consider paid bookkeeping software when:
- You have more than 50 transactions per month and categorizing manually feels like a real time drain
- You are paying a contractor or employee and need to track payments for tax purposes
- Your accountant requires software exports in a specific format
- You want bank-feed automation to reduce manual data entry

**Wave:** Free accounting software that handles invoicing, expense tracking, and basic reporting. The free tier is sufficient for most solopreneurs under $100K in annual revenue. Paid add-ons for payroll and payments processing.

**QuickBooks Self-Employed:** $15/month. Designed specifically for freelancers and solopreneurs. Automatically separates personal and business transactions from a connected bank account and estimates quarterly taxes. The tax estimation feature alone is worth the cost for many people.

**FreshBooks:** Starts around $17/month. Better invoicing workflow than QuickBooks and cleaner interface. Good choice if invoicing is a significant part of your workflow and you want clients to pay directly through the invoice.

**Notion + manual tracking:** Some solopreneurs build their entire financial tracking inside Notion. This works well if you are already in Notion daily and prefer consolidated tools. It requires more setup and lacks the automation of accounting software, but the familiarity reduces avoidance.

### Receipts and Documentation

Keep receipts for every business expense. In practice:
- Email receipts: create a folder called "Business Receipts [Year]" and move every business receipt there immediately
- Paper receipts: photograph them with your phone. Google Drive, Dropbox, or Apple Photos with an album called "Receipts [Year]" works. The photo needs to be readable, not beautiful.
- Subscriptions: the renewal email is your receipt. It lives in your email folder.

The IRS (and most tax authorities) require documentation for business deductions. A bank statement entry of "$12.99 to Adobe" is a record. The receipt that shows it was for the Creative Cloud photography plan is the documentation. You need both.

You do not need a physical filing system for receipts in 2026. Digital documentation is accepted. Keep digital receipts for seven years from the tax filing date.

## Part 5: Tax Preparation Without the Panic

Bookkeeping and tax preparation are different. Your bookkeeping is what feeds tax preparation. If your bookkeeping is current, tax season is a two-day process instead of a week of reconstructing months of records.

### Quarterly Estimated Taxes

In the US, self-employed individuals are expected to pay estimated taxes quarterly. Failure to pay quarterly can result in an underpayment penalty at the end of the year, even if you pay the full amount at filing.

Quarterly due dates: typically April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15 of the following year.

Your estimated payment should be roughly 25-30% of your net profit for the quarter. If you have been transferring to your tax savings account consistently, you already have this money set aside. The payment is just a transfer from savings to the IRS.

Use IRS Form 1040-ES to calculate estimated payments or use the EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System) to pay online. Most states with income tax have an equivalent system.

### Annual Tax Preparation

In January and February, you will need:
- A total of all income received for the year (from your income tracker or accounting software)
- A total of all expenses by category (from your expense tracker or accounting software)
- Any 1099 forms from clients who paid you more than $600 (clients are required to send these; cross-reference against your income records)
- Documentation for home office deduction if applicable
- Receipts for any expenses you intend to deduct

A good accountant or enrolled agent can typically prepare a Schedule C (the self-employment income form) for $200-$500 depending on complexity. If your business is simple (under $150K revenue, no employees, standard expense categories), tax software like TurboTax Self-Employed handles it for under $150.

The value of a real accountant is not the tax preparation itself. It is their knowledge of deductions you might miss and their ability to structure your business advantageously for future years. A one-hour consult annually often pays for itself in tax savings.

### Common Deductions Solopreneurs Miss

- Home office: if you have a dedicated workspace used exclusively for business, you can deduct a portion of rent/mortgage and utilities proportional to the office square footage
- Self-employed health insurance premiums: if you pay for your own health insurance, the premiums are deductible
- Retirement contributions: SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) contributions reduce your taxable income. Contribution limits are generous for self-employed people
- Professional development: courses, books, conferences related to your business
- Internet and phone: the portion used for business (keep this documented and reasonable)
- Bank fees and payment processing: every transaction fee Stripe, PayPal, or your bank charges you is a deductible expense

## Part 6: Reading Your Numbers

Once you have two to three months of records, you can start reading your business's financial health.

### The Three Numbers to Know

**Monthly net income:** Revenue minus expenses. Is this number growing, stable, or declining? A three-month trend is more meaningful than any single month.

**Effective hourly rate:** Take your monthly net income and divide it by the number of hours you worked that month. This is your actual hourly rate, not your stated rate. If your stated rate is $75/hour and your effective rate is $23/hour, you have a scope creep problem, a pricing problem, or a time tracking problem.

**Expense percentage:** What percentage of your revenue goes to expenses? For a service-based solopreneur, this should typically be under 30%. If your expenses are 50% of revenue, you need to audit what is eating the margin. Not all expenses are equal. A software subscription that directly enables client work is different from a subscription you forgot to cancel.

### Red Flags in Your Records

- A client who represents more than 40% of your revenue (extreme dependence risk)
- Month-over-month expense growth without matching revenue growth
- Subscriptions you cannot name what they do (cancel immediately; you will not miss them)
- Income that is inconsistent in ways you cannot explain (either a collection problem or a scope problem)
- A pattern of late-paying clients that is creating cash flow gaps

## The Starting Point

The perfect bookkeeping system you do not use is worth nothing. The simple system you actually maintain is worth everything.

Start here, this week:
1. Open a separate business bank account if you do not have one
2. Create your Google Sheets tracker with the three tabs described above
3. Record every transaction from the past 30 days
4. Set up a recurring calendar block on the first of each month: "Bookkeeping - 2 hours"
5. Transfer 25-30% of your current bank balance to a savings account as your first tax set-aside

That is it. You now have a bookkeeping system. It is not beautiful. It works. Once it is working, you optimize it. You do not optimize a system you have not started.

Your future self will thank you in April.
