# Solopreneur's Annual Planning System

**By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike**

---

## The Thesis

Annual planning fails for most solopreneurs because they borrow a tool designed for teams and try to force it onto a one-person operation. Corporate annual planning assumes delegation, departments, and quarterly reviews with accountability structures built in. You have none of that. What you have is time, attention, and the ability to make fast decisions. The system in this guide is designed for that reality, not the corporate one.

---

## Part 1: Why Most Annual Planning Fails

The failure usually happens in January, two weeks after the plan was made.

Here is the typical sequence. December arrives. You feel the pull to reflect. You open a notebook or a doc and write down revenue targets, content goals, fitness milestones, and a list of projects you want to ship. January 1st, you feel motivated. By January 14th, the daily grind has reasserted itself. The plan sits untouched. By February it is irrelevant.

This is not a discipline problem. It is an architecture problem.

The core issue: most solopreneurs plan at the wrong altitude. They set annual goals but do not build the intermediate structure that connects those goals to weekly action. The gap between "make $120,000 this year" and "what I do on Tuesday morning" is enormous. Without a bridge, the goal floats and the daily work drifts.

The second issue: annual plans are usually built around outcomes, not systems. "Launch three products" is an outcome. The planning question should be: what does the system look like that reliably produces three launches? When you plan systems instead of outcomes, you create something you can actually run day to day.

The third issue: most solopreneurs treat the annual plan as a fixed artifact. They build it once and expect it to survive contact with the year. It will not. The plan needs a review mechanism built in from the start, not added as an afterthought when things drift.

## Part 2: The 3-Horizon Planning Model

The foundation of this system is a three-horizon view of time. Each horizon has a different job and requires a different type of thinking.

**Horizon 1: This week and next week.** The execution layer. You are not making strategic decisions here. You are completing tasks, moving projects forward, and maintaining the systems you have already built. Horizon 1 is where the work happens.

**Horizon 2: The next 90 days.** The tactical layer. This is where you decide what to work on at the project level. Which product to build. Which channel to focus on. Which client relationship to develop. Horizon 2 decisions shape what ends up in Horizon 1.

**Horizon 3: The next 12 months.** The strategic layer. This is where you set the overall direction, identify the major constraints, and make decisions about the shape of your business and life. Horizon 3 decisions shape what you work on at the Horizon 2 level.

Most solopreneurs either live entirely in Horizon 1 (reactive, grinding) or try to jump from Horizon 3 directly to Horizon 1 (ambitious goal, no structure). The system in this guide operates at all three levels simultaneously, with each horizon feeding into the next.

The practical rule: you should be actively planning at each horizon, on a regular cycle. Annual review and planning for Horizon 3. Quarterly planning for Horizon 2. Weekly planning for Horizon 1. The cadences are different but they are all required.

## Part 3: Annual Theme vs Annual Goals

This distinction matters more than it might appear.

An annual goal is a measurable target. $150,000 in revenue. 10,000 email subscribers. Ship four products. Goals are useful because they create clear targets and allow you to measure progress.

An annual theme is a qualitative direction. "The year of distribution." "The year of depth." "The year of systems." A theme is not measurable but it is directional. It answers the question: what is the underlying orientation of this year?

The reason to set a theme in addition to goals is that goals are fragile. When circumstances change (a market shift, a personal event, a product that does not perform), specific numeric goals can become either irrelevant or demoralizing. A theme survives disruption. "The year of distribution" is still a useful orientation even if your original revenue goal becomes unreachable after Q2.

How to choose your theme. Start with a question: what would make this the most successful year I can look back on, regardless of specific numbers? Look at where you have been underinvesting. Look at the constraint that is most limiting your progress right now. Often those two things point to the same theme.

Your theme should be memorable enough that you can recall it without looking it up. Write it somewhere you see daily. It functions as a filter for decisions that arrive throughout the year: does this opportunity align with my theme? Does this distraction move against it?

## Part 4: Quarterly Rock-Setting That Actually Gets Done

The term "rocks" comes from the idea of putting the most important things in first, before the smaller items fill the space. For solopreneurs, a rock is a project or initiative that represents meaningful progress toward your annual direction.

The rule for quarterly rocks: three maximum. Not five. Not eight. Three.

This constraint feels uncomfortable because there are always more than three things you want to do. The constraint is the point. When you are forced to choose three things, you choose the three that actually move the needle. When you allow yourself eight, you diffuse your attention across all eight and none of them get the sustained focus they require.

How to set your quarterly rocks:

Start with your annual theme and your annual goals. What is the most important thing you could complete in the next 90 days that would move you meaningfully toward both? That is your first rock.

Ask the same question with that rock removed from consideration. What is the second most important thing? Second rock.

Repeat for the third.

For each rock, define what completion looks like. Not "work on my product" but "product is listed on Gumroad with a live landing page and three testimonials." The completion definition is what allows you to measure progress and know when you are done.

At the start of each week, ask: what am I doing this week that moves each of my three rocks forward? If a rock has nothing scheduled for the week, that is a signal, not a coincidence.

## Part 5: Monthly Sprint Planning from Annual Targets

The monthly sprint sits between your quarterly rocks and your weekly tasks. Its job is to translate a 90-day project into a 30-day action set.

The monthly sprint review and planning session should take no longer than 90 minutes. It has four parts.

**Review.** What did you complete last month? What did not get done, and why? Were the reasons external (unexpected events) or internal (avoidance, underestimation)? This distinction matters because the fix for each is different.

**Carry forward.** What uncompleted items from last month are still worth doing this month? Drop anything that no longer serves your quarterly rocks.

**Sprint plan.** Break each quarterly rock into the specific actions that need to happen this month. These are not big vague actions. They are specific enough that you could look at them on Monday morning and start immediately. "Write the first three chapters of the guide." "Finalize the pricing page." "Get five testimonials from beta users."

**Constraint identification.** What is the one thing that, if it does not happen this month, blocks everything else? Treat that item as the first priority of the month, not one item on a list.

The monthly sprint does not replace weekly planning. It provides the structure that weekly planning pulls from. Without it, your weekly planning is improvised and tends to drift toward whatever feels urgent rather than what is important.

## Part 6: Weekly Review System That Keeps You on Track

The weekly review is the operating system of the whole planning structure. Everything else is strategy. The weekly review is where strategy meets execution.

Run it every Sunday evening or Monday morning, before the week starts. It should take 30 to 45 minutes. Never skip it. A missed weekly review compounds into a lost week faster than any other single failure point.

The weekly review has six steps.

**Clear inboxes.** Email, notes, messages, physical desk. Everything gets processed to zero or scheduled. You are not responding to everything. You are deciding what each item requires.

**Review last week.** What completed? What did not? Honest accounting, no self-judgment. The goal is pattern recognition, not self-criticism.

**Review the quarterly rocks.** Are you on track? What has to happen this week to stay on track?

**Review the monthly sprint plan.** What is scheduled for this month? What needs to happen this week specifically?

**Calendar and commitment audit.** What is already on the calendar? What commitments need to be honored? Block the time for your most important work before the week fills in around the edges.

**Set the week's top three outcomes.** Not your task list. Three outcomes. "Sales page draft complete." "Five outreach messages sent." "Weekly video published." When Friday arrives, these three outcomes define whether the week succeeded.

The weekly review fails when it becomes a mechanical ritual instead of an honest assessment. If you are checking boxes without thinking, you are doing the form without the function. The thinking is the product.

## Part 7: How to Handle Plan Drift Without Abandoning the System

Plan drift is not a failure. It is a guarantee. Any solopreneur who claims their annual plan executed without deviation either planned too loosely or is misremembering.

The question is not whether drift will happen. The question is whether your system has a recovery mechanism.

There are two types of drift. **Tactical drift** is when you fall behind on specific tasks or projects. **Strategic drift** is when your direction shifts in response to market feedback, personal change, or new information.

Tactical drift is handled at the weekly review. You notice the gap, you adjust the sprint plan, you recommit. The system absorbs it.

Strategic drift requires a mid-year review. Most solopreneurs skip this. Do not skip it. At the six-month mark, sit down with your annual theme and annual goals and ask a set of honest questions. Is this still the right direction? What has the market told me in the last six months? What have I learned about my own capacity and preferences? What should I stop doing, keep doing, and start doing?

The mid-year review is not a sign that the plan failed. It is evidence that you are treating the plan as a living document rather than a static artifact. Plans are hypotheses. Evidence updates hypotheses. That is not failure. That is how intelligent systems work.

The one thing you should not do with plan drift: ignore it and hope you catch up. Unaddressed drift becomes a narrative that says planning does not work. That narrative kills next year's planning before it starts.

## Part 8: Revenue Forecasting Without Spreadsheet Obsession

Revenue forecasting for solopreneurs does not require a complex model. It requires three numbers and an honest assessment.

**Current monthly revenue.** What are you generating right now on average? Use a three-month rolling average if your revenue is variable.

**Revenue per unit times estimated volume.** For each product or service, what is the price and what is a realistic unit volume per month? Multiply these together and sum them.

**Growth assumption.** What are you doing specifically this quarter that would grow revenue, and what is a reasonable estimate of the uplift?

From these three numbers you can build a bottom-up forecast for the year that is grounded in reality rather than aspiration. The danger of annual revenue targets is that they are often set aspirationally, disconnected from any mechanism that would produce the result. A bottom-up forecast forces you to identify the specific drivers.

Run this exercise quarterly, not annually. Revenue drivers change. New products launch. Existing products plateau. A quarterly update keeps your forecast honest and keeps your quarterly rocks aligned with what will actually move the revenue number.

One principle worth holding: plan to the unit economics, not the top line. If you know your email list converts at 2% on a $97 product, you can calculate exactly what list size produces a specific revenue target. That is a cleaner planning question than "how do I make $150,000 this year?" The answer to the unit economics question is a specific number of subscribers. The top-line question produces anxiety and vague hustle.

## Part 9: The Year-End Review Process That Makes Next Year Better

Most solopreneurs end the year by looking at the revenue number and deciding whether it was a good year. That is one data point. The year-end review extracts every useful signal from the year so the next one starts better.

Run the year-end review in late December, before the new year planning session. Give it two to three hours. Do not rush it.

**The victory inventory.** List everything you completed, shipped, or achieved this year, in any category. Include small wins. The point is to build an accurate picture of your actual output. Most solopreneurs underestimate what they completed because they are focused on what they did not finish.

**The abandonment audit.** What did you start and not finish? For each item, assess honestly: did it die because of external circumstances, because you realized it was the wrong direction, or because you avoided the hard part? The third category deserves extended scrutiny.

**The revenue breakdown.** Where did the money come from? Which products, services, or channels generated the most? Which generated the least for the time invested? The revenue breakdown is a map of where to invest more and where to stop.

**The energy audit.** What work energized you? What drained you? This matters because sustainable performance requires building your work toward what energizes you and away from what depletes you. This is a slow process but it compounds significantly over three to five years.

**The relationship inventory.** Which collaborations, partnerships, or client relationships moved you forward? Which consumed more than they contributed?

**The lessons, stated as principles.** Not vague takeaways like "I need to be more consistent." Specific principles derived from specific evidence. "My conversion rate on products launched without a list warm-up sequence was 40% lower. Warm-up sequence goes in the standard launch process."

These principles become the operating rules for next year's planning session. They are more valuable than any goal you can set because they are grounded in what you actually learned.

After the year-end review, your annual theme, your annual goals, and your first quarterly rocks should write themselves. The data tells the story. You translate the story into structure.

---

## Closing

The planning system in this guide is not complicated. Annual theme. Annual goals built from unit economics. Three quarterly rocks. Monthly sprint plans. Weekly reviews. Mid-year course correction. Year-end synthesis.

What makes it work is not any individual piece. It is the connection between all the pieces. Strategy without execution structure is wishful thinking. Execution without strategy is busy work. This system provides both, at the right altitude, on a schedule you can actually maintain.

Build the habit of the weekly review first. That single habit, done consistently, will return more value than any annual planning exercise you have ever attempted.
