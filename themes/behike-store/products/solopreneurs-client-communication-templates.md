# The Solopreneur's Client Communication Templates

**Ten complete email templates with commentary that save 3+ hours per week without sounding like a robot wrote them.**

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin

---

## Introduction: The Hidden Cost of Winging It

There is a specific kind of dread that solopreneurs know. A client sends an email. It is a little ambiguous. A little loaded. You know you need to respond but you are not sure exactly how to say what you need to say without damaging the relationship, sounding defensive, or leaving the door open for a conversation you do not want to have.

So you write three drafts. You delete them. You let it sit for a day. You respond eventually, but by then you have spent forty minutes on an email that probably took the client four minutes to read.

Multiply that by every client interaction in a week. Inquiry follow-ups, kickoff emails, status updates, revision conversations, delivery emails, testimonial requests, payment reminders. If you are handling all of these from scratch, you are spending hours per week on writing that is functionally identical from client to client. The details change. The structure does not.

Templates solve this. Not by making your communication robotic, but by removing the blank-page problem from every routine interaction so your creative energy goes toward the work, not the administrative layer around it.

These ten templates are designed to be copied, customized, and sent. Each one includes the full text plus commentary explaining the choices. The goal is not just to give you language you can use today, but to help you understand the structure so you can adapt it naturally over time.

## Template 1: First Inquiry Response

**When to use it:** When a potential client reaches out for the first time, whether through your website contact form, email, or a referral introduction.

**What most people do wrong:** Either respond with a wall of questions that overwhelms the prospect, or respond with such a generic "thanks for reaching out, tell me more" that they feel like a number. Neither moves the conversation forward.

**The goal of this email:** Signal that you are serious, specific, and have already thought about their situation. End with a clear, low-friction next step.

---

Subject: Re: [their subject line or project description]

Hi [Name],

Thanks for reaching out. Based on what you shared, it sounds like you are looking for [brief accurate summary of their need in one sentence]. That is exactly the kind of work I do.

A few things I would want to understand before we go further:

- [Specific question 1 relevant to their project]
- [Specific question 2 relevant to their project]
- [Timeline or launch question]

If everything aligns, the next step would be a 30-minute call where I can hear more about your goals and you can ask me anything you need. I have availability [two specific time options within the next 5 business days].

Does one of those work for you, or would you prefer I send a link to my scheduling page?

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** Notice the structure. Paragraph 1 shows you read their message and understood it. The bullet questions signal professional thoroughness without overwhelming. The closing paragraph presents exactly one next step. The options at the end lower the friction on scheduling without making it feel like you are chasing them. The whole email should take the client about 90 seconds to read and less than that to respond to.

## Template 2: Discovery Call Confirmation and Pre-Call Questionnaire

**When to use it:** After a discovery call is booked. Send within an hour of booking, no later than 24 hours before the call.

**What most people do wrong:** Send a bare confirmation with no context. The client shows up to the call unprepared, which wastes the first ten minutes on questions you could have answered asynchronously.

**The goal of this email:** Confirm the logistics, set expectations, and collect the information you need to make the call immediately useful.

---

Subject: Confirmed: Our Call on [Day], [Date] at [Time]

Hi [Name],

Just confirming our call on [Day], [Date] at [Time] [Time Zone]. We will meet via [Zoom/Google Meet/phone] at [link or number].

This call will be about 30 minutes. My goal is to understand your project clearly enough that if we decide to work together, we can move quickly. Your goal should be to get a clear sense of whether my approach fits what you need.

Before we talk, it would help to know:

1. What is the primary problem this project needs to solve? (Not the deliverable. The underlying problem.)
2. What does a successful outcome look like to you 6 months after this project is complete?
3. Have you worked with someone on this before? What worked and what did not?
4. Do you have a timeline or launch date in mind?
5. What is your approximate budget for this project?

You do not need to write essays. A sentence or two per question is plenty. Reply to this email with your answers before our call and I will come prepared.

See you [Day].

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** Question 5 is the one people skip. Do not skip it. You need to know if the budget conversation is going to be a problem before the call, not on it. Asking it in writing, in advance, as one item in a list of five, removes most of the awkwardness. The person who cannot answer question 5 is usually not a qualified prospect. Better to know early.

## Template 3: Project Kickoff Email

**When to use it:** After a client has signed the contract and paid the deposit. This is the first email of the actual engagement.

**What most people do wrong:** Start the project without formally setting expectations in writing. Verbal agreements drift. Written agreements hold.

**The goal of this email:** Establish the operating structure of the project so neither party has to guess about communication, feedback, or timelines.

---

Subject: We are officially underway — [Project Name] kickoff

Hi [Name],

Your deposit has cleared and the contract is signed. We are officially underway.

Here is how this project will run:

**Timeline:** We are targeting [deliverable] by [date]. The key milestones along the way are:
- [Milestone 1] by [date]
- [Milestone 2] by [date]
- [Final delivery] by [date]

**Communication:** I will send a weekly status update every [day of week]. If something comes up in between, reach me by email at [address]. I typically respond within 24 hours on business days. For anything urgent, [phone/other method].

**Feedback:** Each milestone includes one round of revisions based on your feedback. Please consolidate your feedback before sending it. Multiple feedback rounds submitted separately slow the project and may affect the timeline.

**What I need from you:** To keep us on schedule, I will need [specific asset or information] by [date].

If anything in this email is unclear or different from what you expected, let me know before we get further in. It is much easier to clarify now than later.

Looking forward to building this with you.

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** The feedback clause is the most important sentence in this email. "Consolidate your feedback before sending it" is not just a request. It is the policy. State it clearly at kickoff, before it becomes an issue, and most clients will follow it naturally. The ones who do not will at least know they were told.

## Template 4: Weekly Status Update

**When to use it:** Once per week, on the same day and at roughly the same time, throughout the active project.

**What most people do wrong:** Either update clients too rarely (they get anxious and start pinging you) or update them too frequently with too much detail (they feel overwhelmed and stop reading). Weekly is the right rhythm for most projects.

**The goal of this email:** Eliminate client anxiety by making the project visible. Clients who feel informed do not interrupt.

---

Subject: [Project Name] — Week [N] Update

Hi [Name],

Quick update on where we stand:

**Completed this week:**
- [Item 1]
- [Item 2]

**In progress:**
- [Item in progress and where it stands]

**Coming up next week:**
- [Next deliverable or milestone]

**Nothing needed from you this week.** [Or: I will need [specific thing] from you by [date] to stay on schedule.]

Let me know if you have any questions. Otherwise, next update will be [date].

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** The "nothing needed from you" line is intentional even when it is true. It closes a cognitive loop for the client. They read the update and know they are done. They can file it and move on. If you do need something, be specific about what it is and when you need it. Vague requests sit in inboxes.

## Template 5: Revision Request Response

**When to use it:** When a client submits feedback that is either outside the agreed scope, excessive in volume, or being submitted in a way that violates the project structure.

**What most people do wrong:** Either accommodate every request without comment (setting a precedent that scope has no meaning) or respond defensively (damaging the relationship). The goal is to hold the structure while keeping the tone warm.

---

Subject: Re: [Project Name] revisions

Hi [Name],

Thanks for the detailed feedback. I have reviewed everything.

Most of what you sent falls within our agreed revision scope and I will have it addressed by [date].

A few items are outside what the original brief covered: [brief, non-accusatory description of out-of-scope items]. I want to make sure we get this right, so I have two options for you:

1. We address these additions under the original contract at [rate] for the additional work, estimated at [X hours].
2. We finalize the original deliverable as scoped, and I can quote you a separate phase for the additions.

Which direction works better for you?

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** The word "additions" is deliberate. Not "extra work," not "out of scope requests." Additions. It frames the situation as a natural evolution of the project rather than a conflict. The two-option close gives the client agency while making it clear that free unlimited changes are not on the menu. Both options are paid. That is not an accident.

## Template 6: Project Delivery Email

**When to use it:** When delivering the final project or a major milestone deliverable.

**What most people do wrong:** Send a bare download link with no context. The client does not know what they are looking at, how to review it, or what happens next.

**The goal of this email:** Make delivery feel like an arrival, not just a file transfer.

---

Subject: [Project Name] — Final Delivery

Hi [Name],

[Project deliverable] is ready. Here is what you have:

[Brief description of what is included, in plain language. Not a technical spec. What they actually have in hand now.]

**To access:** [Link, password, instructions]

**A few things to note:**
- [Any usage instructions or technical notes]
- [Anything they need to do within X days, if applicable]

**Next steps:** Please review and confirm receipt. If everything looks good, I will consider this project complete. If you have questions or notice anything that needs attention, reply to this email within [5-7 business days] and I will address it.

It has been genuinely satisfying to work on this. [One specific, honest observation about something that worked well in the project.]

Thank you for the trust.

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** "Within 5 to 7 business days" is a hard close on the revision window without sounding like a legal notice. Most clients will not read it as a deadline. But if a client comes back six weeks later saying something needs to be redone, you have documentation that the review window was communicated. The final two sentences are not filler. They are the human part of the email. Write them sincerely each time.

## Template 7: Testimonial Request

**When to use it:** Two to three days after project delivery, once the client has had time to use or review the deliverable.

**What most people do wrong:** Send a generic "would you be willing to write a review" email. Generic requests produce generic testimonials that say nothing useful. Useful testimonials are specific, and specificity requires prompting.

**The goal of this email:** Get a testimonial that is specific enough to convince the next client who is exactly like this one.

---

Subject: One quick question

Hi [Name],

I am really glad [project outcome described in specific terms].

I have a quick ask. If you have had a chance to use [deliverable] and you found it valuable, I would appreciate a brief testimonial for my site. Two or three sentences is plenty.

The most useful testimonials I have gotten answer this specific question: **Before we worked together, what were you worried or uncertain about, and after, what changed?**

You do not need to use those exact words. But if you can speak to that shift, it tends to be more useful to someone deciding whether to work with me than a general "it was great to work with [Name]."

Here is a link if you would like to leave it directly: [link]

Or just reply to this email and I will handle the formatting.

No pressure either way. Thanks for the project.

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** "Before and after the worry" is the magic question. It is specific enough to produce real content and emotionally resonant enough that it actually convinces prospects. Fear of a bad outcome is the primary reason prospects hesitate. A testimonial that names a specific fear and resolves it removes that exact barrier. This single framing change will improve your testimonial quality dramatically.

## Template 8: Payment Reminder, First Notice

**When to use it:** The day after a payment was due and has not been received.

**What most people do wrong:** Wait too long, or apologize excessively for asking. You are a business. Payment terms exist. Following up is professional, not aggressive.

**The goal of this email:** Surface and resolve the issue quickly while preserving the relationship.

---

Subject: Invoice [#] — just following up

Hi [Name],

Just a quick note that invoice [number] for [amount] was due on [date] and I have not seen it come through yet.

Sometimes these things get caught in email filters or lost in a busy week, so wanted to flag it.

If you have already sent payment, please disregard this email and let me know so I can check on my end. If not, [payment link or instructions].

Let me know if you have any questions.

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** The "caught in email filters" line does two things. It gives the client a face-saving explanation if they simply forgot, and it signals that you are not accusing them of anything. Tone here is intentionally casual. This is a reminder, not a warning. The warning comes later if needed.

## Template 9: Payment Reminder, Second Notice

**When to use it:** Five to seven business days after the first reminder with no response or payment.

**What most people do wrong:** Either back off completely (payment does not arrive) or escalate aggressively in tone (relationship is damaged). The second notice increases firmness without hostility.

---

Subject: Invoice [#] — second notice

Hi [Name],

Following up again on invoice [number] for [amount], originally due [date].

I have not heard back from my previous message sent [date]. I want to make sure there is not an issue on my end or yours that is preventing this from processing.

Could you take a moment to confirm the payment status? If there is an issue with the invoice, timeline, or payment method, let me know and we can work it out.

If I do not hear from you by [specific date, 5 business days out], I will need to pause any ongoing work until this is resolved.

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** "Pause any ongoing work" is a consequence, not a threat. State it plainly and specifically. Most non-paying clients are not malicious. They are disorganized, having cash flow issues, or waiting for someone else to approve the payment. This email surfaces the real issue so you can solve it, while also being clear that continued non-payment has a tangible effect.

## Template 10: Client Offboarding Email

**When to use it:** When a project is fully closed, payment is complete, and the working relationship is transitioning to post-project.

**What most people do wrong:** Let the client relationship go cold after delivery. The offboarding email is the last impression that determines whether this client refers other people to you and returns for future work.

**The goal of this email:** Close cleanly, plant the seed for the next engagement, and make the client feel like the investment was worth it.

---

Subject: [Project Name] — officially wrapped

Hi [Name],

With everything delivered, approved, and the final payment through, [Project Name] is officially complete.

A few practical notes:

- [Any access credentials, file locations, or logins they need to know]
- [Any maintenance instructions, if applicable]
- [Where to find documentation or support materials, if applicable]

It was a good project. [One genuine, specific thing you valued about working with them.]

I work with a small number of clients at a time, so I keep a short list of people I would want to return to for future phases or related work. You are on that list. If [related service or next logical project] ever makes sense, reach out directly and we will skip the formal inquiry process.

If you know someone who could use similar help, I appreciate introductions.

Thanks, [Name].

[Your name]

---

**Commentary:** "You are on that list" is the most important sentence in this email. It signals exclusivity without being manipulative. It is true: you do prefer working with clients you already know. It plants the idea of a return engagement without making the client feel pressured. The referral ask comes at the end, briefly, without being a full sales pitch. The client is leaving in a good emotional state. That is the right moment to ask.

## How to Use This Template Pack

Copy each template into your email client as a saved draft, canned response, or text expander snippet. Label them by the trigger condition so you can find the right one in under 10 seconds.

Do not send templates verbatim. Spend 90 seconds personalizing each one before sending. Change the specifics, add a detail from your actual conversation with the client, and adjust the tone to match the relationship. Templates are a starting structure, not a final product.

Track which templates you use most. The ones you use most are the ones worth refining over time. After six months, your adapted versions of these templates will be better than these originals, because they will reflect your voice, your clients, and your specific service context.

The time savings compound. Removing the blank-page problem from routine communication returns 3 to 5 hours per week to most solopreneurs who implement this fully. That is one additional full workday of client work per week, every week.

That is not a small thing.
