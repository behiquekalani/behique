# The Creator's Newsletter Decision: Substack vs. Owning Your List

**By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin**

*A practical guide for creators who are serious about building something that lasts.*

---

## The Real Question Nobody Asks

Most newsletter advice starts in the wrong place. It talks about open rates, subscriber counts, and which platform has the cleanest UI. Those things matter eventually. But before any of that, there is a more important question: who actually owns the relationship you are building?

When you publish on Substack, Substack knows your subscribers. When you publish on a self-hosted platform, you know your subscribers. That distinction sounds minor until you hit 10,000 subscribers and realize the platform can change its terms, throttle your reach, or sunset a feature you built your workflow around. At that point, it stops being a product preference and starts being a business continuity problem.

This guide is about making that decision clearly, without the usual platform tribalism. There are creators who belong on Substack right now. There are creators who should have left Substack six months ago. Understanding which category you fall into is the only thing that matters.

---

## What Substack Gets Right

Substack solved a real problem. Before Substack, a creator who wanted to start a paid newsletter had to: set up a Stripe account, configure a transactional email service, build a subscription management system, and maintain all of it indefinitely. That is a significant technical and financial burden for someone who just wants to write.

Substack collapsed that entire stack into a single sign-up form. That is not a small achievement. It is the reason Substack grew as fast as it did, and it is the reason it remains the right choice for a specific type of creator.

**Discovery is real.** Substack's recommendation system routes readers to new writers based on what they already read. This is not theoretical. Writers who publish consistently on Substack receive organic subscriber growth from the platform itself. No self-hosted newsletter gives you that. ConvertKit does not send your emails to potential subscribers. Ghost does not recommend your posts to its users. Only Substack does this, and for a creator in the early stages of building an audience, it is worth real money.

**Zero setup friction means zero excuses.** If the choice is between starting on Substack today or spending three weeks configuring Ghost, the creator who starts on Substack wins. Execution beats optimization at every stage of early growth. The creator who has 500 subscribers on an imperfect platform will always outperform the creator who has zero subscribers on a perfect one.

**Community features are genuinely useful.** Substack Notes, the chat functionality, and comment sections are built into the platform without additional configuration. For creators whose business model depends on community engagement, these features remove friction that would otherwise require a separate tool.

The honest summary: Substack is excellent at getting creators started and at giving them an initial distribution advantage. Both of those things have real dollar value.

## What Substack Gets Wrong

The problem with Substack is not that it is a bad product. The problem is that its incentives and your incentives diverge as you grow.

**You do not own the relationship.** When a subscriber joins your Substack, they create a Substack account. Their primary relationship is with Substack as a platform, and your newsletter is content within that platform. This matters because Substack can email your subscribers about other writers, recommend competing publications in your niche, and surface content from its recommendation engine inside your subscriber's inbox experience. You have no control over any of this.

When you export your subscriber list and move to a self-hosted platform, you take the email addresses but you leave behind the Substack account relationship. Some subscribers who engaged primarily because of Substack's interface will not re-engage on your new platform. This is not a catastrophic number, but it is real, and it grows with every month you spend building on top of Substack's infrastructure.

**Monetization flexibility is limited.** Substack's model is subscriptions. If your business model includes selling courses, digital products, one-time purchases, affiliate offers, or anything other than a recurring subscription, Substack creates friction. You are not prohibited from doing these things, but they require workarounds. ConvertKit, Ghost, and Beehiiv all offer more flexibility for creators who want to sell products alongside their newsletter.

**Your audience data lives on someone else's server.** Analytics, engagement data, behavioral signals, purchase history. On Substack, you see what Substack chooses to show you. On a self-hosted platform, you own the data and can use it however you choose.

**Branding is constrained.** Substack newsletters look like Substack newsletters. For some creators, that is fine. For creators who are building a distinct brand, the platform's visual uniformity is a limitation. Ghost and Beehiiv both allow far more design flexibility.

**Ghost** is right for creators who want full ownership, maximum design flexibility, and are comfortable managing a self-hosted installation or paying for Ghost's managed hosting.

## The Ownership Question: Why Platform Dependency Matters

Every platform company eventually makes decisions that benefit the platform more than the creators on it. This is not cynicism. It is the structural reality of venture-backed businesses with investors who want returns.

Substack has already changed its moderation policies, revenue share terms, and content guidelines since launch. Ghost has been acquired. ConvertKit rebranded to Kit. Beehiiv has raised multiple funding rounds. Every one of these companies is making decisions based on their business needs, not yours.

Platform dependency is a specific type of business risk. It means your audience access is contingent on a third party's continued operation, pricing, and policies. The larger your list, the larger that risk.

The creators who understand this think about their newsletter as an asset that should be under their control. Email addresses in a CSV file that you own represent a relationship you can take to any platform, any email service provider, any business model. Email addresses in a Substack database represent a relationship mediated by Substack.

This is not an argument for paranoia. It is an argument for making a deliberate choice about acceptable risk. A creator with 500 subscribers and no revenue has little to lose by staying on Substack. A creator with 30,000 subscribers and $10,000 per month in subscription revenue has a meaningful business that deserves more resilient infrastructure.

## When to Start on Substack: The Case for Ease Over Control

There are specific conditions under which starting on Substack is the right decision, even if you know you will eventually migrate.

Start on Substack if:

- You have not sent your first 10 emails yet. Execution matters more than infrastructure at this stage.
- Your niche overlaps with Substack's existing audience. Politics, culture, finance, and media commentary all have strong Substack communities that will generate organic discovery.
- You do not have a product to sell yet. If your only revenue model is subscriptions, Substack's tooling is sufficient.
- You want to test whether there is demand for your writing before investing in a more complex setup.
- You do not have budget for a paid email platform and cannot manage a self-hosted installation.

The key insight is that Substack is not a permanent mistake. Migrating off Substack is a solved problem. Thousands of creators have done it successfully. Starting on Substack and growing to 5,000 subscribers is far better than spending three months configuring Ghost and publishing nothing.

## When to Migrate Off Substack: The Signals That It's Time

There are concrete signals that the tradeoffs have shifted and migration is worth the friction.

Migrate off Substack when:

**You are generating meaningful revenue.** When your newsletter income is material to your finances, a 10% revenue cut to Substack becomes expensive. At $5,000 per month in subscriptions, that is $500 per month going to the platform. Beehiiv at $99 per month is significantly cheaper.

**You want to sell products other than subscriptions.** Courses, templates, consulting packages, digital downloads. If your business model has expanded beyond subscriptions, you need infrastructure that supports that.

**You are building a distinct brand.** If your newsletter is a flagship product with its own visual identity, Substack's uniformity becomes a constraint. Your newsletter should look like yours.

**Your list is large enough that discovery is no longer your primary growth lever.** Once you have established consistent growth through your own content, social channels, and word of mouth, Substack's discovery benefit is less valuable. You are paying in ownership for a distribution advantage you no longer need.

**You are building a business, not a newsletter.** If the newsletter is one part of a larger product ecosystem, it needs to be on infrastructure that integrates with the rest of your tools.

## Migration Mechanics: How to Move Subscribers Without List Damage

Migration feels scary because it involves risk to the list you have built. Handled correctly, the damage is minimal.

**Step 1: Export your Substack subscriber list.** Substack allows full exports from your dashboard. Download the CSV immediately and store it somewhere you own.

**Step 2: Set up your new platform before announcing anything.** Configure your new email setup, import your subscribers, and send a test campaign to confirm deliverability before touching your Substack at all.

**Step 3: Send a migration email from Substack.** Tell your current subscribers you are moving. Give them a direct link to subscribe on your new platform. Be specific about what is changing and why. Framing this as an upgrade to better serve them is more effective than framing it as a technical migration.

**Step 4: Import your Substack subscribers to your new platform.** Your new platform will let you import the CSV. This puts all your subscribers in the new system even if they do not actively click your migration email.

**Step 5: Send your first email from the new platform.** Make it excellent. First impressions on a new platform affect open rates in the weeks that follow.

**Step 6: Keep Substack active for 60-90 days.** Continue posting cross-listed content or simple redirects. Catch subscribers who missed the migration email.

Typical attrition during a well-executed migration is 10-20%. Some of those were inactive subscribers who were not opening anyway. Your engaged list usually survives intact.

## The Hybrid Approach: Publishing on Substack While Owning the List

The most pragmatic option for creators who want Substack's discovery benefits without full platform dependency is the hybrid model. This means:

- Publishing on Substack to capture its discovery and recommendation traffic
- Directing engaged readers to your primary platform for a deeper relationship
- Maintaining your own email list on a separate provider as the source of truth

This works because Substack allows you to embed links to external sign-up forms. A creator can publish on Substack, grow through its recommendation engine, and simultaneously funnel their best readers to a ConvertKit or Beehiiv list that they own outright.

The cost of this approach is complexity. You are managing two publishing workflows instead of one. For creators with systems and assistants, that is manageable. For solo creators with limited time, it may not be worth it.

The hybrid model makes the most sense when:

- You are in active growth mode and Substack discovery is a meaningful traffic source
- You have a paid product outside of subscriptions that requires your own email infrastructure
- You have already built a team or systems that can manage parallel publishing

The final answer on where to build your newsletter is not a platform answer. It is a business model answer. Figure out what you are building, what you need to own, and what tradeoffs you are willing to accept. The platform decision follows from that.

---

## Summary: The Framework for Your Decision

Start on Substack if you need to start today and have not yet validated demand.

Stay on Substack until discovery is no longer your primary growth lever and until your revenue makes the 10% fee meaningful.

Migrate when your business model exceeds what Substack supports, when your brand needs its own identity, or when the cost-to-benefit ratio tips.

Consider the hybrid model if you are in active growth and want discovery without giving up ownership.

The goal is not to be on the right platform. The goal is to build a list you own, an audience that trusts you, and a business that does not depend on any single company's continued goodwill.

That clarity is worth more than any feature comparison.
