## Introduction: Why You Are Leaving Money on the Table

Most creators price their digital products by asking one question: "What would I pay for this?"

That question is flawed from the start. You built the product. You know everything inside it. You remember the hours spent, the failures before the final version, the resources you consumed to get here. You are the worst person to estimate the value a stranger receives from downloading your work.

The result is predictable. Creators underprice by 30 to 60 percent as a default behavior. They set $7 prices on $47 products. They apologize in their launch posts. They add disclaimers. They discount before anyone even asks. And then they wonder why conversions feel hard, why buyers treat the product as disposable, or why their revenue plateaus at a number that looks nothing like the income they imagined when they started.

This guide is not about charging more for the sake of charging more. It is about understanding how price shapes perception, how humans psychologically interpret cost as a proxy for value, and how to build a pricing architecture that serves both the buyer and the creator.

You will leave this guide with:

- A working model of how buyers actually process price signals
- Specific frameworks for anchoring, bundling, and tiered pricing
- A method for testing prices without guessing
- The rules for urgency that converts without manipulation
- A system for raising prices over time without losing momentum

Let us start with the foundational truth that changes how you approach this entirely.

## Part One: Price Is Not a Number. It Is a Signal.

### Chapter 1: The Psychology of Price Perception

When a buyer sees your price, their brain does not perform a rational cost-benefit analysis. That framing, borrowed from economics textbooks, does not describe how purchasing decisions actually occur. The brain runs pattern recognition. It scans for signals about whether this product belongs in the category of "things worth my attention" or "things I can ignore."

Price is one of the loudest signals in that scan.

Research in consumer psychology, including work by Dan Ariely and Priya Raghubir, has repeatedly shown that price anchors quality expectations. A product priced at $9.99 arrives with an invisible label: "this is a low-cost item." A product priced at $97 arrives with a different invisible label: "this is something a serious person would buy."

This is not about deception. It is about signaling. And signals have to match the product's actual quality to work long-term. But the implication is critical: underpricing is not a safe or humble choice. It is a signal that works against you.

**What buyers infer from low prices:**
- The creator lacks confidence in the product
- The content is probably widely available elsewhere for free
- The product is untested or unproven
- The creator is desperate for any sale

**What buyers infer from appropriate prices:**
- The creator has conviction in the outcome
- The product is positioned in a premium category
- Other serious people are buying it
- There is likely a meaningful transformation or result inside

The buyer does not consciously think these thoughts. They feel them as friction or as pull. A low price produces friction: "is this worth my time?" A premium price produces pull: "this seems like the real thing."

### Chapter 2: The Anchoring Effect and How to Use It

Anchoring is one of the most studied phenomena in behavioral economics. It describes how the first number a person sees functions as a reference point that all subsequent numbers are measured against.

Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman's work on anchoring showed that even arbitrary numbers, completely unrelated to the product being evaluated, influence what people are willing to pay. The brain latches onto the first number it encounters and uses it as a gravitational center for its assessment.

For digital product creators, anchoring is a tool, not a trick. Here is how it works in practice:

**Scenario A: No anchor present**

You list your guide for $27. The buyer sees $27. They have no reference point except what similar-looking products cost elsewhere. If they have seen $7 guides on Gumroad, your $27 feels expensive.

**Scenario B: Anchor present**

You frame the guide as: "This replaces a $500 consulting call. It contains exactly what I would tell a client who paid me to solve this problem. The guide is $27."

Now $27 is measured against $500, not against other $7 guides. The perceived value gap is real. The buyer calculates what they are getting relative to the anchor. $27 feels like a bargain.

**Types of anchors you can use legitimately:**

1. **The consulting call anchor.** Compare to what it would cost to hire someone to solve the same problem. This works when you have real consulting experience.

2. **The course anchor.** Compare to what a full course covering this topic costs. "This cuts to the specific framework. Full courses on this topic run $300 to $1,000. This is the part that actually moves the needle, priced at $47."

3. **The time anchor.** Compare to the hours a buyer would spend figuring this out alone. "It took me 18 months of trial and error to build this system. You can skip that. $37."

4. **The crossed-out price.** Use carefully. Showing a previous or original price works as an anchor, but only when the original price was real. Manufactured crossed-out prices are psychologically effective short-term and corrosive to trust long-term.

The anchor must be credible. If you cannot defend it in a conversation, do not use it.

### Chapter 3: Why Low Prices Kill Sales

This is counterintuitive and creators resist it, so it is worth dwelling on.

Low prices do not lower the barrier to purchase. They raise a different barrier: the quality doubt barrier.

When a product is very cheap, the buyer's brain adds a tax that is not on the price tag. The tax is the cost of being wrong. "What if I spend $7 and this is garbage? Then I wasted $7 and an afternoon reading it." At $7, the perceived quality doubt is high enough that many buyers simply do not engage.

At $37, something different happens. The buyer has already mentally committed more. They feel the decision is weightier. They read more carefully before deciding. And when they do buy, they treat the purchase seriously. They implement. They get results. They tell others.

This is the low-price trap. It does not just reduce revenue per sale. It reduces the quality of buyers you attract, the engagement with the content, the results buyers report, and therefore the social proof you are able to build.

**The low-price trap in sequence:**
1. Price too low
2. Attract price-sensitive buyers who expect a lot for nothing
3. Buyers do not implement because the cost was not significant enough to create commitment
4. No results, no testimonials, no word of mouth
5. No word of mouth means more discounting to drive volume
6. Cycle repeats

The exit from this cycle is not a 20% price increase. It is a clean repositioning at a price that signals the product belongs in a different category.

## Part Two: Pricing Architectures That Work

### Chapter 4: The Product Ladder Model

A product ladder is a deliberate structure of products at different price points, each designed to serve a different level of buyer readiness.

The concept comes from Robert Cialdini's work on commitment and consistency, and from the business practice of segmenting buyers by where they are in their relationship with you.

**The basic ladder structure:**

- **Entry product ($7 to $27):** A single, specific, actionable product. One problem. One solution. Fast result. This product exists to create buyers, not profit. Its job is to convert strangers into people who have paid you money, experienced a win, and now trust you at a higher level.

- **Core product ($37 to $97):** Your main offer. This is where the majority of your revenue should come from. It solves a meaningful problem in depth and delivers a transformation that a buyer can point to.

- **Advanced product ($127 to $297):** A deeper, more comprehensive version of your core offer. This might include templates, workbooks, community access, or additional modules. It serves buyers who got results from the core product and want more.

- **Premium tier ($500+):** Consulting, done-for-you work, or cohort programs. Not every creator needs this, but it serves buyers who want the outcome and want direct access to you.

The ladder matters because it matches buyer readiness to price. A new visitor is not ready to buy your $297 product. They are not going to take that risk with someone they just found. But they might spend $17 on a focused guide that proves you know what you are talking about. Once they get a win from that, $97 feels reasonable. Once they get a win from that, $297 makes sense.

**Building your ladder:**

Start with your core product. Price it at $47 to $97 based on the depth of transformation. Then design backward: what $17 product would make someone ready to buy the core? What $9 product would make someone ready for the $17 product?

Design forward: what additional product would someone who got results from the core naturally want next?

### Chapter 5: Bundle Psychology and How to Structure Offers

Bundles serve two purposes. They increase average order value. And they solve a problem that single products cannot: the "do I need this specific one?" doubt.

When a buyer sees a single product, they evaluate whether that specific product solves their specific problem. They might decide it does not quite fit and leave.

When a buyer sees a bundle, their evaluation changes. The question becomes: "Is there enough value in this collection to justify the price?" The bundle makes yes easier because there is almost certainly something inside they can use.

**The four bundle types:**

1. **The starter bundle.** Three to five entry products grouped around a theme. Priced at 30 to 40% off individual prices. The discount is real, not manufactured. This bundle converts well for new audiences.

2. **The solution bundle.** Multiple products that together solve a complete problem. "Everything you need to go from idea to first sale." The frame is the complete journey, not a collection of things. This commands premium pricing because it promises completion.

3. **The companion bundle.** A main product plus supporting tools. A guide plus a spreadsheet plus a prompt library. The main product does the heavy lifting. The companions make it faster to implement. This bundle type increases the perceived value without requiring you to create entirely new core content.

4. **The vault.** All products you have created in a category, priced at one flat rate. This appeals to the buyer who wants comprehensive coverage and does not want to buy piecemeal. Vault pricing creates a sense of access rather than a transaction.

**Bundle pricing rules:**

- The bundle price should be at least 25% less than buying everything separately at full price
- The lead item in the bundle should be something the buyer already wants; the other items add value but are not required for the decision
- Name the bundle around the outcome, not the contents

### Chapter 6: The Price Tier Method

Price tiers, also called versioning, offer the same core content at two or three price points with different inclusions at each level.

This approach comes from the pricing literature on "good-better-best" structures, popularized in product design but directly applicable to digital products.

**Example structure:**

- **Tier 1 ($37):** The guide in PDF format. Core content. Complete transformation.
- **Tier 2 ($67):** The guide plus a Notion template plus a one-page reference card.
- **Tier 3 ($97):** Everything in Tier 2 plus three recorded walkthroughs and a private community.

The psychology here is dual. First, having three options makes the middle option feel reasonable by comparison. Tier 1 looks sparse. Tier 3 looks expensive. Tier 2 looks like the sensible choice. This is not an accident. It is the decoy effect, where a deliberately less attractive option makes a target option more appealing.

Second, tiers serve different buyer personalities. Some buyers want the minimum. Some buyers want everything. Most buyers want a balanced choice. The three-tier structure serves all three without requiring you to negotiate or create custom offers.

**What to include in each tier:**

The core transformation lives in Tier 1. Everything above Tier 1 should speed up implementation, reduce friction in applying the content, or add accountability. Good additions for Tiers 2 and 3 include:

- Templates that operationalize concepts from the guide
- Checklists that compress the guide into a reference tool
- Recorded walkthroughs that explain complex sections
- Community or cohort access for accountability
- Direct access to the creator for a defined period

Bad additions are things that add perceived value but do not help the buyer achieve the outcome: extra ebooks unrelated to the topic, filler content, or duplicated material repackaged.

## Part Three: Testing, Urgency, and Raising Prices

### Chapter 7: Price Testing Frameworks That Work

Price testing is not about finding the lowest price that generates sales. It is about finding the price point that maximizes revenue per buyer while maintaining the perceived value that drives long-term trust.

There are three workable approaches for creators without large audiences or the ability to run true A/B tests.

**Approach 1: Sequential testing.**

Launch at one price. Run for 60 days. Track conversions from traffic to purchase. Then raise the price by 20 to 30 percent and run for another 60 days. Track conversions again.

If conversions drop dramatically, you likely crossed a psychological threshold. If conversions hold or improve (because the higher price attracted more committed buyers), you have headroom to go further.

This approach is slow but clean. You will not have perfect data because your traffic mix changes over time. But you will have directional information that is better than guessing.

**Approach 2: Audience polling before launch.**

Before launch, show your audience descriptions of what the product includes and ask them to choose from a range of prices: "$X feels like a bargain, $Y feels fair, $Z feels expensive." The clustering point in the "feels fair" category gives you a starting price. The "feels expensive" threshold tells you where your audience's mental model breaks.

This approach has a flaw: people do not always pay what they say they will. But it gives you data that is directionally useful, especially if you can poll 50 or more people.

**Approach 3: Waitlist pricing.**

Announce the product before it is finished. Offer a waitlist price to early subscribers. The waitlist price is 20 to 30 percent below launch price. This does two things: it generates pre-launch revenue and it reveals how many people will pay at that price.

If your waitlist conversion is strong, you know the offer resonates. If it is weak, you have time to adjust the framing or positioning before full launch.

**What to track:**

Regardless of which approach you use, track these numbers:

- Traffic to sales page (visitors)
- Clicks on the purchase button (expressed interest)
- Completed purchases (conversions)
- Conversion rate (purchases divided by visitors)
- Revenue per visitor (total revenue divided by visitors)

Revenue per visitor is your real north star metric. A lower price with a higher conversion rate can still produce lower revenue per visitor than a higher price with lower conversion. Do not optimize for conversion rate alone.

### Chapter 8: Urgency Without Manipulation

Fake scarcity is one of the most common mistakes in digital product marketing. "Only 10 copies left" on a digital file. "Price goes up in 24 hours" on a page that has said that for three months. "This offer expires soon" without a specific date.

Buyers are not naive. They have seen these tactics across hundreds of products and sales pages. When they recognize manufactured urgency, trust collapses. Not just for that specific product. For everything you will ever sell them.

Urgency, however, is a legitimate psychological driver when it is real. The problem is not urgency itself. The problem is inventing urgency that does not exist.

**Real urgency you can use:**

1. **Launch pricing.** Your product will cost more after launch. This is a real constraint because you are committing to raising the price on a specific date. State the date clearly. Honor it. Buyers who want to save money have a genuine reason to act now.

2. **Cohort enrollment.** If your product includes a live component, group call, or community review period, enrollment windows are real. Once the cohort starts, new buyers do not get the same experience. This is genuine scarcity.

3. **Bonus removal.** You are including additional resources for the first 100 buyers or through a specific date. The bonuses are real. The deadline is real. After that, buyers get the core product without the extras. This is legitimate.

4. **Price increase announcements.** When you plan to raise your price based on sales milestones or a calendar date, announce it transparently. "The price goes to $97 on [specific date] because I am adding more content and the early-buyer discount closes." This is honest. It motivates without manipulation.

**What you should never do:**

- Countdown timers that reset when the visitor returns
- "Limited copies" language on digital downloads
- Fake testimonials or fake sale counts
- Implied community pressure from fabricated buyers

The builders who last in this space are the ones whose launches can stand scrutiny. Fake urgency works once. Real urgency, backed by products that deliver, compounds.

### Chapter 9: When and How to Raise Your Prices

Price increases are not admissions of failure. They are evidence of traction. When demand for a product is consistent, the market is telling you the price is not at its ceiling yet.

Most creators wait too long to raise prices because the increase feels confrontational. It is not. It is an update to signal that demand has increased and new buyers are paying the current market rate.

**The milestone method:**

Set specific sales milestones at which you raise prices. "At 50 sales, the price moves from $27 to $37. At 100 sales, it moves to $47." This approach has two advantages. It gives early buyers a genuine reason to buy now. And it removes the subjective judgment call from your pricing decisions. You do not decide to raise prices. The system raises them automatically when the milestone is hit.

**The time-based method:**

Schedule a price review every 90 days. At each review, look at conversion rate, revenue per visitor, and customer feedback. If conversion rate is above 3 to 4 percent, you have headroom to raise. If revenue per visitor is growing, raise and maintain the trajectory.

**The addition-based method:**

Every time you add meaningful content to a product, the price goes up. Added two new chapters. Added a template pack. Added a video walkthrough. Price increases by $10 to $20. New buyers pay the new price. Existing buyers get a loyalty email with the new content at no charge. This maintains goodwill while growing average price over time.

**Communicating price increases:**

Always announce upcoming increases to your existing audience first. Give them at least 72 hours at the current price. This rewards your existing subscribers and creates a real reason to act. It also positions the increase as a positive signal: "the product has grown, the price reflects that growth."

## Part Four: Specific Product Categories and Their Pricing Logic

### Chapter 10: Pricing Guides, Ebooks, and Written Products

Written digital products are the most underpriced category in the creator economy. The perception that "writing is just words" leads creators to price them at commodity levels.

The correct frame is outcomes, not format. What does the buyer know, be able to do, or avoid after reading this? That is what you are pricing.

**Pricing by outcome tier:**

- Single-concept guides (one specific tactic or framework): $7 to $27
- System guides (how to do an entire process): $37 to $67
- Comprehensive references (everything on a topic): $67 to $147
- Flagship guides (rare, definitive resources): $147 to $297

The depth of content matters less than the depth of outcome. A 15-page guide that hands a buyer a complete system that produces results in 48 hours is worth more than a 150-page guide that surveys a topic without giving clear next steps.

**Pricing levers for written products:**

- Length: Longer guides justify higher prices but do not require them
- Specificity: Niche-specific beats general; higher specificity commands higher price
- Implementation tools: Including templates, spreadsheets, or checklists adds $10 to $30 per item
- Companion resources: A reference card or checklist adds perceived value without adding reading time

### Chapter 11: Pricing Templates and Operational Tools

Templates are systematized thinking. The buyer is not paying for a spreadsheet. They are paying for the architecture of a decision that would otherwise take them weeks to build.

Templates are frequently overpriced relative to outcomes when sold as decorative products, and underpriced relative to outcomes when they operationalize complex processes.

**The key question:** Does this template replace hours of thinking, or does it replace hours of formatting?

A Notion template that is mostly aesthetic (looks like someone's well-organized workspace) should be priced at $7 to $27. A spreadsheet that automates a complete financial tracking system for a creator, with built-in formulas and decision logic, should be priced at $37 to $97.

**Template pricing principles:**

- Price by time saved, not by file count
- Price by complexity replaced, not by visual complexity
- Bundle templates with guides that teach their use; the guide justifies the price, the template delivers the implementation

### Chapter 12: Pricing Mini-Courses and Video Products

Video products exist on a spectrum from short, focused trainings (under 2 hours) to comprehensive courses (10+ hours). Pricing should reflect outcome depth, not hour count.

A 90-minute recording that teaches a complete, specific skill and gets buyers to a result outperforms a 15-hour course that covers everything without getting buyers to a clear next action.

**Mini-course pricing ($47 to $197):**

Mini-courses solve one problem completely. They do not try to cover everything about a topic. They pick one outcome and deliver it as efficiently as possible. This format is growing because attention is limited and buyers are suspicious of lengthy courses they know they will not finish.

Price in this range based on: the value of the outcome, whether the outcome is professional or personal, and whether the buyer can point to a specific result after completing it.

**Comprehensive course pricing ($197 to $997):**

Comprehensive courses justify higher prices when they replace a professional education or significant consulting expense, when they deliver results that buyers can attribute directly to the course, and when they include community, accountability, or direct access to the creator.

The mistake is pricing comprehensive courses on hour count alone. "100 hours of video, $997" is a worse frame than "Complete system for building a six-figure freelance business, $997." Buyers do not want 100 hours of video. They want the outcome.

## Part Five: The Long Game

### Chapter 13: Building Pricing Power Over Time

Pricing power is the ability to charge more than competitors for the same category of product. It is built through:

1. **Track record.** Buyers who report results become evidence. Every testimonial, case study, and outcome story adds to your credibility stack and allows you to price against proof rather than against arbitrary category norms.

2. **Specificity.** The more specifically you name who your product is for and what it does, the more you can charge. "Productivity system" competes with everything. "Time blocking system for ADHD solopreneurs" competes with almost nothing.

3. **Positioning.** Where you are seen matters as much as what you make. A creator with 1,000 engaged followers who are deeply aligned with the topic can charge more than a creator with 50,000 general followers. Size is less important than fit.

4. **Consistency.** Creators who show up consistently with useful content build an authority differential over time. The person who has been writing about a topic for three years charges more than the person who started six months ago, even if the content is similar in quality.

**Building toward premium pricing:**

The sequence most creators get backwards is: they try to charge premium prices before they have built the trust infrastructure that makes premium pricing feel natural to the buyer.

The sequence that works:

- Launch entry product at fair price
- Collect testimonials from buyers who got results
- Publish those testimonials consistently
- Raise price to reflect evidence
- Add depth to product to justify the price
- Repeat

This is not fast. But it is the only way to raise prices that sticks.

### Chapter 14: The Refund Rate as a Pricing Signal

Refund rates communicate something most creators ignore: misalignment between what the product promised and what it delivered.

A high refund rate (above 5 percent) at a given price point is a signal worth investigating. It might mean:

- The price created expectations the product does not meet
- The product description attracted buyers who were not the right fit
- The product does not deliver the stated outcome
- The product is priced in a tier where buyers expect more than they receive

A low refund rate (below 2 percent) suggests alignment. Buyers received what they expected, or more.

**Using refund rates to calibrate pricing:**

If you raise prices and refund rates increase, the new price created expectations you are not meeting. You have two choices: lower the price back, or increase the product to meet the expectation.

If you raise prices and refund rates stay flat or decrease, the higher price is attracting buyers who are more committed to the outcome and therefore more likely to implement and succeed.

This is real data. Use it.

### Chapter 15: Quarterly Pricing Review Process

Pricing is not set once and forgotten. It is a living part of your business that requires periodic calibration.

**The 90-day pricing review:**

Every quarter, sit with these numbers:

1. Revenue per product (total sales revenue, not units)
2. Units sold per product
3. Conversion rate per sales page
4. Refund rate per product
5. Average time between first visit and purchase (your sales platform may provide this)

From these numbers, answer:

- Which products are underperforming on revenue per visitor?
- Which products have refund rates above 3 percent?
- Which products have conversion rates above 4 percent (possible underpricing)?
- Which products have had no price change in 6 or more months?

Create a priority list of one to three pricing changes to test next quarter. Implement one at a time so you can isolate the effect.

**The annual repositioning review:**

Once per year, look at where your products sit in the market. Has the category become more crowded? Have competitors raised or lowered prices? Have buyer expectations shifted because of platform changes or economic conditions?

Update your product ladder to reflect current conditions. Retire products that no longer fit the ladder. Add products that fill gaps.

Pricing is strategy. Treat it like one.

## Part Six: Common Mistakes and How to Correct Them

### Chapter 16: The Race to the Bottom

When a creator sees a competitor launch a similar product at a lower price, the instinctive response is to match or beat that price. This is the most expensive mistake in digital product pricing.

Competing on price is only a viable strategy if you have structural advantages in production cost or volume that allow you to sustain lower margins. Independent creators have neither. You cannot win a price war against someone who has fewer bills, a larger audience, or is willing to operate at break-even.

The correct response to a lower-priced competitor is to move up, not down. Increase the depth of your product. Add elements the competitor does not have. Specialize more narrowly. Differentiate on quality signal rather than on cost.

"Their version is $17 and mine is $47, but mine includes a template pack and a 30-minute implementation walkthrough" is a defensible position. "My version is also $17" is a race to a floor that does not benefit you.

### Chapter 17: Apologetic Pricing Language

The language you use around your price reveals your confidence level, and buyers read it accurately.

**Apologetic pricing language:**
- "I know it might seem expensive but..."
- "For only $X..."
- "Just $X"
- "If you're on a budget..."
- "I didn't want to charge too much for this..."

Each of these phrases communicates a lack of confidence in the product's value. They invite the buyer to question the price rather than accepting it. They transfer doubt from the creator to the buyer.

**Confident pricing language:**
- "$47." (stated without apology)
- "This sells for $47."
- "The price is $47."
- "The investment is $47."

You do not need to justify the price in your language. You justify it through the quality of your content, your track record, and your specificity of outcome. Say the number without softening it.

### Chapter 18: The Perpetual Discount Problem

Running a permanent sale is not a discount strategy. It is a statement that your price is wrong.

If your product is always on sale at 40 percent off, buyers learn that $47 is not the real price. The real price is $28. They wait for the sale. They tell others to wait for the sale. Your full price becomes fictional.

Discounts serve a specific function: they reward a specific behavior at a specific time. Early buyers. Email subscribers. Bundle purchasers. Past customers receiving a loyalty offer. These are targeted, time-limited, and meaningful.

Running sitewide sales repeatedly teaches buyers that patience is rewarded. The opposite behavior pattern is what you want to train: acting now is rewarded, waiting costs.

**Discount rules:**
- Never more than once per quarter on any given product
- Always attached to a real event (launch, milestone, seasonal period)
- Always with a real end date, honored
- Always communicated as exceptional, not as the normal state of the offer

### Chapter 19: Pricing for the Wrong Audience

Your price is not for everyone. It is for your specific buyer.

When you price for the most price-sensitive person in your potential audience, you lose the serious buyers who would have paid more and gotten more value from the product. You fill your customer list with people who were looking for the cheapest option, which is a trait that compounds poorly over time.

When you price for your actual target buyer, the person who needs the outcome and has the means and motivation to pursue it, you attract buyers who implement, report results, and return for more products.

**Defining your target buyer's pricing psychology:**

Ask these questions:

- What does my buyer spend on professional development in a year?
- What are they paying for other tools in the same category?
- What does the problem I solve cost them if unsolved?
- Are they buying for themselves or for their business?

A solopreneur who is already spending $300 per month on software tools will not blink at a $97 guide that helps them extract more value from those tools. A beginner who has never spent money on digital products before will experience $97 as a significant barrier.

Which one do you want as a customer?

Both have merit. But be deliberate about the choice, and price accordingly.

## Conclusion: The Pricing Mindset That Sustains Creators

Pricing is not a one-time decision. It is a practice.

The creator who treats pricing as a practice, who reviews it regularly, tests it systematically, and adjusts it based on data rather than emotion, builds compounding advantages over time. Each year their products command more. Their audience trusts them at higher price points. Their revenue per buyer increases even if their audience size stays constant.

The creator who prices by instinct, who sets a number because it "feels about right" and never revisits it, stays locked at whatever tier they started in. More products at the same price. More volume required for growth. More work for diminishing returns.

The frameworks in this guide are not complicated. They require consistency and courage more than intelligence. The courage to set a number that reflects the product's actual value. The courage to test higher prices. The courage to hold a price when someone complains.

Your product is worth what you build it to be. Price it accordingly.

## Appendix B: The Pricing Review Checklist

Use this every 90 days:

**Revenue check**
- Total revenue this quarter vs. last quarter
- Revenue per product
- Revenue per visitor (most important)

**Conversion check**
- Overall store conversion rate
- Individual product conversion rates
- Which products are above 4% (underpriced?)
- Which products are below 1% (positioning or price problem?)

**Refund check**
- Overall refund rate
- Refund rate per product
- Any product above 5% needs investigation

**Market check**
- Have competitors changed pricing?
- Have new products launched in your category?
- Have platform dynamics shifted (new features, algorithm changes)?

**Price change decisions**
- Which products to raise (evidence: consistent sales, low refunds, high demand)
- Which products to test at a different price point
- Which products to retire or bundle differently

## Appendix C: Price Anchoring Statement Templates

Use these frameworks to write your own anchoring copy:

**Consulting anchor:**
"A one-hour session with a [profession] who specializes in [topic] costs $[X]. This guide contains the exact framework I have used to [result]. It is $[price]."

**Course anchor:**
"Most courses on [topic] charge $[X] and take [time] to complete. This guide cuts to the specific methodology that produces [outcome] and takes [time] to implement. $[price]."

**Time anchor:**
"It took me [time period] of [effort type] to develop this system. This guide hands it to you directly. $[price]."

**Outcome anchor:**
"If [desired outcome] is worth [value] to you, this guide is priced appropriately. If it is not, this is not the right product."
