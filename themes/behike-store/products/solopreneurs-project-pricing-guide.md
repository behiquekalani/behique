## Chapter 1: Why Your Pricing Is Broken and What That Costs You

Before you can fix your pricing, you need to understand exactly how it breaks. Pricing breaks in predictable ways, and most solopreneurs have at least three of these breaks happening simultaneously.

Break one: you price based on time, not value. You estimate hours, multiply by a rate, and send a quote. The problem with this approach is that it caps your income at your hours and punishes you for getting better at your work. The faster you get, the less you make per project. Clients who buy time are buying your ceiling, not your capability. This model incentivizes slow, not skilled.

Break two: you price based on what you think the client will pay. This is the most common and damaging form of broken pricing. You look at the client's website, make an assumption about their budget, and price to that assumption before you've ever asked a single question about the scope, the deadline, the stakes, or the value of the outcome. You're leaving money on the table every time you do this, because you're assuming the worst about clients who haven't shown you their hand yet.

Break three: you have no floor. A floor is the minimum you'll accept for a given type of work. Without one, every project negotiation starts from zero. The client applies pressure and you have no principled reason to hold the number, so you fold. A floor isn't stubbornness. It's math. Below a certain number, the project costs you money when you account for your actual time and overhead.

Break four: scope creep is priced into the original quote and never invoiced separately. You budget for the project as described in the initial conversation. Then the client "adds a few things" over the course of the engagement and you absorb those additions without renegotiating. This is the most common source of solopreneur burnout because you feel like you're doing extra work but can't identify where the bleeding started.

The cost of these four breaks is not abstract. It's hourly. Every hour you work on an underpriced project is an hour you could have spent on a correctly priced one. Solopreneurs who fix their pricing systems, even without changing anything else about their business, routinely double their effective hourly rate within 90 days. Not because they work less. Because they stop working for free.

The calculation that makes this concrete is your Target Effective Hourly Rate (TEHR). Take your monthly revenue goal. Divide it by the number of billable hours you want to work per month. That's your TEHR. If you want $8,000 per month and you want to bill 80 hours, your TEHR is $100 per hour. Every project you take should meet or exceed that rate when you divide the total revenue by the total hours spent. Most solopreneurs who run this number for the first time discover that 40 to 60 percent of their projects are below their TEHR. They're subsidizing their lowest-paying clients with their most valuable resource: time.

## Chapter 2: The Scope-First Method

Most pricing conversations go in the wrong order. The client asks "what do you charge?" and the solopreneur answers. That's backwards. Price before scope is chaos. Scope before price is discipline.

The Scope-First Method is a structured discovery process that happens before any number leaves your mouth. It produces a complete understanding of what the project actually is, so that when you name a price, you're naming it for a defined deliverable, not a vague description.

The method has five questions, asked in order. Every project, every time.

Question one: What does success look like for this project? This is not "what do you want?" It's "how will you know it worked?" The answer tells you the stakes. A website redesign that's "just a refresh" is priced differently from a website redesign that's intended to double conversion rates for a $3M business. Same deliverable, different stakes, different price.

Question two: What is the deadline, and why is that deadline important? Deadlines are pricing signals. A client who needs something in three weeks is in a different situation than a client who needs something in six months. Rush work costs more, not just because of the compressed schedule but because of the opportunity cost of deprioritizing other projects. If the deadline is arbitrary, push back on it. If it's real, price it.

Question three: What have you tried before, and what happened? This question reveals whether you're starting from scratch or inheriting someone else's problem. It also reveals how clear the client's thinking is. Clients who can articulate what they've tried and why it didn't work are much easier to scope than clients who have vague ideas and no prior attempts.

Question four: Who else is involved in decisions about this project? Scope expands when decision-makers are added after the project starts. If the CEO is going to weigh in during the review cycle and you didn't know that, your two-round revision budget becomes five rounds. Get the decision tree before you price.

Question five: What's your budget for this? Clients lie when asked this question because they've been trained to underdeclare to protect against inflated quotes. The way to get real information is to give them a range and watch the reaction. "Projects like this typically run between $3,000 and $8,000 depending on scope. Does that align with what you were expecting?" Their response tells you whether you're in the right conversation.

After running these five questions, you have enough information to write a Scope Document before any pricing happens. The Scope Document is a one to two page summary of what you understood from the conversation: the deliverables, the timeline, the revision rounds, the decision-making structure, and any explicit exclusions. When the client confirms the Scope Document, you name the price. Not before.

This sequence eliminates two of the most common pricing disasters. It eliminates quoting before you understand what you're quoting, which produces underpricing. And it eliminates scope creep, because the Scope Document becomes the contract boundary. When the client asks for something outside the document, you have a written baseline to reference when you charge extra.

## Chapter 3: Building Your Tiered Pricing Structure

Offering a single price for each service is a missed opportunity. Clients make decisions within ranges. When you give one price, the client's only decision is yes or no. When you give three options at different price points, the client's decision is which option, and most clients self-select to the middle tier. This is not manipulation. It's how humans evaluate choices.

The Three-Tier Architecture for solopreneur pricing works as follows. Tier one is the Starter option: the core deliverable with no extras, a longer timeline, and fewer revision rounds. Tier two is the Standard option: the full deliverable with standard revisions and your normal timeline. This is what most clients will buy and what your revenue projections should be based on. Tier three is the Premium option: the full deliverable plus extras (rush delivery, additional revisions, ongoing support, expanded scope), priced at a premium that reflects the additional value.

The specific extras you include in each tier depend on your service type. For a designer, tier three might include brand guidelines, additional file formats, and 60 days of post-delivery support. For a copywriter, it might include two rounds of revision plus a competitor analysis. For a developer, it might include deployment, documentation, and 30 days of bug fixes. The extras in tier three should be things clients legitimately want, not padding. If the extras aren't compelling, tier three won't sell and you'll have created the impression that your premium offering has nothing worth paying for.

Pricing the tiers correctly requires a spread of at least 40 percent between each tier. If tier one is $2,000, tier two should be around $3,000 to $3,200, and tier three should be around $4,500 to $5,000. A spread that's too small makes all three tiers feel equivalent, and clients will default to tier one because there's no meaningful reason to step up. A spread that's too large creates sticker shock at tier three and makes the whole conversation feel adversarial.

Present the tiers in writing before the verbal pricing conversation. A one-page PDF or a clean email table showing each tier's deliverables and price gives the client time to review before they have to respond in the moment. When you present verbally only, clients react emotionally to the number without fully processing what's included. Written presentation produces better decisions and fewer objections.

One important rule about tiers: do not negotiate within tiers. If a client wants something from tier three at the tier two price, that's not a negotiation, it's a custom scope. Run the Scope-First Method for the custom request and price it accordingly. The tiers are fixed structures, not starting points for a back-and-forth.

The tiered structure also serves a psychological function for you. When you know your tiers before the client conversation, you show up with something concrete to offer rather than numbers you're calculating under pressure. Confidence in pricing comes from preparation, not from temperament. Build the tiers. Know them cold. Present them without apology.

## Chapter 4: The Pricing Conversation

The moment you name a price is where most solopreneurs lose ground they didn't need to lose. The price comes out, there's a pause, the client says "that's a bit more than I expected," and the solopreneur immediately apologizes, offers a discount, or adds something for free. None of this is necessary. All of it is damaging.

The pricing conversation has a structure. Once you understand the structure, you can navigate it without anxiety.

Step one is the delivery. You name the price clearly, without hedging. Not "I was thinking maybe around $3,500?" with a rising inflection that turns a statement into a question. "The standard package for this project is $3,500." Period. Then you stop talking. The impulse to keep talking after naming a price, to justify it, to soften it, to explain it, is the voice of pricing anxiety. It signals to the client that you're uncertain, and uncertain sellers get negotiated down.

Step two is the silence. There will be a pause after you name the price. That pause can feel like five minutes when it's actually five seconds. Resist the urge to fill it. The next person to speak loses ground. If you speak first, you're negotiating against yourself. Let the client respond.

Step three is handling the response. There are four common responses and a specific answer for each.

Response A: "That works for us." Confirm the scope, send the contract, collect the deposit. Nothing else.

Response B: "That's higher than we expected." Acknowledge without apologizing. "I understand. Most clients are surprised when they see a fully scoped number versus an estimate. The $3,500 covers [specific deliverables]. Is there anything in the scope we could adjust to bring the number down, or would you like to explore the starter option at $2,000?" You're not discounting. You're offering a real trade: less scope for less money.

Response C: "Can you do it for $2,500?" This is a negotiation attempt, not necessarily a deal-breaker. Answer directly. "I can do it for $2,500 if we adjust the scope. The main item I'd remove is [specific extra]. Would that work for you?" If they say yes, you've held your rate per unit of work. If they say no and want the full scope at $2,500, the answer is also no. You don't need to explain this at length. "The full scope is $3,500. I can't do it for $2,500 and maintain the quality you're hiring me for."

Response D: "We'll think about it." This is either a soft no or a genuine need for time. Give them a clear timeline. "Absolutely. I'll hold the scope and pricing until [date, typically 5-7 days out]. After that I may have conflicts with other clients." Then follow up once on that date. Once. Not three times.

The meta-skill of the pricing conversation is staying relaxed when the client is uncomfortable. Clients test pricing for the same reason buyers at a market try to negotiate: it's worth a try. Your job is not to get defensive or apologetic. Your job is to stay anchored to the scope and the math that produced the number. The clearer you are on your own pricing logic, the easier it is to hold.

## Chapter 5: Scoping Against Creep

Scope creep is not a client problem. It's a boundary problem. Clients add requests because they don't know where the project boundary is, and most solopreneurs have never drawn one clearly. The solution is not to find better clients. It's to build a scoping and change management system that makes the boundary visible from day one.

The three tools that prevent scope creep are the Scope Document, the Change Order Process, and the Mid-Project Check-in.

The Scope Document was introduced in Chapter 2. Its function in creep prevention is to serve as the definitive reference point for what was agreed. When the client sends an email asking you to "also include a section on X," you reference the Scope Document. "I'd love to include that. It's outside what we outlined in the scope document. I can add it as a change order for an additional $[amount]. Want me to send that over?" This response is professional, not adversarial. It acknowledges the request, gives a path to fulfill it, and makes the cost clear.

The Change Order Process is a lightweight system for pricing and documenting additions to the original scope. A change order is not a new proposal. It's a one-paragraph email or document that states: what's being added, how much it costs, and any adjustment to the timeline. The client confirms in writing. You do the work. Simple. The discipline is in doing this for every addition, no matter how small. A "quick favor" that takes an hour is billable. A "minor tweak" that requires two rounds of revision is billable. The moment you start absorbing small additions without billing for them, the client learns that additions are free, and the additions multiply.

The Mid-Project Check-in is a scheduled call or message at the midpoint of every project with a duration of two weeks or more. Its purpose is to identify scope drift before it becomes scope crisis. You review what was in the original scope, confirm that the deliverables are still accurate, and surface any client requests that have come in informally (via email, Slack, text) that haven't been formally scoped. The mid-project check-in gives you a structured opportunity to either bill for additions or formally exclude them. Doing this verbally and proactively feels like client service. Doing it retroactively after the project has been delivered feels like a dispute.

The psychological shift required to implement this system is accepting that clear boundaries are an act of respect, not an act of aggression. Clients who are serious about their projects want to know what they're getting. They want a partner who tracks scope and communicates proactively, not someone who silently absorbs requests and then delivers half the project at the deadline because the scope doubled and the solopreneur ran out of capacity. The solopreneurs who clients trust most are the ones with the most explicit processes.

## Chapter 6: Raising Rates on Existing Clients

New pricing systems are relatively easy to implement with new clients because you're starting from zero. The harder problem is existing clients who are paying rates you set before you knew what you were doing. Raising rates with existing clients triggers anxiety because the relationship has history, and you don't want to damage something that's been working.

The anxiety is mostly unfounded. Existing clients who value your work expect rates to increase over time. What they don't expect is a rate increase with no notice, no explanation, and no transition plan. The system for raising rates preserves relationships while correcting the economics.

Step one is the audit. Before raising any rate, identify which existing clients are most below your current Target Effective Hourly Rate. Not all clients need a rate increase at the same time. Start with the most extreme case. If you have a client paying $50 per hour for work that you now price at $100, that's a 100 percent gap. If you have another paying $85, the gap is smaller. Prioritize the largest gaps first.

Step two is the communication timing. Rate increases should be announced at natural inflection points: the start of a new project, the renewal of a retainer, or the completion of a long engagement. Never announce a rate increase mid-project. The timing signals respect and makes the increase feel like a business decision rather than an emergency response.

Step three is the message. Keep it factual and forward-looking. "I wanted to give you advance notice that starting [date], my rate for [service type] will be [new rate]. This reflects my current market rates and the quality of work I'm committed to delivering. I want to honor everything in our current scope at the existing rate and then transition on [date]." No apology. No lengthy justification. A fact, a timeline, and a transition plan.

Step four is the transition period. Give existing clients 30 to 60 days at the current rate before the new rate applies. This is not a negotiation concession. It's basic professionalism. A client who has been paying you $2,000 per month for six months deserves at least 30 days to adjust their budget or decide whether to continue. During the transition, they may push back. Handle that pushback the same way you handle it in a new client pricing conversation: acknowledge, don't apologize, offer a scope adjustment if they need a different number.

Step five is acceptance. Some clients will not continue at the new rate. That's the correct outcome. A client who leaves because your rate went from $50 to $100 per hour was paying you a temporary favor rate, not a sustainable business rate. Their departure creates capacity for a new client at the right rate. This is how pricing systems compound over time: each client who leaves below the floor creates space for a better client above it.

The rate increase conversation is also a signal to yourself about your positioning. If raising your rate triggers fear that all your clients will leave, that fear is telling you that you feel replaceable. The solution to that fear is not keeping your rates low. It's building more visible proof of the results you produce so that clients understand the cost of losing you.

## Chapter 7: Identifying and Declining Unprofitable Work

The ability to say no is worth more than any pricing tactic in this guide. Unprofitable work doesn't just cost you money. It costs you time, energy, and the mental bandwidth to pursue better opportunities. Every hour spent on a $25 per hour project is an hour not spent building the systems, relationships, or content that would produce $100 per hour projects.

The Profitability Filter is a four-question evaluation you run on every inquiry before you respond. It takes three minutes and prevents months of regret.

Question one: Does this project meet my Target Effective Hourly Rate? Do the math. If the client's described budget divided by your realistic hour estimate is below your TEHR, the project is either repriced or declined.

Question two: Is this the type of work I want to do more of? Solopreneurs who take every project that pays become generalists with no clear offer. Every project you do is a portfolio entry, a reference, and a signal about what kind of work you want. If a project wouldn't make the highlight reel of your portfolio, it's worth passing on unless the money is genuinely compelling.

Question three: Is this client set up for a successful engagement? Some clients are structurally difficult regardless of how good you are. They make decisions by committee, they have unclear goals, they've already gone through two other service providers on this project, or they open the inquiry by negotiating on price before you've even described what you'd do. These patterns are visible in the first conversation. The Scope-First Method surfaces them. Clients who won't answer your scoping questions clearly are telling you something about how the project will go.

Question four: What does saying yes cost me? Every yes is a no to something else. If you have three current clients and you add a fourth that takes you to 110 percent capacity, the cost is the quality degradation across all four clients, not just the new one. Solopreneurs who are always at capacity are also always at risk of a quality decline that damages the relationships that took years to build.

When you decline work, do it clearly and without guilt. "Thank you for reaching out. After reviewing the scope and timeline, this isn't a fit for my current capacity. If you're open to it, I can refer you to someone who would be a great match." A referral turns a declined project into goodwill, and goodwill compounds. The people you refer other clients to often send clients back to you. Declining with clarity is the foundation of a healthy client ecosystem.

The final point on declining: getting comfortable saying no requires having said yes too many times and suffering the consequences. If you haven't yet experienced the full cost of an unprofitable project, you will. When you do, the memory of it becomes the filter. You don't need to wait for that experience. Run the Profitability Filter on every inquiry from today. The no decisions will start to feel as natural as the yes ones.

## The System in Practice

The Solopreneur's Project Pricing Guide is a complete system, not a checklist of tips. The components work together. Your TEHR defines your floor. The Scope-First Method defines the project before you price it. The tiered structure gives clients a buying decision. The pricing conversation holds the number with confidence. The Scope Document and change order process prevent creep. The rate increase protocol corrects historical underpricing. The Profitability Filter keeps bad projects out of the pipeline.

In practice, running this system looks like this. A new inquiry comes in. You schedule a discovery call and run the five scoping questions. You write a Scope Document and send it for confirmation. You present the three-tier options in writing. You name the price clearly and let the client respond. You handle the response without discounting by default. You send the contract and collect a deposit before starting work. Mid-project, you run a check-in. Any additions become change orders. At the end of the project, you note the actual hours spent and compare against the quoted rate to calibrate for next time.

Every project produces data. That data improves the next estimate. Within 90 days of running this system consistently, your estimates will be accurate, your prices will hold, and your TEHR will be trending in the right direction.
