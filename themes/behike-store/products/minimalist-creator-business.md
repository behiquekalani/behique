## Introduction: The Complexity You Built Is Costing You

You started building an online business to have more freedom. What you built instead is a second job that demands more of your attention than the first one.

You have five content platforms, three newsletters, two product lines, a Notion system with 200 pages, four email automations that half-work, and a weekly schedule that looks impressive in theory and collapses every Thursday.

This is not a productivity problem. It is an architecture problem.

The online creator space is designed to make you add. Every tool promises to simplify your workflow and adds three new variables. Every guru tells you about the platform you are not on yet. Every case study features someone doing more than you, earning more than you, and building faster than you.

The result is a business built by accumulation rather than design. You add without removing. You start without finishing. You scale systems before validating them.

The minimalist creator's business is not about doing less because you lack ambition. It is about doing less so that what you do actually works. Constraints force quality. Simplicity enables focus. And focus, sustained over time, is what actually produces results.

This guide is a framework for stripping your business down to what matters and building it back up deliberately.

## Part One: The Complexity Trap

### How You Got Here

Nobody decides to build a complicated business. You build a complicated business by making a series of individually reasonable decisions that collectively produce a system no one can manage.

You added Instagram because everyone said you needed it. You added a second newsletter list because you wanted to segment your audience. You created five products because you had five ideas. You bought a course management platform, an email service, a scheduling tool, a landing page builder, and a graphic design subscription because each one solved a specific problem.

Each individual decision made sense at the time. The aggregate result is a business that requires constant maintenance just to stay functional. You are spending more time managing the business than building the business. You are spending more time creating content than thinking about what the content is supposed to accomplish. You are busy, but you are not making progress.

This is the complexity trap. You cannot see it from inside because each individual component feels necessary. The trap becomes visible only when you step back and ask: if I were starting from zero today, would I build this?

The honest answer is usually no.

### What Complexity Costs

Complexity has four costs that most creators do not account for.

**Time cost.** Every tool in your stack requires time to use, maintain, update, and troubleshoot. Every platform requires content creation, community management, and algorithm-watching. Every product requires customer support, updates, and marketing. None of these costs are visible when you add the thing. They compound over time.

**Attention cost.** Your attention is not infinitely divisible. Each additional platform, tool, or project fragments your attention further. The quality of your thinking on any one thing degrades as the number of things claiming your attention increases. The creator who is focused on one channel for six months consistently outperforms the creator who is managing five channels simultaneously, even if the five-channel creator appears to be doing more.

**Opportunity cost.** When you are maintaining what already exists, you are not building what should exist. Every hour spent managing a product that is not working is an hour not spent creating a product that could. Every week spent trying to grow a platform that does not fit your strengths is a week not spent doubling down on the one that does.

**Clarity cost.** When your business is complicated, you cannot see what is working. You have data from five platforms, three products, two funnels, and four different audience segments. You cannot tell which of these things is actually driving results because they are all tangled together. Complexity makes it impossible to learn from your own business.

### The Myth of the Full-Stack Creator

The online creator space promotes a vision of the full-stack creator: one person who handles everything at scale. Content production, product development, marketing, customer support, community building, email marketing, paid ads. All of it.

This vision is aspirational but misleading. The creators you see doing all of this successfully either have teams, have been doing it for five or more years and have automated or delegated most of it, or are burning out in ways that are not visible from the outside.

The full-stack creator model is not a business model. It is a performance. And performances are exhausting to maintain.

The minimalist creator's business looks much simpler from the outside. One primary content channel. One or two products. One email list. One clear offer. It looks small. But it is actually optimized. The simplicity is the result of deliberate decisions about what matters, not a lack of ambition about what is possible.

## Part Two: The Case for Building Less

### The 20% That Produces 80% of Results

Every creator's business has a version of the Pareto principle operating within it. A small percentage of your activities produces a large percentage of your results.

In practice: one of your products generates 70% of your revenue. One content format drives 80% of your new audience growth. One channel delivers 90% of your email list subscribers. One type of content consistently outperforms everything else you create.

Most creators know this if they look at their analytics. But knowing it and acting on it are different things. Acting on it means cutting the 80% of activities that are generating 20% of results. That is psychologically difficult. Those activities feel like they could work. Cutting them feels like giving up.

But building less is not giving up. It is concentrating your resources on what you have evidence works. The creator who puts full energy into one channel where they are already gaining traction will out-earn the creator who splits effort across five channels every time.

The constraint is the advantage. Less surface area means more depth on what remains.

### Simplicity and Revenue Are Not in Conflict

There is a pervasive belief that more products equal more revenue. If you have ten products and you add five more, you should earn more. If you have three content channels and you add two more, you should grow faster.

This belief is not supported by the data from creator businesses.

More products create more complexity without necessarily increasing conversion. If your audience is confused about what you sell or cannot find the right product for their specific situation, they buy nothing. A focused product lineup with a clear progression path converts better than a catalog.

More channels dilute the quality of your output and make it harder for your audience to know where to find you. A creator who is excellent on one platform builds a more loyal audience than one who is mediocre on five.

More systems create more failure points. A simple funnel that works reliably outperforms a sophisticated funnel that breaks regularly.

The minimalist approach is not about earning less. It is about removing the friction that prevents you from earning what your best work deserves.

### Focus Creates Compound Returns

Focus is not just about eliminating distraction. It is about allowing compound returns to build.

When you focus on one channel for 12 months, your content quality improves. Your understanding of the platform deepens. Your audience trust grows because you are reliably there. The algorithm rewards your consistency. Old content continues to drive new followers. Each month adds to a body of work that all points in the same direction.

When you spread across five channels for 12 months, none of this compounds. You are always at an early stage on each platform. Your content does not have the same quality as someone who has been focused there. Your audience on each platform is smaller and less engaged. The return on each hour is lower because the investment is divided.

Compounding requires time. Time requires commitment to a direction. Commitment to a direction requires saying no to alternatives.

## Part Three: The Minimum Viable Business Model for Creators

### What a Minimum Viable Creator Business Looks Like

The minimum viable creator business has four components.

**One primary content channel.** This is where you build your audience. Not where you repurpose content from elsewhere. Where you do your primary thinking and publishing. You are fully committed to this channel and you understand its culture, its algorithm, and its audience better than anyone who is splitting their attention across five platforms.

**One email list.** Email is the only audience you own. Every social platform can change its algorithm, deprioritize your content, or disappear. Your email list is yours. Build one. Grow it from your primary content channel. Do not split it into segments until you have 5,000 or more subscribers and a clear reason to do so.

**One to two products.** Not five, not ten, not a whole catalog. One product that serves the core problem your audience has, or two products at different price points that work together. You can expand the lineup later. Start with one and make it excellent.

**One clear path from audience to customer.** How does someone who finds your content end up buying your product? That path should be direct, simple, and obvious. Not a complex funnel with seven steps and three upsells. A clear path: here is what I make, here is who it is for, here is how to buy it.

Everything else is optional. It may be valuable eventually. But it is not required to build a real business.

### The Revenue Math on a Minimal Model

The minimum viable creator business can generate serious revenue without requiring complexity.

Consider: one product at $29. If you have an email list of 2,000 subscribers and 5% buy the product, that is 100 sales, $2,900 in revenue. From a list of 2,000 people. That is not passive income fantasyland. That is a real result from a focused, modest-sized audience.

Now add a second product at $79 and convert 2% of the same list: 40 sales, $3,160. Combined revenue from two products to the same list: $6,060. One launch cycle.

The math favors depth over breadth. A small, highly engaged audience that trusts you will out-convert a large, disengaged following every time. You do not need 100,000 followers to build a profitable creator business. You need the right 2,000.

The minimum viable model keeps your cost structure low (fewer tools, fewer platforms to maintain), your conversion path clear (one or two products with obvious use cases), and your audience relationship deep (full focus on one community rather than spreading thin across many).

### When to Expand Beyond Minimal

The minimum viable model is not a permanent ceiling. It is a foundation.

You expand beyond it when you have evidence that the foundation is working. Evidence means: consistent revenue from your existing products, a growing email list, an audience that regularly tells you what else they need from you, and operational capacity that is not currently strained.

Expansion should be pulled by demand, not pushed by ambition. If your audience tells you they need a more advanced version of your product, create it. If your primary channel has maxed out its growth potential and you have the capacity to add another, add one. If your product line has a clear gap that multiple customers have identified, fill it.

Expansion driven by audience demand is strategic. Expansion driven by "I should probably add this" is how you end up back in the complexity trap.

## Part Four: Eliminating What Is Not Working

### How to Identify Dead Weight

Dead weight in a creator business is anything that consumes resources (time, attention, money) without producing results proportional to that investment.

The challenge is that dead weight rarely announces itself. It is usually defended by one of several rationalizations:

"I just have not given it enough time yet." There is a difference between something that needs more time to compound and something that is simply not working. Time alone does not fix a strategy problem. If something has been running for six months with no meaningful results, it needs evaluation, not patience.

"It is building brand awareness." Brand awareness is not a business outcome. Unless you can trace a path from that awareness to revenue or meaningful audience growth, it is not a result.

"I already invested in it." Sunk cost reasoning is the most dangerous trap in business. The money and time you spent building something are gone whether you continue or stop. The only relevant question is whether continuing will produce results worth the ongoing investment.

"Other creators are doing it." What works for another creator's business model, audience, and skill set may be completely wrong for yours. Your job is to figure out what works for you, not to mirror what someone else is doing.

To identify dead weight, run a simple audit. List every regular activity in your business. For each one, answer three questions: What does this produce? What does it cost in time and money? Is the output worth the input? Be honest.

### The Cutting Framework

Cutting things from your business feels riskier than it is. The fear is that cutting something will cause a collapse. In practice, cutting dead weight usually creates clarity and frees up energy for what matters.

Use this framework to decide what to cut.

**Category 1: Cut immediately.** Things that produce no measurable result and consume significant time. A social platform you have maintained for six months with zero audience growth and zero conversions. A product that has never sold. An email sequence that has a 2% click rate on the most valuable content in your business. These are not investments. They are liabilities.

**Category 2: Pause and evaluate.** Things that show some signal but have not proven themselves. A content format you have tried fewer than 20 times. A product you launched once with minimal marketing. A channel you started three months ago. Give these a clear trial period, define what success looks like, and evaluate at the end. Do not keep them indefinitely.

**Category 3: Consolidate.** Things that are doing similar jobs and could be merged. Two email lists that could be one. A paid tool that duplicates the functionality of a free tool you already use. A product that overlaps significantly with another product and confuses buyers. Consolidation reduces complexity without cutting capability.

**Category 4: Keep with intention.** Things that are producing clear results. Your best-performing content format. Your top-converting product. Your primary channel where your audience is most engaged. Not just keeping these. Doubling down on them.

### Announcing What You Are Stopping

Many creators feel they have to maintain everything they have ever started because stopping something feels like failing publicly.

This is not how your audience experiences it. Most people who follow you do not keep careful track of every platform you are on or every product you have launched. When you stop doing something, most of your audience either does not notice or does not care.

For things that require a public announcement (stopping a newsletter, closing a community, discontinuing a product), a brief, honest explanation is all that is needed. "I am focusing my energy on [primary channel] and stepping back from [secondary channel]. If you want to stay connected, [primary channel] is where I am." Clean, direct, no over-explanation.

Stopping something is not failure. It is information. You tried something, learned it was not the right fit, and redirected resources to what is working. That is sound business practice.

## Part Five: Simplifying Your Tech Stack

### The Cost of Tool Bloat

Tool bloat is when your collection of software and apps has grown beyond what you can use effectively. You have subscriptions to tools you barely use. You have overlapping tools doing similar jobs. You have tools you set up and never finished configuring.

The direct cost is money. $29 per month for a scheduling tool you use once a week. $49 per month for a course platform with two courses on it. $19 per month for an email automation tool you have not touched since you set it up.

The indirect cost is attention. Every tool in your stack is a place where notifications happen, decisions get made, and context-switching occurs. Each one is a small but real tax on your cognitive capacity.

The goal of a minimal tech stack is to cover your core needs with the fewest tools possible, at the lowest cost consistent with doing your work well.

### The Minimal Creator Stack

The minimum viable creator stack has five categories.

**Writing and content creation.** One writing environment. Pick a plain-text editor, a Google Doc, or whatever allows you to write without distraction. The writing tool is not where your workflow complexity should come from. It should be frictionless.

**Email marketing.** One email service provider that handles list management, broadcasting, and basic automation. You do not need the most sophisticated option. You need the one that does what you actually use without charging you for what you do not.

**Product delivery.** One platform for hosting and delivering your digital products. Gumroad handles payment processing and file delivery in one place and charges no monthly fee. You do not need a separate payment processor, a separate file hosting service, and a separate checkout page builder when you are starting out.

**Scheduling.** One tool for scheduling social content if you use one at all. Many creators do fine publishing in real time or using platform-native scheduling. A separate scheduling tool is a luxury, not a requirement.

**Analytics.** The native analytics on your primary platform plus your email service provider's basic reporting tells you almost everything you need to know. You do not need a unified analytics dashboard until your business is large enough that the time savings justify the cost.

That is five categories. Most people need two to three tools total, not ten.

### Auditing Your Current Stack

Open your credit card or bank statements and list every subscription you are paying for. Then open each tool and ask: am I using this regularly? Would cutting it create a problem I cannot solve another way?

For each tool on your list:

If you have not used it in 30 days, cut it. No exceptions.

If you use it but another tool you already pay for could do the same job, cut it.

If you are using it but do not actually need it at your current scale, cut it and revisit later.

If you are using it regularly and it would take significant effort to replace, keep it.

Most creators cut 30% to 50% of their subscriptions in a single audit and feel no impact on their business. The tools they cut were maintaining the appearance of a sophisticated operation without contributing to actual output.

### Resisting Tool Addition

After you simplify, you will be tempted to add things back. New tools will be recommended. Workflows will feel incomplete in ways that prompt you to find something to fill the gap. The instinct to add is strong.

Before adding any new tool, run a brief checklist.

Does something I already use cover this need? If yes, use what you have.

Will this tool save me more time than it takes to set up, learn, and maintain? If no, skip it.

Do I need this now, or am I solving a problem I do not have yet? If the latter, skip it and revisit when the problem is real.

Can I test this without a subscription? Use free tiers and trials before committing.

The bar for adding a tool should be high. Every tool you add to your stack is a commitment of ongoing time and money. Make that commitment deliberately.

## Part Six: The One-Channel Focus Strategy

### Why Single-Channel Dominance Works

The creator space defaults to multi-platform thinking. Be everywhere. Reach people wherever they are. Maximize distribution.

The problem is that each platform has a distinct culture, algorithm, content format, and audience behavior. Being genuinely good on one platform requires significant time and attention. Trying to be genuinely good on five platforms simultaneously is not a strategy. It is a recipe for mediocrity everywhere.

Single-channel dominance means choosing one platform as your primary building ground and committing to it fully. Not posting on other platforms at all, or posting only repurposed content that requires minimal original effort. Your focus, your best thinking, your most consistent effort goes to one place.

What happens when you do this: your content quality on that platform improves rapidly because you are making focused iterations. Your understanding of what the platform rewards deepens. Your audience grows more quickly because you are showing up consistently in one place. Your reputation builds because people start to associate you with that platform and topic.

This is how you become known. Not by being on every platform. By being excellent in one place for a long enough period that people who care about your topic automatically think of you.

### Choosing Your Primary Channel

The right primary channel for you is not the one with the most users. It is the one where three factors align: your natural communication style fits the platform's format, your target audience is already there, and you have the most competitive advantage.

If you think best in long-form writing, LinkedIn or a newsletter is probably your channel. If you think in short punchy observations, Twitter. If you are comfortable on camera and willing to learn video production, YouTube. If you can create visually compelling short videos consistently, Instagram or TikTok.

Choosing based on where you think you should be rather than where you naturally thrive is a mistake that wastes months. Do an honest assessment of your strengths. Where does your best thinking come out? What format do you produce most naturally? Start there.

The secondary consideration is where your target audience lives. If the people most likely to buy what you sell are primarily on LinkedIn, starting on TikTok is making things harder than they need to be. Confirm that your target audience actually uses the platform before committing to it.

### Going Deep Before Going Wide

Single-channel focus does not mean you will never be on multiple platforms. It means you go deep on one before you go wide.

The threshold for adding a second platform is: you have a consistent, sustainable publishing schedule on your primary channel, your primary channel is growing, you have a buffer of content, and you have operational capacity that is not currently being fully used.

Meet all four conditions before adding. Not two out of four. All four.

When you add a second channel, maintain the primary. The second channel starts as a repurposing destination. Content from the primary, adapted for the secondary format. Over time, if the secondary channel shows strong results, you can invest more original effort there. But it starts as distribution, not creation.

## Part Seven: Building a Business That Runs in Four Hours a Day

### What Four Hours Actually Covers

Four hours a day is not a lot of time. But it is enough to run a lean creator business when that business is properly designed.

Four hours breaks down roughly as follows:

One hour on content creation. Writing, recording, or producing the core content output for the day.

One hour on product and business development. Working on your product, improving your offer, or doing the strategic thinking that moves the business forward.

One hour on distribution and community. Publishing, responding to comments and emails, engaging in the communities where your audience lives.

One hour on operations and learning. Admin, analytics review, reading and research that informs your work.

That is four hours of focused work. Not four hours of being logged in to things. Not four hours of managing notifications and context-switching. Four hours of focused work, clearly allocated.

Most solo creators are not constrained by having only four hours. They are constrained by how fragmented those hours become when the business is not designed for focus.

### Designing for Focus

A four-hour workday requires designing away the things that fragment your time.

Batch all communication. Do not respond to emails, comments, and messages as they arrive. Set one or two windows per day for communication. Outside those windows, notifications are off.

Automate repetitive tasks. If you are doing the same task the same way more than three times a week, find an automation or delegation solution. Scheduling, invoicing, file organization, social posting. These should not require your manual attention.

Create a clean stopping condition for each work block. Know in advance what you are trying to accomplish in each hour. When the hour is done, evaluate whether you accomplished the goal. If yes, stop. If not, identify why and fix the system, not extend the work time.

Protect your mornings. The first hours of the day are typically the highest quality cognitive work time. Do not let them get consumed by email and social media. Do the most important creative or strategic work first.

### The Weekly Operating Rhythm

A four-hour workday, sustained over five days, is 20 hours of focused work per week. In a well-designed creator business, 20 hours covers everything.

A minimal weekly rhythm:

**Monday:** Strategy and planning. Review last week's results. Set priorities for the week. Do any necessary research. This is thinking time, not producing time.

**Tuesday and Wednesday:** Primary creation. Write or record your week's content. Build product or work on the business. These are your two highest-output days.

**Thursday:** Distribution and repurposing. Publish content, engage with your audience, convert primary content into formats for secondary platforms.

**Friday:** Operations and learning. Handle admin, review analytics, read, and do any maintenance tasks on tools and systems.

**Saturday and Sunday:** Off. No work. If inspiration strikes, capture the idea and act on it Monday.

This rhythm assumes consistent batching and a healthy content buffer. If you are always trying to create the day before you publish, you will always be in crisis mode. The buffer is not optional. It is what makes the four-hour day possible.

### The Four-Hour Day Is a Design Outcome

A four-hour workday is not the starting point. It is the result of getting the design right.

When you first simplify, the work will still feel like more than four hours because you are unlearning habits, sunsetting platforms and tools, and rebuilding your operating rhythm. Give it 60 to 90 days.

After the transition period, a well-designed creator business genuinely does not require more than four to five focused hours a day to maintain and grow. The constraint after that is not time. It is the quality of the thinking you bring to those hours.

## Part Eight: What to Cut When You Are Overwhelmed

### Overwhelm Is Information

When you reach the point where your business is drowning you, that is not a signal to work harder. It is a signal that you have built something with the wrong architecture.

Overwhelm is an information signal. It tells you that the load exceeds the system's capacity. You can respond to that signal by trying to increase capacity (work more, work faster, drink more coffee). Or you can respond to it by reducing the load (cut what is not working, simplify what is too complex, stop what should never have started).

Increasing capacity is a temporary fix. You cannot sustainably work 12-hour days on a business that should run in four. Reducing load is a system fix. You change the architecture so the work fits the time you have.

### The Overwhelm Triage Protocol

When you are overwhelmed, do a triage before making any decisions. The goal is to see the business clearly, not to immediately start cutting.

Make a list of every active project, platform, product, and commitment in your business. Everything. The list is probably longer than you think.

For each item, note: Is this producing revenue? Is this producing audience growth? Is this required for something else that produces revenue or growth? Does this have a clear near-term completion date?

If the answer to all four questions is no, it is a candidate for cutting. Not necessarily all at once. But it is on the list.

Now look at the items that are producing results. How much of your time are they consuming relative to the results they produce? Is there an imbalance?

The triage gives you a clear picture of where your time is going versus where your results are coming from. That picture makes decisions easier because they are based on evidence rather than instinct.

### The Minimum Viable Operation

When you have the triage picture, define your minimum viable operation: the subset of your current activities that would let you maintain revenue while you rebuild the system.

Usually this is very small. One product. One content channel. One email list. Publishing once or twice a week. Responding to customers and subscribers. That is it.

Strip back to the minimum viable operation first. Do not try to reorganize everything while maintaining everything. Stop almost everything temporarily. Give yourself a month where you are only doing the minimum viable set of activities.

In that month, two things happen. First, you get your energy back because the load has been dramatically reduced. Second, you start to see clearly which of the stripped-away things you actually miss and which you are relieved are gone. The ones you miss are probably worth rebuilding. The ones that have been absent for a month with no negative consequence were probably not worth maintaining.

### Rebuilding With Intention

After the simplification phase, you rebuild. But slowly and deliberately.

Add one thing at a time. Not five new platforms because you are feeling ambitious again. One thing. Run it for 60 days. Evaluate the results. If it is working and you have capacity, consider adding another.

Each time you consider adding something, ask: what in my current operation am I willing to stop doing to make room for this? If the answer is nothing, the new thing should not be added. Every addition should come with a subtraction. This is what keeps the system from expanding back into complexity.

## Part Nine: The Minimalist Creator's Long Game

### What You Are Building

The minimalist creator's business is not a retirement plan. It is not passive income that runs without your attention. It is a lean, well-designed operation that you actively manage in a sustainable amount of time.

Over years, it compounds. Your body of work grows. Your audience deepens. Your product suite evolves. Your reputation builds. Each year, the same four focused hours produces more than the year before because the foundation under it is stronger.

This is the long game. Not growth at any cost. Not scale before systems. Depth before breadth, quality before quantity, and sustainability over sprinting.

The creators who are still building in year five are almost never the ones who tried to do everything in year one. They are the ones who picked something they were good at, committed to it, simplified relentlessly when things got complex, and showed up consistently for a long time.

### Identity and Clarity

The most underrated benefit of the minimalist model is clarity about who you are and what you build.

When you are on every platform, making every type of content, selling every kind of product, no one knows what you stand for. You are everything to everyone, which means you are memorable to no one.

When you are fully committed to one channel, one offer, and one clear message, your audience knows exactly what you represent. New followers know within 30 seconds whether you are for them. Buyers know immediately whether your product is what they need. The clarity of your positioning does your marketing for you.

Simplicity is not just an operational choice. It is a brand choice. The cleaner your focus, the stronger your brand.

### The Next Action

The system described in this guide takes about 60 to 90 days to fully implement. You will not get there in a weekend.

But you can start today with one action: the triage from Part Eight. Make the list. See your business clearly. From that list, identify one thing you can stop doing this week that will not produce any meaningful negative consequence.

Stop it. Not pause. Stop.

Then identify the one thing in your business that is producing the most results with the least effort. Put more energy there this week.

That is the beginning of the minimalist creator's business. Not a complete redesign. A clear direction. You already know what is working. Build toward it.

## Appendix: Worksheets and Reference

### Business Triage Worksheet

List every active project, platform, product, and commitment in your business.

For each item, score 1-3 on:
- Revenue contribution (1 = none, 2 = indirect, 3 = direct)
- Audience growth contribution (1 = none, 2 = minor, 3 = significant)
- Time cost per week (1 = under 1 hour, 2 = 1-3 hours, 3 = 3+ hours)

Calculate a ratio: (Revenue + Growth) divided by Time Cost. Items with a ratio below 0.75 are candidates for cutting.

### Minimal Tech Stack Audit

List every subscription you pay for.

For each tool:
- Date of last active use
- What it does that nothing else does
- Monthly cost
- Replacement option if you cut it

Cut anything unused in the past 30 days. Evaluate overlap. Keep only what you actively need.

### Minimum Viable Operation Checklist

Check each box only when you have confirmed it is in place:

- One email list with a working subscribe page
- One product that is available and clearly described
- One clear path from content to email list
- One clear path from email list to product purchase
- Analytics tracking on the above paths
- A content buffer of at least two weeks

When all seven are checked, you have a minimum viable creator business. Everything else is optional until you have reason to expand.

### The Subtraction Rule

Before adding anything new to your business, answer:

1. What does this produce that I cannot achieve with what I already have?
2. What will I stop doing to make room for this?
3. What does success look like for this, and by when?
4. What is the plan if it does not work?

If you cannot answer all four questions clearly, do not add the thing yet.
