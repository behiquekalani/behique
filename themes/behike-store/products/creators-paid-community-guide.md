# The Creator's Paid Community Guide

**How to launch and grow a paid community that generates recurring monthly revenue.**

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike

---

## Why Paid Communities Work Better Than Most Creators Expect

The assumption most creators carry into community building is that the hard part is finding members. Get enough people in the door, the theory goes, and the recurring revenue will follow.

The reality is the inverse. Finding members for a paid community is not the hard part. Creating a community worth paying for consistently, month after month, is.

The creators who build sustainable paid communities do something others do not: they design the value before they launch, and they build infrastructure that delivers that value without requiring constant manual effort. They understand that someone paying $30 per month is not buying access to a chat room. They are paying for a specific, ongoing outcome they could not get as reliably on their own.

This guide walks through the full process: deciding whether a paid community is right for your audience, designing the offer, launching to your first members, running operations, retaining members long-term, and scaling without losing quality.

---

## Part 1: Is a Paid Community the Right Product?

Not every creator should run a paid community. The model works best under specific conditions. Evaluate these before committing.

### The Conditions That Make Communities Work

**Condition 1: Your audience has an ongoing problem, not a one-time problem.**

A course solves a problem once. A community continues to provide value as the problem evolves. If your audience is learning a skill that takes years to develop (building a business, improving a craft, parenting, fitness, investing), there is ongoing value in continued membership. If they are solving a single problem (how to file their taxes, how to move to a new city), a community is the wrong vehicle.

**Condition 2: Your audience benefits from peer interaction.**

Some audiences want expert knowledge. Others want community. Most want both. The question is whether the peer-to-peer connection is genuinely valuable or whether members would prefer direct access to you without the social layer.

Test this by observing your existing audience behavior. Do they reply to each other in comments? Do they share personal context and ask questions that only peers could answer? Or do they primarily interact with your content and ignore each other?

**Condition 3: You can commit to showing up consistently.**

A paid community where the creator disappears for weeks at a time fails. Not because members cannot survive without you, but because your presence is what distinguishes a paid community from a free one. You need a realistic estimate of how much time per week you can sustain for years.

Sustainable range: 3-7 hours per week for a community of under 300 members. More than that starts to compete with your other work.

**Condition 4: You have an existing audience of at least 1,000 people.**

This is not a hard requirement. Some creators launch paid communities from smaller audiences with high engagement. But building a community before having an audience means doing two difficult things at once. The conversion rate from audience to paid member on a new launch is typically 1-3%. To hit 30 paying members on launch, you need 1,000-3,000 audience members exposed to the launch.

## Part 2: Designing the Community Offer

The offer is not "access to a private community." That is a feature. The offer is the specific outcome members will have in six months that they would not have outside the community.

### Writing the Outcome Statement

Write one sentence that completes this prompt: "Members of [community name] will [specific outcome] by [timeframe] through [mechanism]."

Example: "Members of Growth Lab will build their first 1,000-person email list within 90 days through weekly group coaching, member accountability pairings, and a step-by-step action curriculum."

This sentence does three things. It specifies the outcome (1,000-person email list). It sets a timeframe that creates urgency (90 days). It identifies the mechanism that makes the community different from reading a free blog post (weekly coaching, accountability, curriculum).

If you cannot write this sentence, you are not ready to launch.

### The Three Pillars of a Paid Community

Every durable paid community has three pillars:

**Pillar 1: Curriculum or Framework.** A structured body of knowledge that gives the community direction and gives new members a clear starting point. This can be a course inside the community, a resources library, a monthly theme, or a mapped progression from beginner to advanced.

Without curriculum, the community becomes an open-ended conversation. Open-ended conversations are fine for free communities. Paid communities need a reason for someone to stay beyond month one.

**Pillar 2: Live Access.** Some form of real-time interaction with you. Weekly calls, monthly Q&As, bi-weekly hot seats. The specific format matters less than the consistency and the depth.

Live access is the part of a paid community that courses cannot replicate. It is also the most valuable part for members and the most difficult to maintain at scale. Design your live access schedule conservatively. Promises you break destroy retention.

**Pillar 3: Peer Community.** The connections between members. This is the part that becomes self-sustaining over time and reduces your dependence as the sole value provider.

Build this intentionally. Members do not automatically form relationships in a chat room. You facilitate introductions. You pair members with similar goals or complementary skills. You highlight member wins publicly. You ask questions that draw people out. In the early stages, you are the social architect.

### Pricing

For most creator communities, the correct entry price is between $27 and $97 per month.

Below $27: the commitment signal is weak. Members who pay very little will often not engage. Low price also signals low value, regardless of actual quality.

Above $97: you need a strong brand, demonstrable results, and typically some form of credentialing (you are known as an expert in your field) to command this price from a cold audience.

The sweet spot for most first communities is $37-$47 per month, which generates meaningful ARR at scale while remaining accessible enough to keep conversion rates reasonable.

Annual pricing: offer an annual option at a 20-25% discount. This improves retention (members who pay for a year have made a stronger commitment), smooths cash flow, and gives you a higher average revenue per member.

## Part 3: The Pre-Launch

The launch of a paid community is not an event. It is the result of a pre-launch period where you prime your audience, collect validation signals, and generate demand.

### The Four-Week Pre-Launch Sequence

**Week 1: Seed the problem.** Create content that articulates the problem your community solves. Do not mention the community yet. Just make your audience viscerally aware of the gap between where they are and where they want to be. Three to five pieces of content: a long post, a thread, a short video.

**Week 2: Introduce the concept.** Share that you are building something. Do not reveal the price or the full details. Ask your audience what they would need inside such a community to make it worth paying for. This creates investment and gives you data.

**Week 3: Build the waitlist.** Create a simple landing page with an email opt-in. The offer: get notified first when it opens, possibly at a founding member price. Promote the waitlist to your audience. Every person on the waitlist is a warm lead on launch day.

**Week 4: Open to founding members.** Launch to your waitlist before your general audience. Give founding members a price lock, a bonus, or access to help shape the community in its early days. Founding member framing creates urgency and rewards people for signing up early.

### Founding Member Positioning

Founding members are the people who join before the community has proven itself. They take a risk on you. In exchange:

- Give them a locked price that never increases as long as they remain members.
- Give them naming rights on something (a resource, a recurring call format).
- Feature their story in your community content.
- Give them a founding member badge or designation.

The founding member cohort is also your best feedback loop. They will tell you what to build, what is not working, and what they need. Treat this group as partners in building the community.

## Part 4: Launch Execution

The launch is three to five days of promotion focused exclusively on driving sign-ups. During this window, you are not posting regular content. You are selling.

### The Launch Content Framework

**Day 1: The Origin Story.** Why you built this. What problem you watched people struggle with. Why existing solutions are not enough. End with an invitation to join.

**Day 2: The Case for the Outcome.** What becomes possible when this problem is solved. Real examples, specific numbers where you have them. End with a case for why now.

**Day 3: Address the Objections.** The most common reasons people hesitate: too busy, not ready, price, not sure it will work for them. Address each directly and honestly. End with enrollment details.

**Day 4: Social Proof and Momentum.** If you have early members, share their reactions. If not, share responses from your pre-launch content. Show that real people are engaged with this. End with urgency.

**Day 5: Last Chance.** Final hours. Direct, short message. No new content. Just: "The founding member pricing closes tonight."

### What to Do With No Audience

If you do not yet have a large audience, your launch strategy shifts. Instead of broadcasting to followers, you are doing direct outreach to your existing connections.

Write personal messages to 50 people in your network who match the profile of your ideal member. Not a mass email. Personal messages that acknowledge what they are working on and invite them to consider the community.

Join communities where your target audience already gathers. Participate genuinely for 30 days before mentioning your community. Then invite people directly.

Run a founding cohort model: a small group of 10-20 people who go through the first iteration of the community together, get intensive access to you, and pay a reduced price in exchange for feedback and testimonials. Use this cohort to build the proof before scaling.

## Part 5: Community Operations

The community is live. Members are paying. Now the work of retention begins.

### The Weekly Rhythm

Consistency in cadence is more important than any single piece of content. Members stay in paid communities when they know what to expect and can plan around it.

Build a weekly rhythm and publish it publicly:

- **Monday**: Weekly prompt or question to spark discussion. One post from you asking members to share where they are with their goal.
- **Wednesday**: Resource drop. One tool, resource, framework, or insight relevant to your community theme.
- **Thursday or Friday**: Live call (bi-weekly or weekly). Q&A, hot seat, or teaching session.

You are doing three things per week. This is 3-5 hours total. Sustainable for years.

What you do not do: post every day. Post randomly. Go weeks without engagement and then flood the community with content. Irregular cadence destroys trust.

### Onboarding New Members

Every new member receives the same onboarding experience. Automate as much of this as possible.

A good onboarding sequence:
1. Welcome email within 1 hour of joining. Includes login link, community guidelines, and one clear first step.
2. Introduce yourself post. Prompt them to share their name, their current situation, and their goal for joining.
3. A "start here" resource that orients them to the community structure.
4. A personal message from you within the first week. Not a template. One or two sentences that reference something they shared.

Members who complete onboarding are significantly less likely to churn in the first 90 days. Members who do not engage in the first two weeks are much more likely to cancel. Prioritize getting new members engaged early.

### Handling Low Engagement

Every community goes through quiet periods. The failure mode is letting silence compound into abandonment.

When engagement drops, do not panic. Do not flood the community with posts. Instead:

Ask one good question. Something specific, something that requires a personal answer, something that low-effort members can respond to in one sentence. Pull people back in gently.

Spotlight a member. Reach out to an active member and ask if you can share their progress in the community. Turn their story into a post. This reminds passive members of the value that others are getting.

Run a challenge. A short, structured challenge (5 days, 10 days) with a clear task and a visible finish line. Challenges reliably spike engagement because they give members an external prompt to take action.

## Part 6: Retention

Retention is the metric that determines whether your paid community is a real business or a recurring churn cycle.

The average paid community has a monthly churn rate of 5-12%. That means if you take no retention-specific actions, you are replacing 5-12% of your community every month just to stay flat.

The communities that grow to 100, 500, 1,000 members are the ones that bring churn below 5% while also adding new members.

### The Reasons People Cancel

Understanding why members cancel is the foundation of retention strategy. There are four primary reasons:

**Reason 1: They achieved their goal.** They joined to accomplish something specific, they accomplished it, and they do not see what staying offers them. Solution: design the community to extend the goal, not just achieve it. "Build your first 1,000 subscribers" becomes "build to 10,000, then monetize."

**Reason 2: Life changed.** Job loss, illness, family circumstances, financial pressure. These cancellations are not within your control. The only retention lever here is goodwill: give members an easy pause option rather than a forced cancel, and they will often return.

**Reason 3: They are not engaging.** They stopped coming to the community weeks ago and paying for it feels wrong. They cancel out of guilt, not dissatisfaction. Solution: re-engage members before they cancel. If someone has not engaged in 30 days, send a personal note.

**Reason 4: The value did not match the expectation.** They joined expecting X and experienced Y. Solution: make your offer description accurate. Overpromising to drive conversions leads to short-tenure members and poor reviews.

### Proactive Retention Actions

**The 90-day milestone.** Send every member a personal note at their 90-day mark. Acknowledge that they have been in the community for three months. Ask: what has changed since you joined? What do you most want to accomplish in the next 90 days?

This message serves two purposes. It makes the member feel seen. And it re-commits them to their goal, which is the primary reason they are paying.

**Wins tracking.** Build a culture where members share wins, however small. A weekly wins thread. A dedicated wins channel. Publicly celebrating member progress creates positive reinforcement for staying in the community and provides testimonial content for marketing.

**Annual review call.** Once per year, offer any member a 15-minute call with you to review their progress and set direction. Most members will not take you up on this. But knowing the option exists communicates that you are invested in them individually.

## Part 7: Growing Beyond the Founding Cohort

After you have a stable core of members and your churn is below 7%, you can focus on growth.

### Content Marketing for Community Growth

Your existing content drives awareness. But the content that converts viewers into paid community members is more specific than general brand-building content.

Create "inside the community" content: summaries of discussions that happened, anonymized member wins, themes from your weekly calls. This gives your broader audience a window into the community without giving them everything. It raises the perceived value and creates FOMO without hype.

### Referral Programs

Paid community members who refer friends are your lowest-cost acquisition channel. Build a referral incentive:

- One month free for every active referral.
- A bonus session or resource for every three referrals.
- Public recognition in the community.

Announce this program directly to your existing members. Do not rely on members to spontaneously refer people without prompting.

### Affiliate Partnerships

Identify creators in adjacent niches with audiences that overlap yours. Propose a simple affiliate arrangement: they mention your community to their audience, they earn a one-time or recurring commission on each member who joins through their link.

This works best when the creator genuinely believes in what you have built. Force a free trial membership before any promotion agreement. If they are not impressed, they will not promote it authentically.

## Part 8: Platform Selection

The platform decision matters more than most creators acknowledge, because switching costs are high once your community is established.

### Platform Options and Trade-offs

**Circle**: Clean design, strong community-building features, no algorithm. Best for creators who want a focused, professional environment. Has a course integration. Monthly cost scales with members.

**Discord**: Free to run, familiar to younger audiences, strong real-time communication. Weaker for structured content and curriculum. Members experience Discord fatigue because of notifications. Best for tech or gaming-adjacent communities.

**Skool**: Growing platform with gamification features and a built-in marketplace. Members can discover your community through Skool's directory. Strong for motivation-driven communities. Less flexible than others.

**Mighty Networks**: Oldest dedicated community platform. High customization. More complex setup. Better for larger, multi-course communities than for starting creators.

**Kajabi Community**: Good if you are already using Kajabi for courses. Integrated billing and membership management. More expensive at early stages.

For most creators launching their first community: Circle or Skool. Both are designed for paid communities, have clean mobile apps, and have the features you need at the early stage.

## Summary: The Path from Zero to 100 Members

**Month 1**: Design the offer. Write the outcome statement. Build the pre-launch waitlist.

**Month 2**: Run the pre-launch sequence. Launch to founding members. Onboard the first cohort with intensive attention.

**Month 3**: Establish the weekly rhythm. Build the onboarding system. Run the 90-day check-in for early members.

**Months 4-6**: Focus entirely on retention and engagement quality. Do not pursue growth aggressively until churn is under 7%.

**Months 7-12**: Layer in growth channels: content marketing, referral program, affiliate partnerships. Systematize operations further to reduce your weekly time investment.

The communities that fail do so in months 2-4. The creator launches, gets initial members, and then does not have a system for delivering consistent value. Members stop engaging. The creator feels guilty about the declining community. Inaction compounds. Churn spikes.

The communities that succeed follow a simple principle: design the system first, then launch. Every component of value delivery should have a documented process before you start charging.
