# The Solopreneur's Quarterly Planning System

**A quarterly planning system built for one-person businesses.**

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike

---

## Why Annual Goals Fail Solopreneurs

The standard annual goal-setting advice was written for organizations with departments, budget cycles, and teams who can execute a strategy over 12 months without the business pivoting under their feet.

A solopreneur's reality is different. Your priorities shift when a client leaves. Your revenue model evolves when you find a channel that works. Your energy fluctuates with health, family, and the general volatility of running everything yourself. A plan built for January is often obsolete by April, and the people who insisted on "sticking to the plan" either lie about how the year went or struggle to understand why their execution fell apart.

Annual planning for solopreneurs is mostly theater. You do it in December or January when motivation is high and uncertainty is low. By March, the plan is a document you feel guilty about not following.

Quarterly planning solves this without abandoning the discipline of planning entirely. A 90-day window is long enough to accomplish something meaningful, short enough that you can adapt without abandoning strategic direction, and frequent enough to stay calibrated to what is actually happening in your business.

This system gives you a complete quarterly planning process: the quarterly review, the goal-setting framework, the weekly tracking system, and the rules that keep the plan honest.

## Part 1: The Quarterly Review

Planning a new quarter without reviewing the previous one is guessing. The review is the foundation of the entire system because it grounds you in what is actually true about your business before you commit to what comes next.

### When to Run the Quarterly Review

Run the quarterly review in the last week of the current quarter, before the new quarter begins. Block two hours. This is not a quick check-in. It is a deliberate examination of four months of effort.

The calendar quarters (January-March, April-June, July-September, October-December) work fine as anchors. Some solopreneurs prefer their own quarterly start dates aligned to their business anniversary or fiscal year. Either works as long as it is consistent.

### The Five Review Questions

**Question 1: What was true about this quarter that was not in my plan?**

List everything that happened that you did not anticipate: new opportunities that emerged, setbacks you did not see coming, changes in your industry or audience, personal circumstances that affected your work.

This is not a complaint list. It is a calibration exercise. If you consistently find that your plans do not account for the type of surprises that routinely show up, your planning process needs to change.

**Question 2: What did I complete, and what did I leave unfinished?**

Go through every major commitment you made at the start of the quarter. For each one, record one of three statuses:

- Done: The work is complete and the outcome was achieved.
- Partial: The work started but was not completed, or was completed without the intended outcome.
- Abandoned: The commitment was dropped intentionally or by neglect.

Be precise. "Done" means done. A product you launched but never promoted is partial. A project you started and quietly stopped working on without making a conscious decision is abandoned, not deferred.

**Question 3: What results did I get, measured in numbers?**

For each area of your business, record the actual numbers. Revenue. New customers or clients. Email subscribers gained. Social following growth. Content published. Website traffic. Whatever metrics you track.

Do not evaluate these as good or bad yet. Just record them. The evaluation comes in the next question.

**Question 4: Which assumptions were wrong?**

When you planned the last quarter, you made assumptions: this product will convert at 3%, this content format will grow my audience, this client work will take X hours. Compare those assumptions to what actually happened.

The assumptions that were consistently wrong need to be updated. If you assumed you would have 30 focused work hours per week and you consistently have 20, your next quarter's plan needs to be built around 20 hours. Planning against reality you wish were true is the most common reason quarterly plans underdeliver.

**Question 5: What one thing, if I had done it differently, would have most changed this quarter's results?**

This is the synthesis question. You are looking for the highest-leverage insight from the review. Not a list of things you could improve. The one thing. Forcing yourself to choose one forces deeper thinking.

The answer to this question often becomes an input to your planning for the next quarter.

## Part 2: The Annual Orientation

Quarterly planning happens inside a larger context. Before setting quarterly goals, you need to know where the year is supposed to go.

### The Annual North Star

Each year, you choose one primary objective. Not a goal with a number attached (that comes later). An orientation. A direction.

Examples:

- This year is about building an audience.
- This year is about stabilizing revenue and reducing dependency on client work.
- This year is about launching and validating a product.
- This year is about deepening craft and positioning as an expert.

The Annual North Star is not a full plan. It is a filter. When you face a decision about where to focus, the North Star tells you which direction is forward and which direction is off course.

Write your Annual North Star at the start of the year. Review it at each quarterly planning session. Ask: does my plan for the next quarter advance the North Star or drift from it?

### Annual Revenue Target

Set one annual revenue target. This is not a stretch goal or a ceiling. It is a working number that tells you whether your quarterly revenue performance is on track.

Break it into quarterly targets. If your annual target is $60,000, your quarterly target is roughly $15,000 with adjustments for seasonality in your business (your Q4 may naturally be higher than Q1, for example).

Having a quarterly revenue target is not optional. Without a number, you cannot tell if a quarter was successful. "It felt okay" is not a planning input.

## Part 3: Setting Quarterly Goals

The quarterly goals are the three to five commitments you make to yourself for the next 90 days. They sit below the Annual North Star and above the weekly action plan.

### The One Primary Goal Rule

Every quarter has one Primary Goal. This is the goal that, if it is the only thing that gets done this quarter, the quarter was a success.

The Primary Goal should:
- Be directly connected to the Annual North Star.
- Be achievable in 90 days with focused effort.
- Have a clear, binary completion state. You either did it or you did not.
- Be within your control. "Get 500 new subscribers" is partly within your control. "Launch and promote a product to your existing audience" is entirely within your control.

Common mistakes with Primary Goals:
- Choosing a goal that is really two goals ("launch a product and build an audience").
- Choosing a goal so large it would require ideal conditions for 90 straight days.
- Choosing a goal that is an activity ("write consistently") rather than an outcome ("publish 12 pieces of content and build a content library of 30 pieces").

### Supporting Goals

Beyond the Primary Goal, you have two to four Supporting Goals. These are commitments that maintain or build other parts of your business while the Primary Goal gets the majority of your focused attention.

Supporting Goals should not require primary-level effort. If they do, one of them is actually a Primary Goal and you have too many priorities.

Example quarterly goal structure:

**Primary Goal**: Launch the paid community and reach 25 paying members by the end of the quarter.

**Supporting Goals**:
- Maintain current client revenue above $4,000 per month.
- Publish two pieces of long-form content per week.
- Complete the Q4 tax documentation.

The supporting goals keep the business running while you build the new thing. They are not growth goals. They are maintenance commitments.

### Anti-Goals

This is the most underused element of quarterly planning. Anti-Goals are things you will not do this quarter, stated explicitly.

Why they matter: solopreneurs are susceptible to opportunism. A new idea arrives, it is exciting, it seems aligned with your goals, and before you know it you are building something that competes for the same time and energy as your Primary Goal.

Anti-Goals close that loop. When the shiny object appears (and it will), you have a written record saying "I decided not to do this type of thing this quarter, and here is why."

Example Anti-Goals for a quarter focused on launching a paid community:
- I will not start a new course or coaching program this quarter.
- I will not take on new custom client work beyond what I already have.
- I will not launch a new social media account.
- I will not redesign my website.

Writing the anti-goals takes 10 minutes. They will save you from at least two significant detours.

## Part 4: Goal Structuring

A goal that is not structured into actions will not happen. This is not a motivational statement. It is a procedural one. "Launch a paid community" is not a plan. A plan is a sequence of actions with owners, timelines, and dependencies.

### The Decomposition Method

For each goal, work backwards from the outcome to the current moment.

**Step 1**: Define the final state precisely. What does completion look like? What will be true when this goal is achieved that is not true today?

**Step 2**: Identify the three to five major milestones between now and completion. These are the checkpoints you pass through, in order.

**Step 3**: For each milestone, identify the two to four tasks that must be completed to reach it.

**Step 4**: For each task, estimate the time required.

**Step 5**: Map the tasks and milestones to your weekly blocks. Week 1 covers this set of tasks. Week 2 covers this set. And so on.

At the end of this process, you have transformed an abstract goal into a concrete weekly plan. You know what you are working on each week of the quarter, how long it should take, and what sequence it follows.

### Identifying Blockers in Advance

Before finalizing the plan, review each milestone and ask: what could stop me from reaching this on schedule?

Common blocker categories for solopreneurs:
- Skill gap: you need to learn something before you can execute.
- Dependency on others: waiting on a contractor, a platform, an approval.
- Unclear decision: you cannot execute until a decision is made.
- Resource constraint: you need money, time, or tools you do not currently have.

For each blocker, note one action you can take before week one begins to reduce or eliminate it. Most blockers can be addressed with one email, one Google search, or one scheduled conversation. They exist because you have not yet engaged with them, not because they are genuinely difficult.

## Part 5: The Weekly Operating Rhythm

The quarterly plan exists to guide your weekly work. Without a weekly operating rhythm, the plan becomes a document you read once and then ignore.

### The Weekly Planning Session

Every Sunday or Monday morning, spend 30-45 minutes on the week ahead. This session has a specific structure:

**Review last week (10 minutes)**:
- What was completed?
- What was not completed? Why?
- What needs to carry over to this week?

**Check quarterly goals (5 minutes)**:
- What milestone are you currently working toward?
- Are you on schedule, ahead, or behind?
- If behind, what adjustment do you make this week?

**Set this week's priorities (15 minutes)**:
- Name your three most important tasks for the week. Not a to-do list of everything. The three things that most advance your quarterly goals.
- Assign each to a specific day and block.
- Review your calendar for appointments, commitments, and constraints that affect your available time.

**Plan the output (5 minutes)**:
- What content will you publish this week?
- What client deliverables are due?
- What does done look like at the end of the week?

**Flag the risk (5 minutes)**:
- What is most likely to knock you off track this week?
- Is there anything you can do before it happens to reduce that risk?

### The Daily Start Protocol

Each morning, before opening email or social media, spend five minutes:
- Reading your three weekly priorities.
- Identifying which one you will work on first.
- Setting a start time for deep work.

This protocol sounds trivial. It produces a measurable reduction in reactive, distraction-driven days because it starts with intention rather than inbox.

### Protecting Deep Work Blocks

Deep work is any work that advances your quarterly goals and requires sustained, uninterrupted focus. It is the work that grows the business.

Shallow work is everything else: email, administrative tasks, social media, scheduling, quick communications.

The default of an unmanaged schedule is that shallow work expands to fill the time available. Email consumes three hours. Notifications interrupt flow. By the end of the day, you have been busy but have not worked on anything that moves the needle.

The fix is non-negotiable: block deep work time before anything else is scheduled. Minimum two hours per day. Ideally four. These blocks are not available for meetings, calls, or any shallow work.

If your schedule currently does not support this, the problem is not time management. The problem is that your commitments are too high for your available time. The quarterly planning session is where you make the choice to reduce commitments until your deep work blocks are protected.

## Part 6: Tracking Progress

Progress without measurement is optimism. You need a tracking system that shows you, week by week, whether you are moving toward your quarterly goals.

### The Quarterly Scorecard

Build a simple spreadsheet or document with this structure:

| Week | Primary Goal Progress | Revenue | Content Published | Key Win | Key Problem |
|------|----------------------|---------|-------------------|---------|-------------|
| 1    |                      |         |                   |         |             |
| 2    |                      |         |                   |         |             |
...and so on through Week 13.

Update this every Sunday. It takes five minutes.

The scorecard has two purposes. First, it shows you trends. Three weeks of low Primary Goal progress before week 6 is a signal to address, not ignore. Second, it becomes the primary input for your next quarterly review. You are not reconstructing what happened from memory. You are reading a 13-week record.

### The 6-Week Check-In

At the midpoint of the quarter, run a shorter review focused on one question: is my Primary Goal still achievable, and should I stay the course or adjust?

The adjustment options are:
- Stay the course. The goal is still achievable with current trajectory.
- Adjust the tactics. The goal is right but the approach needs to change.
- Revise the goal. Something changed that makes the original goal unachievable or no longer the right priority.

Adjusting at week six is normal and healthy. Waiting until week twelve to acknowledge that the goal was off is a waste of six weeks.

Do not change the Primary Goal casually. Changing it because a shiny object appeared is the exact behavior Anti-Goals are designed to prevent. Changing it because the evidence genuinely demands adjustment is responsible management.

## Part 7: End of Quarter Rituals

The last week of each quarter is for closing, not new work.

### The Shutdown Ritual

On the last Friday of the quarter, do the following:

1. Review every open project and task. Close or defer each one deliberately. No open loops should carry over without a conscious decision.

2. Back up all work product from the quarter. Files, documents, databases, anything you created.

3. Write a one-paragraph summary of the quarter for your records. What was the headline? What is the most important thing that happened or that you learned?

4. Run the quarterly review questions from Part 1. Document the answers.

5. Celebrate something. Not everything, not nothing. Name the thing from this quarter that you are most proud of. Tell someone. Write it down.

The shutdown ritual creates psychological separation between quarters. It is the end of one chapter and the beginning of another. Without it, quarters blur together and the planning system loses its rhythm.

## Part 8: The Annual Quarterly Planning Calendar

Here is the full annual calendar view for a solopreneur running this system:

**December (Q4 Review + Q1 Planning)**
- Last week of December: Full Q4 review.
- Annual North Star review and reset for the new year.
- Q1 goals, decomposition, and weekly calendar built.

**March (Q1 Review + Q2 Planning)**
- Last week of March: Full Q1 review.
- Q2 goals set, anti-goals written, blockers identified.

**June (Q2 Review + Q3 Planning)**
- Last week of June: Full Q2 review.
- Q3 goals set. Assess Annual North Star progress at halfway point.

**September (Q3 Review + Q4 Planning)**
- Last week of September: Full Q3 review.
- Q4 goals set. Q4 is the quarter where many solopreneurs either sprint to hit annual targets or intentionally wind down and prepare for the new year.

Each planning session requires two hours for the review and two hours for the planning. Four hours per quarter. Sixteen hours per year. This is a small investment given what it prevents: a year of unfocused effort that looks productive in the moment and produces little that compounds.

## Part 9: Common Planning Failure Modes

Knowing the failure modes in advance lets you design against them.

**Failure Mode 1: Setting too many goals.**

A quarterly plan with eight goals is not a plan. It is a wish list. You will work on whichever goal feels most urgent on any given day, which is usually the one with the closest external deadline, not the one most important to your business. Limit quarterly goals to five maximum, with one clearly primary.

**Failure Mode 2: Confusing activity with progress.**

"Work on the product" is not a goal milestone. "Complete the first draft of all product content" is a milestone. Track milestones, not activities. Activities can fill a day without moving the needle. Milestones cannot be faked.

**Failure Mode 3: Not protecting the planning sessions themselves.**

The quarterly planning sessions will be crowded out by the business if you let them be. A client needs something urgently. A launch is late. A problem needs solving. If you do not block the planning session on your calendar weeks in advance and treat it as a non-negotiable appointment, it will not happen.

**Failure Mode 4: Planning in isolation.**

Many solopreneurs plan alone in a vacuum and never share their goals with anyone. Accountability is real. When someone else knows what you committed to, the psychological cost of not following through is higher. Share your quarterly goals with one person: a peer, a business friend, a community member.

**Failure Mode 5: Abandoning the system after a bad quarter.**

A bad quarter is not evidence that the planning system does not work. It is evidence that something in the execution broke down, or that the plan was wrong, or that circumstances changed. The review process exists precisely to diagnose this. A bad quarter followed by a good quarterly review is how a good system improves. A bad quarter followed by abandoning the system restarts from zero.

## Summary: The Full System in Five Steps

**Step 1: Annual Orientation.** Set one North Star and one annual revenue target. Do this once per year.

**Step 2: Quarterly Review.** In the last week of each quarter, run the five review questions. Document the answers.

**Step 3: Quarterly Goal Setting.** Set one Primary Goal, two to four Supporting Goals, and three to five Anti-Goals. Decompose the Primary Goal into milestones and weekly tasks.

**Step 4: Weekly Operating Rhythm.** Every week, plan priorities, assign deep work blocks, check quarterly progress, and flag risks.

**Step 5: Track and Adjust.** Maintain the Quarterly Scorecard. Run the 6-week check-in. Run the shutdown ritual at quarter end.

The system is simple. The discipline is in running it consistently, even when a quarter is going badly, even when a shiny object appears, even when you are busy. Especially then.
