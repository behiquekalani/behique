# Chapter 2: Know Your Value Before You Open Any Conversation

The most common mistake in creator-brand negotiations: the creator has no number in their head before the conversation starts.

The brand rep asks: "What are your rates?" And the creator panics, underquotes, or says "I'm flexible" which is code for "please lowball me."

You must have a rate card before you begin outreach. Not because you will share it with every brand immediately. But because knowing your number gives you the confidence to hold it when a brand pushes back.

Here is how to calculate your base rate.

Start with your engagement data. Pull your last 30 days of analytics from each platform. Calculate your average engagement rate: total engagements (likes, comments, saves, shares) divided by total followers, expressed as a percentage. For most mid-size creators in a niche, this is between 3 and 15 percent.

Now calculate your average reach per post. On Instagram, this is the average "accounts reached" metric. On YouTube, it is average views. On TikTok, it is average video views.

The CPM model: The simplest starting formula is CPM (cost per thousand impressions). Industry standard CPM for creator content ranges from $10 to $50 depending on niche and platform. Multiply your average reach by your CPM rate divided by 1,000.

Example: 8,000 average reach x $20 CPM / 1,000 = $160 per post as a floor.

But this is just the starting point. Apply multipliers based on:
- Niche premium: Finance, B2B, and health creators charge more because their audiences have higher buying power. Add 30 to 50 percent.
- Exclusivity: If the brand wants you to not work with competitors for 30 to 90 days, charge for that period.
- Usage rights: If the brand wants to repurpose your content in their ads, add 50 to 100 percent.
- Content format: Long-form YouTube videos cost more than a single Instagram story.

Build a simple tiered rate card:
- Instagram Story (3 frames): $150 to $500
- Instagram Feed Post: $300 to $1,200
- Instagram Reel (dedicated): $400 to $2,000
- YouTube Integration (30 to 60 seconds): $500 to $3,000
- YouTube Dedicated Video: $1,500 to $8,000
- TikTok Video (dedicated): $300 to $2,500

These ranges are wide because they scale with your engagement quality, niche authority, and follower count within the 5K to 100K range. You set the specific number. Then you hold it.

One rule that protects your income long-term: never work for free in exchange for "exposure" from a brand that could afford to pay you. Exposure does not pay rent. Brands that respect their creator partners pay them. Brands that pitch free collabs are not partners. They are advertisers looking for a discount.

# Chapter 3: Finding the Right Brands and Building Your Outreach System

Waiting to be discovered is a strategy for creators with infinite patience and finite ambition.

The creators earning consistent brand revenue are not waiting. They are building a pipeline. And the pipeline starts with a list.

Step 1: Define your brand fit categories.
Your content niche has a natural set of adjacent products and services your audience already uses or aspires to use. List five to ten categories of brands that fit your audience. A productivity creator's list might include: SaaS tools, journals and planners, standing desk companies, productivity apps, online course platforms, and ergonomic accessories.

Step 2: Build your target brand list.
For each category, identify 10 to 20 specific brands. Look at what your audience already talks about in comments and DMs. Look at what products you actually use and endorse. Look at what brands sponsor creators one to three levels above you in follower count. Those brands are proven to pay, and they need partners at your level too.

Sources for finding brands: Instagram's sponsored content tag (look at posts from creators in your space), Aspire IQ, Creator.co, and the Grin marketplace. But do not limit yourself to platforms. Direct outreach to brands that fit your niche is often more effective.

Step 3: Find the right contact.
For small to mid-size brands, the contact is often the founder, CMO, or marketing manager. LinkedIn is the best tool for finding them. Search the company name and look for titles like "Head of Partnerships," "Influencer Marketing Manager," or "Brand Marketing." If the company is small, email the founder directly.

For large brands, look for a "creator partnerships" or "influencer" page on their website. Many major brands have a portal for partnership applications.

Step 4: Write a first contact message that earns a response.
The outreach message that works is not a pitch. It is a proof of fit with a clear ask.

Template:
"Hi [Name], I'm [Your Name], a [niche] creator with [X] followers on [platform] and [X%] average engagement. My audience is primarily [demographic] who are [specific trait relevant to their brand]. I've been using [their product] for [time period] and have mentioned it in [specific piece of content]. I'd love to explore a formal partnership. Do you have 20 minutes this week to talk through what that could look like?"

What makes this work:
- It is short. Fewer than 150 words.
- It leads with your data, not your personality.
- It proves you already know and use the product. You are not a stranger asking for money. You are a real customer asking to get paid for what you already do.
- It makes a small, easy ask (a 20-minute call) rather than jumping straight to rates.

Send this message to 10 to 20 brands per month. Expect a 5 to 15 percent positive response rate. That means one to three interested brands per month from cold outreach alone.

# Chapter 4: Negotiating Terms That Protect You

The moment a brand says "we're interested," most creators make the same mistake: they rush to close.

They agree to vague deliverables. They accept "we'll pay net 60" without question. They let the brand use their content in ads without extra compensation. They do not include a kill fee. And then they wonder why the brand experience felt exploitative.

The negotiation is where you protect your time, your content, and your reputation. Here is what needs to be in every deal.

Deliverables in writing: Every piece of content you agree to create must be listed explicitly. Number of posts, format (story, feed, Reel, YouTube), length, and deadline. Vague deliverables lead to scope creep. A brand that initially asked for "a few posts" will suddenly want five if you do not put a number in writing.

Revision policy: Specify how many rounds of revisions are included. One round of revisions before posting is standard. More than that should be billable. Without this clause, some brands will request changes endlessly.

Usage rights: This is the most financially significant clause most creators skip. There are three tiers of usage:
- Organic use: the brand can share your content on their own organic social channels. Usually included in base rate.
- Paid amplification: the brand can run your content as paid ads. This should cost 50 to 100 percent on top of your base rate.
- Exclusivity of usage rights: they own the content and can use it anywhere, indefinitely. This is a buyout and should cost significantly more.

Exclusivity: If a brand wants you to not promote competitors, charge for it. Standard exclusivity rates are 25 to 50 percent of the base deal value per month of exclusivity.

Payment terms: Net 30 is standard. Net 60 is the maximum you should accept. Require a 50 percent deposit before beginning any work. If a brand will not pay a deposit, they are either low on cash or low on respect for your time. Both are red flags.

Kill fee: If the brand cancels a project after you have started work, a kill fee applies. Standard kill fee is 25 to 50 percent of the total project value. This protects you from doing significant creative work for a deal that evaporates.

FTC disclosure: You are legally required to disclose paid partnerships. Include in your contract that you will add #ad or #sponsored to all sponsored content per FTC guidelines. This also protects you from brands who ask you to not disclose.

You do not need a lawyer for a basic creator contract. There are several free and paid creator contract templates available from organizations like Creator.co and the Influencer Marketing Factory. Find one that covers all the above clauses and customize it to your specific situation.

# Chapter 5: Executing Deals That Create Repeat Business

The contract is signed. The deposit is in. Now the real work begins.

Most brand deals are one-time because the creator's execution was mediocre. They submitted content late, needed too many revisions, communicated poorly, and delivered results without data. The brand never came back.

The creators who earn $5,000 to $15,000 per month in brand revenue are not constantly finding new brands. They are getting repeat deals from the same brands because they execute better than anyone else.

Here is the execution process that leads to repeat business.

Onboarding call: Before creating anything, schedule a 30-minute call with the brand contact. Ask these questions: What is the one key message you want your audience to take away? What has worked best in previous creator partnerships? What should I avoid? Is there specific product messaging you need included? What KPIs will you use to measure success?

This call does two things. It ensures you create content the brand actually needs. And it builds a human relationship with the person who controls the budget. That relationship is worth money.

Creative brief in writing: After the call, send a one-page brief summarizing what you plan to create. Headline of the content, core message, call to action, delivery format, and proposed posting date. Ask for written approval before you begin production. This prevents the "that's not what we asked for" conversation after you have already done the work.

Early draft submission: Submit your first draft at least five to seven days before the agreed posting date. This gives the brand time to review, request revisions, and gives you time to revise without stress. Late submissions are the number one reason brands do not return.

Performance report: Within 48 hours of posting, send the brand a simple performance report. Screenshot your analytics: reach, impressions, engagement rate, link clicks if trackable. Add one sentence about audience reception. This is the extra step 95 percent of creators skip. It is the step that makes the brand think "this creator is a professional."

Follow-up email: Two weeks after posting, send a follow-up. Share any additional metrics. If the content performed particularly well, note it. If there were any sales or traffic signals you can track, share them. Close with: "I'd love to explore what a longer-term partnership could look like. Do you have capacity to discuss?"

That last sentence is worth thousands of dollars. Most creators wait for the brand to come back. You ask. Brands respect that. It makes them feel wanted. It makes the conversation easy to continue.

The creators who execute this process consistently turn one-time deals into quarterly or annual retainers. A retainer at $2,000 per month from one brand is $24,000 per year. Three retainers is $72,000. That is a career built on execution, not luck.

# Chapter 6: Building a Brand Partnership Pipeline That Pays Consistently

A single brand deal is income. A pipeline of brand deals is a business.

The difference is system versus luck. Creators who occasionally land a brand deal are relying on luck. Creators who earn consistent brand revenue are operating a sales pipeline. They know exactly how many conversations are open, how many are in negotiation, and how many have converted.

Here is how to build the pipeline.

Stage 1: Prospects. This is your list of target brands you have not yet contacted. Your goal is to always have 30 to 50 prospects in this stage. When you move a brand forward, you add a new one. The pipeline never runs dry.

Stage 2: Contacted. Brands you have sent your first outreach message to. Track the date of first contact. If they have not responded within two weeks, send one follow-up. If still no response, move them back to prospects and try again in 90 days.

Stage 3: Interested. Brands that have responded positively and engaged in some form of conversation. These are live opportunities. Move quickly here. Response time is the metric that matters. Brands evaluate your professionalism by how fast and how clearly you respond.

Stage 4: Proposal sent. You have submitted rates and terms. Follow up within five business days if you have not heard back. Do not be passive.

Stage 5: Active deal. Contract signed, work in progress. This is where your execution process matters most.

Stage 6: Completed. Deal delivered, performance report sent. Begin nurture process for repeat deals.

Track all of this in one place. A Notion table or a Google Sheet with columns for brand name, stage, last contact date, deal value, and notes. You do not need a CRM. You need visibility.

To keep your pipeline active, block two hours every Monday for partnership business development. During this time: send outreach to five new prospects, follow up with any brands in stages two and three, review active deals for upcoming deadlines, and send performance reports for any recently completed deals.

Two hours per week, done consistently for three months, is enough to generate $2,000 to $5,000 per month in brand revenue from a 5,000 to 50,000 follower account. The math is not complicated. The discipline is what most creators lack.

Set the calendar block. Keep the block. Watch the pipeline fill.
