# Client Intake Form System
## Collect the Information You Need Before the Work Starts

---

## What an Intake Form Is Really For

Most solopreneurs treat the client intake form as an administrative formality. You build one because it seems professional, clients fill it out because you ask them to, and the information sits somewhere until you need it. The form exists to capture data.

That is the wrong frame.

The intake form is a diagnostic tool. It is how you learn whether a client is actually ready to work with you, what their project truly involves before you start, and whether the relationship has the conditions it needs to succeed. Done well, an intake form does work that a 30-minute discovery call cannot.

Here is what a good intake form actually accomplishes:

**It surfaces mismatches before they become problems.** A client who is vague about their budget, unclear about their timeline, or uncertain about what success looks like is not ready for the work to start. The intake form reveals this. Better to find out before you sign a contract than three weeks into a project when the scope has expanded and the client's expectations have exceeded your agreement.

**It transfers information asymmetry.** At the start of any engagement, the client knows far more about their business, their history with this problem, and their internal constraints than you do. The intake form is how you close that gap systematically, rather than discovering gaps during execution.

**It sets the professional tone.** Clients who go through a structured intake process understand they are working with someone organized and intentional. This affects how they treat timelines, feedback requests, and payment terms throughout the engagement. The intake form is a signal.

**It protects you legally.** Documented answers about scope, goals, and constraints become part of your project record. If a client later claims the work did not deliver what they expected, your intake form shows what they said they expected at the start.

## The 12 Questions Every Solopreneur Intake Form Should Include

These questions are not arbitrary. Each one serves a specific diagnostic function. The order matters because earlier answers provide context for later ones.

**1. Describe the project in your own words.**

Not "what do you need" but "in your own words." This open-ended framing reveals how clearly the client understands their own request. Clients who write two coherent sentences are in a different place than clients who write three contradictory paragraphs. Both can be good clients. They require different onboarding approaches.

**2. What does a successful outcome look like to you?**

This is the most important question on the form. Success is not self-evident. A client who hires a web designer might define success as "looks good," "drives more sales," "my team can update it themselves," or "my boss approves it." These are different projects. You need to know which one you are building before you start.

**3. What has already been tried?**

Every client comes to you because something did not work before, whether it was a previous vendor, an internal attempt, or a different approach. Understanding what failed and why prevents you from repeating it. It also gives you a baseline for how complicated the problem actually is.

**4. Who are the decision-makers on your end?**

Working with a client who has to get approval from a committee is a different engagement than working with a single founder who makes decisions alone. Find out now. If there are multiple stakeholders, ask for their names and their roles. You need to know who can actually approve work.

**5. What is your target date for completion?**

Not "when do you want this done" but target date. This framing acknowledges that timelines involve your schedule too. Record their answer, then evaluate it against your capacity. If their target is unrealistic, you need to address this before the contract is signed, not after.

**6. What is your budget for this project?**

Budget belongs in the intake form, not the discovery call. This is the question most solopreneurs are afraid to include because they do not want to scare off clients. It is the question that saves you the most time. A client who is not willing to share their budget range before starting a relationship is telling you something important about the working dynamic to come.

Use ranges if necessary: "Under $1,000 / $1,000-$3,000 / $3,000-$7,500 / $7,500+" This makes the question easier to answer and gives you the information you need.

**7. How did you find us?**

Referral, search, social media, content, event. This is useful data for understanding where your best clients come from. Referral clients and cold search clients often have very different expectations and levels of fit. Knowing the source lets you calibrate your onboarding accordingly.

**8. What is the one thing you most want to avoid in this engagement?**

This question surfaces fears and non-negotiables that clients rarely volunteer. Common answers: "I do not want constant back-and-forth revisions," "I need clear communication, my last vendor disappeared for weeks," "I want to avoid scope creep," "I need someone who will tell me if they think my idea is wrong." Each of these answers tells you something concrete about what to prioritize and what to watch out for.

**9. What does the approval process look like on your end?**

How many rounds of revisions are expected? Who signs off? What is the typical turnaround time when you send something for review? This question protects your timeline. If your contract allows for two rounds of revisions but the client's approval process involves six stakeholders who each take a week to review, you have a scope problem waiting to happen.

**10. Do you have any existing assets relevant to this project?**

Brand guidelines, previous work files, content drafts, analytics data, access credentials. Finding out on day one what exists and where it lives saves hours of back-and-forth during execution. If they have assets but cannot locate or provide them, that tells you something about internal organization and potential friction.

**11. Is there anything about your business or situation we should know before starting?**

This open field catches what your structured questions missed. Unusual constraints, internal politics, an upcoming acquisition, a rebrand in progress, a co-founder conflict. Clients will sometimes disclose things here that they would not have mentioned in a structured question.

**12. Do you agree to the project terms and conditions linked below?**

A checkbox with a link to your standard terms. This is not a contract. But it is a documented acknowledgment that your terms were presented before the work started. This matters if you ever have a dispute.

## Why Timeline and Budget Questions Belong in Intake

Many solopreneurs put timeline and budget conversations in the discovery call because it feels more natural to discuss them in conversation. This is understandable. It is also a mistake.

**Written answers are more honest.** In a conversation, clients often give aspirational answers. They say "as soon as possible" instead of "by March 1" because they feel pressure to sound decisive. They say "budget is flexible" instead of "we have $4,000" because they want negotiating room. A written form prompts more accurate disclosure because there is no conversational pressure.

**Written answers create a record.** When a client later says "I told you I needed this by the 15th," you have their intake form showing they wrote "end of month." Written documentation prevents memory disputes.

**It filters uncommitted prospects.** A client who is genuinely ready to move forward will fill out a thorough intake form. A prospect who is still shopping around or not fully committed will either skip questions or give vague answers. This is useful signal before you invest hours in a discovery process.

**It makes the discovery call more valuable.** When you receive a completed intake form before a call, you already know the project basics. Your call can focus on the nuanced questions rather than basic data collection. You sound more prepared, you ask better questions, and you use the client's time more efficiently.

## How to Use Intake Responses to Prepare for Kickoff

A completed intake form is information. What you do with it determines whether it was worth collecting.

**Before the kickoff call:**

Read the intake form completely. Identify the three most important pieces of information: the success definition, the timeline, and anything from the "what to avoid" question. These are your anchors for the entire relationship.

Flag vague or contradictory answers. If the client said their budget is "$3,000-$7,500" but their project description would require $12,000 of work, that gap needs to be addressed before the kickoff, not during it.

Prepare specific questions. The intake form shows you what you do not know yet. Turn those gaps into specific questions for the kickoff call.

Confirm asset availability. If the client said they have brand guidelines and previous work files, confirm you have received them or schedule a handoff time. Waiting until week two to discover that the brand guidelines are on a hard drive somewhere adds friction.

**During kickoff:**

Reference the intake form directly. "You mentioned you want to avoid the constant back-and-forth you experienced before. Here is how we handle revisions in our process." This shows you read their answers and signals that their input shaped the engagement structure.

Confirm ambiguous answers out loud. "You wrote that success means your team can update the site themselves. What does 'update' mean to you specifically, content changes or structural changes?" Confirm before assuming.

**After kickoff:**

Save the intake form in your project folder alongside the contract, communications, and deliverables. It is part of your project documentation. If you use a project management tool, attach it to the project record.

## Intake Automation Options

A good intake form does not need to be complex. The tools that handle it range from free to low-cost.

**Google Forms.** Free. Sends responses to a Google Sheet automatically. Easy to set up. Lacks polish but works reliably. Good starting point if you have zero systems in place.

**Typeform.** Conversational format that many clients find less intimidating than a long form. Has a free tier with limited questions. Paid plans allow conditional logic (different questions based on previous answers) which is useful if you serve multiple service types.

**Notion Forms or HubSpot Free Forms.** If you already use these tools, use their native form functionality so responses land directly in your CRM or project system.

**Dubsado or HoneyBook.** If you want your intake form to be part of an end-to-end client management system that also handles contracts, invoices, and scheduling, these platforms integrate intake into a full workflow. More setup time. More consistent client experience.

**The most important automation:** Make sure the form triggers an automatic confirmation email to the client when they submit. Confirm you received it, give a realistic timeframe for your response, and tell them what happens next. Clients who fill out a form and hear nothing become anxious clients before the work even starts.

## What Happens When Someone Skips or Rushes the Form

This will happen. What you do about it matters.

A client who skips the intake form has told you something. Either they do not have time for your process, they are disorganized, they do not take your requirements seriously, or they are testing whether your policies are actually policies.

**What not to do:** Skip the intake and proceed anyway, then discover the missing information creates problems mid-project. This trains clients that your requirements are optional.

**What to do:** Send a polite, direct follow-up.

Script: "Before we schedule our kickoff call, I need the completed intake form. It takes about 10 minutes and helps me prepare the right way for your project. Here is the link. Once I receive it, I will follow up within [X business days] to schedule our next step."

If a client rushes through the form with minimal answers, treat those vague answers as a prompt for your kickoff call. "You wrote that your timeline is flexible. Can we put a specific target date down so we can build a project schedule?" Do not let vagueness carry forward.

**The pattern to watch for:** A client who is vague about success criteria, vague about budget, and vague about their approval process is a client who will expand scope mid-project and be difficult to satisfy. This is not certain, but it is a pattern. Your intake form gives you early visibility. Use it.

## Templates

**Intake form link message (send to new clients):**

"To prepare for your project, please complete our client intake form. It covers the key information I need before our first call and usually takes about 10 minutes. [Link]. I will follow up within [X] business days with next steps."

---

**Budget question framing (use this exactly):**

"What is your anticipated budget for this project?
[ ] Under $1,000
[ ] $1,000 to $3,000
[ ] $3,000 to $7,500
[ ] $7,500 to $15,000
[ ] Over $15,000
[ ] I am not sure yet (note: a budget range helps us scope correctly)"

---

**Success criteria prompt:**

"Describe what a successful outcome looks like for this project. Be specific. What will be true after we complete the work that is not true now?"

---

**What to avoid prompt:**

"Is there anything specific you want to avoid in this engagement? This could be a communication style, a type of deliverable, a process issue, or anything from a previous experience."

---

**Follow-up template for missing intake:**

"Hi [Name], I want to make sure we have everything we need to start your project on the right foot. I have not yet received your completed intake form. Here is the link again: [link]. Once I receive it, I will schedule our kickoff call and we can move forward. Let me know if you have any questions about the form."

---

The intake form is a small system. It costs 10 minutes of a client's time and protects weeks of yours. Build it once, refine it over a few engagements, and make it a non-negotiable step in your process. The solopreneurs who have the cleanest client relationships are not the ones with the best people skills. They are the ones with the clearest systems.
