## A Note Before You Start

A checklist without explanation is just a to-do list you will ignore.

This guide is different. Every item in every checklist section is accompanied by context: why it matters, what happens if you skip it, and what good execution looks like. The goal is not just to give you tasks. It is to give you understanding so you can execute intelligently, adapt when things change, and make decisions instead of just following steps.

The checklist format works because digital business setup has a real sequence. Some things must happen before others. Some things that feel urgent are actually optional. Some things that feel optional will cost you significantly later if you skip them.

This guide gives you the correct order and the correct reasoning.

Seven sections. The first 90 days. One functional digital business at the end.

## Introduction: What a Digital Business Actually Is

A digital business is any business that delivers value through digital means. That includes: information products (ebooks, courses, guides, templates), digital services (consulting, coaching, freelance work), software (tools, apps, SaaS), communities (memberships, group programs), and content businesses (newsletters, YouTube channels, podcasts monetized through sponsorship or products).

What these all have in common: low physical overhead, global reach, and the ability to sell the same product to many people without proportional cost increases.

A digital solopreneur is a single person running one of these businesses. No team required. You are the creator, the operator, and the marketer. This is a meaningful constraint but also a meaningful advantage: decisions get made fast, costs stay low, and everything is under your control.

The downside of full control is that there is no one else to blame when things do not get done. This checklist exists to make sure things get done.

Work through it in order. Do not skip ahead. The items in Section 2 depend on decisions made in Section 1. The items in Section 5 depend on infrastructure built in Section 3.

Start at the beginning.

## Section 1: Legal and Business Foundation

### Why This Comes First

Most people want to skip legal setup and go straight to building products. That impulse is understandable. Legal work is boring. Products are exciting.

But legal structure determines tax efficiency, personal liability protection, and professional credibility. Getting it wrong or skipping it creates real problems when your business starts generating real money. Fix it now when it costs little. Fixing it later costs more.

---

**[ ] 1.1 Decide on your business structure**

Your two primary options as a solopreneur are sole proprietorship and single-member LLC.

A sole proprietorship is the simplest structure. No registration required in most places. You simply operate under your own name or a trade name (DBA). The downside: your personal assets are not legally separated from your business assets. If someone sues your business, they can come after your personal savings.

A single-member LLC (Limited Liability Company) creates a legal separation between you and your business. This limits your personal liability to your investment in the company. The cost to form an LLC varies by state, typically $50 to $500. It is worth it if you plan to run this seriously.

For a solopreneur selling digital products: start as a sole proprietorship if you want to move fast, but plan to form an LLC within your first six months of revenue.

**[ ] 1.2 Choose and register your business name**

Your business name should be:
- Easy to spell and pronounce
- Available as a domain (check before committing)
- Not already trademarked in your industry (do a basic search at the USPTO if you are in the US)

Register it as a DBA (Doing Business As) with your local government if you are operating under a name other than your legal name. This is usually a simple, low-cost filing.

**[ ] 1.3 Get an EIN (Employer Identification Number)**

Even without employees, a US-based solopreneur should have an EIN. It allows you to open a business bank account, take on clients who require it for payment, and file taxes properly.

You can apply for an EIN for free in about 5 minutes at irs.gov. There is no reason to pay a service to do this for you.

**[ ] 1.4 Open a dedicated business bank account**

A separate bank account for your business is non-negotiable. Mixing personal and business finances creates accounting nightmares, complicates taxes, and looks unprofessional to clients.

Open a free business checking account. Many online banks (Relay, Mercury, Found) offer zero-fee business accounts with good digital interfaces. Traditional banks work too. The key is that the account is used exclusively for business transactions.

**[ ] 1.5 Set up basic accounting**

You do not need an accountant yet. You need a system.

At minimum: a spreadsheet where every transaction in and out of your business account is recorded. Revenue, expenses, category, date.

Better: a simple accounting tool like Wave (free) or QuickTime (basic paid). These connect to your bank account, categorize transactions, and produce income statements when you need them.

Set aside 25-30 percent of every dollar of profit for taxes. Create a separate savings account for this. Pay quarterly estimated taxes if you are US-based and expect to owe more than $1,000 per year.

**[ ] 1.6 Draft basic terms of service and privacy policy**

Every website that collects personal information (including email addresses) needs a privacy policy. Every business selling products or services needs terms of service.

You do not need a lawyer for these yet. Free generators (Termly, Iubenda, or plain text templates) produce acceptable documents for a small solopreneur business. Review them annually as your business grows.

## Section 2: Domain and Hosting

### Why This Comes Second

Your digital home needs to exist before anything else you build. Customers, email service providers, payment processors, and every other part of your infrastructure points back to your domain. Set it up early and set it up correctly.

---

**[ ] 2.1 Purchase your domain name**

Use a reputable registrar: Namecheap, Cloudflare, or Google Domains (now Squarespace Domains). Avoid GoDaddy for new registrations. Their upsells are aggressive and their interface is poor.

Get the .com if it is available. If not, .co or .io are acceptable in the digital business world. Avoid obscure TLDs for a primary business domain.

Buy the domain for two years minimum. Annual renewals are an easy thing to forget, and letting a domain expire after building a business around it is a painful problem.

Do not buy every possible variation and extension. Just get the one you will actually use.

**[ ] 2.2 Choose your hosting or site platform**

Your options depend on what you are building.

If you need a simple landing page or personal site: Carrd ($19/year), Webflow (free to $14/month), or WordPress.com. Fast to set up, no coding required.

If you need a self-hosted site with full control: WordPress.org with a hosting provider (SiteGround, Cloudways, or Namecheap Hosting). More flexible, more work.

If you are building a pure content business: a newsletter platform like Beehiiv, Ghost, or Substack can be your home base. Some of these include a public website component.

For most digital solopreneurs starting out: Carrd for a landing page, or Beehiiv/Ghost if your core product is a newsletter. Keep it simple until you have a reason to add complexity.

**[ ] 2.3 Point your domain to your hosting**

Set up DNS records to connect your domain to wherever your site is hosted. Every hosting platform has documentation for this. It typically takes 15 minutes of configuration and up to 24 hours for DNS propagation.

Test it. Open your domain in an incognito window. Confirm it loads.

**[ ] 2.4 Install SSL/HTTPS**

Every website needs HTTPS. This encrypts the connection between your site and visitors. Most hosting platforms handle this automatically through Let's Encrypt. If your site shows "Not Secure" in the browser address bar, fix this before you send anyone there.

**[ ] 2.5 Set up a professional email address**

Use your domain for email: name@yourdomain.com. Not a Gmail or Hotmail address.

Google Workspace ($6/month) is the standard option. Fastmail is cheaper and private. Zoho Mail has a free tier for a single user.

A professional email address builds trust. It costs almost nothing. There is no reason to use a personal email for business communication.

## Section 3: Payment Processing

### Why This Matters More Than Most People Think

You can have the best product in your niche. If paying for it is difficult, confusing, or inaccessible, you will lose sales. Payment infrastructure is not a backend detail. It is a conversion element.

---

**[ ] 3.1 Choose your primary payment processor**

For digital products, your best options are:

**Stripe**: The industry standard. Accepts cards globally, has excellent documentation, integrates with nearly everything. 2.9 percent plus 30 cents per transaction. Apply for a Stripe account early. They sometimes request identity verification and it can take time.

**PayPal**: Widely trusted by buyers who are reluctant to enter card details directly. Less ideal as your only payment option, but worth having as an alternative.

**Gumroad**: If you use Gumroad as your product delivery platform, payment is built in. They charge a percentage per sale. No monthly fee on the free plan. Simple, effective.

Apply for your payment processor account before you need it. Approvals are not instant.

**[ ] 3.2 Complete identity verification**

All payment processors require identity verification to payout funds. This means submitting your government ID, bank account information, and business details.

Do this on day one. Discovering you cannot receive payouts after you make sales is a frustrating problem that is entirely preventable.

**[ ] 3.3 Add payout banking details**

Connect your business bank account to your payment processor. Set your payout schedule (weekly or daily if available). Verify a small test deposit completes successfully.

**[ ] 3.4 Understand your tax obligations on digital sales**

Digital product sales are subject to sales tax in some US states (economic nexus laws) and to VAT in many countries. This gets complicated quickly if you sell internationally.

At the start, use a platform like Gumroad or Paddle that handles tax collection and remittance automatically. This removes a significant compliance burden. As your volume grows, revisit this with an accountant.

**[ ] 3.5 Set up a clear refund policy**

Define your policy before your first sale. Common approaches: no refunds (only appropriate for very low-priced items), 7-day refund window, 30-day satisfaction guarantee.

A clear, fair refund policy increases conversion rates. Buyers feel less risk. It also prevents disputes, which damage your payment processor standing.

## Section 4: Email List Setup

### Why Email Is Your Most Valuable Asset

Your email list is the one piece of your digital business you fully own. Social media platforms can change algorithms, suspend accounts, or shut down. Your email list stays with you.

A subscriber who has given you permission to email them is more valuable than 100 social media followers. They are showing active interest. They are reachable. The average email open rate far exceeds the average organic social reach.

Build the list from day one. Every other marketing activity should funnel back to growing it.

---

**[ ] 4.1 Choose an email service provider (ESP)**

Your ESP is where you store subscribers, send emails, and build automation sequences.

**ConvertKit (now Kit)**: The standard choice for solopreneur content creators and course sellers. Excellent automation, clean UI, good deliverability. Free up to 10,000 subscribers. Creator-first features like the link page and tip jar built in.

**Beehiiv**: Strong choice if newsletters are your core content format. Built-in referral program, analytics focused, and a growing monetization ecosystem. Free up to 2,500 subscribers.

**MailerLite**: Simple, affordable, and beginner-friendly. Good for straightforward email marketing without complex automation needs.

**Flodesk**: Beautiful email designs, flat monthly pricing. Good for creators where aesthetics matter. Slightly less automation capability than ConvertKit.

For most digital solopreneurs: start with ConvertKit's free plan. It handles everything you need early, the automation is powerful, and you can stay on it well into a real business.

**[ ] 4.2 Configure your sender domain**

Set up custom domain authentication for your email. This means configuring SPF, DKIM, and DMARC DNS records. Your ESP will give you the exact records to add.

This is technical but important. Without proper email authentication, your emails land in spam folders. Your ESP will walk you through the setup. It takes about 20 minutes.

**[ ] 4.3 Create your welcome sequence**

The welcome sequence is the first series of emails a new subscriber receives automatically after opting in.

A minimum viable welcome sequence is three emails:

Email 1 (immediate): Deliver the promised lead magnet or welcome message. Tell them who you are and what they can expect from you.

Email 2 (day 2 or 3): Share something genuinely useful. A story, a framework, a tool. Build trust before you sell anything.

Email 3 (day 5 to 7): Introduce your most relevant product or service. This is a soft introduction, not a hard sell. Tell them what it is and who it helps.

**[ ] 4.4 Create your lead magnet**

A lead magnet is the free resource you offer in exchange for an email address.

Effective lead magnets are:
- Immediately useful (solvable in one sitting)
- Specifically relevant to your product
- High perceived value relative to their cost (zero)

Common formats: PDF guide, checklist, template, short video, email course, quiz.

The lead magnet should address the same problem your core product solves, at a surface level. Someone who downloads your lead magnet is raising their hand as a person with the exact problem you help solve.

**[ ] 4.5 Create an opt-in page**

A single-purpose page with one goal: get the visitor to subscribe and receive the lead magnet.

No navigation. No distractions. One headline that describes the benefit of subscribing, one brief description of what they get, one email field, one button.

Every piece of your marketing should drive traffic to this page.

**[ ] 4.6 Set your email sending schedule**

Decide how often you will email your list. Once a week is standard for most creator businesses. Twice a week if you have a lot to say and your audience expects it.

Consistency matters more than frequency. Emailing weekly for a year builds a relationship that emailing daily for two months and then going silent does not.

## Section 5: First Product

### Why You Should Build This Sooner Than Feels Comfortable

The temptation is to wait until you have an audience before building a product. But your product informs your marketing. Knowing exactly what you are selling makes every piece of content you create more targeted and more effective.

Build a product early. Build a small one. The act of selling, even one copy, teaches you more than six months of audience building without a product.

---

**[ ] 5.1 Validate the topic before building**

Do not build based on assumption. Before you create anything, confirm that people want to pay for the outcome you are offering.

Validation methods:
- Direct outreach to 10 to 20 relevant people asking if they would buy
- Pre-sale offer: price the product, try to sell it before it exists
- Forum and community research: find threads where people ask for exactly this help
- Competitor research: are other products in this space selling?

If you cannot find evidence of commercial demand, adjust the topic until you can.

**[ ] 5.2 Write the outcome statement**

In one sentence: "After buying and using this product, my customer will be able to ___."

This statement drives every product decision. Content that does not serve the outcome gets cut. The price is justified by the value of the outcome. The marketing is built around the gap between where the customer is now and where the outcome puts them.

Write the outcome statement before you write a single word of product content.

**[ ] 5.3 Choose the right product format**

Match the format to the problem.

PDF guide or ebook: best for comprehensive overviews, frameworks, reference material. Low production cost. Easy to create and update.

Checklist or template: best for process-based problems where the buyer needs a tool, not just information. High perceived value for simple content.

Online course: best for multi-step skill development where the sequence and explanation matter. Higher production cost, higher price ceiling.

Swipe file or resource list: best for buyers who need curated resources, not instruction. Fast to build, high value for the right audience.

Start with the simplest format that delivers the outcome. Add production value after you have validated the demand.

**[ ] 5.4 Write or record the product content**

Set a deadline. A product that is perpetually "almost done" is not a product.

For a PDF guide or ebook: write daily, even 500 words at a time. A 5,000-word guide can be drafted in 10 days of focused writing.

For a course: record one lesson per day. A 10-lesson course is done in two weeks.

Done is better than perfect. You can update the product after launch. You cannot make money from a product that does not exist.

**[ ] 5.5 Design basic product packaging**

The cover image or thumbnail of your product is a sales asset. A well-designed cover communicates professionalism and increases perceived value.

Use Canva. There are templates for ebook covers, course thumbnails, and product mockups. Spend 30 to 60 minutes getting the design to a professional standard.

You do not need a graphic designer. You need a clean, readable design that reflects your brand colors and includes the product title clearly.

**[ ] 5.6 Write the product sales page**

Your sales page has six components:

1. Headline: the outcome the customer achieves.
2. Problem: the specific pain they are experiencing before buying.
3. Agitation: why common solutions fail.
4. Solution: what this product does and how it works.
5. Proof: testimonials, results, your own credentials or story.
6. Call to action: the price, the buy button, and any guarantee.

Each section builds on the previous one. Read it aloud. If it sounds like a human wrote it for another human, you are on the right track.

**[ ] 5.7 Set up the product delivery**

However the customer pays, they should receive the product automatically.

On Gumroad: upload the file, set the price, connect Stripe or PayPal. When someone buys, Gumroad emails the download link automatically.

On Teachable or Kajabi: the student account is created on purchase and they get immediate access.

Test the purchase flow yourself using a test card before sending anyone to the page. Confirm the product downloads or the access is granted correctly.

## Section 6: First Sale and First 100 Subscribers

### Why These Two Milestones Are the Real Proof Points

Your first sale is not about the money. It is proof that someone you did not know before found your product, read your page, decided your solution was worth paying for, and handed over their card details.

That is not a small thing. That is a real business transaction between a stranger and you. The psychology of it matters. The first sale changes how you think about what you are building.

Your first 100 subscribers is the proof that you can attract and hold the attention of an audience relevant to your product. 100 is not enough to build a comfortable business on, but it is enough to learn from, get feedback from, and make your first sales from.

Both milestones are achievable within 30 days of having your basic infrastructure in place.

---

**[ ] 6.1 Make a list of your warm network**

Write down every person you know who could be the right buyer, or who knows someone who is. Former colleagues, friends building businesses, people in communities you participate in.

This is your launch list. Not for spam. For genuine personal outreach.

**[ ] 6.2 Send 20 direct messages about your product**

Not a mass email. Individual messages to individual people. Tell them what you are building. Tell them who it is for. Ask if they are the right buyer or know someone who is.

This is the highest-converting first sales activity available to you. More effective than any ad or social post in the first 30 days.

Be direct. Be honest about what you are selling. Do not apologize for selling. You built something useful. You are sharing it with people who might benefit.

**[ ] 6.3 Post your lead magnet offer on at least two platforms**

Share your free opt-in resource on Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Reddit, a relevant Discord or Slack community, or wherever your potential buyers spend time.

The post should communicate: what the lead magnet is, who it is for, and how to get it. Include the link to your opt-in page.

Do this once per week for the first 60 days, on different platforms.

**[ ] 6.4 Guest in a relevant community**

Find a Reddit subreddit, Facebook group, Discord server, or Slack community where your target buyer is active. Participate genuinely. Answer questions. Add value without pitching.

After a week or two of real contribution, share your free resource when the context is right. Do not spam. One well-timed post in the right community can bring 50 subscribers overnight.

**[ ] 6.5 Ask for your first sale directly**

Send an email to your list, however small. Tell them you have a product. Tell them what it does. Tell them the price. Tell them how to buy.

Do not be clever. Be clear. "I built this for people who [problem]. It costs [price]. Here is where to get it." That is a complete sales email.

Your first sale will often come from the most direct ask you make.

**[ ] 6.6 Analyze what worked and what did not**

After your first 100 subscribers and your first sale, sit down and review the numbers.

Where did most subscribers come from? Which posts performed? Which outreach got responses? Which platform drove traffic to your opt-in page?

Double down on what worked. Stop doing what did not. This is the foundation of your customer acquisition strategy going forward.

## Section 7: Content System Checklist

### Why a Content System Is Not Optional

Content is how you get found. It is how trust is built before someone becomes a customer. It is the engine that fills your email list, brings traffic to your product page, and builds your authority in your niche.

A content system means you produce consistently without burning out, and every piece of content serves your business goals, not just your creative impulses.

---

**[ ] 7.1 Choose one primary platform**

You cannot be everywhere at once. Choose one platform as your primary content channel and focus there for the first 90 days.

Options: YouTube (long video), Twitter/X (short text and threads), LinkedIn (professional audience, B2B), Instagram (visual and short video), TikTok (short video, algorithmic reach), a newsletter/blog (owned, SEO, deep content).

Match the platform to where your specific buyer spends time. A B2B freelancer's buyer is on LinkedIn. A personal finance course buyer is on TikTok and YouTube. A copywriting course buyer is on Twitter. Go where the buyers are.

**[ ] 7.2 Define your content pillars**

Content pillars are the 3 to 5 topic areas you consistently cover. They should all relate to the problem your product solves.

Example for a course on freelance web design:
- Pillar 1: Getting clients as a designer
- Pillar 2: Running a freelance business (contracts, pricing, scope)
- Pillar 3: Design tools and workflows
- Pillar 4: Designer career stories and case studies

Every piece of content you make fits into one of these pillars. This keeps your feed coherent and attracts a consistent, relevant audience.

**[ ] 7.3 Build a minimum viable content calendar**

A content calendar does not need to be complex. A simple spreadsheet with: date, platform, topic, status (idea, draft, published).

Batch creation is more efficient than daily creation. Block 2 to 4 hours one day per week to create all the content you will post that week. This is more sustainable than trying to create something new every day.

Start with three posts per week. Build the habit before building the volume.

**[ ] 7.4 Build a content repurposing workflow**

One long-form piece of content should produce multiple shorter pieces.

A 1,000-word blog post becomes: three Twitter threads, two LinkedIn posts, five short-form social captions, one email newsletter.

A 15-minute YouTube video becomes: three short clips for YouTube Shorts or TikTok, a transcript that becomes a blog post, quotes for social media.

You do not need to create new ideas for every piece. You need to distribute one good idea across multiple formats.

**[ ] 7.5 Connect every piece of content to your opt-in**

Every piece of content should have a clear next step: subscribe to your list.

Your bio on every platform should link to your opt-in page. Relevant posts should mention your free resource. Videos should direct viewers to the link in bio.

This is the only content metric that matters in your first 90 days: how many subscribers did this content generate?

**[ ] 7.6 Review and adjust your content strategy monthly**

Once per month, review your top-performing content for that month. Look for patterns: topics that got more engagement, formats that performed better, times of day that got more reach.

Your content strategy should evolve based on what is actually working, not what you originally planned. Stay flexible.

## Section 8: The First 90 Days Roadmap

### Making the Checklist Time-Bound

A checklist without a timeline is a wish list. This section maps the sections above to a 90-day execution window so you know what to do and when.

---

**Days 1 to 14: Foundation**

Complete Sections 1, 2, and 3. Legal structure, domain, hosting, payment processor. These are the non-glamorous pieces that make everything else possible. Do not skip them.

By day 14 you should have:
- [ ] Business name decided and registered if applicable
- [ ] Domain purchased and pointing to a basic landing page
- [ ] Payment processor account created and verified
- [ ] Business bank account open
- [ ] Professional email address active

**Days 15 to 30: Build Your List Infrastructure**

Complete Section 4. Choose your ESP, configure email authentication, create your lead magnet, build your opt-in page, write your welcome sequence.

By day 30 you should have:
- [ ] ESP account set up and authenticated
- [ ] Lead magnet created and uploaded
- [ ] Opt-in page live and working
- [ ] Welcome sequence written and active
- [ ] First 10 to 25 subscribers on your list

**Days 31 to 60: Build and Launch Your First Product**

Complete Section 5 and the first three items in Section 6.

By day 60 you should have:
- [ ] Product topic validated
- [ ] Product content complete
- [ ] Sales page live
- [ ] Product delivery tested and working
- [ ] First 20 direct outreach messages sent
- [ ] First sale made

**Days 61 to 90: Content System and Growth**

Complete Sections 6 and 7 in full.

By day 90 you should have:
- [ ] 100 email subscribers
- [ ] At least 3 to 5 product sales
- [ ] Primary content platform chosen and active
- [ ] Content calendar built and running
- [ ] Repurposing workflow in place
- [ ] First month revenue reviewed and documented

---

**[ ] 8.1 Week 1 review: infrastructure complete**

After week one, audit: do you have a domain, hosting, email, and payment processor in place? If not, what is blocking you?

Name the blocker. Work the blocker. Do not move on until Section 1 and 2 are done.

**[ ] 8.2 Week 2 review: email list live**

After week two, your opt-in page should be live and your welcome sequence active. Have you started promoting the lead magnet? Have you sent it to your warm network?

Your first subscribers will come from people who already know you. That is normal. The goal at this stage is to get the infrastructure tested, not to scale.

**[ ] 8.3 Week 4 review: first product in progress**

By the end of week four, you should have a validated topic and at least a draft outline of your first product. Ideally, you have a pre-sale page and have reached out to your first potential buyers.

If you have not started the product yet, name the reason. Is it a technical blocker or a fear of commitment? The answer changes what you do next.

**[ ] 8.4 Week 6 review: first sale**

By the end of week six, you should have made at least one sale. Even a pre-sale counts. Even a $9 sale counts.

The first sale is not about revenue. It is about proving that your product and your marketing work together to convert a stranger. If it has not happened yet, review: did you actually ask people to buy? Did you send the sales email? Did you reach out personally?

**[ ] 8.5 Month 2 review: content system running**

By the end of month two, you should be posting consistently on your chosen platform and growing your email list from that content.

Measure two things: list growth rate (subscribers per week) and content engagement rate (are people responding, commenting, sharing?). If both are flat, change the content approach.

**[ ] 8.6 Month 3 review: 90-day audit**

At the end of month three, do a full audit.

Revenue: how much did you earn? What is your best-selling product?
Audience: how many subscribers do you have? Where did they come from?
Content: what worked and what did not?
Operations: what systems do you have? What still requires your manual attention?
Next 90 days: what is the single most important thing to work on next?

The 90-day audit is not a test you pass or fail. It is a calibration. Every 90 days, you get better data and you get better at the game.

## Conclusion: The Business You Actually Build

There are thousands of people who know exactly how to build a digital business but never do it.

They have read the books. They have watched the YouTube videos. They have followed the right people on Twitter. They know what to do. They do not do it.

The difference between knowing and doing is not intelligence. It is execution, one checklist item at a time.

This guide exists to remove the "I do not know what to do next" problem. You now have the entire sequence, from legal foundation to your first 90 days of content. Every major decision is addressed. Every step has context.

There is nothing left but execution.

Start with Section 1, Item 1. Decide on your business structure. That is the entire task. Not "plan your whole business." Not "figure out your niche." Decide on your structure. Mark it complete. Move to item 2.

That is the method. One item at a time. One day at a time. Ninety days to a functional digital business.

The hardest part is not any individual item on this list. The hardest part is choosing to start and then choosing to keep going when it gets tedious, which it will.

The builders who succeed are not more talented. They are more consistent.

Start today.
