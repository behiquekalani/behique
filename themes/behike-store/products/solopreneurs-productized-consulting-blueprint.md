# Productized Consulting Blueprint
## Package Your Consulting Into a Scalable Offer

---

## The Custom Consulting Ceiling

You are good at what you do. You know this because clients keep coming back, because referrals arrive without effort, and because the results you deliver are real.

But your revenue is capped.

Not capped because you are not talented enough or not working hard enough. Capped because custom consulting is a fundamentally limited model. Every engagement is unique. Every scope is negotiated from scratch. Every delivery is different. You cannot systematize what you cannot predict. And because you cannot systematize it, you cannot scale it.

The ceiling is not your ceiling. It is the model's ceiling.

Here is how the math works. If you bill $200 per hour and work 40 billable hours per week, you make $8,000 per week. This assumes 100% billable utilization, which no consultant achieves. Most consultants bill 20 to 25 hours per week when accounting for sales, admin, and non-billable project work. So the realistic ceiling is $4,000 to $5,000 per week, or roughly $16,000 to $20,000 per month.

To break through that ceiling in custom consulting, you raise your rates. Raising rates works up to a point, then the market pushes back. You cannot 10x your rates without fundamentally changing your positioning. And even if you succeed, you are still trading time for money in a one-for-one exchange. There is no leverage.

Productized consulting introduces leverage. It packages your expertise into a defined offering with a defined process and a defined price. You stop charging for time and start charging for outcomes. You stop negotiating scope and start defining scope. You build delivery systems that require less direct involvement over time.

This guide walks through how to build that.

## What Productized Consulting Actually Is

Productized consulting is not a course. It is not a template or a tool. It is a consulting service that has been standardized enough to be sold like a product.

The key elements:

**Defined scope.** The client knows exactly what they are buying before they pay. Not "marketing strategy help" but "a 30-day brand audit with three deliverables: competitor analysis, positioning matrix, and 90-day content roadmap." The scope is fixed. The price is fixed. What the client receives is predictable.

**Defined process.** You have a repeatable delivery methodology. Onboarding happens the same way every time. Week one looks the same for every client. The questions you ask, the frameworks you apply, and the deliverables you produce follow a documented sequence.

**Defined price.** A single price on a sales page, not a custom proposal after a discovery call. The price signals what kind of offer this is. It reduces sales friction because the prospect can make a purchase decision without a meeting.

**Defined timeline.** The engagement has a start date and an end date. Not open-ended retainers that drift into undefined ongoing work. "Four weeks" or "90 days" or "a single intensive day." The client knows when it begins and when it ends.

When you have all four, you have a productized offer. When any of these elements is undefined, you have a custom offer with some productization features.

## The 5 Productization Frameworks

There are five main structures for productizing consulting work. Choose the one that fits how your expertise is best delivered.

**Framework 1: The Sprint.**
A focused, intensive engagement delivered in a short time frame. Typically two days to two weeks. The sprint is designed to solve one specific, well-defined problem.

Example: "Website Copy Sprint. Three days. I write all primary web copy for your five core pages. You get copy for home, about, services, contact, and one landing page. Price: $3,500. Delivery: 72 hours after kick-off call."

Best for: Work that produces discrete deliverables, where the client needs fast results, and where the core work can be done quickly by a skilled practitioner. Copywriting, design, technical audits, strategy documents.

**Framework 2: The Roadmap.**
A single, comprehensive strategy session that produces a plan the client then executes. The consultant delivers clarity and direction, not implementation.

Example: "90-Day Growth Roadmap. One intensive half-day session plus a written action plan delivered within 48 hours. We cover your current state, your goal, the three primary obstacles, and the exact sequence of actions for the next 90 days. Price: $2,500."

Best for: Strategic advisors, business coaches, and consultants whose most valuable output is structured thinking about a client's specific situation.

**Framework 3: The System Build.**
A fixed-scope engagement where the consultant builds something that the client then owns and operates. Not ongoing support. A complete system, delivered and handed over.

Example: "Content Pipeline Setup. Four weeks. I build your complete content creation and distribution system: editorial calendar, template library, publishing workflow, and analytics dashboard. Includes two training calls for your team. Price: $6,000."

Best for: Operations consultants, technical implementers, and anyone whose work results in a tangible system, process, or infrastructure.

**Framework 4: The Recurring Package.**
A monthly retainer with a defined scope and defined deliverables. Different from open-ended retainers because the client knows exactly what they receive each month.

Example: "Monthly SEO Package. Each month: keyword research report for five target terms, on-page optimization for two pages, one backlink outreach campaign, and a monthly performance review call. Price: $1,800/month."

Best for: Work that is genuinely ongoing (not project-based) and where monthly deliverables can be standardized. SEO, content production, analytics reporting, social media management.

**Framework 5: The Cohort.**
A group delivery model where multiple clients go through the same program simultaneously. The consultant's time is spread across multiple paying clients, dramatically increasing revenue per hour of delivery.

Example: "B2B Positioning Workshop. Six clients max. Eight weeks. Weekly 90-minute group session plus one 30-minute individual call per client. Price: $2,400 per client. Cohort revenue: $14,400 for 8 weeks."

Best for: Consultants with process-based expertise that translates to a curriculum, and who are comfortable with group delivery dynamics.

## Pricing a Productized Consulting Offer

The most common pricing mistake with productized consulting is anchoring the price to time.

Custom consulting is priced per hour because the scope is undefined and time is the variable the consultant controls. Productized consulting has a defined scope, which means the price should be anchored to the value of the outcome, not the hours required to deliver it.

This distinction produces dramatically different prices.

A marketing strategist who charges $150 per hour and takes 20 hours to deliver a positioning audit would price it at $3,000 based on time. But if the positioning audit helps a $1 million revenue company increase conversion rates by 1%, that is $10,000 in additional annual revenue. The value of the outcome is $10,000 per year. The time-based price of $3,000 captures 30% of the first year's value. Most consultants undercharge dramatically because they price time instead of outcomes.

Value-based pricing for productized consulting:

1. Define the specific outcome the engagement produces.
2. Estimate the economic value of that outcome for your target client.
3. Price the engagement at 10 to 20% of the first year's expected value.
4. Ensure the price is high enough to signal premium quality but low enough to be a clear yes-or-no decision for your target market.

For most productized consulting offerings, the sweet spot price range is $1,500 to $8,000 for sprint-type engagements and $800 to $2,500 per month for recurring packages. Below $1,500 for a sprint, you are likely undervaluing the outcome and attracting clients who will be high-maintenance because they are cost-sensitive. Above $8,000 for a sprint, the buying decision often requires internal approval processes that slow sales.

Price anchoring: place your most expensive package first on any pricing page. This makes your other options appear accessible by comparison. If you have three tiers, the middle tier gets the most purchases. This is called the decoy effect and it works reliably.

## Building the Delivery Process That Makes It Repeatable

The delivery process is what separates a productized offer from a custom offer with a fixed price.

If you have to reinvent the delivery process for each client, you have not productized. You have just stopped negotiating scope.

True productization means your delivery process is documented, systematized, and could theoretically be partially delegated. Even if you have no intention of delegating today, the discipline of building a delegatable process ensures you have actually systematized the work rather than just pricing it differently.

The delivery process documentation for any productized offer should include:

**Onboarding checklist.** Every client goes through the same onboarding. Welcome email sent within one hour of purchase. Intake questionnaire sent within 24 hours. Kick-off call scheduled within 48 hours. Access credentials, assets, and materials collected by kick-off call. The same sequence every time.

**Project phases with time allocations.** Break the engagement into phases. Phase one: discovery and audit. Phase two: strategy development. Phase three: deliverable creation. Phase four: review and handoff. Each phase has a defined time allocation. If a phase regularly takes more time than allocated, the scope is wrong and needs adjustment.

**Templates for every deliverable.** Every report, document, and presentation should start from a template. Templates are not for producing generic work. They are for ensuring nothing important gets left out and for reducing the setup time for each new deliverable. A template that takes two hours to customize is still faster than building from scratch every time.

**Client communication scripts.** Onboarding call agenda. End-of-phase update email. Deliverable presentation structure. Closing call script. These scripts ensure every client receives the same quality of experience regardless of where you are in your week.

**Quality checklist per deliverable.** Before delivering anything, run it against a checklist. Not a generic "does this look good" review. A specific list of elements that must be present in every deliverable of this type. Did the positioning document include the competitive differentiation section? Did the roadmap include the priority matrix? Checklists prevent the errors that happen when you are busy and moving fast.

## Scoping to Prevent Custom Requests

The most common way productized consulting breaks down is through scope creep that clients initiate innocently and consultants allow nervously.

You deliver the positioning audit. The client asks if you can also look at their homepage copy while you are at it. You say yes because the request seems reasonable and you want to be helpful. You spend two additional hours on copy feedback. The next client makes a similar request. Now you are spending two hours per client on work outside your scope, which eats the margin that makes the productized offer financially viable.

The solution is explicit scope documentation in your sales process, not just in your contract.

Before a client purchases, they should know exactly what is and is not included. The sales page, the proposal, and the contract should all say the same thing.

What to define explicitly:

**Deliverables list.** Name every document, report, session, and asset included. Not categories. Specific items. "One 20-page positioning report" is specific. "Strategic documents" is not.

**Revision policy.** Most productized offers should include one round of revisions. Unlimited revisions is not productized consulting. It is custom work with a fixed price and an open-ended time commitment.

**Communication channels and response times.** Email response within 24 business hours. Slack access during business hours. No WhatsApp. No weekend messages. The communication parameters protect your time and train clients to work within the structure.

**Out-of-scope process.** When a client requests something outside the defined scope, you acknowledge the request, confirm it is outside the current engagement, and offer them a path to get it done. "That is outside what we are covering in this engagement. I can add it as a scope extension for $X, or we can address it in a follow-on engagement. Which makes more sense for you?"

This language is not unfriendly. It is professional. Clients who have bought productized services before understand scope discipline. Clients who are accustomed to custom work may need one or two scope conversations before they adapt. Stick to the process.

## Selling Productized Consulting to Custom-Service Buyers

Most of your early clients will come from your existing network, and most of your existing network is accustomed to custom consulting relationships.

They want to tell you their situation and get a proposal. They expect negotiation. They expect to have input on scope. They are comfortable with the custom process.

Selling productized consulting to this audience requires repositioning the conversation.

When someone reaches out for a custom engagement, you have two options. You can tell them you only do productized work and explain why, or you can listen to their situation and map it to your existing productized offer if it fits.

The second approach is more effective. Listen. Identify which of your productized offers addresses their core problem. Then present that offer as the solution.

"Based on what you have described, the positioning work you need is exactly what my Positioning Sprint was built for. It is a four-week engagement that delivers three specific outputs: your positioning statement, your competitive differentiation matrix, and your messaging hierarchy. The price is $4,500. Let me send you the scope document so you can see if it fits what you need."

You are not asking them to accept a new relationship model. You are presenting a specific solution to their specific problem. The fact that it is productized is context, not the lead.

Common objection: "We have some unique circumstances that might need a custom approach."

Response: "All of my clients have unique circumstances. The sprint process is flexible enough to account for them. Here is where customization happens within the scope, and here is what stays fixed. Walk me through the unique elements you are concerned about."

This response acknowledges the concern without abandoning your productized structure. Ninety percent of the time, their "unique circumstances" are variations that your process already accommodates.

## Scaling Beyond One Engagement at a Time

The first scaling lever in productized consulting is capacity.

When your process is documented and systematized, you can begin to delegate repeatable components. The research phase of an audit. The data collection for a competitive analysis. The initial draft of a report based on a template. These are components that a skilled assistant or junior contractor can execute under your direction.

Delegation in consulting is often resisted because of a belief that clients are paying for you specifically. This is partially true. Clients are paying for your judgment, your expertise, and your quality standards. They are not paying for you to personally collect data or format spreadsheets.

The delegation test: would the client care if they knew this task was done by someone on your team rather than you? If the answer is no, the task is delegatable. If the answer is yes, it is not.

Most consulting engagements are 40 to 60% tasks the client would not care about being delegated.

The second scaling lever is parallel engagements.

With a documented, systematized process, you can run two engagements simultaneously where you previously ran one. This doubles revenue per month at the cost of some additional management overhead. The key is that both engagements follow the same process, so context switching between them is low. You are not managing two completely different projects. You are running two instances of the same system.

The third scaling lever is the cohort model.

Once you have run your productized offer enough times to know it inside out, you can begin offering group versions. Instead of one client at $4,500 for a sprint, six clients at $2,000 each for a cohort version of the same content. Revenue goes from $4,500 to $12,000. Your time investment increases by perhaps 30% for coordinating the group. Revenue per hour roughly doubles.

The cohort model works best for engagements that have a strong educational component, where peer interaction adds value, and where clients are not sharing sensitive competitive information. B2B positioning, content strategy, operational systems, and financial planning all work well in cohort formats.

## The Transition Plan

If you are currently running a custom consulting practice and want to shift to a productized model, do not flip everything overnight.

Transition in three phases.

Phase one: productize one service while keeping custom work for existing clients. Identify your most frequently requested engagement. The one you deliver most often, with the most predictable process. Document it, scope it, price it, and put it on a simple sales page. Keep taking custom work from existing clients while the productized offer develops traction.

Phase two: set a ratio target. For every two custom engagements you take on, close one productized engagement. This ratio shifts your business toward the new model without cutting off revenue from the existing model. Run this ratio for three to six months.

Phase three: stop accepting fully custom engagements. When productized revenue has replaced custom revenue, you have the option to decline custom requests entirely. Some consultants choose to keep a small number of premium custom engagements at very high prices alongside their productized offers. Both approaches are viable.

The transition takes six to twelve months for most consultants. Patience during the transition is required because early productized offers rarely achieve immediate traction. The first version of any productized offer needs refinement based on early client experience. Plan for two to three iterations of the offer before it is working reliably.

Document everything you learn from each client. The questions they ask before purchasing tell you what is unclear in your positioning. The problems they encounter during delivery tell you where your process has gaps. The outcomes they achieve tell you what to emphasize in your sales materials.

## Build the System, Then Build the Business

Custom consulting is a job you own. Productized consulting is a business.

The difference is whether the business can produce revenue without you being fully present in every engagement. That is the definition of a business: a system that produces value independent of constant owner input.

You will still be deeply involved in your productized consulting work, especially early on. But you will be involved in ways that improve the system, not just in ways that execute individual engagements. This is the shift that allows the business to grow beyond your personal capacity.

Define the scope. Build the process. Price for value. Protect the scope. Delegate the components you should not be doing yourself.

The consulting ceiling is not yours to accept.
