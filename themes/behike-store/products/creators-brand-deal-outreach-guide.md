# The Creator's Brand Deal Outreach Guide

**How to pitch and land brand deals without an agent**

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike

---

## The Thesis

Brand deals are not awarded to the most popular creators. They are awarded to creators who understand what a brand needs, communicate that understanding clearly, and make the ask professionally. Most creators either wait to be discovered, which is passive and slow, or they send generic pitches that brands delete without reading. There is a third path: systematic outreach built on research, relevance, and a pitch that solves the brand's actual problem.

This guide teaches you how to identify the right brands to approach, build the pitch that gets opened, structure a deal that works for both parties, and negotiate without underselling yourself. No agent required. No massive following required. What you need is a clear value proposition, a documented audience, and the willingness to do the work that most creators will not.

## Part 1: The Reality of Brand Deals

Before building an outreach system, you need an accurate picture of how brand deals actually work.

### Who Gets Brand Deals

The common assumption is that brand deals go to creators with the largest audiences. This is partially true for major campaigns with seven-figure budgets. But the majority of brand budget does not flow to mega-influencers. It flows to mid-tier and micro creators, because:

- Micro-creators (1K-50K followers) typically have 3-5x higher engagement rates than macro-creators
- Niche audiences convert at higher rates for relevant products because the audience trust is higher
- Brands with smaller marketing budgets cannot afford macro-influencers but still need content
- Multiple micro-creator partnerships spread risk and create more authentic touchpoints

A creator with 8,000 highly engaged followers in personal finance is worth more to a budgeting app than a lifestyle creator with 200,000 mostly passive followers. The brand deal market rewards specificity and engagement, not just scale.

### What Brands Actually Buy

Brands do not buy your follower count. They buy:
- Access to a specific audience they want to reach
- Content they can use in their own marketing channels
- Association with the trust and authority you have built with that audience
- The efficiency of reaching an engaged niche cheaper than traditional advertising

Understanding what they are buying helps you frame your value correctly. Do not pitch yourself as an exposure vehicle. Pitch yourself as an audience access point where the audience trusts your judgment.

### The Spectrum of Brand Deals

**Gifted/Seeding:** Brand sends product in exchange for a post, no payment. This is standard for small creators breaking in and is acceptable for products you would genuinely use. Never accept gifted deals for products that do not align with your content. The association costs you more in audience trust than the product value.

**Flat-fee sponsored content:** You are paid a fixed amount to create one or more pieces of content featuring the brand. This is the most common deal structure for mid-tier creators.

**Affiliate partnerships:** You receive a commission on sales generated through your unique link or code. No upfront payment. High-performing affiliates in the right niche can earn more from commissions than flat fees, but it requires an audience that actually buys.

**Long-term ambassadorships:** Ongoing partnership over 3-12 months with recurring payments. The most valuable structure because it provides predictable income and deepens the brand association in your audience's mind.

**Usage rights:** Some brands pay for the right to use your content in their own advertising. This is a separate fee from your creation fee and is often more valuable than creators realize, especially when brands run paid ads with creator content.

## Part 2: Building Your Media Kit

Your media kit is the document that represents you to a brand before they decide whether to respond to your outreach. It needs to answer the questions a brand manager will have before they can evaluate you.

### What a Media Kit Must Include

**Creator overview:** Two to three sentences that describe who you are, what you create, who you create for, and why your audience follows you. This is your thesis translated into business language. Not "I make fitness content." Instead: "I create practical strength training content for people in their 30s and 40s who are returning to fitness after a gap and want programming that fits around a full schedule."

**Audience demographics:** Platform, follower count, age breakdown, gender breakdown, top geographic locations. If your analytics show a strong concentration in a specific demographic that is valuable to a specific type of brand, surface that explicitly.

**Engagement metrics:** Average views per post, average engagement rate (likes plus comments divided by followers), average story views or click-through rates. Calculate these numbers honestly. Brands have tools to audit this independently and padding your metrics will end the relationship before it starts.

**Content examples:** Three to five examples of your best work, ideally including at least one previous brand integration. If you have no previous brand integrations, include your highest-performing organic content with the caption that the brand's product would fit naturally in this type of content.

**Rate card:** Your pricing. Not a range. Specific rates for specific deliverables. One short-form video: $X. One Instagram Reel: $X. One dedicated YouTube video: $X. One Instagram Story set: $X. Including a rate card reduces back-and-forth and signals that you operate professionally.

**Contact information:** Your name, email, and any social handles. Simple.

### Media Kit Design

A media kit does not need to be elaborate. A clean, readable PDF is standard. If your content has a strong visual identity, let that inform the design. If not, black and white with your logo and a clear hierarchy of information works.

One page for small creators. Two to three pages maximum for established creators. A media kit that takes more than two minutes to read will not be read.

You can build a media kit in Canva, Google Slides, or Notion. Export as PDF. Send as an attachment or hosted link.

### What to Build Before You Have a Rate Card

If you are starting from zero and have no context for rates, research before you guess. Use these methods:

- Ask other creators in your niche directly. Many creators will share their rates if asked sincerely and privately.
- Join creator communities on Discord, Twitter, or industry-specific Slack groups where rates are discussed.
- Use published rate calculators as a baseline (they are imprecise but give you a range to anchor from).

A rough baseline: for short-form content, $100 per 10,000 followers is a commonly cited starting benchmark. Your actual rate should be higher if your engagement rate is strong or your niche is high-value (finance, health, technology). It can be lower if you are building a relationship with a brand you genuinely want to partner with long-term.

## Part 3: Finding Brands to Pitch

Waiting for inbound inquiries is not a strategy. Outbound outreach is how you build a pipeline before you have an agent or a management team.

### Tier-Based Targeting

Build a three-tier target list.

**Tier 1: Dream brands.** Brands you use personally and genuinely love. Your pitch here is authentic and your content quality will be highest because you are actually a customer. These are harder to land but the content performs best because your audience can tell you mean it.

**Tier 2: Aligned brands.** Brands that serve your audience's needs even if you do not personally use them. You have seen their products, you understand their value proposition, and they fit naturally in your content world. These are your primary outreach targets.

**Tier 3: Research brands.** Brands that are adjacent to your niche. They are not an obvious fit but there is a case to be made. Approach these only after you have found all Tier 1 and Tier 2 targets.

For each target, research:
- Are they actively running influencer campaigns? (Check their Instagram: do they repost creator content?)
- Do they have a creator program or affiliate program listed on their website?
- Have they worked with creators at your follower count range?
- What brands have similar creators in your niche worked with? (Look at their sponsored content)

### Where to Find Brand Contacts

**LinkedIn:** Search for the brand's company page, then look for titles like: Influencer Marketing Manager, Partnership Manager, Brand Partnerships, Content Marketing Manager, Social Media Manager. These are the people who manage creator relationships. Connect with a brief note, not a pitch.

**Instagram:** Many brands have a partnership or business email in their bio. If not, a DM to their main account is appropriate for smaller brands where the social media manager handles partnerships.

**Creator marketplaces:** Aspire, Creator.co, Grin, and Similar platforms connect creators with brands actively looking for partnerships. These are lower-leverage than direct outreach but require less research per lead. Use both.

**Brand websites:** Many brands have a "Work with us" or "Creator program" page. Starting here shows you did your research and ensures you are using the correct submission process.

## Part 4: The Pitch

Most creator pitches fail for one of three reasons: too long, too generic, or too creator-centric. A strong pitch is concise, specific to the brand, and framed around what the brand gets.

### The Pitch Structure

**Subject line:** "Partnership proposal — [Your Name] x [Brand Name]" is cleaner than "Collaboration opportunity!!!" The former looks professional. The latter looks desperate.

**Opening sentence:** State who you are and why you are reaching out to this specific brand. Not "I've been a fan of [brand] for years" (generic and unverifiable). Instead: "I create strength training content for 30-40 year olds returning to fitness, and I noticed [Brand's protein product] recently launched a line specifically for active adults in that age range."

This opening demonstrates that you researched them specifically and that there is a genuine audience overlap. It earns you the next sentence.

**Value statement:** Describe your audience with specificity. How many followers, what platform, what engagement rate. Most importantly, describe who they are and why they are relevant to this brand. "My 22,000 followers are primarily women ages 28-42 in the US who are actively making purchasing decisions around fitness equipment and nutrition. My last three sponsored posts averaged a 7.2% engagement rate."

**The ask:** Be specific about what you are proposing. Do not say "I'd love to work together." Say "I'm proposing a single Instagram Reel and two Stories featuring [Product] integrated into my weekly training log content. My rate for this package is $850."

**Social proof:** One line. A previous brand you have worked with, a strong recent performance metric, or a specific result a previous partner saw. If you have no brand experience, use organic content performance: "My most recent training video reached 47,000 people with no paid promotion."

**Call to action:** "If you'd like to discuss further or receive my full media kit, I'm happy to send it over. You can reach me at [email]."

Total length: 150-200 words. If you cannot fit it in 200 words, your pitch is too complicated.

### The Follow-Up

Send the initial pitch. If no response in 7 days, send one follow-up. Keep the follow-up shorter than the original. "I wanted to make sure my note reached you. Happy to answer any questions or send over additional examples of my work." One follow-up only. Two unanswered messages is the limit. More than that becomes harassment.

Keep a tracking spreadsheet of every pitch you send: date, brand, contact, outcome. You want to see your response rate across all outreach. If you are sending 20 pitches a month and getting no responses, something in your pitch structure needs to change. If you are getting responses but no conversions, the issue is in your negotiation or your media kit.

## Part 5: Negotiation

Most creators either accept the first number offered or avoid negotiation entirely. Both are mistakes. Negotiation is a normal and expected part of business, and most brands expect you to counter.

### Knowing Your Rates

Your rates should be based on value delivered, not on what you think you can get away with. The value you deliver is a function of:
- Audience size and engagement rate
- Audience specificity and relevance to the brand
- Content quality (production value, writing, creative approach)
- The deliverables included (usage rights, exclusivity, number of pieces)

Build your rates from the bottom up. What does your time and equipment cost to create one sponsored video? That is your floor. What is the expected return on investment for the brand based on your audience? That sets your ceiling.

### Common Negotiation Scenarios

**Brand offers less than your rate:** Counter at your original rate with a brief explanation of the value. "My rate for this package is $X because my audience's purchase intent for this category is demonstrated by [specific metric or data point]. I'd like to work with [Brand] and believe the return will justify the investment."

**Brand asks for usage rights beyond the campaign period:** Usage rights are a separate fee from content creation. A standard approach is to charge 30-50% of the original creation fee for each additional 6-month period of usage rights. Unlimited usage rights in perpetuity should cost more than double the original creation fee.

**Brand asks for exclusivity:** Exclusivity (you cannot work with competing brands for a period) restricts your ability to take other deals and should be compensated accordingly. A 90-day category exclusivity clause typically adds 25-50% to the base rate.

**Brand wants to pay in product instead of cash:** Evaluate the cash value of the product objectively. A $200 product is not the same as $200 in cash because you cannot use product to pay rent. Accept product-only deals only when the product has direct use in your content creation process or the brand relationship has significant strategic value beyond the individual deal.

**Brand has no budget:** Sometimes this is true, especially with small brands or startups. If you want to work with them, propose a performance-based deal: lower base fee plus a commission on sales generated through your link. This aligns incentives and often produces better content because you are personally motivated by conversions.

## Part 6: Contracts and Deliverables

Every paid brand deal should have a contract. Not because you do not trust the brand, but because a contract protects both parties and ensures expectations are aligned. Disputes almost never happen when everything is in writing. They happen when either party remembers the agreement differently.

### Minimum Contract Elements

- Deliverables: exact number and type of content pieces
- Timeline: creation date, review period, posting date
- Compensation: total amount, payment schedule, method
- Usage rights: where and for how long the brand can use your content
- Exclusivity: any restrictions on working with competing brands, and the time period
- Approval process: how many revision rounds, what turnaround time
- FTC disclosure: confirmation that sponsored content will be properly disclosed
- Kill fee: if the brand cancels after you have created content, what you are owed

Kill fees are often overlooked by new creators. A standard kill fee is 50% of the total deal value if the brand cancels after content creation has begun. Include it in your contract template and negotiate it if they push back.

### Sending Your Invoice

Invoice upon completion of each deliverable or upon posting, depending on what you negotiated. Invoice with a 30-day payment term as a standard. Include the deal reference or contract number on the invoice. Keep a record of every invoice sent and every payment received.

If payment is 30+ days overdue, send a polite reminder. If 60+ days overdue, escalate to the contact's manager and reference the contract. Late payment is common in the brand deal industry and persistence on invoicing is normal and expected.

## Part 7: Building Long-Term Brand Relationships

A single deal is income. A long-term partnership is a reliable revenue stream. After a successful first deal, your goal is to turn it into a recurring relationship.

After posting:
- Send the brand the performance metrics from the sponsored content one week after posting (views, engagement, click-through rate if trackable, any sales data from your affiliate link)
- Note anything that worked particularly well or that you would do differently
- If the brand did not have a tracking link, follow up asking if they saw any uplift and offer to provide one for the next campaign

This follow-up is something 90% of creators do not do. Brands notice. They remember the creators who made their jobs easier and who took the partnership seriously beyond the initial posting. This is how gifted deals become paid deals, how single campaigns become quarterly partnerships, and how $500 deals become $2,000 deals over twelve months.

## The Outreach Plan

Week 1:
1. Build your media kit
2. Create your outreach tracking spreadsheet
3. Build a list of 20 Tier 2 brands with contact information

Week 2:
4. Send the first 10 pitches
5. Follow up on any pitches from week 1 that received no response

Month 1:
6. Send 20 pitches total
7. Evaluate response rate and adjust pitch based on what is working
8. Close your first deal, even if it is gifted, to begin building portfolio

Month 3:
9. Aim to have 2-3 paid deals completed
10. Use the performance data from those deals to update your media kit

The creators who land brand deals consistently are not the ones who went viral once. They are the ones who built outreach as a practice and treated it like the business it is.
