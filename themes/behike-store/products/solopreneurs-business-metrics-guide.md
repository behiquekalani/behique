# Solopreneur's Business Metrics Guide

**The only numbers a solo operator actually needs, how to read them, and how to build a monthly dashboard in under two hours.**

By Kalani Andre Gomez Padin | Behike Digital

---

## Introduction: Most Metrics Are Noise

The internet has convinced solopreneurs that tracking everything is the path to clarity. Dashboard apps, analytics suites, funnel visualizations, cohort reports. The implicit message is that more data equals better decisions.

This is false.

More data equals more time spent interpreting data instead of building, creating, and selling. For a one-person business, this is a particular kind of trap because the person interpreting the data is the same person generating the revenue. Every hour spent in your analytics is an hour not spent in your business.

This guide is a corrective. It identifies the specific metrics that actually predict the financial health of a solo operation, explains what each one tells you and what it does not, and shows you how to build a simple monthly dashboard that takes twenty minutes to update and gives you a complete picture of where your business stands.

If you are a solo operator running a services business, a digital product business, a content business, or some combination of these, this guide is written for you.

What this guide does not cover: enterprise metrics, SaaS-specific metrics like daily active users or retention cohorts, paid advertising metrics beyond what is necessary for solo decision-making, or metrics that require a team to generate or act on.

## Part One: Revenue Metrics

### Chapter 1: The Only Revenue Numbers That Matter

Revenue is the most important number in your business. This sounds obvious until you realize how many solopreneurs track revenue in ways that actively mislead them.

**Gross Revenue**

Gross revenue is the total amount invoiced or charged before any deductions. This is the number most people mean when they say "my business made X this month." It is a useful top-line number, but it is not a measure of business health by itself.

Gross revenue inflates your sense of business performance in two ways. First, it includes refunds that have not yet been processed. A $10,000 month that produces $2,000 in refunds in the following month is actually an $8,000 month. Second, it can mask declining unit economics. If you are grossing more this month than last month but your average transaction value has dropped and you are doing significantly more transactions to achieve it, your business is working harder for less.

Track gross revenue monthly. Know the trend direction. But do not make strategic decisions based on gross revenue alone.

**Net Revenue**

Net revenue is gross revenue minus refunds, chargebacks, and platform fees. This is your actual top-line number. On a platform like Gumroad, Stripe, or Teachable, your net revenue is what hits your account after the platform takes its cut and any refunds are processed.

For most solo digital product businesses, the difference between gross and net is 3 to 15 percent depending on platform fees and refund rates. Know this percentage for your business. If it changes significantly month to month, something in your payment or refund pattern has changed.

**Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)**

If any portion of your revenue is subscription-based, MRR is your most important single metric. MRR is the predictable monthly revenue generated by subscriptions, calculated as total active subscribers multiplied by average subscription price.

MRR gives you a revenue floor. If your MRR is $3,000, you know that regardless of what you sell this month, at least $3,000 is coming in. This floor changes how you make decisions, what risks you can take, and how much stress you carry.

For a purely non-subscription business, MRR does not apply. But if you have even one recurring revenue stream, track its MRR separately from your one-time revenue.

**New Revenue vs. Renewal Revenue**

Most solopreneurs track total revenue without distinguishing between revenue from new customers and revenue from existing customers. This distinction is critical.

A business that generates $5,000 per month entirely from new customers has a very different risk profile than one that generates $5,000 per month with $3,000 from renewals and repeat purchases and $2,000 from new customers. The second business is more stable and has proven customer loyalty. The first is entirely dependent on acquisition.

Track these two numbers separately: new customer revenue and returning customer revenue. If your returning customer revenue is growing as a percentage of total revenue, your business is building a foundation. If it is shrinking, you are on a treadmill where you have to acquire new customers constantly just to maintain revenue.

### Chapter 2: Profit Metrics

Revenue tells you how much money is coming into your business. Profit tells you how much money your business is actually producing. The gap between the two is where many solo operators lose sight of what they are actually building.

**Gross Profit and Gross Profit Margin**

Gross profit is revenue minus cost of goods sold (COGS). For a digital product business, COGS includes platform fees, payment processing fees, hosting costs, and any direct costs associated with delivering the product (for example, a contractor who helps fulfill a service).

Gross profit margin is gross profit divided by revenue, expressed as a percentage. A digital product business with no physical components and no contractors should have gross profit margins of 70 to 90 percent. If yours is significantly lower, your delivery costs are too high.

For a services business, COGS includes any subcontractors, software you use specifically to deliver client work, and direct time costs if you are tracking your own hourly rate.

**Net Profit and Net Profit Margin**

Net profit is gross profit minus all operating expenses. Operating expenses include everything that is not a direct cost of delivering your product or service: software subscriptions, marketing spend, courses and education, equipment, insurance, taxes.

Net profit margin is net profit divided by revenue. For a lean solo operation, healthy net profit margins range from 40 to 70 percent. If your net margin is below 30 percent, your operating expenses are eating your business.

**Owner's Discretionary Earnings (ODE)**

This is the metric that most matters for a solopreneur and the one that is least commonly tracked. ODE is net profit plus your owner's compensation (salary you pay yourself) plus any personal expenses run through the business.

ODE represents the total economic benefit the business produces for you as the owner. It answers the question: what am I actually making from this business, including both what I pay myself and what the business retains?

If you are paying yourself $3,000 per month from a business that shows $1,000 in net profit, your ODE is $4,000 per month. This is the real number to compare against your alternatives.

### Chapter 3: The Revenue Tracking System

You do not need accounting software to track the revenue metrics above. You need a spreadsheet with these columns:

| Month | Gross Revenue | Refunds | Net Revenue | MRR | New Customer Revenue | Returning Customer Revenue | Operating Expenses | Net Profit |

Update this once per month. It takes fifteen minutes. At the end of twelve months, you have a complete financial picture of your business.

The one tool worth adding: an expense tracking system. Whether you use a dedicated app or a spreadsheet category, every business expense should be recorded at the time it is incurred, not reconstructed at the end of the month. Reconstructed expense tracking is always incomplete. Real-time tracking is always accurate.

## Part Two: Conversion Metrics

### Chapter 4: Understanding Your Funnel

Conversion metrics tell you how effectively your business turns strangers into buyers. For a solo operator, the funnel has four stages: awareness, consideration, decision, and purchase.

At each stage, some percentage of people move forward and some drop off. Understanding where the drop-off is happening tells you where to focus your effort.

The trap most solopreneurs fall into is optimizing conversion rates instead of fixing whatever is causing people to stop. These are different problems with different solutions. Optimizing conversion rates is about psychology and presentation. Fixing drop-off is about offer clarity, trust, and value communication.

**Visitor to Lead Rate**

For a content-driven business, this is the percentage of website visitors who take an action that moves them into your funnel, typically by subscribing to an email list, downloading a free resource, or registering for a webinar.

Benchmark: A visitor-to-lead rate of 1 to 3 percent for cold traffic is typical. Above 5 percent indicates strong alignment between your traffic source and your lead magnet. Below 0.5 percent indicates a mismatch.

What to do when this number is low: The problem is usually either traffic quality (you are attracting the wrong people) or lead magnet clarity (your free offer does not connect to what the visitor actually wants). Fix traffic first. Then fix the lead magnet.

**Lead to Sale Rate**

The percentage of leads who eventually purchase. For email subscribers, a healthy lead-to-sale rate for digital products is 1 to 5 percent. For high-ticket services, lower volumes make this harder to calculate, but a rate of 10 to 30 percent for qualified leads is typical.

What to do when this number is low: If your lead-to-sale rate is low, the gap is usually in the nurture sequence, the positioning of your offer, or the offer price relative to perceived value. A low lead-to-sale rate with high traffic is a funnel problem. The solution is in the middle of the funnel, not the top or bottom.

**Sales Page Conversion Rate**

For digital products, the conversion rate of your sales page, visitors to purchases, is one of the most actionable metrics you have. You can test changes and see results within days.

Benchmark for a direct traffic (email list) sales page: 5 to 15 percent.

Benchmark for cold traffic sales page: 0.5 to 3 percent.

What to do when this number is low: Most sales page conversion problems come from one of three sources. First, unclear problem statement: the page does not immediately show the visitor that you understand their specific problem. Second, weak social proof: there are no credible testimonials or results to anchor trust. Third, price objection not addressed: the reader reaches the price and is surprised or unprepared for it.

Test one change at a time. The biggest impact usually comes from the headline, the first paragraph, and the testimonials. Start there.

### Chapter 5: Email Metrics That Predict Sales

Email marketing is the highest-ROI channel for most solo digital product businesses. Unlike social media, you own your email list. Unlike paid ads, the cost does not scale with use. Unlike SEO, results are immediate and predictable.

But email metrics are commonly misread in ways that lead to bad decisions.

**Open Rate**

Open rate measures the percentage of recipients who opened your email. It is a useful engagement signal but an imperfect one. Apple Mail Privacy Protection, widely used since 2021, marks emails as opened whether or not the recipient actually opened them, which inflates open rates for any list with significant iOS readership.

Treat open rates as directional data, not absolute data. A consistent decline in open rates over time signals list fatigue or deliverability problems. A consistent rate with occasional spikes (from particularly compelling subject lines) signals a healthy list.

Do not chase high open rates by writing clickbait subject lines. A lower open rate with high conversion is a better outcome than a high open rate with no purchases.

**Click-Through Rate (CTR)**

CTR is the percentage of recipients who clicked a link in your email. This is a more reliable engagement metric than open rate because it requires actual human action.

Benchmark for a list of engaged subscribers: 1 to 5 percent CTR.

A CTR below 0.5 percent consistently indicates that your emails are not generating enough interest to motivate action. Either the content is not compelling enough, the call-to-action is unclear, or the list has accumulated too many disengaged subscribers.

**Revenue Per Subscriber (RPS)**

This is the most important email metric for a solopreneurs business and the one almost nobody tracks. RPS is total email-attributed revenue divided by list size.

Example: If your list has 2,000 subscribers and generates $4,000 in revenue per month through emails, your RPS is $2.00.

The industry benchmark for a healthy, monetized list is $1 to $3 per subscriber per month. Above $3 indicates excellent monetization. Below $0.50 indicates either an unmonetized list or a misalignment between list content and offers.

RPS is the metric that tells you the most about the relationship between your audience and your business. A list of 500 subscribers at $4 RPS ($2,000/month) is more valuable than a list of 5,000 at $0.20 RPS ($1,000/month). List size is vanity. Revenue per subscriber is signal.

## Part Three: Content Metrics That Predict Sales

### Chapter 6: The Content Metrics Trap

Most content metrics are designed for media companies, not solopreneurs. Pageviews, impressions, reach, engagement rate, these metrics matter if your revenue comes from advertising. They are mostly noise if your revenue comes from selling products or services.

The content metric trap is spending time optimizing for metrics that have no direct connection to your revenue. A creator who grows their Instagram followers from 5,000 to 50,000 while keeping their revenue flat has optimized the wrong metric.

For a solopreneur, the only content metrics worth tracking are the ones that have a demonstrated or plausible connection to sales.

**Subscriber Growth Rate**

If your email list is your primary sales channel, your email subscriber growth rate is a content metric with direct revenue implications. Each new subscriber represents a potential future customer.

Track: Net new subscribers per month (new subscribers minus unsubscribes). A healthy growth rate for an active content business is 5 to 15 percent per month. Below 5 percent suggests either low content volume or ineffective lead capture. Above 20 percent is excellent, but worth watching for quality, large influxes of low-quality subscribers (from viral posts, giveaways, or misaligned traffic) can dilute your list.

**Click-Through Rate on Content CTAs**

Every piece of content you publish should have a call-to-action, whether it is to join your email list, download a free resource, or visit a product page. The CTR on these CTAs measures how effectively your content converts readers into engaged prospects.

Track this for your highest-volume content channels. If your blog posts generate 1,000 reads per month and 50 of those readers click through to your email signup, you have a 5 percent CTA CTR. If that drops to 0.5 percent, either your content is attracting the wrong readers or your CTA is poorly positioned.

**Content to Revenue Attribution**

For solopreneurs who publish consistently, understanding which content pieces contribute to sales is valuable data. This does not require sophisticated attribution software. It can be as simple as asking new customers "how did you find us?" and tracking the answers.

If your email newsletter consistently generates the most revenue attributions, that tells you to invest more in your newsletter. If your YouTube channel consistently generates the most attributions, that tells you YouTube deserves more of your time.

The goal is not perfect attribution, which is impossible. The goal is a rough signal about which channels are most connected to purchasing decisions.

### Chapter 7: The Content Quality Signal

Traffic and engagement are lagging indicators. By the time they show up in your analytics, the content decision has already been made. The leading indicator of content quality is reply rate.

**Reply Rate**

For email: What percentage of emails generate replies from subscribers?

A reply rate above 2 percent indicates that your content is generating genuine thought and emotion in readers. People do not reply to emails they found mildly interesting. They reply to emails that changed how they think about something, challenged a belief, or gave them something they needed.

Replies are also qualitative data. The questions and responses in your replies tell you what your audience is struggling with, what they want more of, and what they disagree with. This is the most actionable research available to a solo operator, and it costs nothing.

Track your reply rate monthly. If it drops, your content has become more generic or more predictable. The fix is to take a stronger position, share a more personal perspective, or challenge an assumption your audience holds.

## Part Four: Client and Customer Metrics

### Chapter 8: For Service-Based Solo Operators

If any part of your revenue comes from client services, consulting, or freelance work, you have a different set of metrics to track beyond the revenue figures above.

**Utilization Rate**

Utilization rate is the percentage of your available working time that is billed to clients. For a solo service provider working forty hours per week, a 75 percent utilization rate means thirty billable hours. At a rate of $100 per hour, that produces $3,000 per week.

Tracking utilization reveals the real constraint on your revenue. If your utilization is already at 80 to 90 percent and you want more revenue, the only lever is raising your rates. You cannot work more hours without sacrificing everything else. This is the math that forces the rate conversation that most freelancers avoid.

Utilization above 90 percent is a warning sign. You have no buffer for administrative work, business development, or the inevitable disruptions that every business faces. If you are consistently at 90 percent or above, you are one sick day away from a missed deadline.

**Average Client Lifetime Value**

In a service business, some clients engage once and end the relationship. Others become long-term accounts. The difference in revenue is enormous.

Track the total revenue generated per client over the entire client relationship. What is the average? What separates the high-LTV clients from the single-engagement clients?

High-LTV clients usually share characteristics: they have ongoing problems that need ongoing solutions, they have high trust in your work, they were referred by someone you already know. Low-LTV clients usually engaged you for a specific project, were acquired through cold outreach or advertising, and made their decision largely on price.

Knowing this pattern tells you where to invest in business development. If referral clients have 3x the LTV of cold-acquired clients, your time is better spent building relationships that generate referrals than running advertising.

**Client Satisfaction Metric**

Client satisfaction is a leading indicator of repeat business and referrals. The simplest way to track it is the Net Promoter Score (NPS) question: "On a scale of 0 to 10, how likely are you to recommend our work to a colleague or friend?"

Ask this after every engagement. Track the scores. Average scores above 8 indicate clients who are satisfied enough to refer. Scores below 7 indicate clients who are unlikely to return and may not refer.

Pay attention to the actual distribution, not just the average. A 7.5 average with most scores clustered at 6 to 8 is very different from a 7.5 average with half the scores at 10 and half at 5. The bimodal distribution tells you something about inconsistency in your delivery.

## Part Five: Financial Health Metrics

### Chapter 9: Cash Flow vs. Profit

Profitable businesses fail. This seems paradoxical but it happens frequently in small businesses, including solo operations, because profit is an accounting concept and cash flow is reality.

You can show a profit on paper while being unable to pay your bills if your revenue is coming in slower than your expenses are going out. This is a cash flow problem, not a profitability problem.

For most solo digital product businesses, cash flow is simple because revenue is immediate. You sell something, the money is in your account within days. There are no invoices to collect, no net-30 payment terms, no accounts receivable. If this describes your business, cash flow is rarely a problem.

For service businesses that invoice clients, cash flow requires active management. Track:

**Outstanding Invoices**

The total dollar amount of invoices that have been issued but not yet paid. If this number is growing month over month while your revenue is flat, your clients are paying slower or you are not following up effectively on overdue invoices.

**Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)**

The average number of days it takes a client to pay an invoice. Calculate it as: (Outstanding Invoices / Monthly Revenue) x 30.

A DSO of 30 days means clients pay in roughly one billing cycle. A DSO above 45 days signals that you either have clients with slow payment habits or that your invoicing and follow-up process is loose. High DSO creates cash flow pressure even when the business is profitable.

**Cash Runway**

How many months can your business operate without any new revenue? Cash runway is cash on hand divided by monthly operating expenses.

A solo operator should maintain a minimum of three months of operating cash as a buffer. Six months is better. This runway protects you from the revenue interruptions that every business experiences: a slow month, a lost client, a product launch that underperforms.

If your runway is under two months, your next financial priority is building the buffer before anything else.

### Chapter 10: The Expense Audit

Most solopreneurs have more operating expenses than they think. Subscriptions accumulate silently. Software tools get added during a productive weekend and never canceled. The monthly total is only discovered when you finally do the accounting.

Conduct a quarterly expense audit. List every recurring subscription, software tool, and service you are paying for. For each one, answer three questions:

1. Did I use this in the past thirty days?
2. Does this directly contribute to revenue generation or would stopping it noticeably affect my output?
3. Is there a free or cheaper alternative that would do the same job?

Any subscription that fails questions 1 and 2 should be canceled immediately. Most solo operators recover $50 to $200 per month in unused subscriptions from a single audit. Over a year, that is $600 to $2,400 in recovered margin.

Beyond subscriptions, look at your three largest expense categories. For most solopreneurs, these are software, education, and marketing. Each deserves its own analysis.

Software: Are you paying for tools that overlap in function? Are there free tiers that would meet your current needs?

Education: Courses, books, memberships. These are real costs that produce real returns, but the return should be trackable. If you bought a $500 course and cannot identify a specific change in your business resulting from it, that course was an entertainment expense, not a business investment.

Marketing: If you are spending money on advertising, what is your cost per acquisition? If you cannot calculate it, stop spending on advertising until you can.

## Part Six: The Monthly Dashboard

### Chapter 11: Building Your Dashboard

Your monthly dashboard should answer six questions in under five minutes:

1. Is my revenue growing, flat, or declining?
2. Am I profitable this month?
3. Is my conversion working?
4. Is my content building my business?
5. Are my clients or customers satisfied?
6. Is my business financially healthy?

One metric answers each question. Here is the dashboard structure.

**The Six-Metric Monthly Dashboard**

| Metric | This Month | Last Month | 3-Month Avg | Trend |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Net Revenue | | | | |
| Net Profit Margin | | | | |
| Email List CTR or Sales Page CVR | | | | |
| Net New Email Subscribers | | | | |
| Customer Satisfaction Score | | | | |
| Cash Runway (months) | | | | |

Fill this in on the first working day of each month using the previous month's data. The trend column is the most important: up arrow, down arrow, or flat line. You do not need perfect numbers. You need to know which direction each metric is moving.

If three or more metrics are trending down simultaneously, you have a business problem that requires attention. One metric trending down is noise. Three moving together is signal.

**The One-Hour Monthly Review**

Once per month, spend sixty minutes with your dashboard and a blank document. Write answers to these prompts:

- What drove the revenue results this month? Specific sources, not general causes.
- What was my most effective acquisition or retention activity this month?
- What did I spend money on that did not produce a return?
- What would I do differently if I were starting the month over?
- What is the single most important thing I should focus on next month based on the metrics?

This process converts data into decisions. Data without this review process is an archive. Data with this review process is a feedback loop.

### Chapter 12: Metrics to Deliberately Ignore

Knowing what not to track is as important as knowing what to track. These are the metrics that create the illusion of progress while distracting from the metrics that actually matter.

**Social Media Follower Count**

Followers are a vanity metric unless your revenue model is directly tied to follower count (advertising-based media businesses). For a solopreneur selling products or services, a list of 500 highly engaged email subscribers will consistently outperform 50,000 social media followers in terms of revenue generation.

If your follower count is growing, that is fine. Do not optimize for it.

**Website Pageviews**

Pageviews tell you traffic volume. They do not tell you traffic quality. A post that goes viral and generates 100,000 pageviews from people who are not your target customer is a worse business outcome than a targeted piece that generates 2,000 pageviews from highly qualified prospects.

Track subscriber growth, not pageviews.

**Gross Revenue (in isolation)**

As covered in Part One, gross revenue without the context of expenses and profit margin tells you nothing about whether your business is healthy. A $20,000 gross revenue month with $18,000 in expenses is worse than a $10,000 month with $3,000 in expenses.

Always pair revenue with margin.

**Email Open Rate**

Unreliable due to privacy protection changes across email clients. Use click-through rate as the primary email engagement metric instead.

**Any Metric You Cannot Act On**

If a metric is consistently in a range you cannot change with a specific decision, stop tracking it. Metrics are only useful when they are actionable. If you do not know what you would do differently based on the number being higher or lower, the metric is noise.

## Part Seven: Advanced Metrics for Growth Stage

### Chapter 13: When Simple Is Not Enough

The six-metric dashboard described above is appropriate for a solo operator generating under $10,000 per month. It captures the essential signals without creating data overload.

Once your business is consistently above $10,000 per month and you are making decisions about where to allocate meaningful time and capital, a few additional metrics become useful.

**Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)**

CAC is the total cost of acquiring a new customer, including marketing spend, time invested in content creation (valued at an hourly rate), and any paid channels.

For a solopreneur who relies on organic content, CAC includes the time cost of content production. If you spend four hours per week on content and your content generates three new customers per month, your time CAC per customer is (4 hours x 4 weeks x your hourly rate) divided by 3. At a $100 hourly rate, that is $533 per customer.

Whether that is acceptable depends on your average customer LTV. If your average customer spends $100 once, a $533 CAC is catastrophically unprofitable. If they spend $2,000 over two years, it is excellent.

Knowing your CAC lets you evaluate channel investments rationally rather than emotionally.

**Customer Lifetime Value to CAC Ratio (LTV:CAC)**

LTV:CAC is the ratio of how much a customer is worth over their lifetime to how much it costs to acquire them. A ratio of 3:1 means you earn three dollars for every dollar spent on acquisition. This is generally considered the minimum healthy ratio. Above 5:1 means you are likely under-investing in acquisition. Below 2:1 means your acquisition costs are eating your margin.

**Refund Rate**

Refund rate is the percentage of purchases that are refunded. For digital products, a healthy refund rate is 2 to 5 percent. Above 10 percent indicates that your product is not delivering on its promise, that your marketing is attracting the wrong buyers, or that your refund policy is too liberal.

A rising refund rate is an early warning signal. It often appears before revenue declines because customers who cannot get a refund will simply stop purchasing and stop referring rather than filing a dispute. When refund rates start climbing, investigate what changed: a new product, a new traffic source, a new marketing angle.

### Chapter 14: Product Portfolio Metrics

For solopreneurs with multiple digital products, understanding the performance of each product is necessary for portfolio decisions.

**Revenue Per Product**

Track gross revenue, net revenue, and unit sales for each product monthly. Look for:

- Products that consistently sell without significant marketing effort (your best products)
- Products that require significant promotion to generate any sales (positioning or product problems)
- Products that have declining sales trends (market saturation or relevance decay)

**Conversion Rate by Traffic Source**

If different traffic sources send visitors to your product pages, track the conversion rate for each source separately. Email list traffic almost always converts at higher rates than social media traffic. If your email list conversion rate is dropping, something in your nurture process has degraded. If cold traffic conversion is dropping, either your ads or your landing page has a problem.

**Bundle and Upsell Attachment Rate**

If you offer bundles or upsells, the attachment rate is the percentage of single-product buyers who add the bundle or upsell. A healthy attachment rate varies by price point and positioning. If you are not tracking this at all, you are missing one of the highest-margin revenue opportunities in your business.

## Part Eight: Metrics and Decision-Making

### Chapter 15: From Metrics to Decisions

Metrics are not the destination. Decisions are. Every metric you track should have a clear decision tree: if this metric is in range X, I do nothing; if it drops to range Y, I investigate; if it drops to range Z, I act.

Here is the decision framework for the six core dashboard metrics.

**Net Revenue**

- Trending up 5 to 20 percent: Healthy growth. Continue current strategy.
- Flat for three consecutive months: Investigate. Is the problem acquisition, conversion, or retention?
- Declining: Immediate attention. Identify the component that changed (traffic, conversion, churn) and address it.

**Net Profit Margin**

- Above 40 percent: Healthy. Review expenses annually.
- 25 to 40 percent: Acceptable but review whether expenses can be reduced without affecting output.
- Below 25 percent: Expense audit required. Identify what is consuming margin.

**Email CTR or Sales Page CVR**

- At or above benchmark: Leave it alone. Optimizing things that are working is a distraction.
- 20 percent below benchmark: Test one change. Give it two to four weeks before evaluating.
- 50 percent below benchmark: Structural problem. Evaluate the entire sequence or page from the beginning.

**Net New Email Subscribers**

- Growing month over month: Content and lead capture are working.
- Flat: Evaluate your lead magnet and content CTA placement.
- Declining: Something in your acquisition channel has changed. Find it.

**Customer Satisfaction Score**

- Above 8: Strong. Document what you are doing that drives this and systematize it.
- 6 to 8: Acceptable. Ask clients who score you 6 or 7 specifically what would have made their experience a 9.
- Below 6: Urgent. Investigate delivery quality immediately.

**Cash Runway**

- Above 6 months: Strong position. Can take calculated risks.
- 3 to 6 months: Adequate. Prioritize building the buffer when there is slack.
- Below 3 months: Build the buffer before any other financial priority.

### Chapter 16: The Metrics That Predict the Next Twelve Months

If you could track only two metrics for the next twelve months, track these.

First, net new email subscribers per month. The health of your email list is the leading indicator of the health of your business. Email subscribers who have opted in because of your content are your most likely future customers. If this number is growing consistently, your business has a healthy acquisition engine. If it is flat or declining, your revenue ceiling is approaching.

Second, MRR or returning customer revenue percentage. This metric measures whether your business is building cumulative momentum or resetting every month. A business where 40 percent of revenue comes from returning customers in month twelve is fundamentally more stable than one where 95 percent comes from new customers every month, regardless of total revenue.

These two metrics together tell you: are you building an audience, and are you converting that audience into loyal customers? If both are growing, your business is on a compounding trajectory. If either is stagnant, you have a clear single focus for the next quarter.

## Final Note

The simplest financial truth in a solo operation: you cannot manage what you do not measure, and you cannot act on what you measure but do not review.

Twenty minutes per month updating this dashboard and sixty minutes reviewing it will give you more clarity about your business than any amount of daily analytics checking. The creators who compound their businesses year over year are not the ones who track the most metrics. They are the ones who track the right metrics and actually do something with them.
