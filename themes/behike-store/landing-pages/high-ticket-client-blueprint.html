Why You Are Still Underpriced

Three structural reasons the rate stays low.

None of them are about the quality of your work. The work is usually fine. The structure is not.

01

Volume thinking keeps the ceiling low

Twenty clients at $300 each produces the same monthly revenue as two clients at $3,000. The $300 version requires twenty contracts, twenty onboarding calls, twenty communication threads, and twenty invoices. The math of volume-based service delivery looks fine on a spreadsheet and destroys you in practice.

02

Generalist positioning commoditizes the rate

"I do [category]" competes with every other person who does that category. The buyer's decision criterion becomes price. When price is the decision criterion, the rate converges toward the cheapest option. Specific positioning removes you from that comparison entirely.

03

The offer is not structured for high-ticket pricing

High-ticket clients are paying for certainty about an outcome. An unstructured service offering creates uncertainty. Specific deliverables, defined timelines, documented processes, and clear revision parameters are what separate a $300 project from a $3,000 one, even when the underlying skill is identical.
