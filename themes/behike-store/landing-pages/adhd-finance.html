The problem

Every other system was built for a different brain.

ADHD is not a discipline problem. Budget apps that fail you are a design problem.

Standard budgeting apps assume you will remember to open them.

YNAB, Mint, and spreadsheet templates require you to log in regularly, categorize every transaction, and feel motivated by watching numbers move. That assumption does not describe you.

ADHD makes time blindness a financial problem.

The ADHD brain has a weaker response to delayed consequences. Future-you running out of money feels abstract. Present-you wanting something feels urgent and real. Systems built around delayed-reward motivation miss you entirely.

Most budget systems have no recovery mechanism. You fall behind, the gap feels too big to close, and the system dies. That is not a character flaw. It is a predictable output of bad system design.

Weekly, not daily.

Daily check-ins fail because one missed day feels like failure. This system runs on a 10-minute weekly review. One session per week is a commitment most ADHD brains can keep. Three bad days do not break the whole month.

Visual, not numerical.

Five categories, color-coded. You see where your money goes without parsing rows of figures. The goal is instant comprehension, not accounting precision. You understand your budget in under thirty seconds.

Design decisions

Why this system is built the way it is.

Every structural choice has a documented reason. Here are four that matter most.

Five categories only

Cognitive load research is clear: the more categories in a tracking system, the less likely a person is to maintain it past month one. This effect is stronger with ADHD because every categorization decision is a small cognitive cost. With five categories, the question "where does this go?" almost always has an obvious answer in under three seconds.

24-hour rule

Willpower depletes faster for ADHD brains. The fix is structural, not motivational. A 24-hour pause before unplanned purchases over $20 removes the decision from the moment of maximum impulsivity. You do not say no. You say "ask me tomorrow." Research consistently shows desire drops significantly within hours of the initial trigger.

Weekly not monthly reviews

Monthly feedback loops are too long for ADHD brains. By the time you review, the data from week one is emotionally irrelevant. A week is short enough that the numbers are still fresh and short enough to course-correct before drift becomes a pattern.

Built-in recovery rule

Every system fails occasionally. Most systems have no protocol for what happens when they do, so a miss becomes a quit. This system has one rule: never miss two reviews in a row. One miss is data. Two misses is avoidance. The rule reframes the first miss as normal instead of shameful.
