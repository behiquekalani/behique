Inside the Guide

A sample of what you will read.

Part 1

The problem with hourly rates

When you charge hourly, every efficiency gain you make penalizes you. You get faster at a skill, you earn less for the same outcome. You find a better tool that saves two hours, the client pays less. You are financially rewarded for being slow and penalized for being good. Project pricing fixes both problems. The client pays for the outcome, not the time.

Part 2

Calculating your real floor

Most freelancers skip the minimum viable rate calculation and then wonder why the money feels tight even when business seems good. A realistic figure for a solo freelancer is 20 to 25 billable hours per week. Not 40. The formula: monthly expenses plus profit target, divided by actual billable hours per month. This number is not what you charge. It is what you cannot go below.

Part 3

Price resistance is not one objection

"That's too expensive" is almost never a complete sentence. It is the start of a conversation about something else. Understanding what it actually means determines how you respond. There are three distinct objections behind those words, and treating them as one leads to solutions that address none of them.

Part 4

Rate increases are not negotiable

Discounting your rate increase for one client signals to all clients that rate increases are negotiable. They are not. The principle applies consistently: a rate increase stated professionally and with appropriate notice is a sign of a healthy, growing freelance practice, not an act of aggression against the relationship.
