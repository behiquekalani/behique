Section Previews

A look inside

On Case Study Structure

A case study that converts has a five-section architecture. It is not a testimonial, not a blog post. It is a structured argument that a specific buyer, facing a specific problem, hired someone and got a specific result.

  • The client in one line (who they are, not their title)
  • The problem before, in concrete terms
  • Why other approaches failed
  • What you did, specifically
  • The result, with timeline

On Testimonial Questions

Most solopreneurs ask: "Would you write a testimonial?" The client agrees, sits down, has nothing specific to say, and produces a generic paragraph. One question changes everything: "What was true before we worked together, and what is different now?"

  • The one question that always produces specifics
  • Three follow-up questions for deeper answers
  • How to lightly edit without losing the client's voice
  • When to ask and when to wait

On Before/After Framing

The before has to be specific enough that your ideal prospect recognizes themselves in it. "She was struggling with content strategy" is a weak before. "She was posting daily and had not booked a client through social in four months" is a before that converts.

  • Making the before concrete and recognizable
  • Adding timeline to make the after credible
  • The emotional layer that numbers cannot capture
  • How specificity eliminates the feeling of bragging

On Deploying Results

A results portfolio that lives in a folder on your desktop is not a marketing asset. It becomes one when it is deployed. The same case study can appear on your sales page, in your proposal, in your social content, and in your discovery call without being recreated each time.

  • Leading the sales page with results, not credentials
  • The results section in proposals that reduces perceived risk
  • One client win per week as a complete content strategy
  • Result stories that close discovery calls
