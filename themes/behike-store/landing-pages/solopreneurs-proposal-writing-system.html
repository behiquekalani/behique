Why proposals fail

The proposal was lost before it was read

Most proposals fail for one of three reasons. None of them require more talent to fix. They require a better process.

The length problem

A four-page proposal signals one thing to the client: the person writing it is not confident enough to distill the work. Length does not communicate thoroughness. It communicates uncertainty.

Leading with credentials

Opening a proposal with your background makes the document about you. The client is trying to answer one question: can this person fix my problem? They cannot find that answer in a credential list.

Pricing with apology

Hedging language around the number, burying the price, presenting a range instead of a figure: each behavior signals that you are not confident in your own investment. Clients take that signal seriously.

No follow-up system

Proposals stall for reasons unrelated to interest. A follow-up sequence that adds value with each message converts stalled deals. "Just checking in" does not. Most solopreneurs send one or neither.

What is inside

The 1-page proposal that wins at higher rates

The 1-page framework has five sections, each with a specific function. Problem statement. Proposed solution. Success criteria. Investment. Next steps. Nothing more. The constraint forces clarity, and clarity communicates confidence.

This guide covers the full system: writing scope that protects you without killing the deal, presenting pricing without qualification, placing social proof where it reduces risk rather than padding the document, the three-message follow-up sequence, and the retrospective log that turns lost proposals into a compounding improvement process.
