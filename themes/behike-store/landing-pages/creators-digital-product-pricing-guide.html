The Core Argument

Pricing is not a math problem. It is a perception problem. The number you put on a product signals the kind of customer you expect and the kind of product you believe you built.

Most creators price based on what they feel comfortable charging. That feeling is calibrated by insecurity, by comparison to the wrong competitors, and by a fear of rejection that gets reframed as staying accessible. This guide corrects that.

What the guide actually says

No summaries. The reasoning inside the guide, as it appears.

On value-based pricing
If your guide helps a freelancer raise their rates by $500 per month, the guide is worth some fraction of that ongoing gain. Pricing it at $49 is not audacious. It is conservative. Your customer does not care how long you took. They care what changes in their life after they apply what you taught.
On the $9 tier
A $9 product is a short, focused deliverable. The buyer is not expecting a transformation. They are buying a specific tool that solves one discrete problem. Your cheapest product needs to over-deliver. Not in length, but in usefulness. The buyer who feels like they stole something at $9 will tell people about you.
On limited-time pricing
Buyers are not naive. They recognize fake urgency immediately, and once they do, they discount everything you tell them about value. The rule: only use limited-time pricing when the limit is real. Trust is a more valuable long-term asset than the extra conversions from a fake countdown.
On raising prices
Do not apologize for the increase. "I've invested significantly in expanding this guide and the price reflects that" is honest. Apologetic pricing signals that you are not confident the product is worth the new price, which makes buyers less confident as well.
