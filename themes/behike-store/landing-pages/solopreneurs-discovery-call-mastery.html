What you will actually read

On questions

Impact questions transform vague dissatisfaction into a concrete business problem

When a prospect says "it is costing me about 15 hours per week and I could easily bill $200 of that if I had the time back," you have created the value anchor for your proposal. That calculation comes from one question: "What does that end up costing you in terms of time?" Ask it on every call.

On pricing

The moment you justify your price before the prospect reacts, you lose leverage

State your number with the same tone you would use to state the time of day. Then stop talking. Many solopreneurs immediately start defending the price because the silence feels uncomfortable. That silence is not a problem. It is the prospect processing. Let them process.

On objections

A maybe is a question the prospect has not asked out loud yet

When a prospect says they need to think about it, something specific is unresolved. Ask: "As you think it over, is there a specific part I can clarify right now?" That question surfaces the real objection in most cases. A resolved objection on the call is always better than one left to grow in their inbox.

On follow-up

The Day 14 email gives the prospect an easy out and gets more responses because of it

People who feel pressured go quiet. The Day 14 message says: "If the timing is not right, completely understood." That sentence removes pressure. Ironically, prospects who receive that message respond more often than those who receive a harder close. Respect converts better than pressure.
