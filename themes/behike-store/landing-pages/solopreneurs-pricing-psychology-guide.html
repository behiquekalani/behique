Section Previews

A few excerpts from the guide.

On what buyers are actually paying for

Buyers do not pay for your time. They pay to close a gap between where they are and where they want to be. Your price should reflect the size of that gap, your ability to close it, and what alternatives they have for closing it.

A business owner who needs a website that generates leads is not buying web development hours. They are buying the ability to stop losing customers to a competitor with a better site. That framing changes the pricing conversation completely.

On the decoy effect

Design your product suite with the middle tier as the true target. The entry tier establishes that a lower option exists. The premium tier makes the middle feel affordable by comparison. The middle tier should have the highest margin, the clearest deliverables, and the strongest positioning.

For the decoy to work, the premium tier cannot be linearly priced. If entry is $1,200 and standard is $2,800, your premium should sit at $5,000-$7,000, not $3,500. That distance is what creates the decoy pull toward your middle offer.

Most buyers choose the middle. Foundation makes it feel reasonable. Intensive makes it feel within reach.

On the alternative to discounting

If a buyer needs a lower price point and you want to close the deal, offer less scope rather than less price. A reduced deliverable at your full rate communicates that your pricing is based on value. A full deliverable at a reduced rate communicates that your pricing was arbitrary.

"I can do the core deliverables for $1,800 or the full package at my standard $2,800. I cannot do the full package for $1,800." That sentence is complete and clear. Most buyers who were price objecting were actually scope objecting.
