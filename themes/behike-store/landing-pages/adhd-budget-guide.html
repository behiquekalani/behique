The problem

Every budget you've tried was built for a different brain.

Traditional budgeting tools are built on a set of assumptions. They assume you'll remember to log every transaction. That you can sit through weekly review sessions. That 47 spending categories will feel like control rather than overwhelm. That willpower is enough to stop impulse purchases.

None of those assumptions hold for an ADHD brain. The problem is not your character. It is that the systems were not designed for how your brain actually works.

ADHD creates impulsive spending, because your reward system is wired to prioritize immediate gratification. It makes tracking hard to sustain, because maintaining low-stimulation routines requires exactly the executive function ADHD impairs. It creates "out of sight, out of mind" problems with subscriptions and bills. And it makes complicated systems collapse within weeks.

This guide is built around the constraints of an ADHD brain, not against them.

"Adults with ADHD are significantly more likely to report financial problems, including difficulty paying bills on time, impulsive spending, and failure to maintain a budget. Research consistently shows these are not motivational failures. They are direct consequences of ADHD's impact on executive function, working memory, and impulse control."

Based on research published in the Journal of Attention Disorders and ADHD financial behavior studies (2015-2022)
