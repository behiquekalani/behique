The ownership question nobody asks first

When you publish on Substack, Substack knows your subscribers. When you publish on a self-hosted platform, you know your subscribers. That distinction sounds minor until you hit 10,000 subscribers and realize the platform can change its terms, throttle your reach, or sunset a feature you built your workflow around.

Substack discovery is real and worth real money

Writers who publish consistently on Substack receive organic subscriber growth from the platform itself. No self-hosted newsletter gives you that. ConvertKit does not send your emails to potential subscribers. Ghost does not recommend your posts to its users. Only Substack does this, and for a creator in the early stages of building an audience, it is worth real money.

At $5,000/month, the 10% fee is $500/month

When your newsletter income is material to your finances, a 10% revenue cut to Substack becomes expensive. Beehiiv at $99 per month is significantly cheaper. This is not a small calculation. Over a year, the difference is meaningful capital that could fund other parts of your business.

The hybrid model explained

A creator can publish on Substack, grow through its recommendation engine, and simultaneously funnel their best readers to a ConvertKit or Beehiiv list that they own outright. This works because Substack allows you to embed links to external sign-up forms. The cost is complexity. The benefit is discovery without dependency.
