On cost

The real question is what it costs you not to hire

If you are billing at $150 per hour and spending 10 hours per week on tasks worth $20 per hour, you are losing $1,300 per week in opportunity cost. A $1,000 per month assistant pays for itself many times over. The math is not complicated. The decision is.

On filtering

The attention-to-detail test eliminates 80 percent of weak applicants

End your job post with one specific instruction: "Start your application with the word pineapple so I know you read the full post." Anyone who doesn't include it gets no response. The people who remain read carefully and follow instructions. Both are non-negotiable in a hire.

On delegation

One hour of SOP creation saves five hours of confusion

Document every task you plan to delegate before your hire starts. A Loom recording plus a written outline is enough. You will also find inefficiencies during this process. Fix them before you delegate. Never hand off a broken process.

On management

Clear systems plus decreasing check-ins

Most first-time hirers either micromanage or abdicate. The right approach is clear SOPs on day one, daily check-ins for the first two weeks, weekly check-ins after that, and expanding scope the moment they've proven they can handle the current one.
